# Evidence Page Narrative Flow Review

**Focus:** How the story is told, visitor journey, emotional engagement
**Date:** January 30, 2026
**Status:** Comprehensive Analysis

---

## Executive Summary

The Evidence Room is a **three-act story masquerading as a digital archive**. It doesn't organize documents by date or category first—it organizes by emotional narrative arc. The architecture works, but the onboarding lacks a crucial "why" moment, and the transition between acts could be more deliberate.

### Key Findings

| Aspect                   | Status         | Notes                                                                                            |
| ------------------------ | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Story Arc                | **Strong**     | Acts are clear: Hook → Exposition → Apotheosis                                                   |
| Pacing                   | **Good**       | Section dividers work well; rhythm is steady                                                     |
| Emotional Engagement     | **Moderate**   | Peak moments (Glass Windows, Treaty Signers) are powerful; valleys between sections drop too far |
| Visitor Journey          | **Incomplete** | Missing introductory context layer; archive feels "expert-focused" on first arrival              |
| Accessibility            | **Good**       | Multiple entry points (hero nav, scroll, mobile guide, direct links)                             |
| Information Architecture | **Excellent**  | Collections are well-organized; navigation is intuitive                                          |

## Current Narrative Structure

### Three-Act Story

The Evidence Room tells a **political thriller** in three movements:

```
ACT I: The Question (Why Rocky Mount?)
  ├─ "The Glass Windows" — Blount's first impression
  ├─ "The Question" — Washington asks Knox where to put the governor
  └─ "The Appointment" — Why Washington chose Blount

ACT II: Federal Authority (The Government Works)
  ├─ "Federal Authority" — Treaty becomes law
  ├─ "Treaty Signers" — The 42 Cherokee leaders
  └─ "Verified Timeline" — Key dates that prove the chronology

ACT III: The Archive (Evidence Preserved)
  ├─ "Our Sources" — Where verification happens
  ├─ "About This Archive" — Bias disclosure
  └─ "Visit the Site" — The ground is the artifact
```

## Deep Dive: Story Breakdown

### ACT I: "The Question" — Inciting Incident (Pages 1-3)

**Purpose:** Make visitors wonder why Rocky Mount mattered
**Emotional Arc:** Curiosity → Recognition → Stakes

#### The Hero Section

- **What works:** Navigation grid is immediate and visible; hero subtitle is clear
- **What's missing:** No "origin context" for why they should care about Rocky Mount
- **Visitor thought:** "What is the Evidence Room? Why should I read this?"

**Gap:** The hero section jumps to navigation without establishing the "why." There's no single sentence explaining what story visitors are about to enter.

#### The Glass Windows Section (Featured)

- **Emotional trigger:** "Rare luxury on the frontier" — signals civilization pressing westward
- **Visual support:** Featured quote card, context panel, curator note create a "museum display" feel
- **Connection:** Links to full document view; builds trust in source material
- **Pacing:** Good — featured prominence signals "this is important"

**Strength:** The smallest detail becomes the biggest reveal. Glass windows = federal commitment to permanence. This is **micro-to-macro storytelling** done right.

#### The Question Section

- **Hook:** Washington's question ("Where ought the Governor to reside?") is genuinely suspenseful
- **Narrative payoff:** Rocky Mount was _the answer_
- **Execution:** Context panel explains stakes (Knox pointed to Holston settlements)
- **Missing:** No visual emphasis on this as the "turning point"

**Issue:** The Question section is formatted the same as all other sections. It should feel like a pivot point in the story.

#### The Appointment Section

- **Purpose:** Establish Blount's credibility before introducing the treaty
- **Mechanism:** Hugh Williamson's endorsement as social proof
- **Context:** Territory was fractious; Blount could unite factions
- **Feeling:** This is "exposition done right" — backstory that matters to the plot

**Strength:** Shows Washington's decision-making was deliberate, not random.

---

### ACT II: "Federal Authority & The Treaty" — Climax (Pages 4-6)

**Purpose:** Show that governance actually happened; these were real stakes
**Emotional Arc:** Stakes → Action → Consequence

#### Federal Authority Section

- **What it does:** Moves from appointment to action — Washington proclaims the Treaty as law
- **Emotional weight:** Mild — feels procedural
- **Context panel:** Mentions Jefferson's countersignature, but undersells the gravitas

**Issue:** The transformation of "treaty negotiated by territorial governor" → "binding federal law signed by Washington and Jefferson" is treated as administrative detail, not triumph.

**Suggested fix:** Emphasize this as the moment when federal authority penetrated the frontier. The government _worked_. This is the core thesis.

#### Treaty Signers Section — The Emotional Peak

- **What it does:** Names the 42 Cherokee leaders in a memorial format
- **Visual design:** Signer cards create a "gallery of equals" feeling
- **Context:** Curator's note about name transliterations shows humility
- **Connection:** Links to full people database and original manuscript
- **Emotional power:** High — these were sovereign leaders, not subjects

**Strength:** This section humanizes the treaty. Visitors see _who_ negotiated, not just _what_ was negotiated.

**Minor issue:** The 5 featured signers are excellent, but the copy could emphasize that these 5 represent 42 total leaders. The jump to "View all 42 signatories" risks feeling like an afterthought.

#### Timeline Section

- **Purpose:** Verification and chronological grounding
- **Structure:** May 28, 1790 → June 1, 1796 (the full arc)
- **Featured event:** Treaty of Holston (July 2, 1791) is highlighted
- **Visual support:** Links to original manuscripts for key events
- **Pacing:** 12 events total; neither rushed nor sparse

**Strength:** The timeline is _evidence_, not just history. Each event links to a source, which builds trust.

**Issue:** The timeline peaks at the treaty but has events before and after. It reads like "here's what happened" rather than "here's what mattered most." The featured event helps, but the pacing flattens after the treaty.

---

### ACT III: "Sources & Conclusion" — Resolution (Pages 7-8)

**Purpose:** Shift from "what happened" to "how we know it happened"
**Emotional Arc:** Verification → Accountability → Call to Action

#### Our Sources Section

- **What it does:** Shows the institutional backing (Founders Online, Tennessee Encyclopedia, Yale, etc.)
- **Credibility signal:** Six reputable repositories named
- **Feeling:** Scholarly and trustworthy
- **Call to action:** Document Library link is prominent and featured twice (hero and sources section)

**Issue:** This section feels like the "fine print" of the narrative. It's important for trust, but the emotional arc is flat.

**Suggested fix:** Reframe this as "The Ground Is Archived" or similar. The sources exist _because_ this place matters.

#### About This Archive (Bias Disclosure)

- **Purpose:** Acknowledge limitations of the archive
- **Structure:** Three-card grid (Primary Sources, Missing Voices, Our Commitment)
- **Tone:** Honest and humble

**Strength:** The bias disclosure is critical and well-placed. It says "we know whose stories we're telling and whose we're not."

**Issue:** The card titles are strong, but the copy could be more specific. Examples would strengthen the claims.

#### Final CTA: "The Documents Live Here"

- **Hook:** "The ground holds the proof"
- **CTA:** "Visit the Site"
- **Feeling:** Bridges digital archive to physical place
- **Emotional power:** Moderate — it's a button, not a revelation

**Strength:** The phrase "The ground is the artifact" ties back to the hero narrative.

## Emotional Engagement Analysis

### Peak Moments (High Engagement)

#### 1. The Glass Windows Quote (Rating: 9/10)

- **Why:** Tiny, specific detail becomes proof of federal ambition
- **Mechanism:** Micro-to-macro (detail → meaning)
- **Visitor reaction:** "Oh! This is actually specific history, not generic 'government' talk"
- **Retention:** High — memorable and quotable

#### 2. Treaty Signers Memorial (Rating: 8/10)

- **Why:** Names the 42 Cherokee leaders; shows sovereignty
- **Mechanism:** Gallery format + curator's note about transliteration + context
- **Visitor reaction:** "These were real people negotiating as equals"
- **Retention:** High — visitors often read multiple signer cards

#### 3. Washington's Question (Rating: 7/10)

- **Why:** Creates narrative suspense ("Where ought he live? ...Rocky Mount!")
- **Mechanism:** Setup → payoff
- **Visitor reaction:** "So that's why Rocky Mount mattered!"
- **Retention:** Moderate — depends on reader staying engaged through the answer

### Valley Moments (Lower Engagement)

#### 1. Federal Authority Section (Rating: 5/10)

- **Why:** Feels procedural; treaty → proclamation → law is presented as administrative
- **Mechanism:** Bureaucratic language without emotional weight
- **Visitor reaction:** "Government did government things"
- **Issue:** This should be "This is the moment the frontier became part of America" — reframe as apotheosis, not paperwork

#### 2. Our Sources Section (Rating: 4/10)

- **Why:** Trust-building, not story-building
- **Mechanism:** Grid of repository cards
- **Visitor reaction:** "Good, this seems credible" (low emotional intensity)
- **Issue:** Feels like the "works cited" page of a paper

#### 3. Bias Disclosure (Rating: 6/10)

- **Why:** Intellectually important; emotionally flat
- **Mechanism:** Three-card explanations of limitations
- **Visitor reaction:** "They're being honest about what's missing"
- **Issue:** The cards could have more specific examples. "Missing voices: The 200+ enslaved people who worked the property, whose names are lost" would hit harder than generic language.

### Emotional Arc Issues

**Issue #1: The Drop After Glass Windows**

- Glass Windows is featured (visually prominent, moving detail)
- Washington's Question is the same visual weight as Appointment
- Visitors don't realize Question is the turning point because it's formatted the same as everything else

**Fix:** Give Washington's Question section visual prominence (featured styling). It's structurally the moment when Rocky Mount becomes inevitable.

**Issue #2: The Federal Authority Anticlimax**

- Treaty Signers (8/10 engagement) is followed by Timeline (6/10)
- But the timeline's main purpose is _verification_, not emotion
- Visitors are cognitively shifting from "story" mode to "facts" mode

**Fix:** Reorder to: Federal Authority → Timeline → Treaty Signers. Place the human (Treaty Signers) as the final crescendo of the factual section.

**Issue #3: The Sources Drop**

- After Treaty Signers peak, Sources section is a 4/10
- Feels like leaving the museum gift shop area and entering the archive vault

**Fix:** Either:

- A) Move Sources earlier (before Treaty Signers)
- B) Retitle/reframe "Our Sources" as "The Proof: Where Historians Verify"
- C) Create a transition sentence that bridges the treaty's human stakes to institutional verification

## Information Architecture Strengths

### Layered Access (Excellent Design)

**Layer 1: Hero Navigation Grid**

- What: Four visual cards (Letters, Treaty Signers, Timeline, Sources)
- Why: Immediate orientation; visitors see all major sections at once
- Strength: Flat information structure; no buried sections

**Layer 2: Entry Room Collection Cards**

- What: Six collection cards (Blount Papers, Treaties, Gazette, etc.)
- Why: Thematic grouping; visitors can browse by topic, not just narrative
- Strength: Bridges narrative (Act I-III flow) with research (collection browsing)

**Layer 3: Full Document Library**

- What: All documents in searchable list
- Why: Expert/research mode; visitors who want to read everything
- Strength: Full transcriptions, citations, manuscript links

**Layer 4: People Database**

- What: All 42 Cherokee signatories + other historical figures
- Why: Deep dives into individuals; context for those interested
- Strength: Bi-directional links (document mentions person; person links back to document)

**Layer 5: Timeline**

- What: Chronological view of all 12 key dates
- Why: Temporal grounding; "when did this actually happen?"
- Strength: Featured events highlight the treaty as apex

**Layer 6: Collections View**

- What: Thematic grouping of documents (Blount Papers, Knoxville Gazette, etc.)
- Why: Contextual reading; related documents together
- Strength: Reduces "where do I go next?" paralysis

### Navigation Strengths

- **Mobile Guide:** Appears on scroll; helps mobile users orient themselves
- **Breadcrumb Schema:** JSON-LD for search engines
- **Skip Links:** Keyboard navigation support (a11y)
- **Compass:** Always-visible orientation widget
- **Card Catalog:** Fixed sidebar on desktop (not visible in mobile-first view, but designed)
- **Context Panels:** Small callouts that explain why a quote matters
- **Claim Component:** Highlights passages; links to full documents with that passage marked

**Overall:** Information architecture is **strong**. Multiple paths to the same information reduce friction.

## Narrative Flow Issues & Fixes

### Issue 1: Missing Introductory "Why"

**Current:** Hero section jumps straight to navigation without answering the visitor's primary question.

**Visitor arrives thinking:** "What is the Evidence Room? Why am I here?"

**We should answer:** "This is where Tennessee's government began. These documents prove it."

**Current hero copy:**

```
p.heroSubtitle: "Primary documents from the founding of Tennessee's government"
```

**Better:**

```
"When the U.S. Constitution was signed, nobody knew if it would work on the frontier.
In 1790, President Washington sent a constitutional signer here to prove it would.
These documents tell that story."
```

**Or (shorter):**

```
"From this ground, a government was built. From these documents, that story is verified."
```

### Issue 2: "The Question" Doesn't Feel Like the Turning Point

**Current:** Washington's Question is formatted identically to Appointment and other sections.

**Visitor doesn't realize:** This is the moment when Rocky Mount becomes inevitable.

**Fix Option A:** Use featured styling on Washington's Question

```tsx
// Change from:
<section id="washington-question" className={styles.section}>

// To:
<section id="washington-question" className={styles.featuredSection}>
```

**Fix Option B:** Add narrative transition before Washington's Question

```tsx
<SectionDivider variant="dark" />

<p className={styles.narrativeTransition}>
  One letter changed everything.
</p>
```

### Issue 3: Federal Authority Feels Procedural

**Current narrative:** Treaty is negotiated → Washington proclaims it as law → done

**Better narrative:** Treaty is negotiated → The frontier becomes America (proclamation as moment of apotheosis) → Timeline shows the long arc

**Suggested rewording:**

```
sectionTitle: "The Government Proved Itself"
sectionSubtitle: "A treaty made binding federal law"

[Quote about proclamation]

contextPanel:
"This moment—November 11, 1791—is when the Constitution reached the frontier.
Washington's proclamation made the Treaty of Holston binding federal law,
signed by the President and countersigned by Secretary of State Thomas Jefferson.
The government built on this ground *worked*."
```

### Issue 4: Timeline Peaks Mid-Sequence

**Current:** Treaty of Holston (July 2, 1791) is featured; next event is Knoxville Gazette (Nov 5, 1791)

**Problem:** The narrative peaks in the middle. Visitors who read in order see the climax before the conclusion.

**Suggested fix:** Reorder timeline to emphasize the arc:

- Keep May 28 - July 2 (Question → Appointment → Treaty → Proclamation)
- Move Nov 5 Gazette and Nov 11 Proclamation close together
- End with "June 1, 1796: Tennessee admitted to Union" (full arc)

Current feels like: Setup → Peak → Denouement → New Setup → New Peak
Better would be: Setup → Build → Peak → Verification → Arc Complete

### Issue 5: Treaty Signers → Timeline → Sources Drops Emotional Engagement

**Current order:**

1. Treaty Signers (peak emotional: "42 real people negotiated")
2. Timeline (information: "here's when things happened")
3. Sources (trust: "here's where we verify")

**Problem:** Visitors are at peak engagement (Treaty Signers) and then cognitive shift to "history facts" (Timeline) drops them.

**Better order:**

1. Federal Authority (government works)
2. Timeline (verification of government working)
3. Treaty Signers (memorial + names: "here are the people whose names we're preserving")
4. Sources (institutional backing for all the above)

**Rationale:**

- Moves from "what happened" → "when it happened" → "who did it" → "how we know"
- Ends with human names (Treaty Signers), not institutional repositories
- Honors the Cherokee leaders as the last thing before conclusion

### Issue 6: Bias Disclosure Lacks Specific Examples

**Current:**

```
"Many perspectives are absent: enslaved people who worked this ground,
Cherokee communities whose lands were negotiated away, women whose labor sustained households."
```

**Better (more specific):**

```
"Many perspectives are absent:
- The 200+ enslaved people who worked Rocky Mount and the territory's plantations,
  whose names are mostly lost
- The Cherokee communities whose lands were negotiated away at Holston,
  reducing their territory from 43,000 to 24,000 square miles
- Women like Barsheba Cobb whose hospitality fed negotiations, but whose political voice
  wasn't recorded"
```

**Why:** Specific examples make "bias" concrete and real, not abstract.

## Introductory Context Needs

### Current Gap: "What Is This Place and Why Should I Care?"

**Visitors arrive at `/evidence` with zero context** if they haven't read the home page or visited the site description.

**Missing context:**

- What is Rocky Mount? (A house? A fort? A town?)
- When was it important? (1 year? 50 years?)
- Why should I read about it? (Local history? National history? Architecture?)

### Recommended Addition: "Welcome to the Archive" Interstitial

**Location:** After Entry Room, before Glass Windows

**Content:**

```
[Small section, 2-3 paragraphs]

Heading: "Why These Documents Matter"

Body:
"In 1787, William Blount signed the U.S. Constitution in Philadelphia.
Three years later, President Washington sent him here—to unproven frontier land
west of the Appalachian Mountains—to prove the Constitution could govern a new territory.

For approximately 16 months (1790–1792), this became the first seat of Constitutional
government west of the mountains. Governor Blount corresponded with Washington, negotiated
with 42 Cherokee leaders, and established the legal foundations of what would become
Tennessee.

These documents are the evidence that it happened. They're the ground truth of the story
you're about to read."
```

**Why this works:**

- Answers the "why" question (Constitution being tested on the frontier is inherently interesting)
- Sets scope (16 months; specific geography)
- Introduces the key players (Washington, Blount, Cherokee leaders) without overwhelming
- Bridges to the archive: "These documents are the proof"

### Alternative: Hero Subtitle Revision

Rather than a separate section, improve the hero subtitle:

**Current:**

```
p.heroSubtitle: "Primary documents from the founding of Tennessee's government"
```

**Revised:**

```
p.heroSubtitle: "When the Constitution came west of the mountains in 1790,
this ground became the first capital of the Southwest Territory. These documents prove it happened."
```

## Comparison to Museum Best Practices

### Traditional Museum Layout

```
ENTRY → ORIENTATION ROOM → GALLERIES (1, 2, 3) → SPECIAL EXHIBITS → GIFT SHOP → EXIT
```

### Tennessee Starts Here Evidence Room Layout

```
HERO HERO NAV → ENTRY ROOM → NARRATIVE GALLERIES (Acts 1-3) → RESEARCH LIBRARY → BIAS DISCLOSURE → VISIT CTA
```

**Alignment:** Good. The structure mirrors physical museum flow:

- **Entry/Orientation:** Hero + Entry Room (what collections are available?)
- **Galleries:** Acts I-III (the narrative experience)
- **Deep dives:** Documents, People, Collections (expert mode)
- **Transparency:** Bias Disclosure (curator credibility)
- **Exit:** Visit CTA (next step)

### Missing Element: Interpretive Panels

In a physical museum, small explanatory panels appear near artifacts. In the Evidence Room, we have:

- Context Panels (good)
- Curator Notes (good)
- Claim component links (good)

**But:** These feel scattered. A visitor might not realize how deeply linked the archive is.

**Suggested enhancement:** Add a small "How to Use This Archive" panel early on, explaining:

- Featured quotes are story beats
- Context panels explain why passages matter
- Full documents available via links
- Collections group related documents
- Timeline shows chronology

## Conclusion

The Evidence Room is a **sophisticated narrative archive**. Its three-act structure works; its information architecture is excellent; its sourcing is credible.

**What holds it back:** The introductory "why" is missing, and the emotional arc dips too sharply between acts.

**Key insight:** Visitors don't need more information. They need clearer **emotional guideposts** showing why each section matters to the overall story.

The Glass Windows quote is brilliant because it's a detail that carries meaning. The Washington's Question quote is powerful because it's a moment of suspense. The Treaty Signers are moving because they're human names preserved in history.

**The fix:** Reframe the narrative to emphasize story beats over information delivery. Promote Washington's Question visually. Reorder timeline and Treaty Signers. Add a single "why this matters" paragraph early on.

These changes would transform the Evidence Room from a **credible archive** to a **compelling story that happens to be an archive**.
