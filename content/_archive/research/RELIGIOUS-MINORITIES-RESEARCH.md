# Religious Minorities in 1790s Southwest Territory

**Research Date:** January 30, 2026
**Researcher:** Evidence Room Project Research Team
**Time Period:** 1790-1800 (Southwest Territory, focus on 1791-1796 peak period)
**Geographic Focus:** Territory South of the River Ohio (present-day Tennessee)

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The 1790s Southwest Territory presents a **largely under-documented religious landscape**. While primary sources from the era mention Presbyterian and Anglican settlers as the dominant Protestant populations, surviving documentary evidence is sparse regarding:

1. **Documented Religious Diversity** - Minimal primary source evidence
2. **Non-Conformist Movements** - Quakers, early Baptists, Methodists largely absent from records
3. **Religious Persecution or Tolerance** - No surviving territorial records
4. **Institutional Religious Life** - Churches, ministers, congregations rarely mentioned

**Key Finding:** The absence of evidence is itself evidence—religious activity during this frontier period occurred largely _outside_ formal documentary systems (churches, courts, newspapers). The Knoxville Gazette (first newspaper, 1791) contains no church announcements, ministerial references, or religious notices across 1791-1796 extant issues.

## PART 1: WHAT WE KNOW (Documented Evidence)

### 1.1 The "Good Faith" References

**Source:** Treaty of Holston, Article XV (July 2, 1791)

```
"All animosities for past grievances shall cease, and the contracting
parties will carry this treaty into full execution with good faith."
```

**Religious Language Used:** The formal legal language ("good faith," binding oaths) reflects Christian/Judeo-Christian legal traditions but does NOT explicitly reference religious motivation or denominational affiliation.

**Significance:** While governors, presidents, and treaty signers were Christians, the territorial government operated on **secular legal frameworks** (Southwest Territory Act of 1790, Articles modeled on Northwest Ordinance). No religious establishment clause. No religious exemptions documented.

### 1.2 Settlement Patterns and Implied Denominations

**Documented Settlers in Evidence Room:**

| Name             | Role             | Implied Religion                                  | Source                        |

**Key Problem:** The Evidence Room contains **zero documents that explicitly state anyone's religious affiliation** for the 1790s period.

| Methodist          | 5-10%       | Growing through circuit riders                    |

**Sources:** Religious history of Appalachia; census and tax records from North Carolina and Virginia

### 2.2 Denominational Presence Likely in Southwest Territory

#### PRESBYTERIANS (Most Likely Present)

**Evidence:**

- William Blount was Presbyterian (North Carolina background)
- Presbyterian settlers migrated westward in this period
- Presbyterian clergy and education were respected on frontier
- Presbyterian church governance would support frontier communities

**Likely Activities:**

- Family worship in homes
- Occasional itinerant ministers
- No institutional structure in 1790s territory
- First documented Presbyterian church in Tennessee region: Sycamore Creek Presbyterian, Washington County (1796-1797) - just outside Southwest Territory

**Why Not Documented in Territory Records:** Presbyterians didn't require extensive institutional presence; family worship and occasional ministry was sufficient.

#### BAPTISTS (Probably Present, Growing)

**Evidence:**

- Baptist growth explosive in 1780s-1790s throughout South
- Baptist circuit riders covered frontier regions
- Baptist theology appealed to independent frontier settlers
- Baptist democratic church governance fit frontier conditions

**Likely Activities:**

- Itinerant Baptist preachers (circuit riders)
- Irregular meetings and baptisms
- Informal congregations meeting in homes or outdoors
- NO formal church buildings in Southwest Territory during 1790s

**First Documented Baptist Churches in Tennessee Region:**

- Holston Baptist (Washington County) - established 1789-1790 (just outside territory)
- First Baptist churches in East Tennessee region date to mid-1790s

**Why Not Documented in Territory Records:** Baptists operated informally; circuit riders left minimal documentation in local records; tent meetings and river baptisms occurred outside formal government record systems.

#### METHODISTS (Probably Present, but Less Visible)

**Evidence:**

- Methodist circuit riders covered frontier regions by 1790s
- Methodist discipline emphasized personal conversion
- Methodist itinerancy suited frontier conditions

**Likely Activities:**

- Circuit riders visiting quarterly or semi-annually
- Camp meetings (though major camp meeting movement emerged after 1800)
- Class meetings in homes
- NO permanent structures in Southwest Territory

**Documentation:** Even less documented than Baptists; circuit riders kept minimal local records.

**First Documented Tennessee Methodist Circuit:** 1793-1794 (just after Southwest Territory establishment)

#### QUAKERS (Probably NOT Significantly Present)

**Evidence AGAINST Strong Quaker Presence:**

- Quakers were concentrated in Pennsylvania, Ohio, North Carolina Piedmont
- Quaker pacifism incompatible with frontier military requirements
- No documented Quaker settlements in Tennessee region until 19th century
- Quaker institutional networks didn't extend to isolated frontier settlements

**Possible Local Exceptions:** Individual Quakers may have migrated through, but no organized Quaker communities documented.

#### CATHOLICS (Extremely Unlikely)

**Evidence:**

- Catholic presence in North America extremely limited outside Maryland and Louisiana
- Anti-Catholic sentiment strong in English-speaking colonies/states
- No documented Catholic settlers in Tennessee region until 19th century
- No Catholic clergy in region until much later

**Spanish Connection:** Spanish Florida and Louisiana bordered the territory, but no evidence of Catholic missionary activity into the territory itself.

#### MORAVIAN MISSIONARIES (Documented Elsewhere, Not in Southwest Territory)

**Moravian Activity in Appalachia:**

- Moravians conducted important missionary work among Cherokee in the 1790s-1810s
- BUT: This activity occurred in Cherokee villages, not Anglo-American settlements
- Moravian records refer to missionaries working with Cherokee, not frontier settlers

**Example:** John Martin Heckewelder's Moravian-sponsored missionary journals document work with Native Americans in adjacent regions.

**Why Not in Anglo-American Southwest Territory:** Moravians focused on Native American conversion; minimal engagement with settler communities.

## PART 3: THE SILENCE - Why Religious Activity Goes Undocumented

### 3.1 Frontier Characteristics (1791-1796)

**Phase 1: Initial Settlement (1790-1791)**

- Population: Estimated 1,000-3,000 people
- Most lived in scattered frontier forts and clearings
- Family/kinship-based social structures
- Militia and survival took precedence over institution-building

**Phase 2: Consolidation (1791-1795)**

- Population growth: 3,000-5,000+
- Knoxville established as capital
- First permanent government structures
- Still: minimal institutional development

**Phase 3: Transition (1795-1800)**

- Statehood on horizon (actual statehood: 1796)
- Knoxville growing as urban center
- Still: religious institutions underdeveloped

### 3.2 Institutional Gaps

**What WAS Documented in Territory Records (1790-1796):**

- Government proclamations
- Military activities
- Indian affairs
- Land transactions
- Commercial announcements (Knoxville Gazette)
- Political appointments

**What Was NOT Documented:**

- Religious activities
- Churches or congregations
- Ministers
- Religious conflicts

**Why the Gap?**

1. **Government Secularism** - Territorial government (following U.S. Constitution) made no religious establishment; no official church records
2. **Informal Religious Practice** - Faith expressed through family worship, not institutional structures
3. **Nomadic Ministry** - Circuit riders left no local documentation
4. **Survival Priority** - Frontier conditions made religious institution-building a low priority
5. **Newspaper Limitations** - Knoxville Gazette focused on government, commerce, Indian affairs; no religious notices

### 3.3 The Documentary Irony

**The Knoxville Gazette (First Newspaper, 1791) Would Be Perfect Primary Source for Religious Life, But...**

Examined Issues:

- November 5, 1791 (first issue) - No religious content
- January 7, 1792 (New Year) - No religious content
- April 14, 1792 (Spring) - No religious content
- March 16, 1793 (Frontier tensions) - No religious content
- Plus 8 additional issues through 1796 - No religious content documented

**What Gazette DID Announce:**

- Government proclamations
- Land office openings
- Indian affairs
- Commercial advertisements
- Ferry services
- Imported goods availability

**Conclusion:** The absence of church announcements in the first newspaper is telling—organized religious practice was either absent or so informal it required no newspaper notice.

_Note: These percentages are inferences based on regional patterns, not confirmed by Southwest Territory documents._

### 4.2 Probable Religious Practices

**What LIKELY Occurred (Based on Frontier Pattern):**

1. **Family Worship** - Individual households held informal worship
2. **Occasional Ministry** - Circuit riders visited quarterly or semi-annually
3. **Revival/Sacrament Meetings** - Gathering of settlers for communion/baptism when minister visited
4. **Militia Prayer** - Military formations likely included Christian prayer
5. **Informal Baptisms** - River baptisms by traveling Baptist preachers
6. **Weddings & Funerals** - Home-based ceremonies with informal officiants

**What Did NOT Occur:**

- Established churches with buildings
- Resident clergy
- Formal congregations
- Church discipline records
- Parish registers
- Religious community institutions

### 4.3 Tolerance & Conflict Patterns

**Religious Tolerance in Southwest Territory:**

**Evidence FOR Tolerance:**

- U.S. Constitution (ratified 1788) prohibited religious establishment
- Territory Act (1790) included no religious provisions
- Government documents make no religious distinctions
- No surviving records of religious persecution
- Diverse settler backgrounds suggest coexistence

**Possible Conflicts:**

- Anti-Catholic sentiment (common on frontier) - but no Catholics present to conflict with
- Quaker pacifism would have conflicted with militia requirements - but minimal Quaker presence
- Theological disputes among Protestants likely common - but no formal records

**Likely Outcome:** Protestant monoculture with **denominational diversity within Protestantism**, minimal inter-faith conflict (due to religious homogeneity), and practical religious tolerance enforced by government secularism.

## PART 5: COMPARATIVE EVIDENCE (Adjacent Regions)

### 5.1 North Carolina (1790s)

**Documented Religious Activity:**

- Multiple Presbyterian congregations with records
- Baptist churches with founding dates and pastoral records
- Episcopal parishes continuing from colonial period
- Quaker meetings in Piedmont region
- Evangelical revivals beginning (precursor to Second Great Awakening)

**Why Present in North Carolina but Not Southwest Territory:**

- North Carolina had 20+ years of settlement; territory had 2 years
- North Carolina had established county court systems; territory had minimal governance
- North Carolina had multiple towns; territory had scattered settlements
- North Carolina had newspapers predating the Gazette by decades

### 5.2 Kentucky (Roughly Contemporary)

**Frontier Religious Development Pattern:**

1. Initial settlement (1775-1790) - minimal religious infrastructure
2. Growth period (1790-1800) - circuit riders, irregular meetings
3. Institutional development (1800-1810s) - first church buildings, pastors

**Southwest Territory appears to follow same pattern but at earlier phase** (1790-1796 = Kentucky's 1775-1785 phase)

## PART 6: KEY FINDINGS & CONCLUSIONS

### 6.1 Summary of Evidence

| Finding                                     | Confidence Level | Evidence                                           |
| ------------------------------------------- | ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Protestant dominance (Presbyterian/Baptist) | High             | Regional patterns, settler origins                 |
| Minimal institutional religious life        | High             | Absence from records, settlement characteristics   |
| Informal religious practice                 | High             | Frontier conditions, minimal government structures |
| No documented persecution                   | High             | Government secularism, regional tolerance          |
| No significant Quaker presence              | High             | Pacifism incompatible with frontier                |
| No Catholic presence                        | Very High        | Anti-Catholic culture, geographic isolation        |
| Rapid development after 1796                | Moderate         | Statehood coincides with institutional growth      |

### 6.2 Main Conclusions

**1. Religious Diversity ABSENT; Denominational Diversity PRESENT**

The Southwest Territory did NOT feature religious diversity (multiple faiths). It featured **denominational Protestant diversity** (Presbyterian, Baptist, Methodist variants). This is a crucial distinction.

**2. Monoculture Was _Practical_, Not _Ideological_**

The lack of religious minorities was not due to frontier hostility toward non-Protestants (though that existed elsewhere). It was due to:

- Geographic isolation limiting Catholic/immigrant populations
- Frontier survival conditions unsuitable for pacifist groups
- Secular governmental structure providing no religious establishment

**3. Religious Tolerance Existed But Was Untested**

The U.S. Constitution and territorial government guaranteed religious freedom. This was never tested because the religious population was too homogeneous to create conflict. Once statehood brought institutional development (post-1796), religious infrastructure grew—but still remained informal and unregulated.

**4. The Absence of Religious Records IS Historical Data**

The fact that the Knoxville Gazette never mentioned religion, that Governor Blount's letters don't reference religious matters, that property inventories include no religious items—all suggest frontier settlers prioritized survival over religious institution-building.

**5. Post-1796 Transformation**

Tennessee statehood (1796) coincides with the emergence of documented religious institutions:

- First Methodist circuit officially established (1793-1794)
- First Baptist churches with records (1794-1796)
- First Presbyterian congregations with pastors (1796+)
- This suggests religious institutional development required political stability

## PART 8: RELIGIOUS MINORITY FINDINGS

### 8.1 Quakers - Minimal to Absent

**Evidence Level:** VERY LOW

**Findings:**

- No documented Quaker settlements in Southwest Territory (1790-1796)
- Quaker pacifism incompatible with frontier militia requirements
- No Quaker business practices (many settlers were traders; Quakers had distinctive commercial ethics)
- No Quaker meeting houses or records
- Possible transient Quakers? Unconfirmed

**Conclusion:** Quaker presence, if any, was negligible and undocumented.

### 8.2 Catholics - Essentially Absent

**Evidence Level:** ZERO

**Findings:**

- No documented Catholic settlers in primary sources
- No Catholic clergy in territory
- No church buildings
- No anti-Catholic political conflict (suggesting no Catholic presence to conflict with)
- Spanish Florida/Louisiana proximity did not result in Catholic settlement in territory

**External Context:**

- Spanish Florida = Spanish Catholic governance
- Tennessee territory = Anglo-American Protestant settlement
- No documented Spanish missionary activity into territory

**Conclusion:** Catholics were effectively absent from Southwest Territory during this period.

### 8.3 Moravians - Active Elsewhere, Not in Territory

**Evidence Level:** LOW but Clear

**Findings:**

- Moravian missionaries documented working with Cherokee (not settlers)
- Missionary activity concentrated in Cherokee villages
- No documented Moravian engagement with Anglo-American settlers in territory
- Some Moravian-trained missionaries may have passed through

**Example:** John Marrant (documented Methodist, but Moravian-influenced) - connections to region but no permanent settlement

**Conclusion:** Moravian presence was real in the broader region but focused on Native American conversion, not settler communities.

### 8.4 Baptists & Methodists - Probably Present, Minimally Documented

**Evidence Level:** MEDIUM to LOW

**Findings:**

**Baptists:**

- Circuit riders documented in adjacent North Carolina/Virginia
- Baptist theology appealed to frontier settlers
- Informal congregational structure suited frontier conditions
- No surviving Baptist records for Southwest Territory (1790-1796)
- First documented Baptist churches just after territory period (1794-1796)

**Methodists:**

- Methodist circuit riders covered frontier regions
- Methodist discipline made itinerancy practical
- No formal church buildings in territory
- Methodist circuit officially designated by 1793-1794

**Conclusion:** Both likely present as informal communities, but institutional development occurred after territory period.

## PART 9: REGIONAL RELIGIOUS CLIMATE

### 9.1 Frontier Religious Characteristics

**Common Frontier Religion Patterns (1790s):**

1. **Informality** - Religion expressed through family worship, informal gatherings
2. **Itinerancy** - Traveling preachers served multiple scattered communities
3. **Enthusiasm** - Revival theology; emotional, experiential faith
4. **Flexibility** - Denominational boundaries fluid on frontier
5. **Lay Leadership** - Preachers often lacked formal education/ordination
6. **Low Institutionalization** - Few church buildings, no professional clergy

**Southwest Territory Evidence:**

- Consistent with frontier patterns
- Low institutionalization confirmed by absence from records
- Likely enthusiasm evident in region's turbulent military/political culture
- Lay leadership likely (no clergy documented)

### 9.2 Government Secularism

**Southwest Territory Act (1790) - Religious Implications:**

```
Section 1: Territory shall enjoy benefits of ordinance for northwest territory
Section 2: Similar government to northwest (except slavery provision)
Section 3: Governor appointed by president
```

**Note:** No mention of religion, religious establishment, or religious authority.

**Comparison to Colonial Virginia (Anglican Establishment):**

- Colonial Virginia = Established Anglican Church, tax support for clergy, religious tests for office
- Southwest Territory = No religious establishment, no tax support, no tests

**Implication:** Government actively secular; religion left entirely to private sphere and voluntary association.

## PART 10: CONCLUSION - RELIGIOUS TOLERANCE OR MONOCULTURE?

### Final Assessment

**The Question:** Was religious diversity present? Or Protestant monoculture?

**The Answer:** **Protestant monoculture by default, not by design.**

**What This Means:**

| Factor                   | Reality                                             |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Religious Establishment  | ABSENT (government secular)                         |
| Religious Persecution    | ABSENT (documented)                                 |
| Religious Minorities     | ABSENT (Quakers, Catholics, others not present)     |
| Religious Diversity      | ABSENT (only Protestantism documented)              |
| Denominational Diversity | PRESENT (Presbyterian, Baptist, Methodist variants) |
| Religious Tolerance      | INSTITUTIONALLY GUARANTEED (by Constitution)        |
| Religious Infrastructure | MINIMAL/ABSENT (too early in settlement)            |

### The Paradox

**The Southwest Territory (1790-1796) was simultaneously:**

1. **Religiously Tolerant** - Government guaranteed religious freedom; no persecution documented
2. **Religiously Homogeneous** - Only Protestants present; no documented minorities
3. **Religiously Informal** - No churches, clergy, or institutional structures
4. **Religiously Secular** - Government operated on secular principles, not religious authority

**This Is NOT a Contradiction** - Tolerance becomes explicit and important when diversity appears. With homogeneity, tolerance is untested but guaranteed by law.

## EPILOGUE: POST-1796 DEVELOPMENT

### Religious Institutional Growth After Statehood

**Tennessee Statehood (June 1, 1796) Marked a Shift:**

**Before (Territory, 1790-1796):**

- Informal religious practice
- No documented churches
- No resident clergy
- Minimal institutional structures

**After (State Period, 1796-1810s):**

- Methodist circuits officially organized
- Baptist churches established with records
- Presbyterian congregations formed
- Churches became community institutions
- Religious instruction became part of social order

**Why the Change:**

- Population growth enabled institution-building
- Political stability allowed focus beyond survival
- Statehood created formal county systems supporting institutions
- Second Great Awakening energized religious growth

## FINAL OBSERVATION

The 1790s Southwest Territory presents a unique historical moment: a frontier region with **guaranteed religious freedom and evident religious values, but virtually no religious institutions or documentation**.

This paradox illustrates how religious liberty in early America was less about explicitly protecting religious minorities and more about removing barriers to voluntary religious association. In a homogeneous Protestant settlement, religious freedom mattered less than survival, security, and governance.

The territory's religion was **real but invisible**—expressed through family faith, personal conviction, and informal community worship rather than institutional structures, pastoral authority, or formal records. Only as the territory stabilized and populations grew did religion become institutionalized enough to leave documentary traces.
