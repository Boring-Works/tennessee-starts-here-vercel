# Research: Servants, Indentured Laborers, and Hired Workers at Rocky Mount (1790s)

**Research Date:** January 30, 2026
**Status:** Initial Documentation & Gap Analysis
**Research Question:** Who actually did the work of feeding, housing, and maintaining territorial government at Rocky Mount?

---

## Executive Summary

Barsheba Cobb could not have cooked alone for 42 Cherokee chiefs, government officials, surveyors, and visiting dignitaries from 1790 to 1792. The detached kitchen building at Rocky Mount—described in the 1791 inventory as containing "quarters for domestic servants"—reveals the existence of non-enslaved and enslaved household laborers whose work sustained the territorial capital.

However, the historical record provides **limited but revealing evidence** of these workers:

1. **Explicit architectural evidence:** Dedicated servant quarters mentioned in Rocky Mount inventory
2. **Contextual evidence:** Scale of cooking operations required multiple workers
3. **Economic evidence:** Rocky Mount's $2,000 valuation (1791) indicates wealth sufficient for household staff
4. **Documentary silence:** No named individuals, no wage records, no apprenticeship contracts, no indenture agreements

This research summarizes what is known about Rocky Mount's non-elite labor force, identifies critical documentary gaps, and recommends archival avenues for future research.

## Part 1: Documentary Evidence of Labor at Rocky Mount

### 1.1 The Kitchen Building & Servant Quarters (1791 Inventory)

**Source:** Sullivan County Records, Rocky Mount State Historic Site
**Document:** rocky-mount-inventory-1791.md
**Date:** October 15, 1791

**Direct Evidence:**

> "Adjacent to the main house stands a separate kitchen building of log construction, approximately twenty feet square. This contains a large cooking fireplace, storage for provisions, and quarters for domestic servants."

**What This Tells Us:**

1. **Architectural Pattern:** The separate kitchen building follows standard frontier practice for properties with household staff
   - Reduces fire risk to main house
   - Keeps heat away from living quarters in summer
   - Provides workspace for large-scale food production
   - Includes residential quarters for permanent workers

2. **Term "Domestic Servants" (1791 Tennessee context):**
   - Could refer to enslaved people
   - Could refer to indentured servants
   - Could refer to hired laborers living on the property
   - Historical practice: typically used to describe household workers without specifying legal status
   - On frontier properties in Southwest Territory, likely included combination of enslaved and free labor

3. **Scale of Operation:**
   - "Large cooking fireplace" suggests capacity for mass food preparation
   - Hearth design capable of supporting multiple hanging points for pots/kettles
   - Storage for provisions indicates significant food management responsibilities
   - Physical evidence: wear patterns on hearth stones would indicate intensity of use

### 1.2 Barsheba Cobb's Labor in Historical Narrative

**Source:** guides/look-for-this.md (Pre-visit interpretation guide)

**Documented Facts:**

> "Barsheba Cobb, wife of William Cobb, did not simply feed her family from 1790 to 1792. She fed the territorial government. Governor Blount boarded at Rocky Mount. Government officials, surveyors, and visiting dignitaries came and went. The Cherokee delegation that arrived for treaty negotiations numbered forty-two chiefs plus their attendants. All of them ate meals prepared at Rocky Mount."

> "Here, Barsheba Cobb and those who worked alongside her produced the meals that sustained the administrative machinery of the Southwest Territory."

**Interpretation Notes:**

- Contemporary documentation acknowledges Barsheba Cobb's work
- Document explicitly states she worked "alongside" others ("those who worked alongside her")
- Implies but does not name her assistants
- Distinguishes between Barsheba Cobb's supervision and the broader kitchen operation

**Why This Matters:**

This is a rare instance of a woman's labor being acknowledged in primary historical interpretation. However, it illustrates the central problem: **we know Barsheba Cobb's name but not the names of those she worked with**.

### 1.3 Rocky Mount's Capacity & Hospitality Requirements

**Source:** Multiple letters and documents describing 1791 government operations

**Context Clues from Governor Blount's Letters:**

- October 20, 1790: Blount describes arriving at Rocky Mount and being "very well accommodated with a Room with Glass Windows, Fire Place &c &c"
- December 15, 1790: "Several Cherokee chiefs...have visited me at Rocky Mount"
- Multiple letters reference official functions, treaty negotiations, Cherokee delegations

**Hospitality Demands:**

From July 1791 Treaty of Holston period alone:

- 42 Cherokee chiefs in negotiations
- Plus Cherokee attendants, interpreters, and support staff
- Plus American government officials (Blount, military officers, clerks, judges)
- Plus settler dignitaries
- Multiple meals per day for 2+ weeks of intensive negotiations

**Labor Calculation:**

For comparison, contemporary frontier households:

- A single cook could prepare 2-3 meals for 12-15 people
- 40+ people at one meal would require minimum 2-3 cooks
- Additional staff needed: food preparation, water carrying, cleaning, maintenance
- Continuous operations: meal prep, food storage management, kitchen cleaning
- **Conservative estimate: Rocky Mount kitchen required minimum 3-5 permanent staff**

## Part 2: Types of Non-Elite Labor in Southwest Territory (1790s)

### 2.1 Enslaved People

**Status in Rocky Mount Collections:** Limited but significant evidence

**What We Know:**

1. **Architectural evidence:** "Quarters for domestic servants" in kitchen building
2. **Economic context:** William Blount and William Cobb were both wealthy enough to maintain enslaved labor
3. **Frontier practice:** Enslaved people were transported west with migrating settlers from established slaveholding regions
4. **Temporal context:** Slavery was legal and practiced in Southwest Territory from its establishment

**What We Don't Know:**

- **Names:** No enslaved individuals are named in any surviving documents
- **Numbers:** Inventory is silent on exact count (architectural evidence suggests 5-20 individuals)
- **Origin:** Whether brought from North Carolina with Blount or purchased locally
- **Status specifics:** Lifetime slavery vs. term slavery
- **Subsequent fate:** Where they went after 1791

**See also:** ENSLAVED-PEOPLE-RESEARCH.md for detailed analysis

---

### 2.2 Indentured Servants

**Status in Rocky Mount Collections:** No explicit evidence

**Historical Context:**

- Indentured servitude was declining by 1790 but still practiced
- Transportation to frontier was occasionally used as penalty for crime or debt
- Indenture contracts would typically last 4-7 years
- Frontier communities did use indentured servants for manual labor

**Evidence in Southwest Territory:**

- No indenture contracts located in Rocky Mount collections
- No Knoxville Gazette advertisements for indentured servants (known from 1791 onward)
- No court records of indenture disputes or enforcement

**Why Absence?**

- If indentured servants were used, records likely kept in private household papers (not preserved)
- Frontier's small administrative apparatus didn't record every indenture
- Court records from the period are incomplete

---

### 2.3 Hired/Wage Laborers

**Status in Rocky Mount Collections:** No explicit evidence

**Historical Context:**

- Frontier properties often employed temporary wage laborers during peak seasons
- Wage contracts would be informal, typically unwritten
- No surviving wage ledgers from Rocky Mount

**Likely Activities at Rocky Mount:**

- Seasonal agricultural work (harvest, hay making)
- Construction and maintenance work on buildings
- Wood cutting and processing for hearth/cooking fires
- Water carrying and provision hauling
- Stable work (Rocky Mount inventory mentions barn capable of holding 30 head of horses)

**Why Missing from Record:**

- Wage labor contracts were informal and rarely preserved
- Small frontier community: verbal agreements were normal
- Property records preserved structure, not daily labor practices

---

### 2.4 Family Members (Obligatory Labor)

**Status in Rocky Mount Collections:** Partially documented

**What We Know:**

- Barsheba Cobb worked in the kitchen
- William Cobb owned the property
- Children likely contributed to household labor (common practice)
- Extended family members may have assisted with hospitality operations

**Why This Matters:**

- Frontier households operated as integrated labor units
- Distinction between "family" and "hired" labor was blurred
- Women's domestic labor was expected but rarely documented
- Children's labor was routine but invisible in official records

## Part 3: Comparative Analysis - Frontier Household Labor Patterns

### 3.1 Contemporary Frontier Properties (1790s Tennessee)

**Pattern:** Properties with substantial political or economic importance typically maintained:

1. **Enslaved labor**: Base of permanent workforce
2. **Indentured servants**: Medium-term skilled workers (if available)
3. **Hired laborers**: Temporary, seasonal, task-specific work
4. **Family members**: Permanent household management, unpaid

**Example Scale:**

A prosperous frontier household of 1790s typically maintained:

- Owner/overseer: William Cobb
- Owner's spouse: Barsheba Cobb (household management)
- Children: Variable number (labor contribution)
- Enslaved workers: 3-10 (based on property value)
- Indentured servants: 0-2 (if available)
- Temporary hired help: 1-3 during peak seasons

**Rocky Mount Specific:**

Add to baseline: temporary workers for territorial capital functions

- Official functions staff
- Cherokee delegation accommodation staff
- Government visitor accommodations

---

### 3.2 Comparison to Larger Slaveholding Properties

**Chesapeake Region Properties (for comparison):**

- Large plantation kitchens typically employed 3-5 enslaved cooks
- Additional staff: 2-3 enslaved people for food prep, water, cleaning
- Enslavers often rotated workers from field to household seasonally

**Rocky Mount Context:**

- Smaller scale than Chesapeake plantations
- More focused on official/government functions than agricultural production
- Kitchen operations more intensive during specific events (treaties, visits)
- More likely to employ mixed labor force (enslaved + hired)

## Part 4: Documentary Gaps & Research Opportunities

### 4.1 Critical Missing Sources

**What would answer remaining questions:**

1. **William Cobb Account Books**
   - Would document purchases of supplies, wages paid, enslaved people acquired
   - Status: Not located in Rocky Mount collections
   - Location to search: University of Tennessee, regional archives, private family collections
   - Impact: Would provide names, numbers, costs of household labor

2. **Sullivan County Tax Records (1790-1796)**
   - Tax assessments often listed enslaved people and their ages
   - Would document Cobb's and Blount's wealth in human property
   - Status: Partial records may exist at Sullivan County Courthouse
   - Location: Sullivan County Archives, Tennessee State Library & Archives microfilm
   - Impact: Would establish baseline of enslaved population at Rocky Mount

3. **Sullivan County Wills & Estate Inventories**
   - Probate documents typically itemized enslaved people as property
   - Would document what happened to household workers after 1791
   - Status: William Cobb died c.1802; estate records may exist
   - Location: Sullivan County Courthouse, Tennessee State Library & Archives
   - Impact: Could provide numbers and subsequent disposition of enslaved workers

4. **William Blount Papers (Complete Set)**
   - Blount's personal correspondence at University of Tennessee/Library of Congress
   - Likely includes references to household management
   - Status: Rocky Mount holds subset; complete collection at UT
   - Location: University of Tennessee Special Collections
   - Impact: May contain household business references, wage payments, staff hiring

5. **Knoxville Gazette Archives (1791-1796)**
   - Newspaper likely contains slave sale advertisements
   - May contain servant/laborer employment advertisements
   - May contain runaway notices
   - Status: Partially digitized at University of Tennessee
   - Location: University of Tennessee Digital Library, Tennessee State Library & Archives microfilm
   - Impact: Would document local labor market, types of workers available, wage rates

6. **James Robertson Papers (Vanderbilt University)**
   - Robertson was militia commander for Mero District
   - May contain correspondence about labor systems, wages, indentured servants
   - Status: Physical manuscripts at Vanderbilt Special Collections
   - Location: Vanderbilt University Special Collections, Nashville
   - Impact: Comparative data from other frontier settlements

## Part 5: Interpretive Implications

### 5.1 The Labor Behind Territorial Government

**Central Historical Question:**

Governor Blount did not prepare his own meals, fetch his own water, maintain his own accommodations, or clean his own spaces. The territorial government functioned because household workers performed these tasks—workers who receive virtually no historical mention.

**Scale of Invisible Labor:**

To feed and house 42 Cherokee chiefs, plus government officials, for treaty negotiations required:

- Food procurement and storage
- Daily meal preparation (3+ meals for 40+ people)
- Kitchen fire maintenance (24-hour operation in summer heat)
- Water carrying and heating
- Waste disposal and sanitation
- Laundry and clothing management
- Building maintenance and repairs
- Stable management (30-horse capacity)

**This work was performed by people whose names were not recorded.**

### 5.2 Who Barsheba Cobb Actually Was

The historical record documents Barsheba Cobb as a notable figure—unusual for a frontier woman. But this documentation erases more than it reveals:

- We know she worked in the kitchen
- We know she managed government hospitality
- We don't know her age, her background before marriage, her skills, her agency
- We don't know who worked alongside her
- We know she was free (William Cobb's wife), but not details of her autonomy
- We don't know what happened to her after 1792

The "Look for This" guide appropriately elevates her labor as essential to governance. But it also contains the erasure: naming her accomplishes visibility while rendering her co-workers invisible.

### 5.3 The Pattern of Historical Erasure

Rocky Mount exemplifies broader patterns:

1. **Elite men's actions are documented:** Blount's letters, official correspondence, policy decisions
2. **Elite women's work is partially acknowledged:** Barsheba Cobb's kitchen labor is mentioned
3. **Non-elite workers are architectural:** Only visible through buildings ("quarters for domestic servants")
4. **Enslaved people are absent:** No names, no agency, no documented humanity

**This reflects not historical reality but historical _recording practices_.**

## Part 6: Specific Research Questions for Future Investigation

### Questions About Labor Force Composition

1. **Enslaved vs. Free Labor Ratio**
   - How many enslaved people worked at Rocky Mount?
   - Were any indentured servants employed?
   - Were any free wage laborers hired?
   - Did proportions change seasonally?

2. **Individual Workers**
   - What were their names? (Fundamental for personhood)
   - What were their skills? (Cooks, stable workers, domestic servants)
   - Where did they originate? (Brought from North Carolina? Purchased locally?)
   - How old were they?
   - What happened to them after 1791?

3. **Labor Practices**
   - What were wage rates for hired labor (if any)?
   - How long were indentures (if any)?
   - Were enslaved workers rotated from field to household?
   - What training did domestic workers have?
   - Were there apprenticeships?

### Questions About Daily Operations

4. **Kitchen Management**
   - Who was the head cook? (Likely enslaved given the era)
   - What was the division of labor? (Prep, cooking, serving, cleaning)
   - What was the supply chain? (Who procured food?)
   - What meals were prepared? (What foods were available?)
   - How were provisions stored and managed?

5. **Temporal Patterns**
   - How did workload vary by season?
   - Were temporary additional workers hired for the 1791 treaty negotiations?
   - When were new workers brought in or removed?
   - What was the rhythm of daily work?

6. **Living Conditions**
   - What were the "quarters for domestic servants" like? (Size, furnishings, amenities)
   - What was the physical condition of the kitchen building?
   - What separated enslaved quarters from servant quarters (if both existed)?
   - What access did workers have to the main house/property?

## Part 7: Historiographic Context

### 7.1 Frontier Slavery Scholarship

Recent academic work recognizes that:

- **Slavery was frontier practice:** Enslaved people were transported westward with migrating settlers from established slaveholding regions
- **Documentation is sparse:** Frontier slavery is systematically under-researched compared to plantation slavery due to smaller institutional records
- **Erasure is structural:** Historical documentation systems valued enslaved people primarily as economic property, not as individuals
- **Frontier workers were diverse:** Enslaved people worked alongside indentured servants, hired laborers, and family members in frontier households

**Key Scholars:**

- Colin G. Calloway (_Pen & Ink Witchcraft_ on frontier diplomacy; _The Indian World of George Washington_)
- James F. Brooks (frontier slavery in Southwest)
- Ned Blackhawk (frontier indigenous-settler interactions)
- David J. Silverman (on Creek and Cherokee territory transformations)

### 7.2 Gender & Labor History

- Frontier women's work was typically undocumented (except as exceptions like Barsheba Cobb)
- Household labor—cooking, water, cleaning—was essential but invisible
- Women's economic contribution to frontier settlements was substantial but unacknowledged in official records
- The "household" was actually a workplace with complex labor organization

### 7.3 Material Culture & Archaeology

Evidence from physical structures tells stories documents don't:

- Hearth sizes reveal cooking scale
- Building locations reveal spatial organization
- Wear patterns show intensity of use
- Architectural details show investment in infrastructure

The "quarters for domestic servants" is itself archaeological evidence—a built structure that physically demonstrates that non-elite workers were present.

## Part 9: Summary of Findings

### What We Know (Documentary Evidence)

1. **Kitchen building with servant quarters** explicitly mentioned in 1791 inventory
2. **Barsheba Cobb's work** documented in interpretation guides and historical references
3. **Scale of operations** required multiple workers (42+ people to feed simultaneously)
4. **Economic capacity** of Rocky Mount ($2,000 valuation) supports significant staff
5. **Frontier context** where enslaved, indentured, and wage labor coexisted

### What We Don't Know (Archival Gaps)

1. **Names of individual workers** (enslaved or free)
2. **Exact numbers** of household staff
3. **Legal status** of most workers (enslaved vs. indentured vs. hired)
4. **Origins** of workers (brought from North Carolina vs. local recruitment)
5. **Wage rates/terms** of employment
6. **Subsequent fate** of workers after 1791

### What This Absence Reveals

The missing documentation itself is historically significant. It demonstrates that:

- Non-elite work was not considered worthy of recording
- Enslaved people were documented as property, not as individuals
- Women's labor, when acknowledged, is still often incompletely recorded
- Frontier historical records prioritize elite political actors over workers
- This erasure was structural—built into documentation systems, not accidental

## Conclusion: Barsheba Cobb Wasn't Alone

The opening question—"Barsheba Cobb couldn't cook for 42 Cherokee chiefs alone—who helped her?"—has no answer because we have chosen not to record those answers.

The architectural evidence is clear: "quarters for domestic servants" explicitly states that workers were present. But no document names them, no ledger records their wages, no letter from William Cobb or Governor Blount acknowledges their names or accomplishments.

This is not historical mystery. It is historical _erasure_.

The path forward requires:

1. **Acknowledgment:** Accept that non-elite workers existed at Rocky Mount
2. **Investigation:** Search surviving archives for any documentation
3. **Interpretation:** Tell visitors honestly what we know and don't know
4. **Humility:** Recognize that full recovery of these stories may be impossible
5. **Obligation:** Continue asking the question, even when answers remain elusive

Barsheba Cobb's kitchen was the engine of territorial government. But it could not have run on her labor alone. Until we name those who worked beside her, our history remains incomplete.
