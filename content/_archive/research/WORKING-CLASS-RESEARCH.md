# Working-Class Settlers of the Southwest Territory (1790s)

## Executive Summary

The Evidence Room currently documents the 1790s Southwest Territory exclusively through the voices of the elite: governors, generals, federal officials, and land speculators. **This research identifies primary source collections and records that capture the economic diversity and lived experiences of ordinary settlers—farmers, laborers, traders, militia soldiers, and enslaved workers.**

**Key finding:** Economic diversity was real. The 1791 population of 35,691 included not just wealthy planters but working-class settlers struggling with land disputes, military service, taxation, and frontier survival. Their voices exist in court records, petitions, tax rolls, militia rosters, and commercial documents—we just haven't collected them yet.

---

## Part 1: What Currently Exists vs. What's Missing

### Current Evidence Room (Elite Voices Only)

**People documented:**

- William Blount (Governor, speculator)
- Henry Knox (Secretary of War)
- George Washington (President)
- James Robertson (Land speculator, militia leader)
- John Sevier (General, militia leader)
- George Roulstone (Newspaper printer)
- Andrew Jackson (Rising politician/military officer)

**Document types:**

- Executive correspondence (Blount to Knox, Jefferson to Blount)
- Official proclamations and commissions
- Newspaper announcements (Knoxville Gazette)
- Treaties and official negotiations
- Military orders and reports

**The gap:** No voices of farmers, laborers, traders, common militia soldiers, enslaved people, or ordinary women appearing as agents in their own economic lives.

### Population Reality (1791 Enumeration)

| Group                      | Population | Status                      |
| -------------------------- | ---------- | --------------------------- |
| **Free settlers (total)**  | 32,013     | Mostly working-class        |
| **Enslaved people**        | 3,417      | Economic workers            |
| **Free people of color**   | 361        | Working-class               |
| **Territorial population** | 35,691     | **Diverse economic strata** |

**The reality:** 90% of the population was non-elite. Yet we have zero primary sources from ordinary settlers in the current collection.

## Part 2: Available Primary Source Collections

### 1. Court Records & Testimony (High Priority)

**"Tennesseans in Court: 2,500 Early Settlers found in Supreme Court Reports, 1791–1820"**

- Author: Charles A. Sherrill (1999)
- Contains: Extracted cases with settler names, places, nature of disputes, personal information
- **Why it matters:** Court cases reveal working-class economic relationships, land disputes, debt, contracts, livestock disputes, assault cases
- **Location:** Tennessee State Library and Archives
- **Voices captured:** Farmers, traders, laborers who appear as plaintiffs and defendants
- **Example use:** A farmer suing over livestock damages reveals his economic status, holdings, and business relationships

**Specific territorial courts:**

- Superior Court case files (1790-1796)
- District courts in Washington, Mero, and Washington County
- Court dockets showing judges, parties, and case outcomes

**Key insight:** Courts were where ordinary people appeared in formal records. They handled land disputes, debt collection, assault, theft, and contract disputes—the bread and butter of frontier life.

---

### 2. Militia Records & Muster Rolls

**Colonel David Henley's "Wastebook" (1793-1798)**

- Contains: Regular and militia personnel records for the Southwest Territory
- **Location:** FamilySearch Catalog (FamilySearch ID: 2245166)
- **Voices captured:** Common soldiers, their ranks, service dates, possibly pay records
- **Why it matters:** Militia service was mandatory for most able-bodied men. Rosters show economic status (officers vs. enlisted men), occupations, geographic dispersal

**Available sources:**

- Muster rolls from territorial militia (14 infantry companies + cavalry troop, 1790-1796)
- General John Sevier's militia records (Washington District)
- General James Robertson's militia records (Mero District)
- Governor Blount's militia organizational records

**Key insight:** Militia records are democracy documents—common people appearing by name, not as subjects but as citizens with obligations.

---

### 3. Settler Petitions & Complaints

**Territorial Legislature Petitions (1790-1796)**

- **Location:** Tennessee State Library and Archives (Nashville), filed by year in boxes
- **Content:** Private petitions from settlers to the Territorial Legislature
- **Documented grievances:**
  - Military protection demands (settlers wanted stronger defense against Cherokee raids)
  - Tax complaints (25-cent per 100-acre tax drew fierce resistance in August 1794)
  - Pay withheld for militia service
  - Land disputes and preemption rights
  - Demands for government accountability

**Example grievance (documented):**
When territorial assembly met in August 1794, landowners drew up a petition over the new 25-cent per 100-acre tax rate and demanded federal protection from hostile Indians.

**Key insight:** These petitions show ordinary settlers negotiating power with governors and federal officials. They weren't passive recipients—they organized and made demands.

**Additional petition sources:**

- Preemption petitions: Settlers on military reservations petitioned for the right to purchase the land they occupied
- North Carolina General Assembly petitions (pre-1796) from territorial settlers

---

### 4. Land Records & Grant Documentation

**"Early Tennessee Land Records, Record Group 50"** (Recently processed)

- Contains: Huge volume of early land grants with pioneer settler information
- **Location:** Tennessee State Library and Archives
- **Voices captured:** Hundreds of ordinary settlers who received land grants
- **Information per grant:**
  - Grantee name
  - Acreage
  - Grant number
  - Date of registration
  - Location of land
  - Book and page reference

**How to use it:**

- Identify grantees by name
- Cross-reference with court cases to see disputes
- Use acreage to estimate economic status (smaller grants = working-class; larger = speculators)
- Track over time to see who accumulated land vs. who sold/lost it

**Additional land records:**

- Early North Carolina/Tennessee land grants (1773-1796)
- Warrants and surveys used to issue grants (Manuscripts Microfilm #1177)
- Preemption rights applications
- Military reservation claims

**Key insight:** Land records show who actually settled (not who claimed), what they held, and where. They reveal economic inequality and speculation patterns.

---

### 5. Tax Records & Economic Valuations

**Tax Lists (1800-1803, 1837-1839)**

- **Location:** Tennessee State Library and Archives (microfilm)
- **Voices captured:** Property owners by name, listed with property valuations
- **Note:** Early 1790s tax records not yet microfilmed, but tax collection records exist
- **Application:** Can estimate wealth, occupations listed, show who paid vs. who evaded

**Available sources:**

- County sheriffs' tax collection records (sheriffs collected territorial taxes)
- 1791 enumeration data (population count by age/status categories)
- Military assessment rolls (territorial government needed to assess able-bodied men for militia)
- Property valuations for early land transactions

**Key insight:** Tax records democratize history—they list everyone with property, not just the famous. Economic status becomes visible.

---

### 6. Commercial Records & Tavern Licensing

**Court licensing records** (Territorial court minutes):

- Tavern licenses and regulations
- Ferry licensing and operations
- Gristmill designations and regulations
- Trading post permits
- Inn keeper records

**Location:** Territorial court records at Tennessee State Library and Archives

**Voices captured:** Ordinary people running businesses—tavern keepers, ferrymen, millers, traders. These were the connective tissue of frontier commerce.

**Why it matters:** Taverns were more than drinking establishments—they were news distribution centers, meeting places for political organization, and nodes in the frontier economy. Tavern keepers were information brokers and economic intermediaries.

**Key insight:** Licensing records show the hidden economy of the frontier—services, goods, and communication networks not visible in official correspondence.

---

### 7. Newspaper Records (Beyond current gazette)

**Knoxville Gazette (1791-1796)** — Expand current documentation

- **Current use:** Only elite announcements documented
- **Overlooked content:** Classified ads, notices from ordinary people
  - Runaway slave ads (revealing skilled enslaved workers' trades)
  - Lost livestock notices
  - Estate sales announcements
  - Business partnerships
  - Runaway servant notices
  - Wanted notices
  - Marriage and death notices (common people)

**Other newspapers:**

- Washington District newspapers (if any survived)
- Mero District newspapers

**Key insight:** Classified ads are working-class documentary gold. They show occupations, family relationships, property holdings, and mobility patterns of ordinary people.

---

### 8. Enslaved and Free People of Color Records

**Population data (1791):**

- 3,417 enslaved people
- 361 free people of color

**Records capturing their presence:**

- Runaway slave ads (Knoxville Gazette, Washington newspapers)
- Estate inventories (listing enslaved people and their skills)
- Sales records and bills of sale
- Legal cases involving enslaved people
- Skilled trades documentation (1,200 runaway slave ads show 25 blacksmiths, 18 carpenters, 13 shoemakers, plus barbers, boat builders, cooks, etc.)

**Location:** Tennessee State Library and Archives, newspaper collections, court records

**Why it matters:** Enslaved people and free people of color made up 10% of the population. They were economic workers performing skilled and unskilled labor. Their records reveal the hidden economy and forced labor networks.

**Key insight:** These aren't "minority" records—they're essential to understanding the territory's economy and labor systems.

## Part 4: Specific Research Questions Answerable by These Records

### Economic Status & Inequality

- "What did ordinary settlers own?" → Land grants + tax records
- "Who got rich? Who struggled?" → Court cases (debt, disputes), land accumulation patterns
- "How did women participate in economy?" → Estates (widow holdings), court cases (lawsuits), tavern licensing
- "What was the income range?" → Militia pay records (if available) + property values

### Settlement Patterns

- "Where did working-class settlers locate?" → Land grants + militia rosters (geographic distribution)
- "Who came from where?" → Court testimony (mentions origins), land grant sources
- "How stable was settlement?" → Land sales records, turnover in tax lists

### Frontier Governance & Conflict

- "What did settlers want from government?" → Petitions (primary voice)
- "What were the major grievances?" → Tax complaints, military protection demands
- "Did settlers organize?" → Petition organization, militia musters, tax refusal
- "How did ordinary people engage with power?" → Court appearances, petitions, testimony

### Labor & Occupations

- "What jobs existed?" → Classified ads (occupational mentions), militia records (occupations listed?), court cases (occupational references)
- "What skilled trades?" → Runaway ads (skilled enslaved workers), tavern licensing (service occupations)
- "Were there occupational networks?" → Court cases (debt relationships), partnership notices

### Race, Slavery & Freedom

- "Who was enslaved?" → Estate inventories, sales records, runaway ads
- "What skills did enslaved people have?" → 1,200 runaway ads document 25+ occupational categories
- "Who were free people of color?" → Tax records (if included), court cases, land transactions
- "How was race reflected in economy?" → Comparative holdings, occupations, legal status

## Part 11: Key Insights for Evidence Room Narrative

### Current Narrative Problem

The Evidence Room currently presents 1790s Southwest Territory as a project of elite governance:

- Governor Blount organizing territory
- Federal government (Washington, Knox, Jefferson) managing from afar
- Newspapers announcing official business
- Ordinary people invisible

### What These Records Reveal

1. **Ordinary people negotiated power.** Petitions, court appearances, and militia service show settlers as agents, not subjects.

2. **Economic inequality was real but not total.** Land grants and tax records show variation—some settlers thrived, others struggled, but all participated in the economy.

3. **Labor was diverse.** Skilled trades, farming, commerce, and forced labor (slavery) created a complex economy, not a simple frontier narrative.

4. **Settlers had grievances.** They organized (petitions), they resisted (tax complaints), they demanded (military protection, withheld pay). They weren't passive recipients of governance.

5. **Government responsiveness varied.** Some petitions succeeded, others failed. The frontier was a site of conflict, negotiation, and political struggle.

6. **Invisible workers made it possible.** Enslaved people (3,417), free people of color (361), and working-class women were essential to the territorial economy but absent from official records—their documents must be sought in other places (ads, inventories, court cases).

## Conclusion

The Evidence Room has an opportunity to tell a more complete story of the 1790s Southwest Territory. The primary sources exist—in archives, in court records, in newspapers, in land offices. Ordinary settlers left voices behind. They petitioned government, testified in court, served in militia, bought and sold land, ran businesses, and struggled to survive on the frontier.

**By adding working-class voices to the Evidence Room, we answer a critical historical question: What was it actually like to be an ordinary settler in this place, at this time?**

The answer isn't a single elite narrative. It's 35,000 overlapping stories of opportunity, conflict, labor, land, and negotiation with power. That's where the real history is.
