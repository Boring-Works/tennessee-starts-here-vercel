# Recommended Documents to Add - Evidence Room Expansion

**Researcher:** Dr. Elizabeth Warren | Frontier Documents Specialist
**Date:** January 30, 2026

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The current Evidence Room contains an excellent foundation of 38 documents but skews heavily toward federal political correspondence (Washington, Knox, Blount) and Knoxville Gazette excerpts. Critical gaps exist in **Cherokee perspectives**, **women's voices**, **local governance records**, **economic documents**, and **military correspondence**. This report identifies 25+ high-value primary sources available through Founders Online, American State Papers, and regional archives that would transform the Evidence Room into a more comprehensive, inclusive, and culturally balanced collection representing all stakeholders in Tennessee's founding.

### 2. Hanging Maw to George Washington, June 15, 1793

- **Type:** Letter (Cherokee to President)
- **Date:** June 15, 1793
- **Author/Source:** Hanging Maw (Uskwa'li-gu'ta), principal Cherokee chief
- **Why it matters:** **CRITICAL CULTURAL BALANCE** - Direct communication from Cherokee leadership to President Washington. Written from Coyatee just days after Hanging Maw was wounded by American militia at his own home while hosting peace talks. Shows Cherokee commitment to peace despite violent provocations.
- **Where to find it:** Founders Online (included in Knox correspondence)
- **Specific URL:** https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Washington/05-13-02-0157 (enclosed in Knox to Washington, July 16, 1793)
- **Digitization status:** Already digitized and transcribed by Founders Online
- **Acquisition effort:** Easy (publicly available, already transcribed)
- **Estimated time:** 1 hour (copy, format, add context)

### 3. Cherokee Delegation Petition in Philadelphia, December 1791 - February 1792

- **Type:** Petition/Diplomatic correspondence
- **Date:** December 1791 - February 17, 1792
- **Author/Source:** Cherokee delegation led by Bloody Fellow (5 chiefs)
- **Why it matters:** **FILLS TREATY RESPONSE GAP** - Cherokee traveled to Philadelphia to object to Treaty of Holston terms and successfully negotiated increased annuities (from $1,000 to $1,500). Shows Cherokee diplomatic agency and successful negotiation. We have the treaty but not Cherokee response to it.
- **Where to find it:** American State Papers: Indian Affairs, Volume 1; Knox correspondence in Founders Online
- **Digitization status:** Fully available online through Library of Congress
- **Acquisition effort:** Easy (publicly available)
- **Estimated time:** 2-3 hours (identify specific documents, extract relevant passages, contextualize)

### 4. Henry Knox to George Washington: Report on Coyatee Attack, July 16, 1793

- **Type:** Federal report on militia violence against Cherokee
- **Date:** July 16, 1793
- **Author/Source:** Henry Knox, Secretary of War
- **Why it matters:** **EXPOSES SETTLER VIOLENCE** - Documents American militia attack on peaceful Cherokee at Hanging Maw's home during peace talks requested by Governor Blount. Daniel Smith called it "as inhuman an act as ever was committed." Shows pattern of settler violence undermining federal peace efforts. Balances narrative by showing Americans violating treaties.
- **Where to find it:** Founders Online
- **Specific URL:** https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Washington/05-13-02-0157
- **Digitization status:** Already digitized and transcribed
- **Acquisition effort:** Easy (publicly available, already transcribed)
- **Estimated time:** 1 hour (copy, format, add context)

### 5. Thomas Jefferson to William Blount, August 1, 1790

## DOCUMENT CATEGORIES MISSING

### 1. **Cherokee Responses** ⚠️ HIGHEST PRIORITY GAP

**What we have:**

- Treaties Cherokee signed (Treaty of Holston, Additional Article)
- American descriptions of Cherokee actions
- Federal correspondence about Cherokee

**What we're missing:**

- Cherokee letters responding to treaty terms
- Cherokee petitions to federal government
- Cherokee complaints about settler violations
- Cherokee perspectives on land cessions
- Letters from peace chiefs vs. war faction leaders

**Why it matters:** Current collection presents Cherokee as passive recipients of American policy rather than diplomatic actors with agency, perspectives, and grievances. This is a **critical cultural imbalance** that undermines the collection's credibility.

**Recommendation:** Add items #1, #2, #3, #10 immediately. Search for additional Cherokee correspondence in:

- War Department territorial papers at National Archives (RG 107)
- Bureau of Indian Affairs records (RG 75)
- Additional Founders Online documents (search "Cherokee" + "letter")

---

### 2. **Women's Perspectives** ⚠️ MAJOR DIVERSITY GAP

**What we have:**

- None. Zero women's voices.

**What we're missing:**

- Letters from settler women about frontier life
- Diaries documenting daily household management
- Women's accounts of Cherokee conflicts
- Letters between women (Mary Blount, Barbara Blount, other elite women)
- Ordinary settler women's perspectives

**Why it matters:** Women comprised half the population but are completely absent from current narrative. This is historically inaccurate and pedagogically problematic for educational programs.

**Challenge identified:** Research shows Mary Blount left **no letters, diaries, journals, or written records**. Barbara Blount was only 4 years old in 1796. Elite women in Southwest Territory may not have left substantial written records.

**Recommendation:**

- **Acknowledge the gap explicitly** in Evidence Room narrative ("Women's voices are largely absent from surviving Southwest Territory records...")
- Search for any women's writings in:
  - North American Women's Letters and Diaries database (Alexander Street)
  - Tennessee State Library & Archives family collections
  - East Tennessee Historical Society collections
  - Sullivan County historical society materials
- Consider including **documents about women** (e.g., court cases involving women, estate inventories mentioning women, letters describing women's work) even if not written by women
- Partner with University of Tennessee Women's Studies program to help locate sources

---

### 3. **Economic Records** ⚠️ MODERATE GAP

**What we have:**

- Rocky Mount inventory (1791)
- Brief references to economic activity in letters

**What we're missing:**

- Land sale records
- Trade agreements with Cherokee
- Tavern licenses
- Tax records
- Merchant account books
- Labor contracts (including enslaved people)
- Estate inventories

**Why it matters:** Economic activity drove territorial expansion but current collection focuses almost entirely on political governance. Visitors can't understand **how people actually made a living**.

**Recommendation:**

- Sullivan County land records (deed books 1775-1796) at Tennessee State Library & Archives
- Washington County court records (economic cases: debt, broken covenants, trade disputes)
- North Carolina land warrants honored by federal government
- Search McClung Historical Collection for merchant records, account books

**Specific targets:**

- William Cobb account books (Rocky Mount store operations)
- Land office records from territorial period
- Tax lists from Sullivan/Washington/Greene counties

---

### 4. **Military Correspondence** ⚠️ MODERATE GAP

**What we have:**

- Knox letters referencing military matters
- Brief mentions of militia actions

**What we're missing:**

- Field reports from militia commanders
- Muster rolls
- After-action reports
- Militia orders from Sevier and Robertson
- Correspondence between militia and federal government
- Soldier accounts of campaigns

**Why it matters:** Frontier defense was central to territorial experience but current collection has almost no military primary sources from actual soldiers or field commanders.

**Recommendation:** Add items #8, #9 immediately. Then search for:

- John Sevier papers at University of Tennessee (military correspondence)
- James Robertson papers at Vanderbilt (militia orders)
- War Department territorial papers (RG 107) for militia pay records
- Murtie June Clark, _American Militia in the Frontier Wars, 1790-1796_ (contains transcribed muster rolls)

---

### 5. **Local Governance** ⚠️ MODERATE GAP

**What we have:**

- Federal-level governance (Blount commissions, acts of Congress)
- Brief mentions of court establishment

**What we're missing:**

- County court proceedings
- Justice of the peace records
- Criminal cases (murder, theft, assault)
- Civil cases (debt, land disputes, slander)
- Grand jury presentments
- Court orders and warrants

**Why it matters:** Federal government established framework, but **county courts governed daily life**. Current collection is too "top-down" - all elite federal officials, no local justice records showing how governance actually worked for ordinary people.

**Recommendation:**

- Sullivan County court records at Tennessee State Library & Archives (deed books 1775-1796, court minutes)
- Washington County court records at Washington County Archives (criminal/civil case files from 1790s)
- Greene County court records
- Search for specific interesting cases:
  - Murder trials
  - Bastardy cases
  - Debt disputes between known figures
  - Land fraud cases
  - Assault cases involving militia

**Specific target:** Washington County judicial case files showing "debt, bastardies, survey and land disputes, probate, theft, assaults, trespass, slander, broken covenants, murder, unlawful retailing of liquor, lewdness, public fighting" (per Washington County Archives).
