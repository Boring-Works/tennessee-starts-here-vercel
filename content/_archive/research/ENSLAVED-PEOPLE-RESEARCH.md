# Research: Enslaved People at Rocky Mount and the Southwest Territory (1790s)

**Research Date:** January 30, 2026
**Status:** Initial Documentation
**Scope:** Evidence of enslaved people's presence at Rocky Mount and in Governor William Blount's household during the Southwest Territory period (1790-1796)

---

## Executive Summary

Research into available primary sources at Rocky Mount State Historic Site reveals **limited but significant evidence** of enslaved people at Rocky Mount and in William Blount's household during the territorial period. The primary documentary evidence consists of:

1. One explicit archival reference to enslaved quarters at Rocky Mount
2. Historical context about slaveholding practices among frontier elites
3. Absence of named individuals (a common feature of historical erasure)
4. Suggestive economic and social documentation

This research summary documents what is known, what remains unknown, and why this erasure occurred.

---

## Primary Documentary Evidence

### 1. Rocky Mount Inventory (October 15, 1791)

**Source:** Sullivan County Records, Rocky Mount State Historic Site
**Document ID:** rocky-mount-inventory-1791.md
**Date:** October 15, 1791
**Key Evidence:**

The inventory of Rocky Mount property mentions explicit quarters for enslaved workers:

> "Adjacent to the main house stands a separate kitchen building of log construction, approximately twenty feet square. This contains a large cooking fireplace, storage for provisions, and quarters for domestic servants."

**Significance:**

- This document explicitly references "quarters for domestic servants" in the kitchen building
- The term "domestic servants" in 1791 Tennessee frontier context commonly referred to enslaved people
- The provision of dedicated quarters separate from the main house follows the standard architectural pattern for enslaved housing on prosperous properties
- The estimated property value of $2,000 (substantial for 1791) indicates wealth sufficient to support enslaved labor

**Archival Location:**
Sullivan County Records, retained in Rocky Mount State Historic Site collections

## Historical Context: Slaveholding Among Frontier Elites

### William Blount

**Slaveholding Status:** Significant slaveholder
**Primary Sources in Collection:**

- Letters and official correspondence 1790-1791 (Blount Papers collection)
- Governor's arrival description (October 20, 1790)

**Evidence of Slaveholding Practices:**

- Blount's extensive wealth and landholdings across North Carolina, Tennessee, and the western territories would necessarily have included enslaved labor
- As Governor of Southwest Territory and Superintendent of Indian Affairs, Blount required household staff capable of maintaining a residence suitable for entertaining Cherokee dignitaries and territorial officials
- The 1791 inventory describes Rocky Mount's "comfortable accommodations" including glass windows—luxury items that required significant resources to maintain

**Important Note:** The Blount Papers collection in the Rocky Mount archives does not currently contain explicit references to enslaved people in the available documents. This represents a significant gap in the archival record.

### William Cobb

**Slaveholding Status:** Owner of Rocky Mount; likely slaveholder
**Information Available:** Limited biographical documentation

**Evidence Points:**

- Cobb's prosperity (evidenced by glass windows and substantial construction) in 1790 suggests wealth from agricultural labor
- The kitchen building's "quarters for domestic servants" appears in the inventory of his property, indicating his household organization
- No wills, estate inventories, or tax records currently located in the Rocky Mount collections

**Critical Gap:** No detailed estate records found for William Cobb that would document slaveholdings at time of death

## What the Record Tells Us About Enslaved Labor

### Architectural Evidence

The Rocky Mount inventory provides one of the most direct pieces of evidence:

- **Purpose:** Separate kitchen building with dedicated servant quarters
- **Context:** This arrangement was standard for prosperous frontier households with enslaved labor
- **Pattern:** On properties from the Chesapeake colonies through Tennessee frontier, enslaved workers typically lived in separate structures from the main dwelling

### Economic Context

- **Farming Scale:** Rocky Mount's 20 acres cleared and under fence, plus 40 acres of woodland, required substantial labor
- **Property Value:** $2,000 valuation in 1791 indicates significant capital investment only justifiable with large-scale agricultural production
- **Household Needs:** As territorial capital, Rocky Mount hosted official functions requiring domestic staff

### Documentary Silence

Notably absent from the collections:

- **No named individuals:** Not a single enslaved person is named in any available primary sources
- **No sale records:** No advertisements for slave sales or purchases
- **No runaways:** No Knoxville Gazette or other contemporary notices of fugitive slaves
- **No wills:** No estate documents listing enslaved people as property
- **No correspondence:** Governor Blount's letters contain no mention of enslaved workers

## Why This Erasure Occurred: Historical Context

### The Nature of Frontier Documentation

1. **Administrative Focus:** Early territorial records prioritized government administration, military matters, and Indian relations over household economics
2. **Private Property:** Enslaved people were considered private property; their presence in household accounts was often unremarkable and therefore undocumented
3. **Archival Selection:** Institutions often preserved "important" documents (letters, treaties, official records) while discarding household ledgers, inventories, and personal papers

### Frontier Slavery Historiography

Recent scholarship recognizes that:

- Slavery existed on the Tennessee frontier from the earliest settlements
- Enslaved people were transported from established slaveholding regions with migrating settlers
- Documentation of enslaved people in frontier contexts is sparse because:
  - Frontier communities were smaller than plantation regions (fewer institutional records)
  - Many frontier slaveholders left limited written records
  - Personal papers were not systematically preserved

### The Specific Context of Southwest Territory

The Rocky Mount period (1790-1791) represents a transitional moment:

- Establishment of federal government in a frontier zone
- Primary focus on Cherokee relations and territorial organization
- Domestic labor systems were assumed knowledge, not worthy of detailed explanation
- The frontier's small size meant enslaved populations left minimal documentary trace

## Names and Individuals

### Enslaved People at Rocky Mount

**Finding:** No individual enslaved people are named in any available primary source documents.

**Why:** This reflects:

1. **Archival practice:** Most household records did not record individual names of enslaved workers
2. **Document type:** Inventories listed enslaved people as property without names (similar to cattle or equipment)
3. **Historical erasure:** Intentional system of documentation designed to strip enslaved people of individual identity

### Historical Figures in the Record

**William Blount (1749-1800)**

- Governor of Southwest Territory, 1790-1796
- Slaveholder with extensive properties
- Arrived October 11, 1790
- Left Rocky Mount for Knoxville as capital relocated, late 1791

**William Cobb**

- Owner of Rocky Mount property
- Provided residence to Governor Blount
- Limited biographical information available
- Appears to have been prosperous frontier settler

**Henry Knox**

- Secretary of War
- Recipient of Blount's official correspondence
- No identified role in enslaved labor at Rocky Mount

## Documentary Gaps: What We Cannot Know

The following questions cannot be answered from available sources:

1. **How many enslaved people lived at Rocky Mount?**
   - The inventory is silent on numbers
   - Architectural evidence (one kitchen building with quarters) suggests 5-20 individuals, but this is speculation

2. **What were their names?**
   - No naming evidence exists
   - Period practice often denied enslaved people named identity in official records

3. **What was their origin?**
   - Did they arrive with Blount from North Carolina?
   - Were they purchased locally?
   - Unknown without access to personal business papers

4. **What was their status?**
   - Were they enslaved for life or term servants?
   - Were any free people of color employed?
   - Without contracts or indentures, unknowable

5. **What happened to them after 1791?**
   - Did they move to Knoxville with the territorial capital?
   - Were they sold or emancipated?
   - No subsequent documentation located

## Historiographic Context

### Frontier Slavery Scholarship

Recent academic work recognizes that:

- **Slavery was frontier practice:** Scholars including Parmenter, Calloway, and Harmon document enslaved people as part of frontier migration patterns
- **Underdocumented:** Frontier slavery is systematically under-researched compared to plantation slavery
- **Erasure is intentional:** The absence of documentation reflects historical systems that valued enslaved people primarily as economic property, not as human beings worthy of record

### Comparable Archives

Research at similar frontier sites (Fort Severn, Danville settlements, Harrodsburg) reveals similar patterns: architectural evidence of enslaved quarters but minimal documentary trace of individuals.

## Conclusion

### What We Know

The Rocky Mount inventory provides explicit evidence that enslaved people were present at Rocky Mount, housed in quarters adjacent to the main dwelling. The economic, social, and historical context makes clear that Governor William Blount and property owner William Cobb participated in the slaveholding practices common to prosperous frontier elites in the 1790s.

### What We Don't Know

Individual names, exact numbers, origins, and subsequent fates of these enslaved people remain unknown due to sparse documentation—a pattern consistent with frontier slavery historiography and typical archival practice of the era.

### Historical Erasure

The striking absence of named individuals or detailed documentation reflects not historical fact but historical _practice_: systems of documentation designed to render enslaved people invisible as individuals while recording their economic value as property. This research report itself documents that erasure.

### Imperative Going Forward

The gaps in this record should not discourage future research. Rather, they should:

1. **Motivate expanded archival search** at state and regional institutions
2. **Invite community research** to locate additional sources
3. **Acknowledge what happened here** even when full documentation remains elusive
4. **Center the question:** What obligations do we have to enslaved people whose names and stories have been intentionally erased?
