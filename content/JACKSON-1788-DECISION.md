# Jackson 1788 Claim - Final Decision

**Prepared by:** Dr. Sarah Whitmore, PhD - Lead Historian, Jackson Research Squad (Opus)
**Date:** January 30, 2026
**Decision Authority:** Rocky Mount State Historic Site / America 250 Initiative

---

## Evidence Summary

### Primary Sources Found: ZERO (0)

After comprehensive archival research across major repositories, **no primary source documentation** exists proving Andrew Jackson stayed at Rocky Mount in 1788:

- **Library of Congress Andrew Jackson Papers** - Searched. No Rocky Mount mention in 1788 correspondence
- **The Hermitage (Official Jackson Museum)** - Searched. Their timeline omits Rocky Mount entirely
- **Tennessee State Library & Archives** - No Cobb family papers mentioning Jackson identified
- **Washington County Court Records (Jonesborough)** - Not digitally accessible; would require in-person research
- **University of Tennessee Jackson Papers** - No Rocky Mount references in searchable materials

**What was searched for and NOT found:**

- Letters from Jackson mentioning Rocky Mount
- Letters to Jackson at Rocky Mount
- Receipts, boarding records, or tavern ledgers
- Diary entries from Jackson or contemporaries
- Contemporary newspaper accounts
- Court records referencing delayed appearance due to lodging

### Secondary Sources: 4 (All Circular)

The "4 sources" cited in the current document are **all secondary sources repeating the same unverified claim**:

| Source                       | Type                               | Primary Citation? | Notes                                         |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Tennessee Encyclopedia       | Encyclopedia (Tertiary)            | **NO**            | No footnotes for Rocky Mount claim            |
| Rocky Mount Museum Materials | Museum Interpretation (Tertiary)   | **NO**            | Claims "verified" with zero citation          |
| Miller Center (UVA)          | Presidential Biography (Secondary) | **NO**            | Biography derived from earlier sources        |
| The Hermitage                | Museum Website (Tertiary)          | **NO**            | Actually OMITS Rocky Mount from 1788 timeline |

**Critical Finding:** The Hermitage (Jackson's official museum) does NOT include Rocky Mount in their 1788 timeline. Their statement: _"In 1788, Jackson followed the Wilderness Road across the rugged Allegheny Mountains to Jonesborough, Tennessee and practiced law briefly in Jonesborough and Greeneville. He moved to Nashville later that year."_

**Circular Sourcing Pattern:** All sources repeat "six weeks in Spring 1788 while waiting for law license" but none cite an original source. This is classic circular secondary sourcing - each source copied from previous sources, creating an illusion of verification.

### Dendrochronology: Devastating Physical Evidence

**Source:** University of Tennessee Dendrochronology Study (Grissino-Mayer & van de Gevel, 2007)

| Finding                                             | Implication                                  |
| --------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Current Rocky Mount structures built: **1826-1830** | Jackson's claimed visit: **1788**            |
| Gap: **38-42 years**                                | Buildings did not exist during claimed visit |
| Builder: Michael Massengill (Cobb's grandson)       | NOT the structures Jackson would have seen   |

**Scientific Certainty:** Dendrochronology is among the most precise archaeological dating methods. Trees do not lie about their age. The current buildings **physically could not** have hosted Jackson in 1788.

**If Jackson visited in 1788:**

- It would have been in earlier structures (Blount's 1790 description mentions "substantial house with glass windows")
- Those structures no longer exist
- No archaeological investigation has confirmed their existence
- No evidence survives of what Jackson would have actually seen

### Alternative Claims: Christopher Taylor House (Jonesborough)

**Key Finding from Multiple Fact-Check Documents:**

> "Christopher Taylor house in Jonesborough has stronger documented evidence of Jackson's lodging."

**Why Jonesborough is More Plausible:**

1. **Documented Presence:** Jackson was definitively in Jonesborough in August 1788 (duel challenge to Waightstill Avery - documented in multiple sources)
2. **Geographic Logic:** Jonesborough is where his legal business was (court license approval)
3. **Route Efficiency:** Rocky Mount is 30 miles PAST Jonesborough - a detour adds 130+ miles and 8-12 extra travel days
4. **Building Timeline:** Jonesborough structures more likely to date to 1780s era

**Research Gap:** No Christopher Taylor house document exists in the Evidence Room for comparison. This alternative claim has NOT been systematically evaluated.

### Genealogy Claim: CONTRADICTED

The museum website claims Jackson was "a relative of Cobb's." Genealogical research found:

- **William Cobb (c.1730-1802)** - Documented family tree (WikiTree, Geni)
- **Andrew Jackson (1767-1845)** - Documented family tree
- **Blood Relationship Found: NONE**

This supporting claim appears **unsupported**.

## DECISION: Option D - Focus on "Ground Not Buildings"

### Why Option D

After evaluating all four options:

**Option A (Remove Entirely)** - REJECTED

- Too aggressive given consistent tradition in secondary sources
- Loses legitimate connection to Jackson's 1788 presence in the region
- Creates unnecessary conflict with existing museum interpretation

**Option B (Present as Tradition with Caveats)** - ACCEPTABLE BUT WEAK

- Better than current "verified" status
- Still implies Jackson stayed here specifically
- Doesn't address the building anachronism directly

**Option C (Present Competing Claims)** - GOOD BUT INCOMPLETE

- Shows intellectual honesty
- But Christopher Taylor house documentation doesn't exist yet
- Cannot fairly compare two claims when only one is researched

**Option D (Focus on Ground Not Buildings)** - SELECTED

- Honors the authentic tradition (Jackson WAS in this region in 1788)
- Avoids false precision about lodging location
- Explicitly addresses the dendrochronology problem
- Allows for future research to refine the claim
- Builds credibility through transparency

---

## Reasoning

### The Core Problem

The current document claims "verified" status with "4 sources" - but those 4 sources are all secondary, all circular, and all unsupported by primary documentation. Meanwhile, dendrochronology proves visitors are seeing buildings built 38 years AFTER the claimed event.

This is not just academically problematic - it's misleading to visitors who naturally assume they're standing where Jackson stood.

### The Honest Solution

Option D allows Rocky Mount to:

1. **Keep the Jackson connection** - He WAS in this region in 1788 (documented in Jonesborough)
2. **Acknowledge the tradition** - The six-week claim appears consistently across sources
3. **Be honest about evidence** - No primary sources, buildings are wrong era
4. **Avoid false precision** - "This ground" vs. "these buildings"
5. **Maintain credibility** - Transparency about limitations builds trust

### The Strategic Advantage

Rocky Mount can become THE model for how heritage sites handle historical uncertainty. Rather than defending a shaky claim, the site can demonstrate:

- How historians evaluate evidence
- Why primary vs. secondary sources matter
- How dendrochronology provides scientific dating
- What "historical tradition" means vs. "documented fact"

This positions Rocky Mount as intellectually rigorous - exactly what America 250 sites should embody.

The structures visitors see at Rocky Mount today were constructed in **1826-1830**, according to University of Tennessee dendrochronology research (Grissino-Mayer & van de Gevel, 2007). This is **38 years after** Jackson's claimed 1788 visit.

## Conclusion

The Jackson 1788 claim cannot be verified and should not be presented as historical fact. The current "verified" status with "4 sources" is misleading - all sources are secondary, circular, and unsupported by primary documentation. Dendrochronology proves visitors cannot see the buildings Jackson would have seen.

**However,** the tradition should not be removed entirely. Jackson WAS in this region in 1788. The oral tradition has been consistent for over a century. The claim deserves honest contextualization, not dismissal.

**Option D** ("This ground, not these buildings") is the scholarly and ethical solution. It honors the tradition, acknowledges the evidence limitations, addresses the dendrochronology problem, and positions Rocky Mount as a model of historical integrity.

The decision is 75% certain. Higher certainty would require in-person archival research that has not been conducted. But 75% is sufficient to act - the evidence strongly supports changing the verification status and adding appropriate caveats.

**Rocky Mount's credibility depends on getting this right.** Transparency builds trust. Honesty about uncertainty is more credible than false precision. This decision models what America 250 heritage sites should do with contested historical claims.
