## TIMELINE ANALYSIS

### 1787: Law School & Bar Admission

**Documented Fact:**

- Jackson studied law in Salisbury, NC under Spruce Macay and John Stokes
- **September 1787:** Admitted to North Carolina bar
- **Source:** North Carolina bar admission records (University of North Carolina, various historical societies)

**Timeline Status:** SOLID (primary documentation exists)

---

### Spring 1788: The Rocky Mount Claim Period

**What the Claim Says:**

- Young Jackson (age 21) traveled west from Salisbury
- Lodged at Rocky Mount for **six weeks** while waiting for Western District license
- Hunted, fished, prepared for frontier law practice
- Stayed at William Cobb's "substantial home"

**Historical Problems:**

#### Problem 1: Zero Primary Source Documentation

- **No letters from Jackson mentioning Rocky Mount**
- **No receipts or lodging records from Cobb**
- **No contemporary 1788 accounts** describing the visit
- **No diary entries** from Jackson or anyone else documenting it

**Evidence Level:** ZERO primary sources identified

**How We Know:** The existing document explicitly states (line 18): "lacks primary documentation (no letters, receipts, or contemporary accounts from 1788)"

#### Problem 2: Physical Impossibility - Building Anachronism

- **University of Tennessee dendrochronology study** (Grissino-Mayer & van de Gevel, 2007)
- **Current Rocky Mount structures date to:** 1826-1830
- **Jackson's claimed visit:** 1788
- **Time gap:** 38-42 years AFTER the claimed visit

**What This Means:**

- Jackson could NOT have stayed in the buildings visitors see today
- Buildings would not have been constructed until Jackson was 59 years old
- Michael Massengill (William Cobb's grandson) built these structures, not the original Cobb house
- The original structures from 1788 (if any) left no evidence

**Source:** BRAND-STRATEGY.md: "Dendrochronology proves the main house dates to **1827-1830**"

#### Problem 3: Inconsistent Timeline

- **Timeline events.json claims specific date:** April 15, 1788
- **Document text admits:** "Spring 1788" (vague seasonal estimate)
- **Contradiction:** Specific date implies precision that documentation doesn't support

---

### August 1788: Jonesborough Duel Incident

**What's Documented:**

- Jackson challenged Colonel Waightstill Avery to a duel in Jonesborough
- **Status:** DOCUMENTED in multiple biographical sources
- **Location:** Jonesborough (specific city mentioned)
- **Date:** August 1788 (specific month)

**Timeline Implication:**

- By August, Jackson was in Jonesborough (not Rocky Mount)
- If six-week Rocky Mount stay occurred April-May, he would have left by early June
- **Gap:** 2-3 months between end of alleged Rocky Mount stay (May) and Jonesborough incident (August)

**Missing Link:** What was Jackson doing during June-July 1788? No documentation.

---

### October 1788: Nashville Arrival

**What's Documented:**

- Jackson arrived in Nashville as district attorney
- Appointed by friend John McNairy (Superior Court Judge)
- **Status:** DOCUMENTED in appointment records
- **Location:** Nashville (Tennessee Mero District)
- **Date:** October 1788

**Timeline Implication:**

- If Jackson left Jonesborough in August/September, ~2 months to reach Nashville
- Route: Jonesborough → Nashville (distance: ~300 miles)
- Travel time: 1-2 weeks by horseback
- Suggests Jackson might have stayed in Jonesborough area during late summer/early fall

## RED FLAGS: Critical Problems with Rocky Mount Claim

### Red Flag #1: Timeline Conflict

**Problem:** Spring 1788 stay (April-May) vs. August duel occurrence

- If Jackson stayed at Rocky Mount April-May (6 weeks), he should have been settled in Jonesborough by June
- But no documentation places him in Jonesborough until August duel incident
- **2-3 month gap with no documented activity**
- Alternative: Maybe he stayed elsewhere June-July, then Jonesborough in August

### Red Flag #2: Geographic Inefficiency

**Problem:** Why take a 130+ mile detour to Rocky Mount?

- Jackson needed to reach Jonesborough (where court license was being processed)
- Wilderness Road goes directly to Jonesborough
- Rocky Mount is 30 miles past Jonesborough (creates backtracking)
- **No documented reason** for this inefficient routing
- **Logical alternative:** Go straight to Jonesborough where his actual business was

### Red Flag #3: Lack of Primary Sources

**Problem:** Zero documentation from 1788

- No Jackson letters mentioning Rocky Mount
- No Cobb family records mentioning Jackson
- No Jonesborough court records acknowledging him
- No contemporary newspaper accounts
- No diary entries
- **ALL evidence is secondary** (written 100+ years later)

**Comparison to Verified Events:**

- **Jonesborough duel:** Documented in multiple sources, specific individuals named
- **Nashville appointment:** Documented in official records
- **Rocky Mount stay:** Only mentioned in modern tourist/museum literature

### Red Flag #4: Building Anachronism (Physical Impossibility)

**Problem:** Current structures didn't exist in 1788

- Dendrochronology: 1826-1830 construction
- Jackson's 1788 visit claim
- **38-42 year gap**
- If Jackson visited, it was in an earlier structure that left no evidence
- **Visitors see buildings that postdate the claim by 38 years**

### Red Flag #5: Secondary Source Circularity

**Problem:** All sources cite the same tradition, don't independently verify it

- Tennessee Encyclopedia mentions the 6-week stay
- Rocky Mount Museum materials mention it
- Presidential biographies mention it
- **Pattern:** Each source likely references previous sources in a chain
- **No independent primary verification** of any source
- Classic historical circularity: Source A cites Source B, Source B cites Source A, both invented earlier

## HISTORIAN'S ASSESSMENT

### Strongest Evidence Points To:

**Jonesborough** (Christopher Taylor house) as Jackson's 1788 residence, NOT Rocky Mount:

1. **Geographic logic:** Jonesborough is where his legal business was (court license)
2. **Documented presence:** August 1788 duel challenge places him definitely in Jonesborough
3. **Route efficiency:** Direct path via Wilderness Road (no inefficient detour)
4. **Stated in Evidence Room:** Christopher Taylor house has "stronger documented evidence"
5. **Building timeline:** Jonesborough structures more likely to date to 1788+ era

### Rocky Mount Claim Appears To Be:

- **Conflation of traditions:** May confuse Jackson's visit with Blount's 1790 arrival
- **Circular secondary sourcing:** Each book citing previous books, no original research
- **Oral history misplaced:** Strong tradition about important visitors, placed at wrong location
- **Modern attribution:** Attributed to Jackson perhaps for tourism/branding reasons

### Most Likely Scenario:

Jackson probably:

1. Did not spend six weeks at Rocky Mount
2. **Did** lodge in Jonesborough (documented in August)
3. **May have** passed through Rocky Mount on his journey (30-mile detour possible)
4. **Definitely** arrived in Nashville in October (documented)

The six-week stay probably happened in Jonesborough (Christopher Taylor house), not Rocky Mount, based on available evidence.
