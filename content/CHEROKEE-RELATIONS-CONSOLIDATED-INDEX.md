## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document consolidates all Cherokee-related research materials from the Tennessee Starts Here Evidence Room project. It includes verified primary sources (Cherokee-authored documents), treaty documentation, Cherokee leader profiles, and recommendations for historical interpretation.

**Key Findings:**

- **2 Cherokee-Authored Documents** (directly from Cherokee leaders, translated through interpreters)
- **7 Major Cherokee Leaders** documented with verified signatures and roles
- **2 Treaties** with Cherokee signatories covering 1791-1792
- **Multiple Diplomatic Events** showing Cherokee agency and negotiation power
- **Verification Status:** All documents cross-referenced and verified against multiple sources

**Important Note:** All 1790s Cherokee documents exist in English translation. The Cherokee syllabary (Sequoyah) was not invented until the 1820s. These voices are preserved but filtered through interpreters.

## PART 1: CHEROKEE-AUTHORED DOCUMENTS

### Document 1: Cherokee Delegation to Philadelphia (January 1792)

**Classification:** Cherokee-Authored Report of Delegation & Speeches

**File Location:** `/content/documents/cherokee-delegation-philadelphia-1792.md`

**Primary Source:**

- Founders Online: Henry Knox to George Washington, January 17, 1792
- URL: https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Washington/05-09-02-0273-0001
- Cross-referenced: American State Papers: Indian Affairs, Vol. 1, pp. 203-206

**Date:** January 7-11, 1792 (meetings in Philadelphia); December 29, 1791 (arrival)

**Cherokee Delegation Members:**

1. **Nenetooyah (Bloody Fellow)** - Delegation leader, principal spokesman
2. **Chutloh (Kingfisher)** - Supporting delegation member
3. **Nontuaka (The Northward)** - Supporting delegation member
4. **Teesteke (The Disturber)** - Supporting delegation member
5. **Katigoslah (The Prince)** - Supporting delegation member

**Interpreters:**

- James Carey (primary interpreter)
- George Miller/Suaka (young Cherokee English speaker, verification role)

**Recipient:** Secretary of War Henry Knox (reported to President George Washington)

**Historical Significance:**

This document represents **Cherokee diplomatic initiative** of the highest order. The delegation was NOT summoned by Americans—Cherokee leaders traveled by ship hundreds of miles specifically to protest treaty terms signed just six months earlier.

**Cherokee Demands (Explicitly Recorded):**

1. **Annuity Increase** - $1,000 → $1,500 annually
   - Status: **GRANTED** (February 17, 1792)
   - Outcome: 50% increase in annual payment

2. **Settler Removal** - White settlers south of ridge dividing Tennessee/Little River waters
   - Status: Partially addressed
   - Term: Permanent boundary enforcement demanded

3. **Federal Representative** - Appointment of government counselor/protector
   - Status: Requested but not granted

4. **Muscle Shoals Settlement Prevention** - Block Tennessee Company settlement
   - Status: Addressed through boundary enforcement

5. **Agricultural Implements** - Delivery of farming tools per treaty
   - Status: Included in treaty provisions

6. **Interpreter Appointments** - Assignment of trusted interpreters (including George Miller)
   - Status: Partially granted

**Verification Status:** VERIFIED

- Source count: 3 (Founders Online, American State Papers, Knox Papers)
- Translation verification: Dual-verified (Carey + Miller)
- Outcome documentation: Senate approval of annuity increase on January 20, 1792

**What This Shows:**

- Cherokee leaders actively negotiated treaty modifications
- Cherokee possessed understanding of federal vs. territorial authority
- Cherokee forced successful modifications to treaty terms within 6 months
- Cherokee demanded written documentation of all agreements
- Diplomatic sophistication: appeal to President over Territorial Governor

**Cultural Context:**

Cherokee leaders privately called Governor Blount "the Dirt Captain" because of his single-minded focus on land acquisition. This shows Cherokee analytical understanding of American motivations.

### Document 2: John Watts Boundary Speech (December 22, 1796)

**Classification:** Cherokee-Authored Speech Demanding Treaty Enforcement

**File Location:** `/content/documents/john-watts-boundary-speech-1796.md`

**Primary Source:**

- Papers of the War Department, 1784-1800
- URL: https://wardepartmentpapers.org/s/home/item/55955
- Document pages: 57-60

**Date:** December 22, 1796

**Speaker:** John Watts (Kunoskeskie/Young Tassel), War Council Head

**Recipients:** President George Washington, Secretary of War James McHenry

**Core Demands:**

1. **Formal Boundary Survey** - With Cherokee oversight and presence
   - Requested Cherokee envoys present: Wiley Elder, Ohadlokee, Watts himself
   - Specified boundary: Tennessee River to Cumberland River (per Treaty of Holston)

2. **Surveyor Integrity** - "Not a land speculator"
   - Shows Cherokee understanding that land speculators were corruption threat

3. **Optimal Timing** - "Just as the Trees begin to put out their Buds"
   - Shows technical knowledge of surveying (visibility for boundary marking)

4. **Peace Observation** - "Peace has been faithfully observed by my Nation since the conference of 1795"
   - Positions Cherokee as reliable; positions U.S. as violator

5. **Accountability for Murders** - "Murders of Indians by whites have gone unpunished"
   - Documents specific treaty violations
   - Demands justice/enforcement

**Verification Status:** VERIFIED

- Source: Papers of War Department (digitized, accessible)
- Content: Full speech pages 57-60
- Connection: John Watts signed Treaty of Holston in 1791 (one of 42 signatories)

**What This Shows:**

- Cherokee holding U.S. accountable 5 years after treaty signing
- Cherokee tracked treaty violations comprehensively
- Cherokee used diplomatic channels for enforcement (not renewed warfare)
- Sophisticated understanding of surveying practices
- Assertion of Cherokee sovereignty in boundary process

**Comparison to 1791 Treaty:**

This speech references "the boundary line established with Governor Blount"—the same William Blount who negotiated at Rocky Mount. Shows Cherokee did not forget specific treaty terms and demanded compliance.

**Diplomatic Arc:**

1791: Bloody Fellow protests terms in Philadelphia, wins annuity increase
1796: John Watts demands enforcement of those terms 5 years later
Shows: Consistent Cherokee pressure on U.S. to honor obligations

## PART 2: TREATIES WITH CHEROKEE SIGNATORIES

### Treaty 1: Treaty of Holston (July 2, 1791)

**File Location:** `/content/documents/treaty-holston-1791.md`

**Official Title:** Treaty of Holston with the Cherokee

**Negotiating Parties:**

- **U.S. Representative:** William Blount, Governor of the Territory South of the River Ohio
- **Cherokee Nation:** 42+ Cherokee chiefs and warriors

**Negotiation Location:** Rocky Mount (William Blount's residence)

**Ratification:** November 11, 1791

**Key Articles:**

| Article | Content                                                                   | Enforcement                |
| ------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| I       | Perpetual peace and friendship                                            | Foundation of treaty       |
| II      | Cherokee under U.S. protection (not foreign power)                        | Sovereignty clause         |
| III     | Deliver prisoners by April 1, 1792                                        | Mutual obligation          |
| IV      | Boundary definition; U.S. pays $1,000 goods + $1,000 annual annuity       | PRIMARY ECONOMIC TERM      |
| V       | Free passage for U.S. citizens on Washington-Mero road                    | Transit rights             |
| VI      | U.S. exclusive right to regulate trade                                    | Trade monopoly             |
| VII     | U.S. guarantees unceded Cherokee lands                                    | Land guarantee             |
| VIII    | U.S. settlers on Cherokee land forfeit protection; Cherokee may punish    | ENFORCEMENT CLAUSE         |
| IX      | No hunting on Cherokee land without passport                              | Boundary enforcement       |
| X       | Capital crimes: Cherokee must deliver offenders                           | Criminal justice           |
| XI      | U.S. crimes against Cherokee: same punishment as crimes vs. U.S. citizens | Equal justice              |
| XII     | Disputes resolved through proper channels before retaliation              | Conflict resolution        |
| XIII    | Cherokee report designs against U.S. peace                                | Intelligence sharing       |
| XIV     | U.S. furnishes farming implements; appoints 4 interpreters                | Agricultural assistance    |
| XV      | Past grievances ended; good faith execution                               | Binding clause             |
| XVI     | Takes effect when President ratifies with Senate advice/consent           | Constitutional requirement |

**Cherokee Signatories (42 total, 7 named leaders):**

1. **Chuleoah (The Boots)**
   - Role: War chief
   - Faction: War/Resistance

2. **Squollecuttah (Hanging Maw)**
   - Birth: ~1730
   - Death: ~1796
   - Role: Principal Chief, Overhill Cherokee
   - Faction: Peace
   - Post-treaty: Continued diplomacy; wounded in 1793 attack by settlers

3. **Enoleh (Black Fox)**
   - Role: Middle Towns representative

4. **Kanetetoka (Standing Turkey)**
   - Role: Cherokee leader

5. **Kunoskeskie (John Watts)**
   - Birth: ~1750
   - Death: ~1808
   - Role: War Council Head, Chickamauga
   - Faction: War (later peace advocate)
   - Post-treaty: Led 1796 boundary speech; negotiated Tellico Blockhouse (1794)
   - Legacy: Returned to diplomacy after realizing military resistance insufficient

6. **Nenetooyah (Bloody Fellow)**
   - Birth: ~1740
   - Death: ~1801
   - Role: War Chief and Diplomatic Spokesman, Chickamauga
   - Faction: War (later peace advocate)
   - Post-treaty: Led Philadelphia delegation (1792); received title "General" from Washington; renamed Iskagua ("Clear Sky")
   - Legacy: Transitioned fully to diplomacy; served as principal chief 1798-1801

7. **Chuquilatague (Doublehead)**
   - Birth: ~1744
   - Death: ~1807
   - Role: War Chief of Chickamauga Cherokee
   - Faction: War (later mixed)
   - Post-treaty: Continued warfare through 1794; later involved in controversial land cessions; assassinated 1807
   - Legacy: Complex figure—fierce warrior who later facilitated land cessions

**Interpreters Appointed:**

- James Carey (John Cery) - Primary interpreter
- John Thompson - Half Creek, also interpreter

**Verification:** 3 sources cross-referenced (Avalon Project, DigiTreaties, National Archives)

### Treaty 2: Additional Article to the Treaty of Holston (February 17, 1792)

**File Location:** `/content/documents/treaty-holston-additional-1792.md`

**Official Title:** Additional Article to the Treaty of Holston

**Date:** February 17, 1792

**Negotiating Party for U.S.:** Henry Knox, Secretary of War

**Cherokee Signatories:** 6 delegation members

- Iskagua (Clear Sky / Bloody Fellow) - **marked with his Cherokee mark**
- Nontuaka (The Northward)
- Teesteke (Common Disturber / The Disturber)
- Chutloh (King Fisher / Kingfisher)
- Kanotah (The Rising Fawn)
- Kanathahee (The New Water)

**Content:**

**Original:** $1,000 annually
**Amended:** $1,500 annually
**Increase:** 50% (responding directly to Cherokee delegation demands)

**Historical Significance:**

This amendment represents a CHEROKEE VICTORY. Within 7 months of signing the Treaty of Holston, Cherokee diplomatic pressure forced the U.S. to increase payment by 50%. This demonstrates:

1. **Diplomatic Efficacy:** Cherokee had leverage
2. **Federal Process:** Cherokee understood Senate/President roles; appealed over Territorial Governor
3. **Quick Results:** Senate approved January 20, 1792 (within 3 weeks of Cherokee request)
4. **Naming Ceremony:** Washington granted Bloody Fellow the name "Iskagua" (Clear Sky/Great Day) during Philadelphia visit—symbolic naming reflecting hope for peace

**Verification:** 3 sources (Avalon Project, National Archives, Cherokee treaty records)

## PART 3: CHEROKEE LEADERS - COMPLETE PROFILES

### Group A: War Faction Leaders

#### Leader 1: Bloody Fellow (Nenetooyah / Iskagua)

**Identification:**

- Cherokee Name: Nenetooyah (Iskagua after 1792)
- Meaning: Iskagua = "Clear Sky" or "Great Day"
- Birth: ~1740
- Death: ~1801
- Town: Lookout Town (Chickamauga faction)

**Role:** War Chief and Diplomatic Spokesman, Chickamauga Cherokee

**Treaty Participation:**

- **Treaty of Holston (1791):** Signatory as Nenetooyah
- **Additional Article (1792):** Signatory as Iskagua ("Clear Sky")
- **Philadelphia Delegation (1792):** Principal spokesman

**Faction:** War (Chickamauga/resistance faction) → Transition to Peace (1792+)

**Major Actions:**

1. **1791 - Treaty of Holston:**
   - Signed as Nenetooyah
   - War faction chief giving credibility to peace treaty
   - Represented Cherokee willing to negotiate despite military capability

2. **1792 - Philadelphia Delegation (January):**
   - Led Cherokee delegation to federal government
   - Protested treaty terms to President Washington & Secretary Knox
   - Successfully demanded annuity increase ($1,000 → $1,500)
   - Signed Additional Article (February 17, 1792)
   - Received title "General" from President Washington (rare honor)
   - Renamed "Iskagua" ("Clear Sky") by Washington—symbolic naming for peace

3. **1792-1801 - Diplomatic Focus:**
   - Transitioned from military leadership to permanent diplomatic role
   - Served as principal chief (1798-1801)
   - Negotiated with Moravian missionaries (1800)—cultural preservation focus
   - Died ~1801, having moved fully from war to peace advocacy

**Verification:** Reconstructed biography from U.S. government records, treaty documents, settler correspondence

**Interpretation Notes:**

Bloody Fellow represents a crucial Cherokee strategy: including both war and peace faction leaders in diplomacy. His willingness to sign the treaty while maintaining warrior credibility gave Cherokee negotiators leverage with multiple American audiences (settlers feared him; government respected his position).

His transformation from Nenetooyah (war name) to Iskagua (peace name) in a single year is not coincidental—it reflects the symbolic and actual transition from resistance to accommodation while maintaining Cherokee sovereignty.

**Missing Evidence:**

No Cherokee-authored personal documents about Bloody Fellow have been located. His perspective comes through:

- His speeches to Washington (recorded by Knox)
- His demands in Philadelphia (recorded by U.S. officials)
- His actions and negotiations (observed by settlers)

But his own written words, letters, or personal accounts: NOT FOUND in accessible archives.

#### Leader 2: John Watts (Kunoskeskie / Young Tassel)

**Identification:**

- Cherokee Name: Kunoskeskie (Ganodisgi, Kunokeski)
- Meaning: Also known as Young Tassel
- Birth: ~1750
- Death: ~1808
- Town: Willstown (Chickamauga faction)
- Family: Son of white trader John Watts Sr.; nephew of Old Tassel, Doublehead, Pumpkin Boy

**Role:** War Council Head of the Chickamauga Cherokee (1792-1808)

**Treaty Participation:**

- **Treaty of Holston (1791):** Signatory as Kunoskeskie
- **Boundary Speech (1796):** Cherokee-authored diplomatic demand

**Faction:** War (militant resistance) → Peace (diplomatic enforcement)

**Major Actions:**

1. **1791 - Treaty of Holston:**
   - Signed as Kunoskeskie
   - War faction signatory
   - Tentative acceptance of peace, with ongoing skepticism about American compliance

2. **1792 - Dragging Canoe's Succession:**
   - When Dragging Canoe died (March 1, 1792), Watts became head of Cherokee war council (skiagusta)
   - Led war operations from Willstown near Fort Payne, Alabama
   - Commanded 280-300 warriors in Buchanan's Station attack (September 1792)—one of largest Native American forces assembled in region
   - Seriously wounded in Buchanan's Station battle

3. **1794 - Diplomacy Shift:**
   - Negotiated Tellico Blockhouse agreement with Governor Blount
   - Effectively ended Cherokee-American wars
   - Transition from pure military leader to diplomatic strategist

4. **1796 - Boundary Speech (December 22):**
   - Delivered formal speech to President Washington & Secretary of War
   - Demanded formal boundary survey with Cherokee oversight
   - Specified technical requirements (timing, surveyor quality, Cherokee representation)
   - **Key quote:** "Peace has been faithfully observed by my Nation since the conference of 1795"
   - Demonstrated that Cherokee, not U.S., were keeping treaty terms

5. **1802-1808 - Later Leadership:**
   - Continued as prominent Cherokee leader until death ~1808
   - His position passed to uncle Doublehead upon death

**Verification:** Reconstructed biography from treaty records, settlers' accounts, government correspondence

**Interpretation Notes:**

John Watts exemplifies the Cherokee evolution from military resistance to diplomatic pressure. His 1792-1794 period shows him simultaneously:

- Fighting settlers militarily (Buchanan's Station)
- Negotiating with Spanish in Pensacola for arms/supplies
- Exploring peace settlements with Blount

By 1796, he had fully adopted diplomacy as the preferred strategy—not surrender, but sophisticated assertion of Cherokee sovereignty through legal/diplomatic means.

His 1796 boundary speech is remarkably technically sophisticated. His demands about surveyor quality and Cherokee representation show deep understanding of how American systems could be used to protect Cherokee interests.

**Documented Evidence:**

This is one of the few Cherokee leaders with a Cherokee-authored document in the Evidence Room:

- **John Watts Boundary Speech (1796)** - preserved in War Department papers
- His other perspectives known only through U.S. official accounts and settler reports

#### Leader 3: Doublehead (Chuquilatague / Tal-tsu'tsa)

**Identification:**

- Cherokee Name: Chuquilatague (Tal-tsu'tsa, Dsugweladegi)
- Birth: ~1744
- Death: ~1807 (assassinated)
- Town: Browns Ferry
- Family: Brother of Old Tassel, Pumpkin Boy; great-uncle of Sequoyah (creator of Cherokee syllabary)

**Role:** War Chief of Chickamauga Cherokee

**Treaty Participation:**

- **Treaty of Holston (1791):** Signatory as Chuquilatague
- Philadelphia visit (1791): Met with President Washington

**Faction:** War (1788-1794) → Mixed/Complex (1794-1807)

**Major Actions:**

1. **1788-1792 - War Leadership:**
   - Murder of brother Old Tassel under flag of truce (1788) transformed him into formidable war leader
   - Operated with great-nephew Bob Benge
   - Conducted raids on Tennessee frontier settlements
   - Feared reputation for ferocity in battle

2. **1791 - Treaty of Holston:**
   - Signed treaty despite being war faction leader
   - Demonstrated willingness to explore diplomacy alongside military resistance
   - His signature gave treaty legitimacy among Cherokee resistance movement

3. **1791 - Philadelphia Visit:**
   - Traveled to Philadelphia with other Cherokee leaders
   - Met with President Washington
   - Demonstrated openness to federal negotiation

4. **1792-1794 - Continued Warfare:**
   - Maintained military operations after treaty
   - Operated from Browns Ferry (1790-1802)
   - Part of triumvirate with Bloody Fellow and John Watts after Dragging Canoe's death

5. **1794-1802 - Transition to Leadership:**
   - After 1794 peace settlement, became leader among Lower Cherokee
   - Married Nannie Drumgoole (~1794)
   - Established himself as prominent in Cherokee society
   - When John Watts died (~1802), became Chickamauga leader

6. **1805 - Land Cessions:**
   - Signed Treaty of 1805 ceding significant Cherokee territory
   - Allegedly received personal payments and reservations
   - Became deeply unpopular for facilitating land cessions

7. **1807 - Assassination (August 9):**
   - Killed by Major Ridge, Alex Saunders, and John Rogers
   - Motivation: Either disputes over cotton trade control OR outrage over land cessions
   - Death marked shift in Cherokee politics toward younger generation committed to preserving lands

**Verification:** Reconstructed biography from treaty records, government documents, settler accounts

**Interpretation Notes:**

Doublehead represents the tragic complexity of Cherokee leadership in this period. He was:

- A fierce defender of Cherokee territory through warfare (1788-1794)
- A treaty signatory (1791)—willing to explore peace
- A political leader adapting to post-war reality (1794+)
- A controversial figure in land cessions (1805+)
- A victim of intra-Cherokee politics (1807—assassination)

His assassination by Major Ridge (himself a rising Cherokee leader) represents the transition from war/peace negotiations to internal Cherokee political conflicts over land management and sovereignty preservation.

**Missing Evidence:**

No Cherokee-authored documents from Doublehead exist in accessible archives. His perspective is known only through U.S. accounts and settler reports.

### Group B: Peace Faction Leaders

#### Leader 4: Hanging Maw (Squollecuttah / Scolacutta)

**Identification:**

- Cherokee Name: Uskwa'li-gu'ta (Squollecuttah, Scolacutta)
- Meaning: "His stomach hangs down"
- Birth: ~1730
- Death: ~1796
- Town: Coyatee (Overhill Cherokee)

**Role:** Principal Chief of Overhill Cherokee (1788-1794)

**Treaty Participation:**

- **Treaty of Hopewell (1785):** Signatory (worked with Old Tassel)
- **Treaty of Holston (1791):** Principal Cherokee representative; signature appears first
- His role spanned peace negotiations across a decade (1785-1795)

**Faction:** Peace (committed to accommodation with Americans)

**Major Actions:**

1. **1788 - Succession:**
   - Following Old Tassel's death, became leading chief of Overhill Cherokee
   - Claimed leadership as chief headman of Overhill Towns
   - Though Cherokee general council had selected Little Turkey as First Beloved Man after capital move to Ustanali

2. **1777-1780 - Early War Support:**
   - Initially supported Dragging Canoe and Chickamauga in resistance
   - After devastating American attacks (1780), shifted to peace advocacy
   - Partnered with Old Tassel in peace negotiations

3. **1786 - Treaty of Coyatee:**
   - Negotiated with State of Franklin
   - Attempted to establish peaceful boundaries with encroaching settlers
   - Demonstrated commitment to diplomatic solutions

4. **1791 - Treaty of Holston:**
   - Principal Cherokee representative at negotiations
   - His signature appears among the first signatories (reflecting status)
   - Governor Blount recognized him as acknowledged leader of Overhill Cherokee
   - His influence helped bring together peace and war faction leaders (though some Chickamauga skeptical)

5. **1793 - Settler Attack & Betrayal:**
   - Led Cherokee negotiating party
   - Knoxville settlers attacked the party
   - Hanging Maw seriously wounded
   - His wife also wounded
   - Several other Cherokee killed
   - Despite this betrayal, continued to advocate for peace

6. **1795-1796 - Final Years:**
   - Continued working for peace agreements
   - Died ~1796 (shortly after treaty violation attack)
   - Spent final years working with other peace chiefs

**Verification:** Reconstructed biography from treaty records, government correspondence, settler accounts

**Interpretation Notes:**

Hanging Maw represents the committed peace faction—leaders who sacrificed military resistance for diplomatic solutions, even when those solutions failed to protect their people.

His signing both Treaty of Hopewell (1785) and Treaty of Holston (1791) shows six years of consistent peace advocacy.

His 1793 wounding while on a peaceful negotiating mission represents the tragic irony of the peace faction: they honored peace commitments while settlers and frontiersmen violated them with impunity.

His continued peace advocacy even after personal betrayal and wounding shows deep commitment to the principle that war could not succeed, and that negotiation was the only path forward.

**Missing Evidence:**

Cherokee-authored documents from Hanging Maw: NOT FOUND

However, his perspective is partially preserved in records documenting his claimed statement (after 1793 attack):

> "Governor Blount always told me that nothing should happen to me as long as I did live, but he hardly got out of sight until I was invaded by them and like to have got killed"

This quote suggests Hanging Maw kept records or made statements about treaty promises vs. actual outcomes.

**Note on Timeline:**

Hanging Maw's chieftainship ended in 1794—approximately the same time John Watts was delivering his boundary speech demanding enforcement. This suggests that the peace faction (Hanging Maw) and war faction (John Watts) had both shifted to legal/diplomatic pressure by mid-1790s, but the peace faction may have given up first (Hanging Maw's death suggests declining health/spirit after 1793 betrayal).

- Treaty signed establishing peace and boundaries
- Boundary line defined (map attached to treaty)

**Date:** December 22, 1796

3. Cherokee envoys present: Wiley Elder, Ohadlokee, Watts himself
4. Survey timing: "Just as the Trees begin to put out their Buds"
5. Justice for unpunished murders of Cherokee by settlers

**Significance:**

- Five years after Treaty of Holston, Cherokee still demanding compliance
- Demonstrates Cherokee tracking of treaty violations
- Shows evolution from military to legal/diplomatic pressure
- Asserts Cherokee sovereignty in boundary enforcement process

**Verification Status:** VERIFIED - Papers of War Department, pages 57-60

## PART 6: TREATY TERMS ASSESSMENT

### Cherokee Interpretation vs. U.S. Interpretation

**Key Point:** The Additional Article (1792) suggests Cherokee interpreted the original $1,000 annuity differently than Governor Blount had presented.

**What Happened:**

1. **During Treaty Negotiations (July 1791):**
   - Cherokee requested $1,500 annual payment
   - Blount claimed he was "limited" and couldn't promise it
   - Blount said he would "write relatively to it" (refer to federal government)

2. **After Treaty Ratification:**
   - Treaty stated $1,000 annual payment
   - No indication of $1,500 alternative in signed document

3. **Cherokee Delegation to Philadelphia (January 1792):**
   - Six months later, Cherokee came to collect on what they believed was Blount's promise to refer the $1,500 request to federal government
   - Knox's report documented Cherokee demand for $1,500, noting: "the sum of fifteen hundred Dollars was urged by the Cherokees at the Treaty"

4. **Result:**
   - Senate approved $1,500 within 3 weeks
   - Additional Article signed February 17, 1792
   - 50% payment increase

**Interpretation:**

This sequence reveals several important points:

1. **Translation/Communication Issues:** Did Blount's promise about referring to federal government get correctly translated? Did Cherokee understand American federal structure? (Evidence suggests yes, but there was potential for miscommunication.)

2. **Cherokee Negotiation Strategy:** Cherokee tracked Blount's statements carefully. They understood that the federal government (President + Senate) might be more favorable than the territorial governor, so they appealed directly to the federal level.

3. **Successful Negotiation:** The $1,500 outcome shows Cherokee had legitimate leverage and knew how to use it.

4. **Power Dynamics:** Neither party was completely trusting. Cherokee felt they had secured a promise. U.S. government formalized it through Additional Article. Both sides treating this as binding diplomatic correction.

## PART 7: VERIFICATION METHODOLOGY & EVIDENCE STANDARDS

### Standards Applied to All Documents

**For Cherokee-Authored Documents:**

1. Source is directly quoted Cherokee voice (speeches, letters) translated to English
2. Translation methodology documented (who translated, how, verification)
3. Original source located and referenced
4. Cross-referenced with multiple historical sources
5. Context of creation documented (why this document exists)

**For Treaties:**

1. Multiple archive copies verified (Avalon, DigiTreaties, National Archives)
2. Signature verification against treaty documents
3. Ratification status confirmed
4. Implementation/outcomes documented

**For Cherokee Leader Information:**

1. Biographical data from treaty signatures and government records
2. Actions documented in U.S. government correspondence
3. Settler accounts cross-referenced
4. Important gaps noted (oral histories not preserved in archives)

### Limitations Acknowledged

**What We Have:**

- Cherokee voices filtered through interpreters
- Most Cherokee documents date to 1790s (post-Dragging Canoe)
- No Cherokee syllabary documents from this period (Sequoyah invented it 1820s)
- All existing records are in English translation

**What We Don't Have:**

- Cherokee personal letters/journals (pre-syllabary period)
- Cherokee internal government records
- Cherokee oral histories documented and preserved in archives
- Cherokee women's voices (very limited documentation)
- Rank-and-file Cherokee perspectives (mostly documented are leaders)

**What Was Lost:**

- Original Cherokee-language communications (no way to recover—pre-syllabary)
- Oral traditions (unless documented by 20th century researchers)
- Personal documents destroyed in warfare and relocation

**Key Concepts to Teach:**

   - Treaty of Holston is between sovereign nation (Cherokee) and U.S. government
   - Cherokee maintained diplomatic independence even during conflicts

2. **Cherokee Agency**
   - Don't teach Cherokee as passive victims
   - They initiated diplomacy (Philadelphia delegation)
   - They forced treaty modifications
   - They tracked violations and demanded accountability
   - They used legal/diplomatic means to protect interests

3. **Treaty as Living Document**
   - Treaty of Holston (1791) was amended within months (1792)
   - Shows ongoing negotiation, not one-time settlement
   - John Watts's 1796 speech shows Cherokee still enforcing terms 5 years later
   - Treaties were process, not conclusion

4. **War vs. Peace Factions**
   - Don't simplify as "peaceful Cherokee vs. warrior Cherokee"
   - Both factions participated in diplomacy

   - John Watts was war leader who shifted to legal pressure (diplomacy)
   - Even Doublehead the fierce warrior signed treaty and met with President

5. **Broken Promises**
   - Hanging Maw's 1793 wounding while on peaceful mission shows pattern: Cherokee honored peace while settlers violated it
   - John Watts 1796 speech documents unpunished murders of Cherokee
   - Cherokee held U.S. accountable; U.S. failed to enforce its own rules

## PART 9: IMPORTANT HISTORICAL CONTEXT

### The Period (1788-1796)

**1788:** Murder of Old Tassel under flag of truce (transforms multiple younger leaders into committed warriors)

**1791:**

- Treaty of Holston signed (July)
- Cherokee expect American compliance with treaty terms

**1792:**

- January: Cherokee delegation arrives in Philadelphia uninvited, protests treaty, wins modifications
- February: Additional Article signed (50% annuity increase)
- September: John Watts leads 280-300 warriors in Buchanan's Station attack (largest force assembled)
- **Key point:** War and peace factions operating simultaneously

**1793:**

- Hanging Maw wounded by settlers while on peaceful negotiating mission
- Shows asymmetry: Cherokee honoring peace; settlers ignoring it

**1794:**

- Tellico Blockhouse agreement effectively ends warfare
- John Watts transitions to peace faction
- War faction accepts federal peace terms

**1795:**

- Peace conference (Cherokee observe treaty peace from this point)

**1796:**

- John Watts delivers boundary speech demanding treaty enforcement
- Five years after signing, Cherokee still holding U.S. accountable
- Shows enduring commitment to legal/diplomatic pressure

### What This Reveals

**Cherokee Strategy Evolution:**

- 1788-1791: Military resistance + diplomatic participation
- 1791-1792: Post-treaty diplomacy to modify unfavorable terms
- 1792-1794: Maintain military capacity while accepting peace terms
- 1794-1796: Shift to legal/diplomatic enforcement of treaties

**U.S. Behavior Pattern:**

- Negotiate treaties
- Fail to enforce own terms
- Allow settlers to violate boundaries
- Don't punish settler crimes against Cherokee
- Acknowledge Cherokee complaints but fail to act

**Outcome:**

- Cherokee realize military victory not possible
- U.S. realizes Cherokee still powerful enough to demand accountability
- Both sides shift to uneasy coexistence
- Tension remains high throughout 1790s
