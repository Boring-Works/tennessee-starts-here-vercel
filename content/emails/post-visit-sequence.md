# Post-Visit Email Nurture Sequence

**Purpose:** Turn one-time visitors into lifelong advocates
**Trigger:** Post-visit (requires visitor email capture at admission)
**Sequence:** 3 emails over 30 days

---

## Email 1: "You Were There"

**Send:** Day 1 (24 hours after visit)
**Goal:** Reinforce the experience, prompt social sharing, capture photos

### Subject Line Options

1. You stood where Tennessee began.
2. That ground you walked today? It still remembers.
3. Before there was a Tennessee, you were there.

### Body

---

**Subject:** You stood where Tennessee began.

**Preview text:** The ground endures. So does the memory.

---

Yesterday, you walked the same ground where William Blount governed and Andrew Jackson lodged. You stood inside the Cobb House. You saw the glass windows he wrote home about.

Most people drive past historical markers. You stopped. You walked in. You let 1791 meet you where you stood.

That matters.

**Did you take any photos?**

We'd love to see Rocky Mount through your eyes. Tag us on Instagram [@rockymounttn](https://instagram.com/rockymounttn) or Facebook [@rockymountmuseum](https://facebook.com/rockymountmuseum) and we'll share the best shots.

No photos? That's fine too. Some moments are better kept than captured.

Thank you for visiting the capital that made a state.

## Email 2: "Go Deeper"

**Send:** Day 7
**Goal:** Drive to Evidence Room (digital archive), introduce sister sites

### Subject Line Options

1. The documents Blount signed. The letters he wrote. They're still here.
2. What you didn't see at Rocky Mount (but can now).
3. Governor Blount's handwriting. Want to see it?

### Body

---

**Subject:** The documents Blount signed. The letters he wrote. They're still here.

**Preview text:** Primary sources you can read yourself.

---

A week ago, you stood in the room where Tennessee's government began.

But here's what most visitors never realize: the paper trail survived too.

**The Evidence Room**

We've assembled the primary sources — letters, treaties, territorial records — that prove what happened on this ground. Not interpretations. Not summaries. The actual documents.

One favorite: On October 20, 1790, William Blount wrote to his half-brother describing his new accommodations at Rocky Mount:

> "I am very well accommodated with a Room with Glass Windows, Fire Place &c &c at this place."

Glass windows were a luxury on the frontier. Blount noticed. He wrote it down. That letter still exists.

[Explore the Evidence Room →](https://rockymountmuseum.com/evidence-room)

---

**Continue the Trail**

Rocky Mount was the center of territorial government, but the Overmountain settlements stretched across the region. If you haven't visited these sister sites, they complete the picture:

- **Sycamore Shoals State Historic Park** (Elizabethton, 15 min)
  Where the Overmountain Men gathered before marching to Kings Mountain.

- **Tipton-Haynes State Historic Site** (Johnson City, 15 min)
  45 acres of restored buildings and the limestone cave where John Tipton hid during the State of Franklin crisis.

- **Carter Mansion** (Elizabethton, 15 min)
  Tennessee's oldest frame house. A National Historic Landmark built circa 1780.

The Army gathered at the Shoals. The State started here. Together, they tell the full story.
