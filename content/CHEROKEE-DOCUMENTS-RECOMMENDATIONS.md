## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: THE PROBLEM

### Current Evidence Room Imbalance

**What we have:**

- Treaty of Holston (1791) - U.S. government document
- Treaty Additional Article (1792) - U.S. government document
- 50 Cherokee biographical profiles (5 full, 45 stubs) - written BY project team ABOUT Cherokee people
- Blount-Knox correspondence - U.S. officials writing ABOUT Cherokee relations

**What's missing:**

- **ZERO documents authored BY Cherokee leaders**
- **ZERO documents representing Cherokee perspectives directly**
- **ZERO Cherokee voices as PRIMARY speakers**

### The Cultural Imbalance

Cherokee people currently appear as:

- **SUBJECTS** of U.S. policy (passive recipients)
- **OBJECTS** of observation (described by others)
- **SIGNATORIES** (names on treaties they didn't write)

They do NOT appear as:

- **AUTHORS** of their own narrative
- **AGENTS** with independent voices
- **NEGOTIATORS** articulating their positions

### What We Need

Documents where Cherokee leaders:

1. Speak in their own words (or as close as 1790s translation allows)
2. Articulate Cherokee positions, complaints, and demands
3. Assert agency and sovereignty
4. Challenge U.S. interpretations
5. Document treaty violations from Cherokee perspective

**This research identifies specific, locatable Cherokee-authored documents from 1791-1796 that can immediately address this imbalance.**

#### 2. **John Watts Speech on Peace & Boundaries, December 22, 1796**

**Status:** CONFIRMED - Located at Papers of the War Department

**Archive:** [War Department Papers](https://wardepartmentpapers.org/s/home/item/55955)

**Signatory Status:** John Watts signed Treaty of Holston (1791)

**Content Summary:**

- Peace had been observed since 1795 conference, but whites killed Indians without punishment
- Watts would abide ONLY by boundary line established with Governor Blount
- Demanded boundary be formally surveyed by U.S. surveyor of integrity (NOT land speculator)
- Insisted Cherokee envoys be present during survey
- Specified optimal time to run line (when trees begin budding for visibility)

**Why Valuable:**

- **DIRECT CHEROKEE AUTHORSHIP** - This is Watts speaking, not being spoken about
- **TREATY ENFORCEMENT** - Cherokee demanding U.S. honor Treaty of Holston terms
- **TECHNICAL KNOWLEDGE** - Shows Cherokee expertise in surveying, land management
- **ACCOUNTABILITY** - Cherokee documenting U.S. violations, demanding justice
- **AGENCY** - Cherokee dictating terms of boundary survey, not accepting U.S. unilateral action

**Unique Value:** This document shows Cherokee AS EQUALS demanding treaty compliance, not as subordinates requesting favors.

**Acquisition:** Papers of the War Department is digitized project (George Mason University) - should be freely accessible

### Category B: Cherokee Speeches (Translated but Cherokee-Authored)

#### 5. **Cherokee Delegation Speeches, Philadelphia, January 1792**

**Status:** CONFIRMED - Multiple speeches documented

**Primary Sources:**

- [Founders Online: Henry Knox to George Washington, January 17, 1792](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Washington/05-09-02-0273-0001)
- [Founders Online: Enclosure Report, January 17, 1792](https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Washington/05-09-02-0273-0002)

**Delegation Members:**

1. **Nenetooyah (Bloody Fellow)** - given name Iskaqua/"Clear Sky" at Holston
2. **Chutloh (Kingfisher)**
3. **Nontuaka (the Northward)**
4. **Teesteke (the Disturber)**
5. **Katigoslah (the Prince)**
6. Suaka (George Miller) - interpreter
7. Jane Dougherty - Cherokee woman
8. James Carey - interpreter

**Meeting Timeline:**

- December 29, 1791: Arrived in Philadelphia via Charleston
- January 4, 1792: Introduced to President George Washington
- January 5, 1792: First meeting with Secretary of War Henry Knox
- January 7, 9, 11, 1792: Presented speeches and detailed objections to treaty

**Key Cherokee Demands (in their own words):**

1. **Annuity increase:** $1,000 → $1,500 (GRANTED by U.S.)
2. **Settler removal:** "White people who are settled to the southward of the ridge which divides the waters of the Tennessee from those of Little River, should be removed"
3. **Boundary enforcement:** That ridge should serve as permanent barrier
4. **Federal protection:** Requested government representative to serve as "their Counsellor and protector"

**Translation Verification:**

- Speeches "translated by James Carey, interpreter, and confirmed by George Miller, a young Indian who understands the english language"
- Dual-verification system provides higher confidence in accuracy

**Why Valuable:**

- **CHEROKEE INITIATIVE** - They sailed from Charleston to Philadelphia (major journey) to voice objections
- **FORMAL PROTEST** - This was official Cherokee diplomatic mission to President and Secretary of War
- **DOCUMENTED AGENCY** - Cherokee forced treaty revision (Additional Article Feb 17, 1792)
- **CLEAR DEMANDS** - Specific, articulated Cherokee positions
- **SUCCESSFUL NEGOTIATION** - Shows Cherokee diplomatic power (won annuity increase)

**Cultural Insight:**
The Cherokee saw Blount's obsession with land acquisition and called him **"the Dirt Captain"** behind his back - this shows Cherokee awareness of U.S. motivations and ability to critique them.

**Acquisition:** Founders Online is freely accessible - extract relevant speech passages for Evidence Room

#### 7. **Hanging Maw to President Washington and Secretary of War, 1793**

**Status:** CONFIRMED - Referenced in historical records

- Signed Treaty of Hopewell (1785) and Treaty of Holston (1791)

- **BROKEN PROMISES** - Shows gap between treaty terms and reality
- **PERSONAL TESTIMONY** - Hanging Maw nearly died because settlers ignored Blount's guarantee
- **PEACE FACTION BETRAYED** - Pro-U.S. Cherokee leader attacked by Americans
- **SIGNATORY VOICE** - Hanging Maw signed BOTH major treaties (Hopewell, Holston)

**Action Required:** Locate full text of Hanging Maw's 1793 letter(s) - check:

## PART 4: INTERPRETER RECORDS & TRANSLATION ISSUES

### The Translation Problem

**Critical Issue:** ALL Cherokee "voices" from 1790s are FILTERED through interpreters.

**Known Interpreters at Treaty of Holston:**

1. **James Carey** (Cery) - signed treaty as interpreter
2. **John Thompson** - half Creek
3. **George Miller (Suaka)** - full-blooded Cherokee, young, English-speaking

### What We Need to Know

**Research Questions:**

1. Who were these interpreters working for? (U.S. government or independent?)
2. What was their translation methodology? (Word-for-word or paraphrase?)
3. Did they keep personal records/journals?
4. Were Cherokee leaders satisfied with translation quality?
5. Did misunderstandings occur? Are they documented?

### Potential Sources

**A. Interpreter Personal Papers**

- Do James Carey papers exist? (Check Tennessee archives)
- John Thompson records? (Check Creek/Cherokee archives)
- George Miller later writings? (He was young in 1791 - lived many more years)

**B. U.S. Official Observations**

- Did Blount, Knox, or Washington comment on interpretation quality?
- Were there disputes about what Cherokee leaders "really said"?
- Do meeting minutes note translation difficulties?

**C. Cherokee Complaints About Translation**

- Did Cherokee leaders ever say "that's not what I meant"?
- Were there documented translation disputes?

### Why This Matters

**Even if we find Cherokee letters, they were likely:**

1. Dictated to English-speaking scribe, OR
2. Written in Cherokee, translated to English

**Cherokee syllabary wasn't invented until 1820s (Sequoyah)** - so 1790s Cherokee couldn't write in Cherokee language.

**This means:**

- All 1790s Cherokee documents are ALREADY translations
- Cherokee voice is preserved, but filtered through language barrier
- We should acknowledge this limitation in Evidence Room

**Recommendation:** Add interpretive content explaining:

- How Cherokee communicated with U.S. in 1790s
- Role of interpreters
- Limitations of translated documents
- But also: Cherokee leaders CHOSE their words, even if through interpreter

## PART 6: WHAT ABOUT ORAL HISTORY?

### The Oral History Question

**Critical Unknown:** Has Cherokee Nation documented oral traditions about Treaty of Holston period?

**Possibilities:**

1. **Oral histories exist** - Cherokee families passed down stories about Treaty signatories, negotiations, what life was like
2. **Oral histories were collected** - Cherokee historians interviewed elders in 20th century about traditional knowledge
3. **Oral histories were never collected** - This knowledge may have been lost
4. **Oral histories exist but are not public** - Cherokee Nation may have cultural knowledge not shared outside tribe

### Research Strategy

**A. Ask Cherokee Nation Directly**
Contact: Cherokee Nation Cultural Resources, Oral History Program

**Questions:**

- Does Cherokee Nation have oral history archives about 1790s?
- Are there stories about Treaty of Holston negotiations?
- Do Cherokee families have traditions about specific Treaty signatories?
- Would Cherokee Nation be willing to share oral histories for Evidence Room?

**Cultural Sensitivity:**

- Some oral traditions may be culturally sensitive
- Cherokee Nation determines what can be shared publicly
- We ask permission, we don't demand access

---

**B. Check Existing Oral History Projects**

**Doris Duke Oral History Collection:**

- Major Native American oral history project (1960s-1970s)
- Included Cherokee interviews
- Check if any discuss 1790s treaty period

**Location:** Multiple universities - check University of Oklahoma, University of North Carolina

---

**C. Commission New Oral History Project**

**If oral histories about 1790s DON'T exist:**

- Consider commissioning NEW oral history project
- Cherokee Nation historians interview Cherokee elders
- Ask: "What have you been told about the Treaty of Holston period?"
- Document whatever traditions survive

**Budget:** $20K-30K for professional oral history project

**Timeline:** 12-18 months

**Outcome:** Even if traditions are fragmentary, documenting them preserves Cherokee voice

---

### Why Oral History Matters

**Written documents show:**

- What happened (events, dates, treaty terms)
- What was said in formal negotiations
- Official positions

**Oral history shows:**

- What it MEANT to Cherokee people
- How Cherokee interpreted events
- What Cherokee remembered vs. what U.S. recorded
- Cultural context Western documents miss

**Example:**

- Written document: "Bloody Fellow attended negotiations"
- Oral history: "Our people called Blount 'the Dirt Captain' because all he wanted was land"

The second gives CULTURAL INSIGHT the first doesn't capture.

### Worst Case Scenario

**We find:**

- No interpreter records

- Limited Cherokee scholar availability

**Result:**

- Evidence Room achieves **10/90 balance** (Cherokee voices : U.S. voices)

- Still better than current 0% Cherokee authorship

- Creates model for "best effort" when sources are limited
- Invites future researchers to continue search

### Risk Mitigation Strategies

#### **Risk 1: Cherokee Nation Declines Partnership**

**Mitigation:**

- Approach Eastern Band Cherokee as alternative
- Commission Cherokee scholars even without tribal partnership
- Continue archival research independently
- Add disclaimer: "We sought Cherokee Nation partnership; they declined/were unavailable"

---

#### **Risk 2: Very Few Cherokee Documents Exist**

**Mitigation:**

- Shift from "equal representation" to "intentional inclusion"
- Feature the few Cherokee documents PROMINENTLY
- Use contemporary Cherokee scholarship to fill gaps
- Create "What We Wish We Had" section - be transparent about losses

---

#### **Risk 3: Translation Issues Undermine Authenticity**

**Mitigation:**

- Research and document interpreter role extensively
- Be transparent: "This is Cherokee voice FILTERED through English translation"
- Explain Cherokee had no written language in 1790s (pre-Sequoyah)
- Frame as "best available access to Cherokee perspectives given historical constraints"

---

#### **Risk 4: Budget Constraints**

**Mitigation:**

- **Phase 1 is essential** ($2K-5K) - do this no matter what
- Phase 2 can be scaled down (grad student researcher instead of professional)
- Phase 3 can be partial (Cherokee scholar essay only, skip oral history)
- Seek grants: National Endowment for Humanities, Cherokee Nation grants, Tennessee historical societies

---

#### **Risk 5: Timeline Pressures**

**Mitigation:**

- Launch Evidence Room with Phase 1 documents
- Add Phase 2 findings as "updates" when research completes
- Add Phase 3 content as separate "Cherokee Perspectives" expansion
- Use rolling release model instead of waiting for completion

**Current State:**

- Cherokee people are OBJECTS in their own history
- Evidence Room tells story of things done TO Cherokee, not BY Cherokee
- Visitors see Cherokee as passive subjects of U.S. policy

**With Cherokee Voice Integration:**

- Cherokee people become AUTHORS of their own story
- Evidence Room tells story of Cherokee agency, resistance, negotiation
- Visitors see Cherokee as diplomatic equals, strategic thinkers, sovereign people

### The Ethical Imperative

**Question:** Can we tell the story of Treaty of Holston without Cherokee voices?

**Answer:** We can, but we SHOULDN'T.

**Why:**

- It's incomplete history
- It's unethical representation
- It perpetuates erasure
- It fails educational mission

### The Practical Imperative

**Good News:** Cherokee-authored documents EXIST and are LOCATABLE.

**This isn't a case of:**

- "We wish Cherokee documents existed but they don't"
- "We'd love Cherokee perspectives but can't find any"
- "This is the best we can do given limitations"

**This is a case of:**

- Documents exist
- Archives are accessible
- Cost is reasonable
- Timeline is achievable

**Therefore:** We have both ethical OBLIGATION and practical ABILITY to include Cherokee voices.
