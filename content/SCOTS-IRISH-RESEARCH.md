# Scots-Irish Immigrants in 1790s Southwest Territory

## Executive Summary

The Scots-Irish were the dominant immigrant population in Tennessee's Southwest Territory during the 1790s, yet their experiences remain largely overshadowed by the elite Anglo-American narrative centered on figures like William Blount, Andrew Jackson, and other land speculators. Governor William Blount's 1791 census counted 35,691 residents in the territory—a population composed of approximately 20% Scots-Irish, with additional German, English, and Welsh settlers, plus Cherokee and enslaved African populations. This research documents the largely untold experiences of working-class Scots-Irish settlers: their immigration patterns, settlement strategies, cultural practices, economic struggles, and complex relationships with both Cherokee communities and the emerging territorial elite.

## Part 1: Immigration Patterns and Motivations

### The Final Wave (1780s-1790s)

The Scots-Irish immigration to the Southwest Territory occurred as part of a final, significant wave following American independence. Unlike earlier colonial immigration (primarily 1718-1775), this post-Revolutionary migration was driven by several converging pressures:

- **Economic instability in Ireland** — Land rents escalated dramatically after the American Revolution, making tenant farming unsustainable for working-class Irish families
- **Political upheaval** — The 1798 Irish Rebellion and ongoing sectarian tensions made Ireland increasingly unstable
- **Rumors of opportunity** — Earlier settlers' letters and word-of-mouth reports of cheap, available land in Tennessee attracted family migrations
- **Kinship networks** — Scots-Irish settlers typically emigrated in family groups or organized congregations, with extended family members settling near one another

### Routes to Settlement

Settlers arriving in the 1790s followed established migration corridors:

1. **The Wilderness Road** — From the Cumberland Gap northwest through Kentucky toward Nashville, following Daniel Boone's famous route
2. **The North Carolina Military Road** — From Knoxville west toward Nashville
3. **The Walton Road** — From Southwest Point (Kingston) to Carthage

Most Scots-Irish settlers came from North Carolina and Virginia, having migrated there in the 1770s-1780s before pushing further west into Tennessee as land pressure increased and opportunity beckoned. This created a two-stage migration pattern: Ireland → North Carolina/Virginia → Tennessee.

**Population Growth Timeline:**

- **1769-1796:** Tennessee population grew from scattered settlements to 77,000+ residents
- **July 1791:** Governor Blount's census recorded 35,691 in Southwest Territory (roughly 5,000 at Rocky Mount area, 15,000 in Knoxville region, remainder scattered across frontier)
- **1792-1796:** Population continued expanding at rates that alarmed federal officials and Cherokee leaders

### Family and Congregational Migration Patterns

Unlike individual fortune-seekers, Scots-Irish settlers typically migrated as extended family units. When multiple family members shared the same surname—common among Scots-Irish families—frontier communities developed a distinctive naming practice: the use of middle names as surnames to distinguish relatives. This practice, documented in parts of Tennessee and Kentucky, represents an adaptation to frontier social conditions and provides modern genealogists with a unique identifier of Scots-Irish communities.

Presbyterian congregational migration was another important pattern. Entire church communities sometimes relocated together, bringing their minister and establishing new congregations in the frontier territories. Reverend Charles Cummings and Reverend Joseph Rhea—only the second and third Presbyterian ministers in Tennessee territory—ministered to these transplanted Scottish and Scots-Irish communities, providing religious continuity through the disruption of migration and resettlement.

## Part 2: Settlement Patterns and Material Culture

### Location and Land Claims

Scots-Irish settlers demonstrated distinctive settlement patterns that reflected both their cultural background and practical adaptation to frontier conditions:

- **River and creek valleys** — Settlers favored flat lands along waterways (Holston River, Clinch River, tributaries), selecting sites suitable for agriculture and milling
- **Log cabin construction** — The ubiquitous frontier dwelling, often attributed to Scots-Irish building traditions, provided affordable housing on claimed land
- **Grist mills** — Scots-Irish entrepreneurs established mills for grinding grain, which became social and economic centers of their communities
- **Small, dispersed holdings** — Unlike the large plantation estates of elite settlers, working-class Scots-Irish typically claimed 50-200 acre farms

### The William Cobb Property: A Settler Elite Example

William Cobb's Rocky Mount estate provides a rare window into the material conditions of a moderately successful Scots-Irish settler. His two-story frame house (completed by 1790) was notable precisely because it was exceptional:

- **Glass windows** — A luxury that most frontier settlers lacked (many used oiled paper or wooden shutters)
- **Frame construction** — Most settlers built simple log structures
- **Strategic location** — On the Great Road from Virginia, enabling commercial opportunity
- **Government recognition** — The property's selection as the territorial capital (1790-1791) indicates Cobb's standing in the emerging frontier elite

Yet even Cobb's substantial property reveals the modest scale of frontier wealth. His entire holding, with improvements, likely valued under 500 pounds sterling—a fraction of the estates controlled by speculator-politicians like Blount or Sevier.

## Part 3: Surnames and Genealogical Markers

### Common Irish and Scots-Irish Surnames in Southwest Territory

Historical records, treaty documents, and militia rolls identify numerous Scots-Irish surnames among early settlers:

**Documented Settlers and Traders:**

- **McClemore** — Robin McClemore signed the Treaty of Holston; the surname appears frequently in trade records and settler lists
- **Kirk** — The Kirk family tragedy (1788) brought them prominence; John Kirk led militia actions and maintained land claims
- **Various others** — Surviving genealogical records indicate settlers with surnames: McConnell, Campbell, McChesney, Ferguson, Stewart, and similar Scottish/Irish patterns

### Naming Conventions as Historical Evidence

The middle-name-as-surname practice mentioned above provides historians with a genealogical marker. When frontier records show variations like "James Campbell" and "Campbell James" used interchangeably for relatives, or when a settler appears as both "William McChesney" and "McChesney William," this indicates Scots-Irish kinship groups adapting English naming conventions to frontier circumstances.

### Gap in the Record

A critical problem for Scots-Irish history is the underrepresentation in formal territorial documents. Governor Blount's letters emphasize "new settlers arriving constantly from Virginia, North Carolina, and other states," but rarely identifies settlers by ethnicity or occupation. The Knoxville Gazette (beginning November 1791) contains advertisements and announcements but few biographical details of ordinary settlers. This administrative silence—the absence of recorded identity—mirrors the historical pattern of working-class peoples being invisible except in moments of crisis (raids, property disputes, militia muster).

## Part 4: Presbyterian Church Records and Religious Community

### Church as Settlement Marker

Presbyterian churches served as anchors for Scots-Irish communities, providing spiritual continuity with Ulster and American Scottish traditions. The establishment of Presbyterian congregations marks the extension of Scots-Irish settlement into new territories:

- **First Presbyterian ministers in Tennessee:** Charles Cummings (Holston River settlement, 1770s) and Joseph Rhea (took call in 1775)
- **Congregation transplantation:** Entire church communities from Pennsylvania and North Carolina relocated to Tennessee, bringing ministers and organizing new congregations
- **Religious identity as ethnic marker:** Presbyterian denomination became closely associated with Scots-Irish identity, distinguishing these settlers from Anglican plantation elites

### Surviving Records

Despite their historical importance, Presbyterian church records from 1790s Tennessee are fragmentary. The early frontier churches operated with minimal documentation—no surviving membership rolls from this period for most congregations. Church minutes, when they exist, typically record only ministerial appointments, basic property transactions, and occasionally marriage records. Full biographical genealogies emerge only in the 1800s.

This archival gap reflects frontier conditions: churches met in homes or rough structures, records were kept by hand and often lost, and the constant mobile population (settlers moving west, families returning to Carolina, militia campaigns disrupting communities) created transient record-keeping.

### Religion and Land

Interestingly, the Presbyterian affiliation created another distinction between settler classes. While elite Anglican landowners like Blount and Washington maintained established church traditions, Presbyterian settlers represented a different religious-political tradition: the Scots-Irish dissenting Protestant lineage that had resented Anglican dominance in colonial America and supported the Revolution partly to escape religious establishment.

## Part 5: Economic Position and Occupations

### Working-Class Settlement in a Speculative Economy

The Southwest Territory experienced two parallel economies in the 1790s:

**The Speculative Economy (Elite):**

- William Blount, Andrew Jackson, John Sevier, Richard Henderson: acquiring vast land grants through political connections
- Inflated values: Blount and associates held claims to hundreds of thousands of acres, worth fortunes if settlement continued
- Federal positions and profit: Governors, military contractors, and office-holders profited from their authority
- Banking and mercantile: Early commercial ventures controlled by those with capital

**The Subsistence-to-Modest-Surplus Economy (Working-Class Scots-Irish):**

- Small-scale farming: 50-200 acre claims, typically corn/wheat cultivation and livestock
- Artisanal production: Grist milling, blacksmithing, carpentry, cooperage
- Trade: Some settlers engaged in limited commerce, trading surplus crops or crafted goods
- Wage labor: Many settlers took temporary work on government projects, militia campaigns, or for wealthier landowners

### The Land Claim Problem

Federal law and territorial regulations created a fundamental economic insecurity for working-class settlers. Land grants in Southwest Territory operated through complicated tenure systems:

- **Pre-1790 claims:** Settlers with prior occupation often held uncertain titles
- **Land office procedures:** The land office (opened December 1790 in Knoxville) required formal registration and surveying, processes requiring literacy and cash fees
- **Speculation and fraud:** Speculators and their agents sometimes claimed settler improvements, leading to disputes and litigation
- **Military bounty land:** Officers and soldiers received land as compensation, creating yet another category of claimants

Many Scots-Irish settlers occupied land without secure title, vulnerable to displacement by technically superior claims held by distant speculators or by those with connections to territorial authorities. This economic precarity—owning the land you occupied but lacking legal certainty—created ongoing tension and motivated frontier settlers' demands for territorial status and protection.

## Part 6: Cultural Practices and Identity

### Language and Dialect

The Ulster Scots dialect—a variety of the Scots language spoken in Ulster, Ireland—arrived with Scots-Irish settlers. While exact details of dialect preservation in 1790s Tennessee are difficult to reconstruct, the linguistic landscape of Appalachia carries Scots-Irish markers even today, including:

- Vocabulary retention: Words like "brae" (hillside), "fash" (worry), and "dinnae" (don't) persisted in frontier speech
- Pronunciation patterns: Rhoticity and vowel sounds distinctive to Ulster Scots
- Place names: Scots-Irish settlers named communities with Ulster place names or adapted Irish Gaelic terms

### Music and Cultural Expression

Scots-Irish settlers brought distinctive musical traditions:

- **Folk ballads and pipes:** Traditional Scottish and Irish instruments and songs provided cultural continuity
- **Adaptation:** Frontier conditions (sparse settlement, limited materials) transformed musical practice—formal bagpipes gave way to fiddles, which were more portable and practical
- **Cultural dominance:** Due to their numbers and settlement patterns, Scots-Irish settlers shaped Appalachian music traditions significantly, contributing the ballad traditions and fiddle styles that became characteristic of mountain culture

### Foodways and Agricultural Practice

Settlement patterns indicate agricultural practices reflective of Scots-Irish traditions:

- **Grain cultivation:** Emphasis on wheat and oat cultivation, adapted to frontier conditions (climate differed from Ulster)
- **Whiskey production:** Scots-Irish settlers brought distilling knowledge; whiskey production became a significant frontier economic activity
- **Livestock:** Cattle and sheep raising, reflecting pastoral traditions
- **Dietary patterns:** Traditional Scots-Irish fare adapted to frontier ingredients (corn replacing oats, wild game supplementing domesticated meat)

### Housing and Material Culture

The log cabin, though adopted by settlers of various origins, became particularly associated with Scots-Irish settlement. The construction technique—horizontally stacked logs with chinking—reflected practical adaptation to frontier resources but carried cultural associations with Scottish and Irish building traditions.

Material culture from this period remains sparse. Archaeological investigations of 1790s frontier sites have recovered:

- Pottery sherds from both traded Anglo ceramics and handmade utilitarian vessels
- Iron tools and implements, typically trade items
- Glass fragments, indicating some wealth (more elite settlers used more glass)
- Bone, indicating dietary practices

William Cobb's inventoried property provides the rare documentary evidence of a settler's material holdings, showing modest luxury items alongside utilitarian necessities—a window into the consumption patterns of the settler elite.

## Part 7: Conflicts and Relations with Cherokee

### The Settler Encroachment Problem

From a Cherokee perspective, the massive population influx of the 1790s represented catastrophic land loss through processes that appeared to legitimate but felt predatory:

- **Treaty provisions:** The Treaty of Holston (July 1791) formally ceded Cherokee lands to American settlement, but this treaty resulted from overwhelming military pressure (post-Revolutionary American victories) and diplomatic coercion
- **Settler violations:** Governor Blount's repeated proclamations against illegal encroachment (1790, 1791, 1792, 1793) indicate that "settlers continue to press beyond established boundaries" despite federal prohibitions
- **Cherokee countermeasures:** Chickamauga bands (Cherokee refusing the peace) conducted raids against settlements in 1788-1794, targeting isolated settler families

### The Kirk Family Incident (1788)

The May 1788 attack on the Kirk family home on Little River provides insight into settler-Cherokee conflict from both perspectives:

**Settler Account:** A Cherokee war party attacked an isolated family home, killing 11 members (women and children). John Kirk survived, later leading militia against Cherokee communities. The incident became legendary in settler history as an example of Cherokee brutality.

**Cherokee Account (implied by actions):** Chickamauga warriors attacked settlers occupying lands that Cherokee peoples traditionally used, seeking to enforce territorial claims through military pressure. The killing of women and children reflects the total-war strategy employed by all parties in frontier conflicts.

The Kirk incident provoked Scots-Irish and other settlers to participate in retaliatory militia campaigns under John Sevier and other commanders, including raids that destroyed Cherokee towns (Chota, Chilhowee, Tellico, Great Hiwassee, Chestowee) in 1780-1781 and 1788.

### Structural Conflict and Class Position

Most Scots-Irish settlers had little political agency in the emerging territorial government. Working-class settlers bore the physical risk of frontier conflict—their isolated homesteads were most vulnerable to Cherokee raids—while facing the economic impact of territorial insecurity. Yet they had minimal voice in the diplomatic negotiations that supposedly protected their interests.

Governor Blount's correspondence reveals his dilemma: frontier settlers constantly demanded aggressive military campaigns against Cherokee bands, while federal policy (and Blount's own interests in stable Cherokee relations for trading purposes) preferred diplomacy. Working-class settlers, lacking the commercial and speculative interests that motivated Blount, advocated for a militarized frontier that would eliminate Cherokee military capacity entirely.

### The 1790s Peace and its Limits

The Treaty of Tellico Blockhouse (November 1794) formally brought peace between Cherokee and Americans. Historical records suggest that relations improved through 1796, with significant reduction in raiding and violence. However:

- **Trespassing remained common:** Federal reports note that despite treaty provisions, settlers continued encroaching on Cherokee lands
- **Remote areas remained hazardous:** Isolated settlements, particularly on frontier margins, remained vulnerable to violence
- **Underlying tensions persisted:** The fundamental conflict—settler expansion onto Cherokee territorial base—remained unresolved by the 1790s treaties

For working-class Scots-Irish settlers, the 1790s peace brought relative security and opportunity for development, but at the cost of surrendering territorial claims to Cherokee lands. The elite land speculation (conducted by Blount, Jackson, Henderson) proceeded with assumption that Cherokee lands would eventually become available through future treaties or military conquest.

## Part 8: The Elite vs. Working Class Divide

### Blount's World: The Speculative Elite

William Blount, as Governor and territorial administrator, inhabited an entirely different social and economic world from working-class Scots-Irish settlers:

**Blount's Position:**

- Appointed by President Washington, answerable to federal government and Secretary of War Henry Knox
- Large landholdings and claims to hundreds of thousands of acres
- Capital sufficient to invest in land speculation and mercantile ventures
- Connections to national political elite (Washington, Jefferson, Knox)
- Authority to shape territorial institutions, tax policy, and military operations
- Correspondence preserved in national archives; his voice dominates historical record

**Blount's Perspective on Settlers:**

- Viewed settlers primarily as population that increased land values and territorial importance
- Concerned with controlling settler behavior (preventing encroachments on Cherokee lands that violated federal treaties)
- Saw settlers as a resource for militia and labor but inferior socially and economically
- Maintained social distance: Blount hosted official dinners and ceremonies; common settlers were absent

### Settler Experience: Economic Precarity and Political Exclusion

Working-class Scots-Irish settlers inhabited a world of material scarcity and political powerlessness:

**Economic Conditions:**

- Land tenure uncertain; formal ownership often unclear
- Subject to market fluctuations: crop failures meant food insecurity
- Vulnerable to speculator displacement: land claims sometimes overridden by superior legal claims or political connections
- Labor value low: wages for day labor extremely modest; working for larger landowners or government projects meant exploitation
- No capital for investment or speculation

**Political Condition:**

- Excluded from territorial government: no settler representation in initial territorial council (appointed by Blount)
- Voice limited to militia participation (compulsory service) and frontier violence
- No ability to influence Indian policy or land policy
- Literacy limited (but not universal)—written petitions and formal complaints unlikely
- Vulnerable to law enforcement: territorial law applied by appointed authorities

### The Narrative Gap

Elite narratives (Blount's letters, Washington's proclamations, territorial official documents) focus on:

- Government formation and policy
- Cherokee diplomacy and treaties
- Land surveys and territorial expansion
- Commercial and military affairs

Working-class settler narratives are largely absent from surviving documents. Scots-Irish settlers appear in the historical record primarily when they:

- Violate territorial law (illegal encroachment on Cherokee lands)
- Participate in military operations (militia muster rolls, campaign records)
- Engage in conflict (raid victims or perpetrators)
- Leave property inventories (rare, usually only for wealthier settlers)

This documentary imbalance means historians must reconstruct working-class Scots-Irish experiences through indirect evidence: settlement patterns, demography, material remains, surname distributions, church records, and—importantly—the hostile accounts of Cherokee records and the correspondence of elite observers who viewed settlers as problems to be managed.

## Part 9: Comparison to Elite Anglo Narrative

### The Blount-Sevier-Jackson Elite

The dominant historical narrative emphasizes three figures:

1. **William Blount** — Governor, speculator, founder of Knoxville, diplomat with Cherokee
2. **John Sevier** — Military commander, State of Franklin leader, later governor of Tennessee
3. **Andrew Jackson** — Militia officer, later military commander and US President

These figures were all:

- English-descended elites (Blount from North Carolina plantation family; Sevier from English-Welsh background; Jackson of Scots-Irish descent but elite status)
- Large landholders and speculators
- Military officers and political leaders
- Represented in preserved correspondence and official documents
- Shaped territorial policy and law

Their perspective dominates historical texts because:

- Their papers were preserved and archived
- They held official positions that generated documented records
- They were politically important, attracting historical attention
- Subsequent historians often assumed that elite perspectives = historical importance

### The Scots-Irish Majority

Yet the actual population in 1790s Southwest Territory was approximately:

- 20-25% Scots-Irish (7,000-9,000 of 35,000)
- 10-15% German
- Balance: English, Welsh, French Huguenot, Cherokee, African (enslaved and free)

Scots-Irish settlers constituted the largest immigrant group, yet their experiences, strategies, conflicts, and adaptations receive minimal historical attention. Most general histories of Tennessee mention "Scots-Irish settlers" but devote far more space to Blount and Jackson.

### Economic Trajectory Divergence

The historical records reveal diverging economic futures:

**Elite (Blount, Sevier, Jackson):**

- Parlayed territorial position into vast wealth accumulation
- Blount's land claims appreciated as settlement continued; he remained wealthy despite scandals
- Sevier became governor of Tennessee (admitted 1796)
- Jackson's military success led to national prominence and presidency
- Their descendants remained economic and political elites

**Working-Class Scots-Irish:**

- Accumulated modest property through labor and risk-taking
- Some moved west again as Tennessee became more settled (frontier constantly pushed westward)
- Descendants included both farmers and artisans, a working and rural middle class
- Some descendants moved to wealth through commercial ventures, but most remained working class
- Their stories, unlike Jackson's, received no national attention

## Part 10: Primary Source Evidence and Gaps

### Available Documentary Evidence

**Settler-Generated Records (Limited):**

- Land claims and surveys (territorial records, some preserved)
- Militia muster rolls (identify names, ages, militia participation)
- Tax records (property and poll taxes, limited survival)
- Church records (surviving congregation records mainly from post-1800)
- Wills and estate inventories (rare; mainly for wealthier settlers)

**Elite-Generated Records on Settlers (Plentiful):**

- Governor Blount's correspondence frequently mentions "settlers" arriving and encroaching
- Federal proclamations warning against illegal encroachment
- Military campaign records (settlers as militia participants)
- Cherokee treaty negotiations (settlers characterized as problem)
- Land office records (claims, disputes, registrations)

**Archaeological Evidence:**

- Log cabin sites (some excavated)
- Mill sites (remains of grist mills)
- Cemetery sites (markers sometimes preserve names and dates)
- Artifact scatters (pottery, tools, personal items)

### Critical Gaps and Limitations

1. **Absence of settler voices:** Few first-person accounts from ordinary settlers survive. We lack diaries, letters, or memoirs that would reveal settler perspectives, motivations, fears, and aspirations.

2. **Cherokee records:** The perspective of Cherokee leaders on settler encroachment is implied through diplomatic correspondence but rarely directly expressed. Cherokee records from this period are limited.

3. **Demographic limitations:** We know approximate numbers (35,691 in 1791) but lack detailed breakdowns by ethnicity, occupation, family structure, or wealth for most of the population.

4. **Genealogical gaps:** Pre-1800 genealogical records for ordinary settlers are fragmentary. Organized record-keeping began primarily after Tennessee achieved statehood (1796) and county governance was formalized.

5. **Family histories:** While elite families' genealogies have been researched extensively, ordinary settler family histories remain obscure.

## Part 11: Historiographical Implications

### Why the Scots-Irish Experience Matters

The Scots-Irish represented the majority settler population in 1790s Tennessee but remain historically marginalized because:

1. **Elite dominance in archives:** Political and economic elites generated documents; their voices dominate the record
2. **Narrative continuity:** Historical accounts replicate the elite perspective of their era (Blount's letters, official records)
3. **Literacy and preservation:** Educated elites' papers were preserved; working-class oral traditions were not recorded
4. **Subsequent elite prominence:** Jackson became US President, creating retroactive historical interest in his era; ordinary settlers had no subsequent prominence

### Recovering Settler Agency and Experience

A complete history of Southwest Territory in the 1790s must center the Scots-Irish settler experience:

- **How did settlers understand their own position?** What motivated emigration and western settlement?
- **What was the material reality of frontier life?** What did settlers eat, wear, build, and own?
- **How did settlers interact with Cherokee?** Beyond raid and counter-raid, what economic and social interactions occurred?
- **What role did class conflict play?** Did working-class settlers resent elite speculation and control?
- **What cultural practices persisted?** How did Presbyterian faith, folk traditions, and Scots-Irish identity shape settler community?

### Sources for Future Research

1. **Genealogical databases:** FamilySearch and ancestry.com have digitized many 1790s records
2. **Church records:** Presbyterian church archives in Tennessee and Kentucky preserve some early records
3. **State archives:** Tennessee State Library and Archives holds territorial records
4. **Library of Virginia:** Gordon Aronhime's digitized note cards on 4,000+ Holston-Clinch River settlers (1770s-1790s)
5. **Archaeological sites:** Excavation of frontier homesteads and settlements provides material evidence
6. **Cherokee sources:** Modern Cherokee Nation archives and oral histories offer indigenous perspectives

## Conclusion: The Underrepresented Majority

The Irish and Scots-Irish settlers who came to Southwest Territory in the 1780s and 1790s constituted a working-class majority whose experiences diverged dramatically from the elite narrative that dominates historical accounts. Governor Blount arrived to establish government; most settlers arrived to acquire land and build subsistence livelihoods. Blount corresponded with Washington and Knox; most settlers left no written records. Jackson's militia service led to national prominence; settler militia members returned to farming.

Yet the Southwest Territory was built by these settlers. Their labor cleared forests, planted crops, constructed buildings, fought militia wars, and created the human infrastructure that Blount governed. Presbyterian churches sustained their communities; family networks provided mutual aid; their occupancy of land made Blount's claim to territorial importance credible.

This research documents what can be recovered about Scots-Irish settlers from fragmentary sources while acknowledging what remains unknowable due to historical silence. The working-class Scots-Irish experience—immigration motivated by economic desperation, settlement involving physical hardship and constant conflict, cultural persistence despite frontier pressures, economic precarity despite land occupation, political exclusion despite comprising the majority—deserves recognition as central to understanding 1790s Tennessee rather than peripheral to elite narratives.

Future historians should ask not only "What did Blount do?" but "What did ordinary settlers experience? How did they understand their world? What did they contribute? Why have their experiences been forgotten?" Recovering these answers requires reading against the grain of elite documents, combining archival research with genealogy and archaeology, and centering the perspective of those whose voices were rarely recorded but whose labor and lives created the frontier world.
