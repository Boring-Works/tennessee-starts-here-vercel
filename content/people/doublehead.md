## Biography

Doublehead, known in Cherokee as Chuquilatague or Tal-tsu'tsa, was one of the most feared warriors of the Cherokee during the Cherokee-American wars. Born around 1744, he was part of an influential Cherokee family that included his brothers Old Tassel and Pumpkin Boy. His sister Wurteh married Nathan Gist and produced Sequoyah, the creator of the Cherokee syllabary, making Doublehead the great-uncle of one of the most celebrated figures in Cherokee history.

The murder of his brother Old Tassel under a flag of truce in 1788 transformed Doublehead into one of the most capable and ruthless Cherokee war leaders. His reputation for ferocity in battle made him feared among frontier settlers. Beginning in 1791, he operated closely with his great-nephew Bob Benge, considered one of the most formidable warriors on the Tennessee frontier. Together they conducted raids that terrorized American settlements.

Following Dragging Canoe's death in 1792, Doublehead became part of the Chickamauga leadership triumvirate alongside Bloody Fellow and John Watts. While living at Browns Ferry from approximately 1790 to 1802, he maintained his reputation as a warrior while also engaging in diplomacy. In 1791, he traveled to Philadelphia and met with President Washington, demonstrating his willingness to explore diplomatic solutions alongside military resistance.

After the 1794 peace settlement, Doublehead transitioned to a leadership role among the Lower Cherokee. When John Watts died around 1802, Doublehead was chosen as leader of the Chickamauga. He married Nannie (Nan-que-se, meaning "The Pain") Drumgoole around 1794, establishing himself as a prominent figure in Cherokee society during this period of uneasy peace with the Americans.

## Role in the Treaty of Holston

Doublehead's signature on the Treaty of Holston represented a significant moment in Cherokee diplomacy. As a respected war chief, his willingness to sign gave the treaty credibility among the Cherokee resistance movement. His name appears on the treaty document as Chuquilatague, and his presence at the negotiations indicated that even the most militant Cherokee leaders were willing to explore peaceful coexistence if the Americans would honor their commitments. However, like other Chickamauga leaders, his adherence to the treaty's terms was contingent on American compliance with their obligations.

## Legacy

Doublehead's later years proved controversial within Cherokee society. After the wars ended, he became involved in land negotiations with the United States, signing the Treaty of 1805 that ceded significant Cherokee territory. His willingness to cede lands, allegedly in exchange for personal payments and reservations, made him deeply unpopular among Cherokee who sought to preserve their remaining territory.

On August 9, 1807, Doublehead was killed by Major Ridge, Alex Saunders, and John Rogers. The assassination was motivated either by disputes over control of the cotton trade or by outrage over his role in ceding Cherokee lands. His death marked a shift in Cherokee politics, as a younger generation of leaders committed to preserving Cherokee lands through legal and diplomatic means rose to prominence. Doublehead remains a complex figure: a fierce defender of Cherokee territory through warfare who later became associated with the very land cessions his earlier resistance had sought to prevent.
