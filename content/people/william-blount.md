## Biography

William Blount (1749-1800) served as the first and only Governor of the Territory of the United States South of the River Ohio, commonly called the Southwest Territory, from 1790 until Tennessee achieved statehood in 1796. He was simultaneously Superintendent of Indian Affairs for the Southern District, making him the federal government's primary representative for relations with the Cherokee, Chickasaw, Choctaw, and Creek nations.

Born into a prominent North Carolina family, Blount served in the Continental Congress and signed the United States Constitution as a delegate from North Carolina. His extensive land holdings and business interests in the western territories made him a natural choice to lead the new government, though these same interests would later create conflicts of interest that shadowed his tenure.

## The Rocky Mount Period

Blount arrived at Rocky Mount, the home of William Cobb, on October 11, 1790, establishing it as the first capital of the Southwest Territory. His famous letter of October 20, 1790, describing the house with its "Glass Windows" and "comfortable accommodations" provides our most detailed contemporary description of the building that would serve as Tennessee's first seat of government.

## The Treaty of Holston

Blount's most significant achievement during the territorial period was negotiating the Treaty of Holston with the Cherokee Nation in July 1791. Meeting at White's Fort (present-day Knoxville), Blount secured the signatures of forty-two Cherokee chiefs, establishing peace and boundaries that would shape the region's development. His handling of the negotiations demonstrated both diplomatic skill and a pragmatic understanding of Cherokee political dynamics.

## Later Career and Legacy

After Tennessee achieved statehood in 1796, Blount was elected as one of its first United States Senators. However, his career ended in disgrace when he was expelled from the Senate in 1797 for involvement in a conspiracy to help Britain seize Spanish-held Louisiana and Florida. Despite this fall from grace, he remained popular in Tennessee and was elected to the state senate, where he served as speaker until his death in 1800.
