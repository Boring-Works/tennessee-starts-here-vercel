## Biography

Black Fox, known in Cherokee as Enoleh or Inali, was an elder statesman and diplomat whose career spanned from the Cherokee-American wars through the early nineteenth century. Born around 1746, he was the brother-in-law of the legendary Chickamauga war chief Dragging Canoe, yet his own path led him toward diplomacy and accommodation rather than armed resistance.

During the Cherokee-American wars, Black Fox accompanied Dragging Canoe on migrations to the Lower Towns, the Chickamauga settlements that formed the center of Cherokee resistance. Despite this association with the resistance movement, Black Fox emerged as a diplomat. He served as the "Beloved Man" (headman) of Ustanali, an important Cherokee settlement in present-day northwestern Georgia that had become a new center of Cherokee political life after the destruction of the Overhill towns.

In June 1792, just months after the Treaty of Holston was signed, Black Fox participated in a grand council at Ustanali where he eulogized Dragging Canoe following the great war chief's death on March 1, 1792. His ability to honor the resistance leader while also working for peace demonstrated the nuanced positions Cherokee leaders were forced to navigate during this period.

Black Fox's diplomatic skills and elder status led to his selection as Principal Chief in 1801, succeeding Little Turkey. He served in this role until 1810, and briefly again from 1810 to 1811. As principal chief, he became the leading negotiator with the U.S. government, guiding the Cherokee Nation through an era of increasing American pressure for land cessions.

## Role in the Treaty of Holston

Black Fox signed the Treaty of Holston on July 2, 1791, his name appearing as Enoleh on the treaty document. His signature represented the peace faction's commitment to accommodation with the new federal government. As an elder statesman with connections to both the peace and war factions (through his relationship with Dragging Canoe), Black Fox's presence lent legitimacy to the treaty. His participation reflected his belief that Cherokee interests were best served through diplomacy rather than continued warfare.

## Legacy

Black Fox's tenure as Principal Chief proved controversial. In 1806, he relinquished nearly 7,000 square miles of Cherokee territory in Tennessee and Alabama under a treaty with the United States, receiving a lifetime annuity of $100 for his role. This land cession, connected to the controversies that led to Doublehead's assassination in 1807, resulted in Black Fox's temporary deposition in 1808. He was reinstated in 1809, suggesting that despite criticism, his leadership was still valued by a significant portion of the Cherokee Nation.

On April 18, 1810, Black Fox signed an act abolishing clan revenge, a significant legal reform that marked the Cherokee Nation's continued adaptation to changing circumstances. He died in 1811 and was buried in an ancient tomb on the boundary between Cherokee and Creek lands in Blount County, Alabama. His legacy reflects the impossible choices faced by Cherokee leaders: how to preserve their people and culture when accommodation meant ceding lands, and resistance meant devastation. Black Fox chose diplomacy, and though his land cessions remain controversial, his efforts to navigate Cherokee survival during an era of relentless American expansion demonstrated both the limits and possibilities of peaceful negotiation.
