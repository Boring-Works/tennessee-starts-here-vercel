# Standing Turkey (Kanetetoka)

Standing Turkey, known in Cherokee as Kanetetoka, was a Cherokee leader and diplomat who participated in critical treaty negotiations during one of the most turbulent periods in Cherokee history. His name, referring to the upright stance of the wild turkey, reflected the Cherokee practice of taking names from the natural world. Born around 1740, Standing Turkey came of age during the Cherokee Wars of the 1760s and witnessed the dramatic transformation of Cherokee-American relations over four decades.

## Early Life and Rise to Leadership

Standing Turkey was born into Cherokee society during a period when the Cherokee Nation still maintained considerable autonomy and territorial control over vast territories stretching from the Ohio River valley to northern Georgia and from the Blue Ridge Mountains to the Cumberland Plateau. The mid-eighteenth century represented a time when Cherokee diplomacy balanced relationships with British, French, and Spanish colonial powers, using European rivalries to maintain Cherokee independence and trading advantages.

As a young man in the 1760s, Standing Turkey would have witnessed the devastating Cherokee War against the British colonies, which resulted in significant Cherokee territorial losses and the destruction of multiple Cherokee towns. British military expeditions under officers like James Grant and William Byrd destroyed Cherokee settlements throughout the Overhill, Middle, and Lower towns, burning crops and food stores that Cherokee communities depended on for winter survival. The scorched-earth tactics employed by British forces killed civilians and destroyed the infrastructure of Cherokee daily life. These traumatic experiences shaped Standing Turkey's generation's understanding of both the terrible costs of warfare and the necessity of diplomatic engagement with European powers.

The Treaty of Long Island in 1761 ended the Cherokee War but required substantial Cherokee territorial concessions. Standing Turkey came of age in the aftermath of this conflict, learning the difficult lesson that military defeat led inevitably to land losses. This knowledge would inform his later diplomatic approach—seeking to avoid warfare that could only result in further Cherokee dispossession.

By the 1780s, Standing Turkey had established himself as a respected leader within the Cherokee community of Tuskegee, one of the important Cherokee settlements in the Overhill region. Tuskegee's location along important trading paths gave it economic and strategic significance. The Cherokee political system during this period was decentralized, with individual towns maintaining considerable autonomy while also participating in broader Cherokee councils. Town leaders like Standing Turkey held authority within their communities and contributed to Cherokee national decision-making through their participation in councils. Standing Turkey's influence extended beyond his own community, as evidenced by his selection as a representative for treaty negotiations with the United States government.

## Role in Cherokee-American Relations

The period following American independence proved catastrophic for the Cherokee Nation. American settlers, no longer restrained by British colonial policies, flooded into Cherokee territories. The State of North Carolina claimed sovereignty over Cherokee lands, and the newly formed State of Franklin attempted to establish itself on Cherokee territory without Cherokee consent. Standing Turkey witnessed the division within Cherokee leadership between those who, like Dragging Canoe, advocated armed resistance, and those who believed accommodation offered the best path to Cherokee survival.

Standing Turkey aligned himself with the peace faction, working alongside leaders like Hanging Maw, Old Tassel, and Black Fox who sought to protect Cherokee interests through negotiation rather than warfare. This position required both courage and political acumen, as peace advocates often faced criticism from Cherokee who viewed accommodation as betrayal. The devastating American military campaigns of the late 1770s and early 1780s, which destroyed Cherokee towns and food supplies, reinforced Standing Turkey's belief that diplomatic solutions, however imperfect, offered greater hope for Cherokee survival than continued warfare.

During the late 1780s, Standing Turkey participated in ongoing negotiations with American territorial officials. The murder of Old Tassel under a flag of truce in 1788 demonstrated the dangers Cherokee peace advocates faced, yet Standing Turkey continued his diplomatic efforts. He recognized that the newly formed United States government under the Constitution might prove more reliable than individual states or frontier settlers, though this hope would be tested repeatedly.

## Treaty of Holston (1791)

In July 1791, Standing Turkey traveled to White's Fort (present-day Knoxville) to participate in treaty negotiations with Governor William Blount. His signature appears on the Treaty of Holston as Kanetetoka, representing his community's acceptance of the agreement. The treaty aimed to establish clear boundaries between Cherokee lands and American settlements, with the United States promising to protect Cherokee territorial integrity in exchange for Cherokee acknowledgment of federal protection.

Standing Turkey's presence at the negotiations reflected the Cherokee practice of including representatives from multiple towns and factions. As a peace advocate, his signature lent legitimacy to the treaty among Cherokee who hoped that formal agreements with the federal government might succeed where negotiations with states and frontier settlers had failed. However, Standing Turkey and other Cherokee negotiators operated from a position of military disadvantage, knowing that rejection of the treaty terms would likely result in continued warfare and further territorial losses.

The treaty promised annual payments to the Cherokee Nation and federal intervention to remove American settlers who illegally occupied Cherokee lands. Standing Turkey likely viewed these provisions with cautious optimism, understanding that the treaty's success depended entirely on whether the United States government would honor its commitments. The alternative—renewed warfare against a growing American population—offered even grimmer prospects for Cherokee survival.

## Later Years and Legacy

Following the Treaty of Holston, Standing Turkey returned to his community to navigate the difficult aftermath of the agreement. The treaty's promised protections proved inadequate, as American settlers continued to encroach on Cherokee lands and federal officials proved unable or unwilling to enforce the boundary provisions. Cherokee frustration with treaty violations led to renewed hostilities in the early 1790s, as Chickamauga leaders like John Watts and Doublehead resumed military operations.

Standing Turkey maintained his commitment to the peace faction's strategy, working with other Cherokee leaders to document treaty violations and petition federal officials for enforcement of the agreed-upon boundaries. This work proved frustrating and often futile, as territorial governor William Blount, though personally sympathetic to Cherokee grievances, lacked the authority and resources to remove the thousands of American settlers who had illegally occupied Cherokee lands.

By the mid-1790s, exhaustion from nearly two decades of warfare led most Cherokee factions to accept an uneasy peace. The 1794 negotiations at Tellico Blockhouse effectively ended the Cherokee-American wars, though tensions remained high. Standing Turkey lived to see the turn of the century, dying around 1800 after witnessing both the diplomatic efforts of the 1790s and their limited success in protecting Cherokee territorial integrity.

## Historical Significance

Standing Turkey's career exemplifies the difficult choices faced by Cherokee leaders during the late eighteenth century. Unlike the dramatic military campaigns of resistance leaders like Dragging Canoe, or the later tragic fame of leaders associated with Cherokee removal, Standing Turkey's diplomatic work occurred in the less celebrated spaces of negotiation and accommodation. Yet his efforts, and those of other peace faction leaders, represented a principled attempt to protect Cherokee interests through available legal and diplomatic channels. The courage required for this path should not be underestimated—peace advocates risked being viewed as collaborators by some Cherokee while simultaneously facing violence from American settlers who made no distinction between peaceful and hostile Cherokee.

The Treaty of Holston, which Standing Turkey signed, ultimately failed to prevent continued American expansion into Cherokee territories. However, Standing Turkey and his contemporaries could not have known that no treaty, however well-intentioned, would prove sufficient to protect Cherokee sovereignty against the tide of American westward expansion. The federal government's inability or unwillingness to enforce treaty provisions against frontier settlers demonstrated that treaty promises meant little without genuine commitment to enforcement. Their diplomatic efforts nonetheless established precedents for Cherokee engagement with the federal government and demonstrated Cherokee political sophistication in attempting to use American legal structures to protect Cherokee rights.

Standing Turkey's legacy reminds us that Cherokee responses to American expansion were diverse and strategic. While history often celebrates military resistance, the diplomatic efforts of leaders like Standing Turkey represented an equally important form of Cherokee agency—the attempt to preserve Cherokee lives, lands, and culture through negotiation when warfare offered only destruction. The peace faction's work documented Cherokee territorial claims, established the principle of Cherokee sovereignty through formal treaty recognition, and created legal precedents that later Cherokee leaders would invoke in their ongoing struggles to protect Cherokee interests.

That these diplomatic efforts ultimately proved insufficient reflects not the inadequacy of Cherokee leadership but the relentless nature of American expansionism during this period. Standing Turkey and his contemporaries faced an impossible situation: negotiate from a position of military disadvantage or face continued warfare that promised only greater destruction. Their choice to pursue peace, maintain diplomatic engagement, and document Cherokee rights through formal treaties represented sophisticated political strategy under conditions of extreme duress. Standing Turkey's life reminds us that Cherokee history includes not just dramatic military resistance but also the persistent, patient, and ultimately tragic work of leaders who sought to protect their people through every available means, including diplomatic engagement with a government that proved unable or unwilling to honor its commitments.
