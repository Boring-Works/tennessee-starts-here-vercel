## Biography

John Watts, known in Cherokee as Kunoskeskie or Young Tassel, was one of the most formidable Cherokee military leaders of the late eighteenth century. Born around 1750, he was the son of a white trader named John Watts Sr. (who served as a government interpreter until 1770) and a sister of the influential Cherokee leaders Old Tassel, Doublehead, and Pumpkin Boy. Under Cherokee matrilineal customs, his uncles held greater significance than his father in determining his clan identity and political standing.

The murder of his uncle Old Tassel under a flag of truce in 1788 transformed Watts from a young leader into an active warrior committed to Cherokee resistance. When Dragging Canoe, the legendary Chickamauga war chief, died on March 1, 1792, Watts succeeded him as head of the Cherokee war council (skiagusta). He based his war party operations at Willstown, near present-day Fort Payne, Alabama, and quickly proved himself a capable military commander.

Contemporary observers described Watts as "an intelligent, clever, capable man who enjoyed eating, drinking, and jocular conversation." This affable exterior masked a skilled strategist. In September 1792, he led a major attack on Buchanan's Station near Nashville with approximately 280-300 Cherokee, Muscogee, and Shawnee warriors, one of the largest Native American forces assembled in the region during this period. Watts was seriously wounded in this battle. He also traveled to Pensacola to negotiate a treaty with the Spanish governor of West Florida for arms and supplies, demonstrating his understanding of the broader geopolitical landscape.

## Role in the Treaty of Holston

John Watts signed the Treaty of Holston in July 1791, though his commitment to its terms remained uncertain. His signature represented the Chickamauga faction's tentative acceptance of negotiations with the American government, but continued settler encroachment on Cherokee lands and violations of treaty provisions led Watts to resume military operations. His presence at the treaty negotiations reflected the Cherokee practice of including both peace and war faction leaders in diplomatic proceedings, even when the war faction's adherence to treaty terms was provisional at best.

## Legacy

Despite his reputation as a warrior, John Watts ultimately became an advocate for peace. In 1794, he negotiated the Tellico Blockhouse agreement with Governor Blount, which effectively ended the Cherokee-American wars. In December 1796, he addressed the federal government directly, emphasizing that "peace has been faithfully observed by his Nation" since the 1795 conference with Governor Blount. He requested formal boundary surveys with Cherokee representatives present, seeking to protect remaining Cherokee lands through legal means rather than warfare.

Watts continued as a prominent Cherokee leader until his death around 1808. Upon his death, his position passed to his uncle Doublehead. His career arc from warrior to peacemaker reflected the broader Cherokee realization that military resistance, while justified, could not ultimately succeed against the growing American nation. His legacy includes both his military prowess and his later commitment to diplomacy as the best means of protecting Cherokee interests.
