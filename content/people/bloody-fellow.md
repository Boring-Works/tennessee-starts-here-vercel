## Biography

Bloody Fellow, known in Cherokee as Nenetooyah, was one of the most significant Cherokee leaders of the late eighteenth century. A supporter of the legendary war chief Dragging Canoe, he rose to prominence as a leader of the Chickamauga faction, the Cherokee communities that most actively resisted American expansion into their territory. His fearsome war name reflected his reputation as a formidable warrior during the Cherokee-American wars.

Born around 1740, Bloody Fellow's career demonstrated the complex navigation required of Cherokee leaders during this tumultuous period. Though initially committed to armed resistance, he became one of the Cherokee Nation's most effective diplomatic spokesmen, frequently representing Cherokee interests in treaty negotiations. Following the death of Dragging Canoe on March 1, 1792, Bloody Fellow became part of a triumvirate of leaders among the Chickamauga, sharing power with John Watts and Doublehead.

In early 1792, just months after signing the Treaty of Holston, Bloody Fellow led a Cherokee delegation to Philadelphia to express objections to the treaty terms. During this visit, President George Washington conferred upon him the title of "General," perhaps the only Cherokee to receive this honor prior to the Civil War. At this Philadelphia meeting, he was given a new name: Iskagua, meaning "Clear Sky" or "Great Day," symbolizing the hope for peaceful relations. He signed the Additional Article to the Treaty of Holston on February 17, 1792, which increased the annual payment to the Cherokee from $1,000 to $1,500.

## Role in the Treaty of Holston

Bloody Fellow's presence at the Treaty of Holston negotiations was crucial. As a respected war leader and spokesman for the Chickamauga faction, his willingness to sign the treaty gave it legitimacy among Cherokee who might otherwise have rejected accommodation with the Americans. On the treaty document, his original Cherokee name Nenetooyah appears, as the name Iskagua was not conferred until his Philadelphia visit the following year. His signature represented a commitment from the war faction to give peace a chance, even as many Chickamauga leaders harbored deep skepticism about American promises.

## Legacy

After the turbulent 1790s, Bloody Fellow transitioned fully from resistance to accommodation. He served as principal chief from approximately 1798 to 1801, guiding the Cherokee Nation through continued challenges from American settlers. One of his last recorded activities was a meeting in September 1800 with Moravian missionaries who hoped to open a mission in the Cherokee Nation, reflecting his later years' focus on cultural preservation and adaptation. He died around 1800-1801, having witnessed the transformation of Cherokee-American relations from open warfare to an uneasy peace. His career embodied the difficult choices Cherokee leaders faced: when to fight, when to negotiate, and how to protect their people when neither option offered guarantees.
