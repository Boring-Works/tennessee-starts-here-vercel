# The Boots (Chuleoah)

The Boots, known in Cherokee as Chuleoah, was a Cherokee warrior and diplomatic representative whose unusual English name reflected the complex cultural exchanges between Cherokee society and European-American culture during the late eighteenth century. His name, likely referring to European-style footwear that distinguished him from others who wore traditional Cherokee footwear or moccasins, suggests either close contact with white traders or a distinctive personal style that became his identifier. Born around 1745, The Boots lived through the transformation of Cherokee-American relations from colonial-era trade partnerships to the post-Revolutionary struggles for Cherokee territorial survival.

## Early Life and Rise to Leadership

The Boots was born during the mid-eighteenth century, a period when the Cherokee Nation maintained substantial power and influence in the southeastern interior. The Cherokee of this era controlled vast territories and engaged in sophisticated diplomatic relationships with British, French, and Spanish colonial powers. As a young man, The Boots would have participated in the deerskin trade that connected Cherokee communities to colonial markets, an economic relationship that brought European manufactured goods, including boots and other clothing items, into Cherokee communities. His distinctive name suggests he was among those Cherokee who adopted certain European material goods while maintaining Cherokee cultural identity and political autonomy.

The name "The Boots" itself offers insight into eighteenth-century Cherokee-European cultural exchange. Traditional Cherokee footwear consisted of moccasins made from deer or elk hide, practical for forest travel and adapted to Cherokee environmental conditions. European-style boots—often made from harder leather with higher tops and sometimes decorative elements—represented imported trade goods that signified wealth, status, or close connections to European traders. The Boots' adoption of this footwear as his identifier suggests he was comfortable navigating between Cherokee and European cultural practices, a skill that would later prove valuable in diplomatic contexts.

During the Cherokee War of the 1760s, The Boots would have been in his late teens or early twenties, an age when Cherokee men typically participated in military campaigns and earned war honors. The devastating British attacks on Cherokee towns in 1760-1761, which destroyed crops and settlements throughout Cherokee territory, shaped his generation's understanding of European military power. British forces under Colonel James Grant destroyed fifteen Middle Cherokee towns in a single campaign, killing civilians and burning food supplies. These experiences informed his later approach to Cherokee-American relations, balancing the need to protect Cherokee sovereignty with recognition of the destructive consequences of warfare.

By the 1780s, The Boots had established himself as a respected figure within the Cherokee community of Coosawatee, located in present-day northwestern Georgia along the Coosawattee River. Coosawatee was part of the Lower Cherokee settlements, communities that had maintained continuity through the turmoil of the Cherokee War and subsequent conflicts. These Lower towns maintained their own local leadership while participating in broader Cherokee national councils, contributing to Cherokee political decision-making while also managing their own community affairs.

The Lower Cherokee towns faced particular pressures during the 1780s. Their geographic location placed them in proximity to Georgia settlements, and Georgia state authorities claimed jurisdiction over Cherokee lands with particular aggressiveness. The Boots and other Lower Cherokee leaders navigated constant tensions with Georgia settlers and officials, attempting to maintain Cherokee sovereignty and territorial integrity against state claims that conflicted with federal treaty provisions. The Boots' selection as a treaty representative reflected both his standing within his own community and his reputation among Cherokee leaders as someone capable of representing Cherokee interests in negotiations with American officials. His experience dealing with Georgia's encroachments gave him particular insight into the challenges Cherokee communities faced from aggressive state governments.

## Role in Cherokee-American Relations

The period following American independence brought unprecedented challenges to Cherokee communities. The Boots witnessed the breakdown of the relatively stable Cherokee-British relationship and its replacement with a far more volatile Cherokee-American dynamic. American settlers, encouraged by state governments and local speculators, flooded into Cherokee territories with little regard for existing agreements or Cherokee sovereignty. The State of North Carolina claimed authority over Cherokee lands, while the State of Franklin attempted to establish itself on Cherokee territory through force and negotiation.

The Boots aligned himself with Cherokee leaders who sought to preserve Cherokee lands through diplomatic engagement rather than renewed warfare. This position reflected a pragmatic assessment of Cherokee military capabilities in the face of American expansion. The devastating American campaigns of the late 1770s and early 1780s, which had targeted Cherokee towns and food supplies, demonstrated the costs of military resistance. While some Cherokee leaders, particularly Dragging Canoe and the Chickamauga faction, continued armed resistance, The Boots joined peace advocates like Hanging Maw and Black Fox in attempting to secure Cherokee interests through negotiation.

The Boots participated in the complex diplomatic maneuvering of the late 1780s, as Cherokee leaders attempted to play federal and state governments against each other while protecting Cherokee territorial integrity. The federal government under the Constitution offered theoretical protection for Cherokee sovereignty through its treaty-making powers, but lacked the practical ability to control frontier settlers or compel state compliance with federal Indian policy. The Boots understood these limitations but recognized that federal treaties offered better prospects than state agreements or continued warfare.

## Treaty of Holston (1791)

In July 1791, The Boots traveled to White's Fort to participate in treaty negotiations with Governor William Blount, the federal representative for the Territory South of the River Ohio. His signature appears prominently on the Treaty of Holston as Chuleoah, making him one of the first Cherokee signatories listed on the document. This prominent placement suggests his importance within the Cherokee delegation and possibly his role as a spokesman for the Lower Cherokee towns.

The Boots' presence at the negotiations reflected the Cherokee practice of including representatives from multiple geographic regions and political factions. As someone from Coosawatee, he brought the perspective of the Lower Cherokee communities, which faced different pressures than the Overhill towns to the north or the Chickamauga settlements to the west. His willingness to sign the treaty represented his belief that formal agreements with the federal government offered the best available protection for Cherokee lands and sovereignty, even though Cherokee negotiators operated from a position of military disadvantage.

The treaty established boundaries between Cherokee and American territories, promised annual payments to the Cherokee Nation, and committed the federal government to removing illegal settlers from Cherokee lands. The Boots likely understood that these promises depended entirely on federal willingness and ability to enforce them against frontier settlers and state governments. However, the alternative—continued warfare—offered even less hope for Cherokee survival. His signature represented a calculated decision to pursue peace while maintaining Cherokee claims to their remaining territories.

## Later Years and Legacy

Following the Treaty of Holston, The Boots returned to Coosawatee to navigate the difficult implementation of the treaty's provisions. Like other Cherokee leaders, he faced the frustrating reality that treaty promises proved inadequate to stop American encroachment. Illegal settlers continued to occupy Cherokee lands, and federal officials proved unable or unwilling to enforce the treaty's boundary provisions with sufficient vigor to satisfy Cherokee expectations.

The Boots witnessed the renewed hostilities of the early 1790s, as Chickamauga leaders resumed military operations in response to continued treaty violations. However, he maintained his commitment to the peace faction's strategy, working with other Cherokee leaders to document violations and petition federal officials for enforcement of treaty provisions. This diplomatic work, though often frustrating, represented an important form of Cherokee political resistance—the insistence that the United States honor its treaty obligations and respect Cherokee sovereignty.

By the mid-1790s, exhaustion from nearly two decades of intermittent warfare led most Cherokee factions to accept an uneasy peace. The 1794 Tellico Blockhouse negotiations effectively ended the Cherokee-American wars, though tensions remained high and American pressure for additional Cherokee land cessions continued. The Boots lived through these difficult years, dying around 1799 after witnessing both the hope represented by the Treaty of Holston and its ultimate failure to protect Cherokee territorial integrity.

## Historical Significance

The Boots exemplifies the Cherokee leaders whose diplomatic efforts, though ultimately unsuccessful in preventing Cherokee dispossession, represented sophisticated attempts to protect Cherokee interests through available legal and political channels. His distinctive English name reminds us of the cultural complexity of this period—Cherokee leaders who adopted certain European material goods while fiercely defending Cherokee political sovereignty and territorial rights.

The prominence of The Boots' signature on the Treaty of Holston reflects his importance within the Cherokee diplomatic delegation, yet relatively little detailed information about his life survives in American archives. This absence reflects a broader pattern in which American records documented Cherokee leaders primarily when they interacted with American officials, leaving much of Cherokee political and social life invisible in the historical record. The Boots' career occurred largely in those undocumented spaces—in Cherokee council houses, in community discussions about how to respond to American expansion, in the daily work of Cherokee leadership that American officials neither witnessed nor recorded.

The Boots' legacy, shared with other peace faction leaders of his generation, includes both the principled attempt to use diplomacy to protect Cherokee interests and the tragic demonstration that no treaty, however well-intentioned, could withstand the pressure of American westward expansion without genuine federal commitment to enforcement. His efforts established precedents for Cherokee diplomatic engagement with the federal government and demonstrated Cherokee political sophistication in attempting to use American legal structures to protect Cherokee sovereignty. That these efforts ultimately proved insufficient reflects not any failure of Cherokee leadership but the overwhelming force of American expansionism during this formative period of United States history.
