## Biography

Hanging Maw (Uskwa'li-gu'ta) was the leading chief of the Overhill Cherokee from 1788 to 1794, following the death of his predecessor Old Tassel. His Cherokee name, meaning "his stomach hangs down," was rendered in treaty documents as Squollecuttah or Scolacutta. He claimed leadership by right of being the chief headman of the Overhill Towns, though the general Cherokee council had chosen Little Turkey as their First Beloved Man after the Cherokee capital moved to Ustanali.

Born around 1730, Hanging Maw's political career spanned one of the most turbulent periods in Cherokee history. Following the failure of peace talks with Virginians in 1777, he initially supported Dragging Canoe and the Chickamauga faction in their resistance against American expansion. However, after devastating American attacks on Cherokee towns in 1780, he joined Old Tassel in pursuing a path of peace and accommodation. In 1786, the two leaders agreed to the "Treaty of Coyatee" with the State of Franklin, attempting to establish peaceful boundaries with the encroaching settlers.

Hanging Maw was instrumental in bringing reluctant Cherokee factions to the negotiating table. His stature among the Overhill towns gave him credibility, and his willingness to work with American officials made him a key partner for territorial governor William Blount. He signed both the 1785 Treaty of Hopewell at Hopewell plantation in South Carolina and the 1791 Treaty of Holston, demonstrating his consistent commitment to diplomatic solutions over warfare.

## Role in the Treaty of Holston

At the Treaty of Holston negotiations in July 1791, Hanging Maw served as one of the principal Cherokee representatives. His signature appears among the first on the treaty document, reflecting his status as the acknowledged leader of the Overhill Cherokee. Governor Blount recognized Hanging Maw as the leading headman of the Cherokee Nation, and the two men developed a working relationship built on mutual respect. Hanging Maw's influence helped bring together Cherokee leaders from both the peace and war factions, though some Chickamauga leaders remained skeptical of American intentions.

## Legacy

Hanging Maw's commitment to peace came at a personal cost. In 1793, land disputes and frontier tensions led Knoxville settlers to attack a Cherokee negotiating party led by Hanging Maw himself. The chief was seriously wounded in the attack, as was his wife, and several other Cherokee were killed. Despite this betrayal, Hanging Maw continued to advocate for peace, recognizing that open warfare would bring even greater devastation to his people. He died around 1796, having spent his final years working alongside other peace-seeking chiefs to broker agreements that would protect Cherokee interests on the Tennessee frontier. His efforts, though ultimately unsuccessful in preventing Cherokee dispossession, represented a principled attempt to find accommodation in an era of relentless American expansion.
