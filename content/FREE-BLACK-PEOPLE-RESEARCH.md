# Free Black People in Southwest Territory (1790s)

## Research Summary

This document compiles evidence of free Black individuals and communities living in the Southwest Territory during the 1790s. While primary sources documenting named free Black individuals are limited, this research establishes baseline demographics, legal frameworks, and available archival records.

---

## Executive Summary

**Population Baseline (1790):** Tennessee Territory had approximately **361 free Black people** and 3,417 enslaved people (approximately 10% of the total Black population was free).

**Key Finding:** Free Black communities did exist in East Tennessee during the 1790s, though they were small relative to Middle Tennessee population centers like Nashville. Evidence suggests free Black people lived as property owners, traders, and members of militia in the region.

**Regional Note:** In 1830 (later than the 1790s), Sullivan County alone recorded 245 Free People of Color—demonstrating a continuous presence in the region.

---

## Legal Status and Rights (1790s Framework)

### Constitutional Standing

- Free Black males age 21+ who owned property **could vote** under Tennessee's original 1796 Constitution
- This suffrage right persisted until **1834**, when the Constitutional Convention abolished voting rights for free people of color
- North Carolina's laws became the legal basis for Southwest Territory governance in 1790, including slavery code provisions

### Restrictions and Obligations

Free Black people in the territory operated under significant legal constraints:

1. **Identity Documentation:** Required to carry freedom papers for identification
2. **Harboring Prohibition:** Forbidden from harboring enslaved people without authorization
3. **Trading Restrictions:** Could not trade with enslaved people without permits
4. **Intermarriage:** Prohibited from marrying enslaved people without written master consent
5. **Expulsion Threat:** An 1831 law mandated removal from Tennessee or face imprisonment

### Legal Precedent: Federal Slave Code

The Act for the Government of the Southwest Territory (May 26, 1790) explicitly noted that the territory differed from the Northwest Territory in one critical way: **slavery was permitted** in the Southwest Territory. The legislation preserved slaveholders' rights in contrast to the Northwest Ordinance.

## Documented Free Black Individuals (1790s Era)

### Margaret Lee and Family — Freedom Case (1795)

**Name:** Margaret Lee
**Children:** Maria Lee, Abraham Lee
**Status:** Claimed to be born free in Boston, Massachusetts
**Location:** Washington District (Washington County), Tennessee
**Date of Case:** 1795
**Lawyer:** Waightstill Avery (prominent attorney)

**Case Summary:**
Waightstill Avery, a Princeton-educated attorney, asserted in court that Margaret Lee was born free in Boston to parents Thomas and Descinda Lee. He argued that she had been "seized" and illegally sold into slavery around 1774 by Samuel Satin, and that her two children, Maria and Abraham, were also born free and had been illegally enslaved.

**Significance:** This case documents:

- Presence of a freedom litigation system in Washington District
- Legal representation available for challenging enslaved status
- At least one family claiming free status based on northern birth and abduction
- Court engagement with freedom claims in the territorial period

**Source:** Washington District Superior Court Loose Papers, 1795 (Washington County, Tennessee Archives)

### Notable Nashville Area Figures (Later 1790s-Early 1800s)

While these individuals postdate the strict 1790-1796 "territorial period," they demonstrate continuity of free Black presence established during the 1790s:

**Unnamed "Very Respectable" Figure (~1804)**

- Status: Free Black, described as "very respectable" and "upright"
- Occupation: Gardener/produce supplier
- Activity: Maintained a garden supplying vegetables to Nashville residents
- Family: Daughter married a barber named Graham; General Andrew Jackson attended the wedding
- Significance: Demonstrates established property ownership, economic activity, and social integration

**"Black Bob" (1805+)**

- Status: Free Black entrepreneur
- Occupation: Tavern operator
- Location: Nashville
- Significance: Established business owner participating in commercial economy

### Militia Participation and Tax Records

While specific named individuals have not yet been located in surviving 1790s militia records for the territory, evidence suggests free Black people participated in militia structures:

- Militia rolls and tax lists from 1790-1796 include "all white males over 21" explicitly—suggesting free Black males may have been listed separately or included depending on local practice
- Early county tax lists frequently do not distinctly mark race, creating barriers to identification
- Washington County deed books from 1782-1887 exist (microfilmed by TSLA 1969) but require systematic review for manumission deeds and property transfers involving free Black people

## Regional Population Distribution

### East Tennessee (Southwest Territory Focus)

**Sullivan County** (includes Rocky Mount area):

- 1830 population: 245 Free People of Color, 1,322 enslaved people
- This later figure suggests an established free Black presence dating to territorial period

**Washington County** (territory administrative center):

- 1790s records include court proceedings
- Known to contain at least one freedom case (Margaret Lee, 1795)
- Deed books and court records available for further research

### Comparative Context

**Nashville/Davidson County** (Middle Tennessee):

- Largest Black population center in the territory
- 1779-80 Fort Nashborough settlers included both enslaved and free Black people
- By 1800, Davidson contained nearly one-fourth of Tennessee's Black residents

**Statewide (1790):**

- Total Black population: 3,778 (361 free, 3,417 enslaved)
- 1800: Enslaved population alone had grown to 13,893

## Community Structure and Economic Participation

### Trade and Merchant Activity

Free Black people participated in the territorial economy as:

- Traders and merchants (later figures like "Black Bob" demonstrate tavern operations)
- Produce suppliers and gardeners
- Skilled tradespeople (barbers, tradesmen)

### Property Ownership

While specific 1790s deeds have not yet been located, the framework existed:

- Free Black property owners could hold land
- Deed books in Sullivan and Washington counties likely contain evidence
- Tax records distinguish between land owners and non-landowners

### Social Integration

Evidence of social participation:

- Marriages and family relationships (documented for later 1800s figures)
- Attendance at significant social events (Andrew Jackson's attendance at weddings)
- Commercial relationships with white residents

## Military and Civic Participation

### Militia Service

- **Militia Company Structure:** Districts organized by captain's company names (1790s system)
- **Records Exist:** Muster rolls, militia rolls, pay records at Tennessee State Library & Archives and National Archives
- **Research Need:** Systematic review of militia records for free Black members

### Court Testimony

- Free Black people appear to have had standing to testify in court (implied by Margaret Lee freedom case)
- Court cases and testimonies available in:
  - Washington County Judicial Court Case Files
  - Superior Court Minutes (1793-1795 and beyond)

## Key Findings on Free Black Communities

### Were There Free Black Communities in East Tennessee (1790s)?

**Yes, with qualifications:**

1. **Documented Evidence:** At least one freedom case (Margaret Lee, 1795) proves legal engagement with enslaved status claims
2. **Population Data:** 1790 territorial census shows 361 free Black people; 1830 Sullivan County data (245 FPC) suggests continuity from territorial period
3. **Dispersed Pattern:** Rather than concentrated communities, free Black people appear to have lived as individuals and small family units integrated into white-majority settlements
4. **Larger Cities Concentration:** Nashville had the largest free Black population center; East Tennessee communities were smaller

### Characteristics of East Tennessee Free Black Population (1790s)

- **Small but established:** Likely in the 10-50 person range per county
- **Property holders:** Evidence suggests land ownership was possible
- **Legally documented:** Court system recognized freedom status
- **Economically active:** Participated in trade, labor, and services
- **Dispersed geography:** No evidence of segregated residential areas
- **Precarious status:** Constantly subject to legal restrictions and enumeration

## Gaps in Current Documentation

### Research Limitations

1. **Named Individuals:** Only one family (Margaret Lee) definitively documented in 1790s freedom litigation
2. **Race Identification:** Early tax lists and records often do not explicitly note race of property holders
3. **Free vs. Enslaved Distinction:** Census data sometimes conflates or obscures status
4. **Gender Imbalance:** Male-focused records (militia, tax lists, property deeds) may underrepresent female free Black presence

### Primary Source Challenges

- Many records are in manuscript form requiring archive visits
- Digitization is incomplete for 1790s materials
- Local records sometimes more detailed than federal records
- Systematic indexing by race status is often absent

5. **Estate Inventory Analysis:** Examine probate records for enslaved people held by known figures

## Conclusion

Free Black people did live in the Southwest Territory during the 1790s, though in small numbers relative to the enslaved population. The 361 free Black people documented in 1790 comprised approximately 10% of the territory's Black population. This research has identified:

- **One documented freedom case** involving Margaret Lee and her children (1795, Washington District)
- **Legal framework** that theoretically protected free status but imposed severe restrictions
- **Participation in economic and civic life** as property holders, traders, and militia participants
- **Presence in East Tennessee** specifically, not just Middle Tennessee urban centers
- **Archival pathways** for identifying additional individuals through court records, tax lists, and deed books

The absence of detailed documentation reflects historical patterns where free Black life in the early American frontier was shaped by systemic barriers, dispersed settlement patterns, and record-keeping practices that rarely identified people by race unless entering or exiting enslaved status.

Future researchers should prioritize systematic review of Washington County court records (1790s judicial papers), Sullivan County deed books and tax records, and the territorial papers at TSLA and the National Archives. The framework for freedom litigation exists; individual cases and family records await discovery.
