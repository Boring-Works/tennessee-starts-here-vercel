# John Sevier - Rocky Mount Research Report

**Research Date:** January 30, 2026
**Prepared for:** Rocky Mount State Historic Site

## Executive Summary

This report documents verified connections between John Sevier and Rocky Mount during the Revolutionary War period (1780) and the Southwest Territory capital years (1790-1792). Research focused on primary sources, archival collections, and documented correspondence rather than assumptions.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Verified Rocky Mount Connections

**September 1780 - Revolutionary War Muster**

- John Sevier called for volunteers at Rocky Mount in September 1780 in response to British officer Patrick Ferguson's threats
- Members of the Cobb family (William Sr., William Jr., Pharaoh, Jerry, and Arthur) answered Sevier's call
- This muster was part of the larger mobilization leading to the Battle of Kings Mountain

**1790-1792 - Territorial Capital Period**

- Rocky Mount (William Cobb's home) served as the first capital of the Southwest Territory from October 1790 to early 1792
- John Sevier was among notable pioneers who "enjoyed Cobb's hospitality" at Rocky Mount during this period
- Other notable guests included Daniel Boone, Daniel Smith, William Campbell, and Richard Henderson

### 2. Sevier's Official Roles During Rocky Mount Period

**1790 Appointment**

- When the Southwest Territory was created in 1790, Governor William Blount appointed Sevier as one of two brigadier generals of the territorial militia
- Sevier commanded the militia of Washington District (James Robertson commanded Mero District)

**1794 Legislative Council**

- In February 1794, President George Washington appointed Sevier to the Southwest Territory council (upper legislative chamber)
- Sevier served alongside Winchester, Donelson, Rutherford, and Taylor

## Documented Correspondence Between Sevier and Blount

### Letter: Sevier to Blount, February 14, 1797

**Source:** Tennessee State Library and Archives (Governor Sevier, box 1, folder 3, document tl033)
**Digitized:** Digital Library of Georgia (https://dlg.usg.edu/record/dlg_zlna_gs033)

**Location:** Knoxville
**Date:** February 14, 1797

**Salutation:** "Sir"

**Body:**
Governor Sevier acknowledges receipt of Blount's letter from the 3rd of the previous month. He informs the Senator that Cherokee Indians have killed individuals in Powell's Valley and on the Cumberland (Harpeth), with one person wounded. Sevier believes these attacks represent retaliation for the murders of two Cherokee by Ned Mitchell and John Levistone near the Kentucky River headwaters.

The Governor expresses hope to "shortly" resolve the situation and restore peaceful relations. He notes that reports regarding potential French conflict and direct taxation are generating significant public discontent and indignation toward the federal government's policies.

Sevier mentions not writing to other Congressional members, anticipating their departure from Philadelphia before the letter's arrival.

**Closing:** "I have the honor to be & c"

**Signature:** "[Signed] J. Sevier"

**Note:** Letter references an enclosure—a copy of correspondence sent to Silas Dinsmore, the Indian agent.

### Letter: Sevier to Jefferson, January 17, 1791

**Source:** Founders Online, National Archives (https://founders.archives.gov/documents/Jefferson/01-18-02-0177)

**Location:** Congress Chamber
**Date:** January 17, 1791

**Salutation:** "Sir"

**Body:**
Sevier informs Jefferson that Mr. Pery, appointed as a judge for the territory south of Ohio, has declined the position. He recommends Waightstil Avery Esquire to fill the vacancy, praising Avery's "competent Abilities integrity, And Uprightness, together with his enterprise And General Acquaintance with the People of the Territory." Sevier suggests this appointment would please the territorial residents.

**Closing:** "I have the honor to be with due respect yours respectively,"

**Signature:** John Sevier

**Editorial Note:** Joseph Anderson ultimately filled this judicial vacancy.

## Sevier's Role: 1780 vs. 1790s

### 1780 - Revolutionary War Period

**Battle of Kings Mountain Context**

- On September 25, 1780, approximately 1,100 volunteer soldiers mustered at Pharaoh Cobb's plantation near Sycamore Shoals
- John Sevier and Isaac Shelby led these "Overmountain Men" - frontiersmen who had settled west of the Appalachian ridgeline
- British officer Patrick Ferguson had threatened: "If you do not desist your opposition to the British Arms, I shall march this army over the mountains, hang your leaders, and lay waste your country with fire and sword"
- Sevier and Shelby agreed to take the offensive

**The Cobb Connection**

- William Cobb Sr. had bought land at Sycamore Shoals for his son Pharaoh (who married William's niece, Barsheba Whitehead)
- In 1780, the Kings Mountain soldiers met at William Cobb's home in "The Fork," where they were fed and mounted on horses furnished by William Cobb
- They then marched to Pharaoh Cobb's home for provisions before departing from Sycamore Shoals for Kings Mountain
- At least five Cobb men fought in the battle: William Sr., Pharaoh, William Jr., Arthur, and Jerry

**Battle Results**

- October 7, 1780: Battle of Kings Mountain resulted in decisive Patriot victory
- The victory forced British General Cornwallis to abandon his invasion of North Carolina
- Considered a turning point in the Revolutionary War's southern campaign

### 1784-1788 - State of Franklin Period

**Separate State Movement**

- April 1784: North Carolina voted to cede western lands to Congress, then rescinded the offer
- August 23, 1784: Delegates convened in Jonesborough to establish separate state
- March 1785: John Sevier elected governor of proposed "State of Franklin" (named for Benjamin Franklin)
- Spring 1788: Sevier's term expired; the state collapsed
- Sevier was arrested and charged with treason but never tried
- Within a year, Sevier took oath of allegiance to North Carolina and was elected to state Senate

### 1790-1792 - Southwest Territory Period

**Official Appointments**

- 1790: William Blount appointed territorial governor; Sevier appointed brigadier general of militia (Washington District)
- 1791: Sevier served from Washington District while Blount was based at Rocky Mount
- 1794: President Washington appointed Sevier to territorial legislative council

**Key Events During Rocky Mount Capital Years**

_1791 Census_

- Early 1791: Territorial census initiated while Blount was based at Rocky Mount
- Midsummer 1791: Census tallied 35,691 residents (including 3,417 enslaved people and 361 free people of color)
- Many census documents were written from Rocky Mount

_Treaty of Holston - July 2, 1791_

- While early correspondence and logistical planning began at Rocky Mount, actual negotiations occurred at White's Fort (present-day Knoxville)
- Governor William Blount negotiated with approximately 1,200 Cherokee attendees
- Treaty signed by 41 Cherokee chiefs, with witnesses from Georgia, North Carolina, Virginia, and Kentucky Territory
- John Sevier was NOT a signatory to the Treaty of Holston
- However, Blount and Sevier later sent agents to harass commissioners during boundary surveys in 1798

### 1796 - Tennessee Statehood

**Constitutional Convention**

- Following 1795 census and referendum favoring statehood, Blount called for election of convention delegates
- January 17, 1796: 55 delegates (five from each county) assembled in Knoxville
- William Blount elected to chair the convention (he had signed U.S. Constitution in Philadelphia 1787)
- John Sevier "played a serious role supporting Blount in the backrooms of the constitutional convention"
- In three weeks, delegates fashioned a state constitution

**First State Governor**

- March 29, 1796: New general assembly convened
- March 30, 1796: John Sevier inaugurated as Tennessee's first governor
- June 1, 1796: Tennessee admitted to the United States
- Sevier served as governor 1796-1801 and again 1803-1809

## Historical Context for Interpretation

### Rocky Mount's Strategic Importance

**Why Sevier Would Have Been at Rocky Mount**

1. **Military Command Center**
   - As brigadier general of Washington District militia, Sevier would have reported to Governor Blount
   - Rocky Mount was Blount's headquarters and thus the military command center
   - Militia matters, Indian affairs, and defense planning all centered there

2. **Legislative Duties**
   - After 1794 appointment to territorial council, Sevier had official business at the capital
   - Council meetings likely convened at Rocky Mount until move to Knoxville

3. **Social Networks**
   - The frontier elite (Sevier, Blount, Robertson, Cobb) formed interconnected networks
   - Political alliances, land speculation, and military coordination required face-to-face meetings
   - Rocky Mount served as natural gathering place for territorial leadership

4. **Geographic Proximity**
   - Sevier's home "Marble Springs" was in Knox County
   - Rocky Mount in Washington County was on main routes between settlements
   - Travel between East Tennessee communities involved stopping at prominent homes

### The 1780 Connection's Significance

**Why September 1780 Muster Matters**

1. **Established Pattern**
   - Sevier's use of Rocky Mount for military muster in 1780 established it as recognized gathering point
   - When it became territorial capital in 1790, this prior military significance added legitimacy

2. **Cobb Family Loyalty**
   - The Cobbs' support in 1780 (providing food, horses, men) created bonds of obligation
   - Blount's choice of Rocky Mount as capital may have recognized this Revolutionary War service
   - Sevier's 1790s association with Rocky Mount honored these earlier connections

3. **Military-to-Political Continuity**
   - Same men who fought together in 1780 governed together in 1790s
   - Rocky Mount symbolized this continuity from Revolutionary War to territorial government
   - Physical space connected military struggle for independence to political struggle for statehood

## Conclusions

### What We Know with Certainty

1. **John Sevier called for volunteers at Rocky Mount in September 1780**, and the Cobb family responded, participating in the Kings Mountain campaign

2. **Sevier enjoyed William Cobb's hospitality at Rocky Mount** during the 1790-1792 period when it served as the Southwest Territory's first capital

3. **Sevier held official positions** (brigadier general 1790, legislative council 1794) that would have required him to conduct business with Governor Blount at Rocky Mount

4. **Documented correspondence exists** between Sevier and Blount, though most available letters date from 1794 onward

5. **Rocky Mount served as the administrative center** for territorial government, including census operations, treaty planning, and militia coordination

### What Requires Further Research

1. **Specific dates and purposes of Sevier's visits to Rocky Mount** during 1790-1792

2. **Nature and frequency of Sevier-Blount correspondence** from the Rocky Mount period

3. **Sevier's role in 1791 census operations** and whether he signed documents at Rocky Mount

4. **Details of legislative council meetings** after 1794 and whether any occurred at Rocky Mount

5. **Personal diary entries** that might describe Rocky Mount visits or interactions with the Cobb family

### Interpretive Recommendations

For Rocky Mount State Historic Site programming and interpretation:

**Strong Claims (Evidence-Based)**

- "In September 1780, John Sevier called for volunteers at Rocky Mount to join the campaign against British forces, and members of the Cobb family answered his call"
- "As Tennessee's territorial capital from 1790-1792, Rocky Mount hosted Governor William Blount and welcomed prominent frontier leaders including John Sevier"
- "John Sevier, as brigadier general of the Washington District militia, would have conducted official business with Governor Blount at Rocky Mount"

**Qualified Claims (Probable but Requiring Archival Verification)**

- "John Sevier likely visited Rocky Mount on multiple occasions during the capital period, given his official roles and the political networks of the time"
- "As a member of the territorial council after 1794, Sevier may have attended meetings at Rocky Mount before the capital's relocation to Knoxville"

**Avoid (Insufficient Evidence)**

- Specific dates of visits (without archival confirmation)
- Detailed conversations or interactions (unless documented in letters/diaries)
- Claims about Sevier's personal relationship with the Cobb family beyond "hospitality"
