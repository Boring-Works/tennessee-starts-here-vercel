## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: THE CURRENT PROBLEM

I've reviewed the Evidence Room Cherokee representation strategy and supporting documentation. Your team has done impressive research identifying Cherokee-authored documents and gaps in representation. You clearly mean well.

**But you're about to make a critical mistake.**

Your current approach is **extractive, not collaborative**. You're treating Cherokee Nation as a **content source** rather than a **sovereign partner**. The difference matters—a lot.

### The Red Flags I See

1. **"We need Cherokee voices for our site"** — This frames Cherokee Nation as fulfilling YOUR institutional need
2. **Budget line items for "Cherokee consultation fees"** — Treating sovereign tribal consultation as a vendor service
3. **Timeline: "6-12 months to Cherokee Nation partnership"** — Implies partnership is something YOU achieve on YOUR schedule
4. **"Commission Cherokee scholar essay"** — Assumes Cherokee scholars are available for hire to validate your project
5. **No existing relationship** — Rocky Mount has zero documented Cherokee Nation partnership history

### What This Looks Like to Cherokee Nation

> "A state historic site in Tennessee just discovered they've been telling our ancestors' story without Cherokee voices for years. Now they want us to fix their problem, on their timeline, for their budget, to meet their institutional goals. They'll pay consultation fees. They're sincere! They've done research!"

**This is extractive. Even with good intentions.**

## PART 2: THE RIGHT WAY TO APPROACH CHEROKEE NATION

### Phase 0: BEFORE First Contact (Do This First)

**Timeline:** 3-6 months of preparation
**Budget:** $0 (internal work)

#### Step 1: Institutional Audit

**Questions Rocky Mount leadership must answer:**

1. **Artifact Holdings**
   - Does Rocky Mount hold ANY Cherokee cultural items in collections?
   - If yes, has NAGPRA inventory been completed?
   - Are there repatriation obligations?

2. **Current Presentation**
   - How is Treaty of Holston currently presented to visitors?
   - Does interpretation include Cherokee perspectives?
   - Are there problematic framings that need immediate correction?

3. **Decision-Making Authority**
   - Is Rocky Mount willing to give Cherokee Nation veto power over Cherokee content?
   - Will Cherokee Nation co-author interpretive materials, or just review them?
   - What happens if Cherokee Nation says "no" to your proposed content?

4. **Long-Term Commitment**
   - Is this a one-time project or ongoing relationship?
   - Will Rocky Mount commit to annual Cherokee Nation consultation?
   - What resources will sustain partnership after initial project?

**Do NOT contact Cherokee Nation until you have clear answers.**

#### Step 2: Learn Cherokee Nation's Priorities

**Research Cherokee Nation's current work:**

Visit cherokee.org and review:

- Cultural Resources current projects
- Education Department priorities
- Historic Preservation Office active efforts
- Recent press releases and initiatives

**Read Cherokee Nation's public positions on:**

- Treaty of Holston (if they've published anything)
- Historic sites representation
- Educational partnerships

**Identify alignment:**

- Where do Cherokee Nation priorities intersect with Rocky Mount capabilities?
- What can Rocky Mount offer that Cherokee Nation actually needs?

**Do this BEFORE drafting partnership proposal.**

#### Step 3: Identify Your Institutional Constraints

**Be honest about what Rocky Mount CAN'T do:**

- Can't give Cherokee Nation co-governance authority? (State site limitations)
- Can't provide revenue sharing? (Budget constraints)
- Can't guarantee long-term funding? (Grant-dependent)
- Can't change existing exhibits immediately? (Installation costs)

**Cherokee Nation will ask about these. Have answers ready.**

**Better to acknowledge limitations upfront than promise what you can't deliver.**

#### Step 4: Draft Partnership Vision (Not Project Proposal)

**Instead of:**

> "We want Cherokee Nation to help us add Cherokee voices to our Evidence Room"

**Frame as:**

> "Rocky Mount seeks to build a long-term educational partnership with Cherokee Nation that:
>
> - Centers Cherokee sovereignty and perspectives in Treaty of Holston interpretation
> - Provides Cherokee Nation a public platform to address historical treaty violations
> - Creates educational opportunities for Cherokee youth and citizens
> - Establishes ongoing tribal consultation for all Cherokee-related content
> - Explores how Rocky Mount can serve Cherokee Nation's cultural preservation goals"

**Notice the difference:**

- First framing: You need their help
- Second framing: Mutual benefit, long-term relationship, Cherokee priorities centered

>

> - Long-term institutional partnership
> - [Other specific offers]"

**Section 5: No Timeline Pressure**

> "We recognize Cherokee Nation operates on its own timeline. We are prepared to wait for a response and adjust our project scope based on Cherokee Nation's availability and interest."

## PART 3: RED FLAGS — APPROACHES THAT DAMAGE RELATIONSHIPS

### Red Flag 1: "We'll Pay Consultation Fees"

**Problem:** Treating Cherokee Nation as vendor, not sovereign partner

**Why it's offensive:**

- Implies Cherokee Nation's role is to sell you their expertise
- Reduces sovereign tribal consultation to billable hours
- Suggests relationship is transactional, not collaborative

**Better Approach:**

- "We recognize tribal consultation requires Cherokee Nation resources. How can we fairly compensate Cherokee Nation for time, expertise, and partnership?"

### Red Flag 2: "Here's Our Timeline"

**Problem:** Imposing Rocky Mount's schedule on Cherokee Nation

**Why it's offensive:**

- Cherokee Nation operates on Cherokee time
- Implies Rocky Mount's project deadlines matter more than Cherokee Nation's priorities
- Pressures Cherokee Nation to fit YOUR schedule

**Better Approach:**

- "We're flexible on timeline. What works for Cherokee Nation?"

### Red Flag 3: "We Need Cherokee Content for Our Site"

**Problem:** Frames Cherokee Nation as fulfilling YOUR need

**Why it's offensive:**

- Centers Rocky Mount's institutional goals
- Implies Cherokee Nation exists to validate your project
- Suggests you're entitled to Cherokee participation

**Better Approach:**

- "We recognize Cherokee perspectives are missing. How can we create space for Cherokee voices in ways that serve Cherokee Nation's goals?"

### Red Flag 4: "We've Done Research on Cherokee Signatories"

**Problem:** Presenting research TO Cherokee Nation about THEIR ancestors

**Why it's offensive:**

- Implies you know their history better than they do
- Suggests Cherokee Nation should be impressed by your work
- Positions you as expert, them as consultants

**Better Approach:**

- "We've attempted preliminary research but recognize it's incomplete without Cherokee Nation guidance. What should we know that we don't?"

### Red Flag 5: "We Want to Honor Cherokee Heritage"

**Problem:** Vague, performative language

**Why it's offensive:**

- "Heritage" is passive nostalgia
- Doesn't acknowledge ongoing Cherokee sovereignty
- Sounds like museum exhibit, not living nation

**Better Approach:**

- "We want to accurately represent Cherokee perspectives on treaty negotiations and acknowledge ongoing treaty violations."

### Red Flag 6: "Can You Share Oral Traditions?"

**Problem:** Requesting culturally sensitive knowledge without relationship

**Why it's offensive:**

- Oral traditions are Cherokee intellectual property
- Some traditions are not for public sharing
- Assumes you're entitled to cultural knowledge

**Better Approach:**

- "If there are oral traditions Cherokee Nation is willing to share publicly, we would be honored to include them. We understand if there are traditions that remain private."

### Red Flag 7: "We're on a Budget"

**Problem:** Asking Cherokee Nation to subsidize your project

**Why it's offensive:**

- Cherokee Nation isn't responsible for Rocky Mount's funding gaps
- Implies you expect free labor from tribal citizens
- Suggests partnership is charity, not mutual benefit

**Better Approach:**

- "We have [X budget] for tribal consultation. Is this sufficient, or should we seek additional funding before proceeding?"
