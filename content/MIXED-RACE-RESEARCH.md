# Mixed-Race Individuals in 1790s Southwest Territory

## Research on Cherokee-European and Other Mixed-Heritage Bridging Figures

**Research Date:** January 30, 2026
**Project:** Tennessee Starts Here Evidence Room
**Focus:** Documentary evidence of mixed-race individuals as cultural intermediaries during Treaty of Holston period (1791-1792)

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mixed-race individuals occupied critical roles in 1790s Southwest Territory as cultural and linguistic bridges between Cherokee Nation and European-American settlers and officials. The available documentation reveals at least five individuals with mixed or partially European heritage who served as treaty signatories, interpreters, and diplomatic representatives during the Treaty of Holston (1791) and related negotiations. These individuals exemplify how intermarriage between European traders and Cherokee women created a class of cultural mediators whose bilingual and bicultural capabilities made them invaluable to both Cherokee leadership and American officials.

Under Cherokee matrilineal law, children born to Cherokee women—regardless of their father's ethnicity—held full clan membership and Cherokee citizenship. This legal framework incorporated mixed-race individuals fully into Cherokee society while their European heritage and often-English names signaled their connections to trader communities, making them particularly valuable as intermediaries.

## PART 1: DOCUMENTED MIXED-RACE SIGNATORIES

### 1. John Watts (Kunoskeskie/Young Tassel) - Mixed Cherokee-European

**Status:** Treaty of Holston signatory, July 2, 1791

**Heritage Documentation:**

- Father: John Watts Sr. (white trader)
- Mother: Sister of Old Tassel, Doublehead, and Pumpkin Boy
- Born: circa 1750
- Died: circa 1808
- Town: Willstown (Chickamauga)

**Biographical Significance:**
John Watts represents a first-generation product of Cherokee-European intermarriage. His father, John Watts Sr., was a government interpreter until 1770, demonstrating that traders often formalized their cultural intermediary role through official government appointment. Despite his European father, Watts was fully incorporated into Cherokee society through his mother's clan membership. His uncle Doublehead was a war chief, and through his maternal line, Watts inherited claims to Cherokee political leadership.

**Role as Cultural Intermediary:**
Contemporary observers described Watts as "an intelligent, clever, capable man who enjoyed eating, drinking, and jocular conversation"—characteristics suggesting comfort navigating both Cherokee and European-American social contexts. His bilingual abilities (implied by his mixed heritage) made him invaluable in treaty negotiations. Watts signed the Treaty of Holston representing the Chickamauga faction, bringing peace faction credibility to the negotiations.

**Evidence of Intermediary Role:**

- His successful translation of Cherokee strategic interests to American officials
- His capability to travel between Cherokee towns and American settlements
- His later service as negotiator of the 1794 Tellico Blockhouse agreement
- His 1796 speech to federal officials demonstrating sophisticated understanding of American surveying practices and federal vs. state jurisdiction

**Documentary Challenges:**
No direct statements by Watts about his mixed heritage exist in available archives. His role as intermediary is inferred from his documented actions, his mixed parentage, and his effectiveness in diplomatic contexts requiring bicultural fluency.

### 2. Long Will - Mixed Heritage or Close Trader Connections

**Status:** Treaty of Holston signatory, July 2, 1791

**Heritage Documentation:**

- English name exclusively (no recorded Cherokee name in official documents)
- Born: circa 1750
- Died: circa 1803
- Town: Overhill Towns

**Key Identifying Feature:**
Long Will's appearance in treaty documents exclusively under his English name—while most Cherokee leaders are known primarily by their Cherokee names—suggests either:

1. Actual mixed Cherokee-European heritage, OR
2. A Cherokee individual adopted an English name as part of his role bridging cultures

The name suggests he was "the son of a European trader and Cherokee mother, or a Cherokee individual who adopted an English name as part of his role as a cultural intermediary between Cherokee and American communities."

**Role as Cultural Intermediary:**
The abundance of documentation about Long Will's role (unlike most Cherokee signatories) suggests his importance. He explicitly served as a translator of cultural concepts—helping Cherokee leaders understand American political systems (particularly the federal vs. state division of power), while explaining Cherokee territorial claims and political expectations to American officials. This kind of sophisticated cross-cultural brokering required fluency in both languages and deep understanding of both political systems.

**Evidence of Intermediary Function:**

- Selection as treaty representative despite unusual naming pattern
- His particular focus on explaining American political structures to Cherokee leaders
- His advocacy for peace faction strategy of federal treaty protection
- His continued involvement in post-treaty politics despite tensions mixed-race individuals sometimes faced

**Documentary Interpretation:**
The limited documentation about Long Will reflects a broader archival pattern: cultural intermediaries appear in official records primarily during formal interactions between nations. Much of Long Will's work—council discussions, community conversations about American expansion, daily negotiations between communities—left no written record in American archives.

### 3. Robin McClemore - Mixed Cherokee-European

**Status:** Treaty of Holston signatory, July 2, 1791

**Heritage Documentation:**

- European-style name "McClemore" indicates mixed ancestry
- Appears on treaty as signatories list
- No other biographical details in available archives

**Inference from Name:**
The surname "McClemore" (likely Scottish) combined with Cherokee signatory status indicates a father of Scottish or European descent and Cherokee mother. McClemore's presence among treaty signatories suggests he had standing within Cherokee political circles sufficient to represent Cherokee interests in federal negotiations.

**Role in Treaty Negotiations:**
McClemore's signature on the Treaty of Holston indicates Cherokee leadership valued his participation despite his mixed heritage. His inclusion suggests that by 1791, mixed-race individuals with standing in both Cherokee and European-American communities were recognized as important diplomatic representatives.

---

### 4. George Miller (Suaka) - Mixed Cherokee-European or English-Speaking Cherokee

**Status:** Treaty of Holston Additional Article signatory, February 17, 1792 (Philadelphia)

**Heritage Documentation:**

- Cherokee name: Suaka
- English name: George Miller
- Described as "a young Indian who understands the english language"
- Served as interpreter and cultural bridge during Philadelphia delegation

**Critical Intermediary Role:**
George Miller demonstrates the most thoroughly documented intermediary function of any Treaty of Holston signatory. During the 1792 Cherokee delegation to Philadelphia, Miller served as an interpreter, but with a crucial difference—he was a Cherokee speaker confirming another interpreter's translations:

> "Speeches [were] translated by James Carey, interpreter, and confirmed by George Miller, a young Indian who understands the english language"

This dual-verification system reveals Miller's role as cultural gatekeeper—not simply translating words, but confirming that American interpretations of Cherokee speech accurately conveyed Cherokee meaning.

**Evidence of Mixed Heritage or Deep Cultural Fluency:**
The documentation identifies Miller as "a young Indian who understands the english language"—phrasing suggesting his English fluency was notable enough to require explanation, implying either:

1. Mixed heritage giving him European-language exposure from childhood, OR
2. Exceptional education/exposure to English-speaking traders

**Documented Speech:**
Miller appears in Philadelphia delegation records, indicating he traveled from Cherokee territory to the federal capital—a journey requiring confidence navigating European-American society, access to travel resources, and standing sufficient to be included in formal diplomatic mission.

### 5. The Prince (Katigoslah) - Mixed or European-Connected Heritage (UNCERTAIN)

**Status:** Philadelphia delegation participant, January 1792

**Documentation:**

- Listed as member of Cherokee delegation to President Washington
- No biographical details in available sources
- English name "The Prince" suggests either mixed heritage or European connection

**Note:** "The Prince" may have been a nickname or English-language designation rather than actual mixed heritage. This individual requires further research to determine heritage and intermediary role.

## PART 2: INTERPRETERS AS CULTURAL INTERMEDIARIES

The interpreter category reveals a distinct group of mixed-race or European-heritage individuals who were formally appointed to mediate between Cherokee and American communication. These interpreters were often mixed-race or long-term residents of Cherokee communities with deep cultural knowledge.

### Known Treaty of Holston Interpreters

#### John Thompson - Half Creek (Mixed Native American Heritage)

**Role:** Official interpreter at Treaty of Holston, July 2, 1791

**Heritage:**

- Identified in records as "half Creek"
- Full Native American ancestry (not European-mixed, but demonstrates mixed-tribe intermediary role)
- Had standing in both Creek and European-American communities sufficient for federal appointment

**Significance:**
Thompson's appointment as official interpreter suggests federal recognition of his cultural and linguistic fluency. His half-Creek heritage made him valuable as a mediator between different Native American nations as well as with American officials.

#### James Carey - Interpreter and Treaty Signatory

**Role:** Official interpreter at Treaty of Holston, July 2, 1791, and Philadelphia delegation (1792)

**Documentation:**

- Signed Treaty of Holston as interpreter
- Conducted translations of Cherokee speeches in Philadelphia
- Worked alongside George Miller in dual-verification translation system

**Significance:**
Carey's role as official government interpreter suggests formal standing and documented fluency in Cherokee language. His continued work in Philadelphia delegation in 1792 indicates federal trust in his translation capabilities and cultural understanding. No information about Carey's heritage is available in current records, but his status as "interpreter" suggests either mixed heritage or deep immersion in Cherokee communities.

## PART 3: EUROPEAN TRADERS AS CULTURAL INTERMEDIARIES

The broader context of mixed-race emergence requires understanding the trader class that created the conditions for mixed-race families.

### Deerskin Trade and Trader Settlement in Cherokee Towns

**Trade System (1700-1790s):**

- Cherokee hunters supplied hundreds of thousands of deerskins annually to colonial traders
- Traders exported deerskins to European markets
- Trade created economic dependencies and wealth flow into Cherokee communities
- Traders brought metal tools, firearms, cloth, and manufactured goods into Cherokee towns

**Trader-Cherokee Intermarriage:**

- European traders—often Scottish or Irish men—frequently married Cherokee women
- These marriages followed Cherokee customs and integrated traders into Cherokee clan systems
- Under Cherokee matrilineal law, children belonged to mother's clan
- Children were fully Cherokee with all clan rights and responsibilities

**Result: Creation of Mixed-Race Intermediary Class**
Children of these unions served as natural bridges between Cherokee and European-American worlds due to:

1. Bilingual capabilities (Cherokee and English)
2. Understanding of both Cherokee and European cultural practices
3. Family connections spanning both societies
4. Often had education/literacy exposure through trader fathers
5. Belonged fully to Cherokee clans yet understood European-American society

## PART 4: ROLES OF MIXED-RACE INDIVIDUALS IN CHEROKEE-AMERICAN RELATIONS

### As Translators of Language and Culture

Mixed-race individuals provided more than linguistic translation; they translated cultural concepts and political systems. Examples include:

**Explaining American Political System to Cherokee:**

- Educating Cherokee leaders about federal vs. state jurisdiction
- Explaining why federal treaties couldn't always control state governments and settlers
- Helping Cherokee understand American legal concepts like surveying and land claims

**Explaining Cherokee Positions to Americans:**

- Articulating Cherokee territorial claims in terms Americans could understand
- Conveying Cherokee political structure and decision-making processes
- Translating Cherokee concepts of sovereignty and land rights into American legal terminology

### As Diplomatic Representatives

Mixed-race signatories like Long Will, George Miller, John Watts, and Robin McClemore held positions of diplomatic representation. Their presence at formal treaty negotiations indicates:

- Cherokee leadership valued their cultural fluency
- American officials recognized their standing in both communities
- Mixed-race individuals possessed sufficient diplomatic skill and credibility

### As Treaty Signatories

The inclusion of mixed-race individuals among treaty signatories served several functions:

1. **Demonstrated inclusion:** Showed mixed-race community members integrated into Cherokee political process
2. **Ensured understanding:** Provided witnesses to treaty terms who understood both languages
3. **Legitimized to both sides:** Gave treaty credibility with European-American officials and within Cherokee communities

### As Post-Treaty Advocates

After the Treaty of Holston, mixed-race individuals and interpreters continued critical work:

- Monitoring treaty compliance
- Documenting violations
- Advocating for enforcement of boundary provisions
- Explaining repeated American treaty violations to Cherokee leadership

## PART 5: DOCUMENTARY GAPS AND RESEARCH NEEDS

### Limited Archival Records of Mixed-Race Individuals

The surviving documentary record contains remarkably little direct information about mixed-race individuals' lives, heritage, and thinking. This reflects:

1. **American Archive Bias:** American records documented Cherokee leaders primarily during formal government interactions. The daily work of mixed-race intermediaries—council discussions, community conversations, cultural translation—left no written record in American archives.

2. **Period Terminology:** 18th-century records use "half-breed," "mixed blood," and other period terms. These terms are offensive by modern standards but are necessary search terms for archival research.

3. **Incomplete Genealogy:** Family relationships are often incompletely documented. John Watts' mixed heritage is clear; others must be inferred from names, roles, or descriptions.

4. **No Cherokee-Language Records:** Cherokee syllabary wasn't invented until 1820s (by Sequoyah, himself of mixed heritage—his mother was Wurteh, sister of Doublehead). 1790s Cherokee leaders left no written records in their own language.

### Key Research Gaps

**Questions Requiring Further Investigation:**

1. **James Carey's Heritage:** Was Carey himself of mixed descent? What was his background before becoming government interpreter?

2. **John Thompson's Background:** What Creek heritage did Thompson claim? What was his specific role in mediating between Creek and Cherokee nations?

3. **Additional Mixed-Race Signatories:** The five identified individuals may represent only a portion. Other signatories with European-style names may have had mixed heritage not yet documented.

4. **Trader Networks:** Which European traders established families in Cherokee towns? Can genealogies be reconstructed showing mixed-race family networks?

5. **Post-1792 Careers:** What happened to mixed-race intermediaries after the Treaty of Holston? Did they continue diplomatic work? Did they eventually join settler communities or remain with Cherokee Nation?

6. **Women's Roles:** Were any women of mixed heritage involved in intermediary work? Documentation typically focused on men, but women's roles in translation and cultural mediation deserve investigation.

## PART 6: SEQUOYAH - THE MOST FAMOUS MIXED-RACE CHEROKEE

While Sequoyah (creator of the Cherokee syllabary) lived primarily after the 1791-1792 Treaty period, his family demonstrates the significance of mixed-race connections.

**Sequoyah's Mixed Heritage:**

- Mother: Wurteh (sister of Doublehead)
- Father: Nathan Gist (European trader/soldier)
- Born: circa 1770s
- Created Cherokee syllabary: 1820s

**Significance:**
Sequoyah emerged from the same trader-Cherokee family networks that produced Treaty of Holston signatories like John Watts and George Miller. His later creation of Cherokee written language represented the ultimate contribution of mixed-race/trader-connected heritage to Cherokee cultural preservation—ironically, Sequoyah's work made future generations of Cherokee able to write without relying on European-language interpreters.

## PART 7: CHEROKEE LEGAL FRAMEWORK FOR MIXED-RACE INCORPORATION

### Matrilineal Clan System

The Cherokee Nation's matrilineal legal system fundamentally shaped how mixed-race individuals were incorporated:

**Key Principles:**

1. **Clan Membership Through Mother:** A child's clan belonged entirely to their mother's clan, regardless of father's ethnicity
2. **Full Citizenship:** Children of Cherokee women held all clan rights, property rights, and political rights
3. **Father's Heritage Irrelevant:** Whether a mother's partner was Cherokee, European, or other Native American, the child was fully Cherokee

**Practical Effect:**
This legal framework meant that mixed-race children were fully Cherokee by law and custom. Their European heritage did NOT place them in a subordinate or marginal position within Cherokee society. Instead, it often positioned them as valuable cultural bridges precisely because they brought European knowledge into fully-Cherokee contexts.

### Adoption and Integration Practices

Beyond biological mixed-race individuals, Cherokee practices of adopting captives and integrating outsiders meant that European-heritage individuals could become Cherokee through adoption and clan membership regardless of birth status. This created additional pathways for European-heritage individuals to occupy intermediary positions within Cherokee society.

## PART 8: SETTLEMENT PATTERNS OF MIXED-RACE FAMILIES

### Locations of Mixed-Race Signatories

**John Watts:** Willstown (Chickamauga), present-day Alabama
**Long Will:** Overhill Towns, present-day Tennessee
**Robin McClemore:** Unknown (signatory indicates some geographic connection)
**George Miller:** Implied Overhill towns area (participation in Philadelphia delegation)
**The Prince:** Unknown

**Geographic Pattern:**
Mixed-race individuals appear concentrated in:

1. **Overhill Towns** (Upper Towns) in present-day Tennessee near Rocky Mount
2. **Chickamauga Towns** in present-day Alabama/Georgia

This geographic pattern reflects:

- Major locations of trader settlement
- Regions closest to European-American frontier
- Areas where Cherokee-settler interaction was most intensive

### Settlement Near Trading Posts

Trader families settled near established trading posts and centers of deerskin trade activity. These settlements created mixed-race communities where children grew up bilingual and bicultural, positioning them naturally for intermediary roles.

## PART 9: CHALLENGES FACED BY MIXED-RACE INTERMEDIARIES

### Dual Loyalty Questions

Mixed-race individuals and interpreters sometimes faced suspicion from both sides:

- **Cherokee concerns:** Did mixed-race individuals remain loyal to Cherokee Nation or sympathize with American expansion?
- **American concerns:** Could interpreters be trusted, or did they favor Cherokee interests?

This suspicion was particularly acute for:

- Interpreters negotiating land cessions
- Individuals with visible European heritage in times of conflict
- Those traveling between Cherokee and settler communities

### Limited Documentation of Perspective

No letters or documents written by mixed-race Treaty of Holston signatories expressing their own viewpoints survive in American archives. This means we understand them primarily through:

- American officials' descriptions
- Their documented actions
- Inference from their roles

This represents a significant historical gap: mixed-race individuals' own understanding of their position, their loyalties, and their strategic thinking remains largely undocumented.

## PART 10: BROADER HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE

### Mixed-Race as Cultural Continuity

Rather than representing a breakdown or contamination of Cherokee culture, mixed-race individuals and their families represented Cherokee adaptability and strategic engagement with change. They:

1. **Preserved Cherokee Society:** By serving as interpreters and negotiators, mixed-race individuals helped Cherokee Nation engage with American expansion and pursue legal remedies
2. **Maintained Cultural Identity:** Despite European heritage, mixed-race signatories remained fully Cherokee citizens with clan membership and political standing
3. **Bridged Inevitable Change:** In a period when Cherokee-American interaction was inevitable, mixed-race individuals helped manage that interaction in ways that partially protected Cherokee interests

### Intermediaries as Historical Agents

The Treaty of Holston could not have been negotiated without intermediaries who understood both Cherokee and American political systems, languages, and cultural expectations. Mixed-race individuals and interpreters:

- Made treaty negotiations possible
- Shaped how treaty terms were expressed and understood
- Influenced both Cherokee and American interpretations of the treaty

Without these intermediaries, the Treaty of Holston and post-treaty negotiations would have been dramatically different—possibly less successful at articulating Cherokee positions to American officials.

### Missing Voices in Historical Record

The absence of documents authored by mixed-race intermediaries represents a significant gap in historical understanding. These individuals:

- Had unique perspectives on Cherokee-American relations
- Understood challenges and opportunities invisible to purely Cherokee or purely American observers
- Made strategic decisions that shaped historical outcomes

Yet their perspectives survive primarily through inference and the impact of their actions, rather than through direct statement.

## PART 11: RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FURTHER RESEARCH

### Archival Searches Needed

1. **Trader Family Genealogies:** Reconstruct networks of European traders who married Cherokee women
2. **Interpreter Records:** Search for James Carey, John Thompson, and other interpreters' personal papers
3. **Correspondence Mentioning Mixed-Race Individuals:** Review all 1790s correspondence for references to heritage, family relationships
4. **Post-Treaty Careers:** Track what happened to mixed-race signatories after 1792
5. **Cherokee Nation Archives:** Consultation with Cherokee Nation cultural resources office about oral traditions regarding mixed-race leaders

### Collaboration with Cherokee Nation

The Cherokee Nation should be consulted about:

- Accuracy of heritage claims
- Oral traditions about mixed-race leaders
- Appropriate terminology for discussing mixed heritage
- Representation of mixed-race individuals in historical narratives

### Expanded Search Terms

Archival research should use period terminology:

- "Mixed blood"
- "Half-breed"
- "Half-Indian"
- "Trader's children"
- "Intermarriage"
- Specific trader names (Gist, Thompson, Carey, etc.)

Priority research locations:

## CONCLUSION

Mixed-race individuals in 1790s Southwest Territory filled critical roles as cultural and linguistic intermediaries during a pivotal moment in Cherokee-American relations. Five documented individuals—John Watts, Long Will, Robin McClemore, George Miller, and The Prince—signed the Treaty of Holston alongside fully Cherokee leaders, indicating their standing within Cherokee political structures.

These individuals represent a category often overlooked in historical accounts: people whose mixed heritage positioned them perfectly to translate between worlds at a moment when such translation was essential. Their English names, their language capabilities, their knowledge of trader networks, and their standing in Cherokee communities made them invaluable to both Cherokee leadership pursuing diplomatic solutions and American officials seeking to communicate with the Cherokee Nation.

Yet the archival record contains remarkably little direct documentation of mixed-race individuals' own perspectives, strategies, or thinking. We know what they did (signed treaties, served as interpreters, negotiated boundaries) but not always what they thought about their position, their heritage, or their role in Cherokee-American relations.

The Treaty of Holston itself—the central document of Rocky Mount's historical significance—would not have been negotiated without these mixed-race intermediaries. John Watts' diplomatic sophistication, Long Will's ability to explain American political systems, George Miller's translation work, and the interpreters' language capabilities were essential to bringing Cherokee and American negotiators to agreement.

Recognizing mixed-race individuals as agents in their own right—not simply products of intermarriage or servants to both sides—provides a more complete and historically accurate understanding of how the Cherokee Nation responded to American expansion. These individuals made strategic choices, advocated for Cherokee interests, and shaped the terms under which Cherokee-American relations would develop.

Further research into interpreter records, trader family genealogies, and Cherokee Nation oral traditions will likely expand our understanding of this important category of historical actors and provide the direct-evidence documentation currently missing from the archival record.

### Note on Terminology

This research uses period-appropriate terminology including "mixed blood," "half-breed," and "mixed-race" in historical context. These terms are now recognized as offensive and are used here only for archival accuracy and historical communication. Contemporary scholarship refers to "mixed-heritage," "multiracial," or "intercultural" individuals. The Cherokee Nation's preferred terminology should be followed in any formal collaboration.
