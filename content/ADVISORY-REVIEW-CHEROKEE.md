### Document 2: John Watts on Peace and Boundaries (1796)

**PASS: Culturally Sensitive and Exceptionally Strong ✓**

#### What Works Exceptionally Well

**1. Cherokee Technical Knowledge Highlighted**

This is the strongest element. The document explicitly notes Watts's sophisticated knowledge:

> "Watts displays sophisticated knowledge of surveying practices:
>
> - He specifies the optimal season for boundary surveys (when trees bud for visibility)
> - He demands Cherokee representatives be present during the survey
> - He identifies the precise geographic points of the boundary line
> - He insists on a surveyor 'of integrity' rather than a land speculator"

**Why this matters:**

- Counters stereotype of Cherokee as "primitive" or lacking technical sophistication
- Shows Cherokee understood American legal/surveying systems
- Demonstrates Cherokee strategic thinking (demand oversight, prevent corruption)
- Treats Cherokee knowledge as equal to or superior to American expertise

**2. Cherokee as Treaty Enforcers (Not Signers)**

Critical framing shift:

> "This document shows Cherokee not as passive recipients of American policy but as active diplomatic agents holding the United States accountable to its promises."

**Why this matters:**

- Most historical sites show Cherokee as treaty SIGNERS
- This document shows Cherokee as treaty ENFORCERS
- Five years after signing, Watts is STILL tracking violations
- Cherokee positioned as watchdogs over American compliance

**3. American Violations Documented from Cherokee Perspective**

> "Peace has been faithfully observed by my Nation since the conference of 1795."

Then immediately followed by Cherokee documentation of American violations:

- Boundary not surveyed (5 years after treaty)
- Settlers encroaching (ongoing)
- Murders unpunished (documented)

**This is Cherokee oral testimony preserved in written record.**

**Why this matters:**

- Centers Cherokee as truth-tellers
- Documents American treaty violations from Cherokee perspective
- Shows Cherokee tracking violations methodically
- Demonstrates Cherokee used diplomatic channels to demand accountability

**4. Translation Notes with Cultural Humility**

> "Language: This speech was delivered through interpreters, as written Cherokee syllabary (developed by Sequoyah) did not yet exist in 1796."

> "Cultural Context: Cherokee diplomatic speeches of this era followed specific protocols and formulas. Watts's direct demands would have been understood by both Cherokee and American audiences as serious assertions of national sovereignty."

**Why this works:**

- Acknowledges pre-Sequoyah limitations (historical context)
- Notes Cherokee diplomatic protocols existed
- Respects Cherokee communication traditions
- Doesn't assume American forms were default

#### Cherokee Leadership Portrayed with Dignity

**John Watts described as:**

- "one of the most important Cherokee leaders of his era"
- Evolution from "war leader" to "principal advocate for negotiated peace"
- "using the treaty itself as leverage to demand U.S. accountability"

**This is sophisticated political evolution:**

- Not "warrior turned peaceful" (redemption narrative)
- But strategic shift: military → diplomatic while maintaining sovereignty demands
- Shows Cherokee adapting tactics, not abandoning goals

**Verdict: PASS with Commendation**

This document is a model for how to present Cherokee diplomatic sophistication with respect and accuracy.
