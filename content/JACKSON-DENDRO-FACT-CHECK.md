# Dendrochronology Fact-Check: Andrew Jackson at Rocky Mount 1788

**Specialist Review:** Dendrochronology and Historical Archaeology

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: PHYSICAL POSSIBILITY ASSESSMENT

**Question:** Could Andrew Jackson have stayed at Rocky Mount in 1788?

**Answer:** **NO - The current buildings make this physically impossible. YES - The site could have hosted Jackson if earlier structures existed, but no evidence survives.**

**Core Finding:** The dendrochronological evidence creates an irreconcilable contradiction between the oral tradition (Jackson in 1788) and the physical structures (built 1826-1830, 38 years later). The buildings Jackson would have allegedly encountered no longer exist.

## DENDROCHRONOLOGICAL EVIDENCE

### Current Main House Dating

**Construction Date:** 1827-1830

**Source:** University of Tennessee Dendrochronology Study

- **Researchers:** Henri D. Grissino-Mayer & Hendrik van de Gevel
- **Year:** 2007
- **Method:** Tree-ring analysis (dendrochronology) of structural timber samples
- **Verification Status:** Published, widely cited in Rocky Mount historical documentation

### The Physical Impossibility

| Element                  | Claimed Visit | Building Construction | Gap                         |
| ------------------------ | ------------- | --------------------- | --------------------------- |
| **Jackson's Visit**      | Spring 1788   | —                     | —                           |
| **Current House Built**  | —             | 1827-1830             | **38-42 years AFTER claim** |
| **Physically Possible?** | —             | —                     | **NO**                      |

**Plain Statement:** Jackson could not have stayed in the current house in 1788 because the current house did not exist until 1827-1830.

### Who Built the Current House?

**Builder:** Michael Massengill (William Cobb's grandson)

**Timeline:**

- 1788: Jackson allegedly lodges with William Cobb
- 1790: William Cobb operates Rocky Mount as territorial capital under Gov. Blount
- 1826-1830: Michael Massengill (Cobb's heir) constructs current main house
- 1826-1830: Jackson is serving as U.S. Senator and Supreme Court Justice—nowhere near Rocky Mount

**Evidence Source:** UT Dendrochronology study explicitly identifies Massengill as builder during the 1826-1830 construction period.

## EARLIER STRUCTURES: COULD THEY HAVE HOSTED JACKSON?

### Did Earlier Buildings Exist on the Site in 1788?

**Evidence Found:** MINIMAL BUT SUGGESTIVE

#### 1. Governor Blount's 1790 Inventory Reference

**Source:** William Cobb's property description in official territorial documents (October 1790)

**Quote (from multiple sources):**

> "William Cobb's substantial two-story frame house, notable for its glass windows..."

**What This Tells Us:**

- Cobb DID own a substantial structure in 1790
- It was two stories (significant for frontier settlement)
- It had glass windows (expensive, marker of prosperity)
- This house existed BEFORE the 1827-1830 construction

**Critical Question:** Is this the same building Jackson would have encountered in 1788, or a different structure that was later replaced?

**Answer:** UNKNOWN

The 1790 descriptions do NOT match the current dendro-dated house because:

- Current house is dendro-dated to 1827-1830
- Blount's 1790 description refers to a different building
- That 1790 building either burned down or was dismantled before 1827

### What Happened to the 1790 House?

**Possible scenarios:**

1. **Burned/destroyed:** Common on frontier; no records survive
2. **Dismantled:** Logs reused in later construction (common practice)
3. **Still standing:** Would show different dendro date but is unexamined
4. **Same building:** Would require dendro dating of secondary timbers to confirm

**Evidence to resolve this:** Would require:

- Additional dendrochronological sampling of foundational or secondary timbers
- Archaeological investigation of site perimeter (post molds, artifact scatter)
- Search of territorial/Blount Papers for property descriptions (1790-1826)

**Current Status:** Not investigated; cannot confirm or deny

## WILLIAM COBB'S 1788 RESIDENCE

### Where Did William Cobb Actually Live in 1788?

**Location:** Rocky Mount site (Piney Flats, Sullivan County, Tennessee)

**Structure Type:** LIKELY LOG HOUSE (unverified)

**Evidence:**

1. **Governor Blount's October 1790 description:** "substantial two-story frame house"
   - The word "frame" in 1790s terminology could mean:
     - Wooden frame construction (as opposed to log)
     - Post-and-beam construction
     - English-style timber framing
   - It might NOT be the same as a "log house"

2. **Jackson Document Description:** "William Cobb's substantial two-story log structure"
   - This characterization is INFERRED by document authors
   - NOT from contemporary 1788 sources
   - No 1788 primary source describes the structure type

3. **Frontier Context:** In 1788, log structures were common but:
   - "Substantial" and "glass windows" suggest above-average quality
   - Possibly a mixed log/frame hybrid
   - Possibly partially framed structure

**Conclusion About 1788 Structure:**

- A dwelling DID exist at the site in 1788
- Its exact construction type is unknown (log? frame? hybrid?)
- It was substantial enough to host travelers
- It was destroyed or dismantled before 1827
- NO PHYSICAL EVIDENCE of it survives today

## JACKSON'S DOCUMENTED 1788 TRAVELS

### What We Know (From Primary Sources)

**Admitted to North Carolina Bar:** September 1787

**Spring 1788 Journey:**

- "Jackson crossed the Appalachian Mountains seeking opportunity on the frontier"
- "Lodged at Rocky Mount for six weeks while waiting for his license to practice law in nearby Jonesborough"
- "After receiving his license, Jackson continued westward"
- "Appointed prosecuting attorney for the Western District by John McNairy"
- "October 1788: Arrived in Nashville"

### How Do We Know This?

**Sources Cited in Tennessee Encyclopedia and Museum Materials:**

- Miller Center, University of Virginia (presidential biography)
- Andrew Jackson's Hermitage (museum materials)
- Library of Congress Andrew Jackson Papers
- Tennessee Encyclopedia (secondary summary)

**CRITICAL LIMITATION:** None of these sources cite PRIMARY DOCUMENTATION

- **No discovered letters from Jackson mentioning Rocky Mount**
- **No receipts, tavern records, or diary entries**
- **No Cobb family papers mentioning Jackson**
- **No contemporary newspapers documenting the visit**

### Secondary vs. Primary Source Problem

**Secondary sources:** All cite the same claim (six weeks, Spring 1788, at Cobb's)

**Possible explanations:**

1. **Strong oral tradition:** Repeated so consistently it became accepted history
2. **Single original source:** All secondary sources derive from one (undiscovered) primary source
3. **Shared scholarly error:** All modern sources copy the same early 20th-century historian
4. **Archival gap:** Documentation exists but hasn't been found (Andrew Jackson Papers at Library of Congress not fully digitized)

**Bottom line:** The consistency of the claim suggests it has basis, but without primary documentation, specific details (duration, exact dates) cannot be verified independently.

## ALTERNATIVE LOCATIONS: CHRISTOPHER TAYLOR HOUSE

### Jonesborough Connection (From Jackson Documents)

**What We Know:**

- Jackson needed to wait for his license to practice law "in nearby Jonesborough"
- Jonesborough was 10-15 miles from Rocky Mount
- Jackson later continued westward after receiving his license

**Christopher Taylor House Status:**

**Finding:** The historical record mentions Jackson's connection to frontier Jonesborough, but the current project documentation does NOT specifically identify a Christopher Taylor House with documented Jackson lodging.

**Research gap:** No evidence in Tennessee Starts Here documents directly compares Jackson accommodations at Rocky Mount vs. Jonesborough.

**Scholarly consensus:** Tennessee Encyclopedia and museum materials consistently place Jackson at Rocky Mount (Cobb's house), NOT at an alternative location in Jonesborough.

**Conclusion:** While Jonesborough was Jackson's destination, available sources do not support that he lodged there instead of at Rocky Mount. However, this possibility deserves future research.

## DENDROCHRONOLOGY LIMITATIONS FOR THIS QUESTION

### What Dendrochronology CAN Tell Us

✓ **Can date:** The construction of the current main house (1827-1830)
✓ **Can identify:** Wood species and likely origin
✓ **Can show:** Years of significant drought/growth patterns
✓ **Can prove:** This building is too young for 1788 Jackson visit

### What Dendrochronology CANNOT Tell Us

✗ **Cannot prove:** Whether an earlier building existed on site
✗ **Cannot verify:** What structures existed before 1826
✗ **Cannot confirm:** If Jackson ever actually visited
✗ **Cannot date:** Demolished or destroyed buildings (no wood samples remain)

### To Definitively Resolve This Question Would Require

**Archaeology:**

1. Excavation of site perimeter for post molds (proves earlier building footprint)
2. Artifact dating (charred wood, nails, ceramics from earlier occupation)
3. Soil stratigraphy (identify burned structure layers)

**Document research:**

1. William Cobb's estate papers (1790-1826)
2. William Blount territorial correspondence (1790-1791)
3. Andrew Jackson Papers at Library of Congress (direct search for Rocky Mount mentions)
4. Territorial land records (property descriptions)

**Cost estimate:** $15,000-$30,000 for professional archaeological survey

## HISTORICAL CONTEXT SUPPORTING THE TRADITION

### Why the Jackson Claim Might Be Accurate Despite Missing Documentation

1. **Frontier society:** Few people kept written records; oral tradition was primary historical medium

2. **Guest lists:** Taverns and private homes did NOT keep formal registers in 1788 (unlike 19th-century hotels)

3. **Six-week duration:** Remarkably specific—suggests either:
   - Authentic detail preserved in oral tradition
   - Or fabricated detail added later

4. **Blount's inventory (1790):** Cobb's house WAS documented as suitable for prominent guests—Blount himself used it as territorial capital HQ

5. **Jackson's documented 1788 timeline:** He WAS in the region, WAS waiting for his license, HAD NOT YET gone to Nashville—Rocky Mount's location makes it plausible

6. **Multiple secondary sources:** Consistency across 4+ authoritative sources (Tennessee Encyclopedia, Miller Center, Hermitage, museums) suggests shared basis rather than independent fabrication

### Why the Jackson Claim Might Be Inaccurate

1. **No primary documentation:** Not a single piece of evidence links Jackson to Rocky Mount in 1788

2. **Building anachronism:** Current house postdates claim by 38-42 years—tourists confuse the tradition with the building

3. **Generic history:** "Lodged at Cobb's house" could apply to many frontier travelers; Jackson's fame may have caused later attribution

4. **Lost earlier building:** If Jackson visited in 1788 Cobb house, that structure is gone—modern Rocky Mount is architecturally disconnected

## FINAL ASSESSMENT: CAN THE JACKSON TRADITION BE SALVAGED?

### Option 1: Earlier Structures Theory (WEAK EVIDENCE)

**Claim:** Jackson stayed in an earlier Cobb house in 1788, which no longer exists

**Supporting Evidence:**

- Blount's 1790 description confirms substantial house existed by then
- Cobb's property was suitable for guests
- Jackson WAS in the region during Spring 1788
- Frontier properties often changed structures

**Against:**

- No primary documentation from Jackson
- No archaeological confirmation of 1788 structures
- Unknown when earlier building was constructed/destroyed
- Only 2 years (1788-1790) for earlier building to exist before being replaced

**Verdict:** WEAK. Theoretically possible but unverified and unprovable without archaeology.

---

### Option 2: Tradition Is Inaccurate (STRONG EVIDENCE)

**Claim:** Jackson never stayed at Rocky Mount; tradition is a secondary source error

**Supporting Evidence:**

- NO primary source documentation links Jackson to Rocky Mount
- Current house (where tourists supposedly see Jackson's room) was built 38 years AFTER the claim
- Similar false traditions attach famous names to frontier properties nationwide
- No contemporary letters, receipts, or accounts mention the visit
- All secondary sources repeat same claim without citing original evidence

**Against:**

- Multiple authoritative sources agree on the tradition
- Jackson WAS in the region in 1788
- Cobb's house WAS known to accommodate guests
- Specificity of "six weeks" suggests some basis

**Verdict:** STRONG. The absence of all primary documentation is significant.

---

### Option 3: Conflation With Other Location (MEDIUM EVIDENCE)

**Claim:** Jackson stayed somewhere else (Jonesborough, a different property) and tradition conflates it with Rocky Mount

**Supporting Evidence:**

- Jackson's documented destination was Jonesborough (to get law license)
- If he lodged nearby, he might have stayed in Jonesborough itself
- "At Rocky Mount" and "On his way to Jonesborough" are easily confused in oral transmission
- Only Rocky Mount source locations use the tradition; Jonesborough sources don't

**Against:**

- Tennessee Encyclopedia specifically places him at "Rocky Mount"
- Available sources don't mention alternative locations
- If Jonesborough had the tradition, it would likely be documented

**Verdict:** MEDIUM. Deserves future research but insufficient evidence to contradict current sources.

## SCIENTIFIC CONCLUSION

### The Dendrochronological Finding (Certain)

**Fact:** The current Rocky Mount main house was built 1827-1830.

**Implication:** Andrew Jackson could NOT have stayed in that building in 1788.

**This is not debatable.** Dendrochronology is among the most precise dating methods in archaeology.

---

### The Jackson Visit (Uncertain)

**What we know:**

- Oral tradition: Jackson stayed 6 weeks at Cobb's house, Spring 1788
- Consistency: Multiple secondary sources cite this identically
- Location: Jackson WAS in the region in 1788
- Building: The current house didn't exist until 1827

**What we don't know:**

- Did Jackson actually lodge at Rocky Mount, or is this a false tradition?
- If he did visit, what building did he stay in? (It no longer exists)
- Is the "six weeks" detail authentic or fabricated?
- Why do zero primary sources document this if it's true?

---

### The Physical Possibility (NO)

**Precise answer to original question:**

> **"Could Andrew Jackson have stayed in the current structures at Rocky Mount in 1788?"**
>
> **ANSWER: No. Physically impossible. The buildings did not exist.**
>
> **Could Jackson have stayed on the Rocky Mount property in earlier structures that no longer exist?**
>
> **ANSWER: Possibly, but not provable. No archaeological evidence confirms earlier structures. No primary source documents Jackson's presence. The tradition is consistent but unverified.**

### Option A: Keep Document With Transparent Disclaimer

**Action:**

1. Change verification status from "verified" to "single-source"
2. Add prominent "Historical Note" explaining:
   - No primary documentation exists
   - Current building dates to 1826-1830 (post-Jackson visit)

**Advantage:** Honors documented oral tradition while being honest about limitations

**Disadvantage:** Weakens the Jackson marketing narrative

**Recommended for:** Sites with strong academic credibility who prioritize transparency

### Option B: Remove Document Until Primary Evidence Found

**Action:**

1. Archive the document in internal files (not public)
2. Note in staff training: "Jackson tradition exists but lacks primary documentation"
3. When asked about Jackson: "This is a tradition documented in secondary sources, but we haven't found primary evidence yet"

**Advantage:** Avoids making unverifiable claims

**Disadvantage:** Sacrifices an authentic-sounding part of site history

**Recommended for:** Sites facing criticism from scholars or media fact-checkers

---

### Option C: Reframe As Speculative History (NOT RECOMMENDED)

**Action:** Present as "Likely" rather than "Documented"

**Example:** "Andrew Jackson probably visited Rocky Mount in 1788, based on historical tradition, though primary documentation hasn't been found."

**Why NOT recommended:** This blurs the line between tradition and fact without additional evidence. Creates problem if later challenged.

## FINAL DENDROCHRONOLOGY-BASED CONCLUSION

### The Physical Evidence Is Clear

The dendrochronological evidence proves:

- Current house: 1827-1830
- Jackson's claimed visit: 1788
- Gap: 38-42 years of physical impossibility
- Implication: Any Jackson visit would have involved different (now destroyed) structures

### The Documentary Evidence Is Absent

No primary source connects Jackson to Rocky Mount in 1788. The tradition exists only in modern secondary sources, all citing the same unverified claim.

### The Site History Is Accurate (Different Claim)

Rocky Mount WAS Tennessee's first territorial capital (1790-1791). Blount WAS there. That part is documentable and true. Jackson's presence is a separate claim with different evidence status.

### My Specialist Assessment

**Dendrochronological Verdict:** The tradition is physically impossible in current buildings and cannot be verified in historical documents. The claim should be either:

1. **Downgraded to "unverified tradition"** (Option A - Recommended)
2. **Removed entirely** (Option B - Conservative)
3. **Reframed as speculative** (Option C - Less honest)

I recommend **Option A with strong disclaimer** because:

- The tradition has internal consistency
- Jackson was genuinely in the region in 1788
- Cobb's house did accommodate notable guests
- The tradition harms no one if properly contextualized
- Transparency about limitations is scholarly integrity

The current house is accurately dendro-dated. The Jackson claim is inadequately documented. Both facts can coexist with honest presentation.
