# Women's Voices from 1790s Southwest Territory: Research Report

**Research Date:** January 30, 2026
**Researcher:** Documentation Project Team
**Status:** Comprehensive archival gap analysis and research recommendations
**Mission:** Identify women's documents (letters, diaries, testimony, property records) from Tennessee's founding era

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Critical Finding:** The Tennessee Starts Here collection currently contains **zero first-person women's voices**. This is not a minor documentation gap—it represents a fundamental erasure of women's agency and experiences from the historical narrative of Tennessee's founding.

However, systematic research reveals that women's presence is documented through:

1. **Indirect evidence** (property inventories, household records, architectural details)
2. **Women's documented labor** (Barsheba Cobb's kitchen operations, domestic service roles)
3. **Male-authored references** to women (letters describing women's work, court cases involving women)
4. **Cherokee women in treaty contexts** (She Reigns as treaty signatory; Cherokee matrilineal legal systems)

**The Primary Challenge:** Elite settler women like Mary Blount (confirmed by Dr. Warren to have left NO documents) and Barsheba Cobb (William Cobb's wife), like their male counterparts, often did not write the kinds of documents that survived. Frontier women—whether enslaved, indentured, or free—left almost no written records. Their labor was essential but invisible.

**Why This Matters:** Women comprised approximately 50% of the population. Their exclusion from documented narratives creates a false historical record that misrepresents whose labor built Tennessee's territorial government and whose voices shaped its early development.

## PART I: WHO WE'RE LOOKING FOR

### Priority Target 1: Barsheba Cobb (Wife of William Cobb)

**CORRECTION NOTE:** Earlier documentation mistakenly identified "Mary Cobb" as William Cobb's wife. The historical truth is that **Mary Cobb was William Cobb's SISTER** (she married Henry Massengill Sr.). William Cobb's actual wife was **Barsheba Whitehead Cobb**, who managed the household at Rocky Mount.

**Status:** **LIMITED DOCUMENTATION FOUND**

**What We Know:**

- Managed household operations at Rocky Mount during territorial capital period (1790-1792)
- Provided meals and accommodations for Governor William Blount
- Fed 42 Cherokee chiefs and attendants during July 1791 treaty negotiations
- Operated detached kitchen with capacity for large-scale cooking
- Worked "alongside" unnamed domestic servants (her labor is documented, theirs is not)

**Documentary Evidence:**

1. **Rocky Mount Inventory (October 15, 1791)**
   - Source: Sullivan County Records
   - States: Kitchen building contains "quarters for domestic servants"
   - Indicates: Barsheba Cobb supervised domestic workers
   - Does NOT document: Her own thoughts, agency, or perspective

2. **William Blount's October 20, 1790 Letter**
   - Source: Blount Papers
   - Describes: Rocky Mount accommodations and hospitality
   - Indirectly references: Barsheba Cobb's role in providing meals
   - Does NOT include: Any statement from Barsheba Cobb herself

3. **"Look For This" Interpretation Guide**
   - Acknowledges: Barsheba Cobb's essential kitchen work
   - States: "Barsheba Cobb and those who worked alongside her produced the meals that sustained the administrative machinery of the Southwest Territory"
   - Does NOT claim: We have her letters, journals, or testimony

**What We Don't Know (Critical Gaps):**

- Barsheba Cobb's maiden name (Whitehead) - now confirmed
- Her birth/death dates (age during capital period unknown)
- Her origins (where she came from before Rocky Mount)
- Her education level or literacy
- Any letters, diaries, or written statements by her
- Her perspective on hosting the territorial government
- What happened to her after the capital moved in 1792
- Whether she survived to old age or died young
- How many children she had or their names
- Her relationship with Blount's family

**Status: PRIMARY SOURCE DOCUMENTS = ZERO**

### Priority Target 2: Mary Blount (Wife of Governor William Blount)

**Status:** **CONFIRMED NO DOCUMENTS EXIST**

**Key Finding from Dr. Elizabeth Warren Research:**

> "Research shows Mary Blount left **no letters, diaries, journals, or written records**."

**What We Know:**

- Wife of William Blount, territorial governor
- Lived during the capital period in territorial mansion
- Likely managed household affairs and possibly some diplomatic entertaining
- Was a member of prominent North Carolina family (Blount family)

**Why Documentation is Absent:**
While her husband's correspondence survives extensively, elite women's correspondence often did not survive due to:

1. **Deliberate destruction** - families burned "private" women's correspondence as unseemly
2. **Different value systems** - men's official and business letters were preserved; women's domestic correspondence was discarded
3. **Survival bias** - women's letters were more fragile, less likely to be archived
4. **Archival practices** - historical institutions prioritized male-authored political documents

**Verified Status: ZERO PRIMARY SOURCE DOCUMENTS**

**Note:** This is not unusual for women of the period. Most elite frontier women left no written records. The problem is not that Mary Blount didn't exist—it's that only men's voices survived to become history.

### Priority Target 3: She Reigns (Cherokee Treaty Signatory)

**Status:** **MINIMAL DOCUMENTATION, HIGH HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE**

**Documentary Evidence:**

1. **Treaty of Holston (July 2, 1791)**
   - Document ID: treaty-holston-1791
   - Signature: "She Reigns" appears as signatory among 42 Cherokee leaders
   - Significance: Possibly only female signatory to major treaty (or name honoring woman)

**Who Was She Reigns?**

- Cherokee name: Keshukaune (translates as "She Reigns")
- Role: Treaty signatory representing Cherokee interests
- Status: Whether this was an individual woman or a title/role is unclear

**Historical Significance:**

- Cherokee society operated under matrilineal law system
- Women held significant political, economic, and property rights
- Women's councils existed alongside men's councils
- Clan membership determined through female lineage
- Women's property rights were protected and substantial

**Why Documentation is Limited:**

- American colonial and federal records largely ignored Cherokee women's political roles
- Most treaty documentation focused on male "representatives"
- Cherokee oral traditions (not written in American archives) likely contain more information
- Name ambiguity: "She Reigns" could be title, nickname, or descriptor rather than personal name

**Status: ONE DOCUMENT IDENTIFIES HER; EXTENSIVE GAPS REMAIN**

**Recommendation:** Partner with Cherokee Nation and University of Tennessee Native American studies to research Cherokee records, oral traditions, and tribal histories that might illuminate She Reigns' role and identity.

### Priority Target 4: Cherokee Women in Treaty Contexts

**Status:** **DOCUMENTED IN SYSTEM, UNDER-INTERPRETED**

**Key Finding:**
Cherokee matrilineal law meant that women held substantial political, economic, and property rights. Cherokee women participated in governance through clan councils and property management, even if American records didn't acknowledge this.

**Women Connected to Treaty Signatories:**

- Wives and mothers of Cherokee negotiators
- Women who managed towns while men traveled to negotiations
- Clan matriarchs whose approval was necessary for land cessions
- Daughters and sisters in leadership families

**Documentary Evidence:**

- Treaty of Holston lists 42 signatories (most identified as male by American records)
- American documents refer to "Cherokee women" in context of adoption practices, captivity, and trade
- No directly authored documents by Cherokee women identified in current collection

**What's Missing:**

- Cherokee women's perspectives on territorial expansion
- Women's council decisions about land and resources
- Oral traditions about how Cherokee women viewed American treaties
- Records of women's economic roles in Cherokee towns

**Status: INDIRECT REFERENCES EXIST; PRIMARY DOCUMENTS NEED SEEKING**

## PART IV: WHY WOMEN'S VOICES ARE MISSING

### Structural Reasons (Not Accidental)

**1. Literacy and Education**

- Frontier women often had limited formal education compared to men
- Girls were taught household skills; boys were taught reading/writing
- Literacy among women varied by class: elite > middling > working-class
- Enslaved women had literacy actively suppressed

**2. Time and Resources**

- Women were occupied with household production (food, clothing, textiles)
- Childcare responsibilities left little time for writing
- No expectation that women's daily work should be documented
- Writing materials were expensive; paper was reserved for "important" (male) business

**3. Cultural Attitudes**

- Women's writing was considered "private" and not worth preserving
- Men's official business letters were kept; women's domestic letters were discarded
- Woman's realm was the home; man's realm was public/recorded history
- "History" was defined as what men did in the public sphere

**4. Archival Practices**

- Historical institutions prioritized collecting political correspondence (male-authored)
- Women's letters were considered personal ephemera, not historical documents
- Family papers that might include women's writing were often destroyed intentionally
- Women's own handwriting was less valued/less preserved than men's

**5. Legal Status**

- Married women had limited legal standing to own property or conduct business
- Enslaved women's voices were systematically suppressed
- Widows had more agency but were still limited compared to married men
- Women could not sign legal documents on their own behalf (in most cases)

**6. Documentary Bias**

- Official records documented men's actions (government, law, property transfer)
- Women's work (cooking, childcare, household management) went undocumented
- Daily life left no record unless documented in letters or diaries
- Letters and diaries often weren't preserved

## PART V: WHAT THIS ERASURE HIDES

### Barsheba Cobb's Untold Story

We know Barsheba Cobb fed territorial government officials and Cherokee diplomats. We don't know:

- What she felt about hosting a diplomatic treaty negotiation
- Whether she was consulted about provisions, housing, or entertainment
- How she managed a household doubling or tripling in size for weeks
- Whether she had help from her own family or only from hired/enslaved workers
- What conversations happened in her kitchen between herself and guests
- What she thought about the Cherokee delegation
- Whether she saw herself as having participated in history-making

**Her agency is invisible.**

### Domestic Servants' Complete Erasure

The Rocky Mount inventory mentions "quarters for domestic servants" but names no one. These workers:

- Cooked meals, carried water, cleaned, did laundry
- Were essential to territorial government functioning
- Left no trace in surviving documents
- Could have been enslaved, indentured, hired, or family members
- Disappeared entirely from history

**Their humanity is erased.**

### Frontier Women's Perspectives Missing

We have no documented settler women's accounts of:

- First impressions arriving at Rocky Mount
- Encounters with Cherokee people
- Experiences of frontier settlement
- Attitudes toward territorial expansion
- Observations about daily life and community formation
- Fears about security, family, future
- Economic strategies for survival

**Their viewpoints are absent.**

### Cherokee Women's Political Roles Under-Documented

American records missed or minimized:

- Women's control over property and resources
- Women's clan-based political authority
- Women's role in deciding on land cessions and treaties
- Women's voice in Cherokee Nation governance
- How American territorial expansion affected Cherokee women specifically

**Their political significance is invisible.**

## PART VII: IF NO WOMEN'S DOCUMENTS ARE FOUND

### Honest Historical Interpretation

**The Absence Itself is Historically Significant**

If comprehensive archival research fails to locate women's first-person documents, this absence teaches important lessons:

1. **Women's labor was essential but invisible.** The territorial capital would not have functioned without food, hospitality, and household management—almost entirely women's work—yet men's political decisions got recorded while women's labor got forgotten.

2. **Documentation systems preserved men's voices.** This is not because women had nothing to say. It's because systems valued male political/business writing while discarding female domestic writing.

3. **Elite status did NOT ensure women's survival in the record.** Even Mary Blount, wife of a territorial governor, left no documents. Frontier poverty made survival even less likely.

4. **Frontier women were economically active but legally invisible.** They managed households, possibly ran taverns or stores, supervised servants, but formal records rarely acknowledged their agency.

5. **Enslaved and indentured women were deliberately silenced.** Their literacy was suppressed; their voices were never expected to survive.

### Recommended Interpretation Strategy

**If the archive is silent, say so—clearly and honestly:**

> "The Tennessee Starts Here collection contains no first-person women's voices from the 1790s Southwest Territory. This is not because women were absent. It is because historical documentation systems preserved men's letters while discarding women's correspondence. It preserved political decisions while erasing domestic labor. It preserved owners' names while leaving workers anonymous. Women built this territory. They fed the government, raised the next generation, maintained the households that held society together. But their own words about their experiences did not survive for us to read today. What we can tell you is what indirect evidence reveals—Barsheba Cobb's kitchen, the mysterious domestic servants whose names were never recorded, the Cherokee women whose political roles American officials largely ignored. Their silence in the archive reflects not their absence but the historical biases that shaped what was preserved."

This honest acknowledgment is more historically accurate and pedagogically sound than pretending the silence doesn't matter.

## PART IX: SUMMARY FINDINGS

### What We Know With Certainty

1. **Barsheba Cobb's documented role:** She managed household operations and hospitality during the territorial capital period (1790-1792), feeding Governor Blount, Cherokee delegation, and government officials.

2. **Her work was documented indirectly:** Through Rocky Mount inventory, Blount's letters, and interpretation guides—but her own words are absent.

3. **She Reigns participated in Treaty of Holston:** One woman (possibly) signed the major treaty document establishing territorial boundaries.

4. **Domestic workers existed but are nameless:** The inventory documents "quarters for domestic servants" but names no individuals.

5. **Zero first-person women's documents have been located:** No letters, diaries, journals, or writings by frontier women of the period are in the Tennessee Starts Here collection.

### What We Can Reasonably Conclude

1. **Women's erasure was systematic, not accidental.** Documentation systems preserved male political/business writing; women's domestic writing was discarded.

2. **Elite status did not ensure survival.** Even Mary Blount, wife of territorial governor, left no written records.

3. **Frontier women's voices are especially scarce.** Working-class women, enslaved women, indentured servants—their literacy was limited or suppressed, and their writings were even less likely to survive.

4. **Indirect evidence can illuminate women's roles.** Kitchen architecture, household inventories, references in men's letters all document women's presence and work, even when women's own words are absent.

5. **The absence teaches us about the bias of historical documentation.** What didn't get preserved tells us as much about 1790s values as what did.

### What Remains Unknown

1. Barsheba Cobb's birth/death dates, origins, feelings about her role (maiden name Whitehead now confirmed)
2. The names, identities, and circumstances of domestic servants at Rocky Mount
3. The identity of "She Reigns" (individual? title? woman or honor name?)
4. The perspectives of Cherokee women on territorial expansion and treaties
5. The agency and roles of enslaved and indentured women in territorial society
6. The daily experiences and viewpoints of ordinary frontier women
7. The economic roles and contributions of working-class women
8. How frontier women experienced displacement of Cherokee people

## CONCLUSION: THE WOMEN WHO BUILT TENNESSEE'S CAPITAL

Barsheba Cobb's kitchen fed Tennessee's founding government. She Reigns negotiated boundaries that shaped a nation. Unknown women—enslaved, indentured, hired, family—performed the labor that made all of this possible.

Yet their voices are nearly silent in the historical record.

This is not because they had nothing to say. It is because historical documentation systems did not consider women's work, women's correspondence, women's perspectives worth preserving. Male political decisions got recorded. Female domestic labor got forgotten. Male business letters survived. Female family correspondence got destroyed.

**The archive does not tell us that women were absent. It tells us that historical biases shaped what survived.**

Rocky Mount State Historic Site has an opportunity to model a different approach:

1. **Name what we know** (Barsheba Cobb's documented hospitality work)
2. **Acknowledge what we don't know** (her background, perspective - though we now know her maiden name was Whitehead)
3. **Explain the archival gap** (why women's voices disappeared)
4. **Search honestly for more** (commissioning research despite low likelihood of success)
5. **Teach critically** (so visitors understand the bias, not just the history)

Tennessee's founding involved people whose names were recorded and people whose names were erased. Both deserve acknowledgment. Both deserve historical honesty.
