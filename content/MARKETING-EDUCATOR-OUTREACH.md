# Evidence Room: Educator Outreach & Adoption Strategy

**Document:** Comprehensive educator recruitment and classroom adoption plan
**Created:** January 30, 2026
**Target Audience:** Tennessee K-12 teachers, college instructors, homeschool educators
**Goal:** 500+ teachers using Evidence Room in classrooms by end of 2026

---

## Executive Summary

Evidence Room is a primary source archive with exceptional foundational design, but adoption requires **strategic outreach, clear classroom integration, and free educator resources**. This strategy identifies how to reach teachers where they are, remove friction to classroom use, and build a community of practice around the archive.

**Three Phases:**

1. **Phase 1 (Q1 2026):** Foundation & Pilot Partnerships
2. **Phase 2 (Q2-Q3 2026):** Scaled Outreach & Community
3. **Phase 3 (Q4 2026):** Sustainability & Growth

**Investment:** Requires part-time educator coordinator role (1 FTE) + freelance curriculum writer (contract)

**Middle School Version** (Grades 6-8): `Document-Analysis-Worksheet-6-8.pdf`

```
Name: _________    Date: _______    Class: ____    Document: __________

SOURCING:
1. Author & role: Who wrote this? What was their job/position?
2. Date & context: When was it written? What was happening then?
3. Audience & purpose: Who was meant to read this? What did the author want?
4. Perspective: Does the author favor any particular group or idea? How can you tell?

READING:
5. Main idea: What is the central message?
6. Evidence: What specific details or quotes support that message?
7. Assumptions: What does the author assume is true (that they don't state)?
8. Gaps: What is NOT being said? Whose voice is missing?

ANALYSIS:
9. Reliability: How trustworthy is this source? Why?
10. Connection: How does this connect to what else you're learning?
11. My interpretation: What do I understand after reading this?
12. Questions: What would I ask the author?
```

**High School Version** (Grades 9-12): `Document-Analysis-Advanced-9-12.pdf`

```
HEADER:
- Author(s):
- Date/Period:
- Type (letter, decree, journal, etc.):
- Archival source/location:

RHETORICAL ANALYSIS:
1. Ethos (credibility of author):
   - Why should readers trust this person?
   - What is their authority/expertise?

2. Pathos (emotional appeal):
   - What feelings does the author evoke?
   - What emotional language is used?

3. Logos (logical reasoning):
   - What claims does the author make?
   - What evidence do they provide?
   - Are the arguments sound?

HISTORICAL ANALYSIS:
4. Social context:
   - What historical forces were at play?
   - How does this document reflect its time?

5. Intended effect:
   - What was the author trying to accomplish?
   - What action or belief did they want to provoke?

6. Bias & perspective:
   - Whose perspective is centered?
   - Whose is marginalized/absent?
   - How might other parties have viewed these events?

SYNTHESIS:
7. Corroboration:
   - What other sources support or contradict this document?
   - How would you evaluate competing claims?

8. Relevance:
   - Why does this document matter today?
   - What does it tell us about power, government, identity?

9. My argument:
   - How does this document fit into my larger thesis/project?
   - What evidence does it provide?
```

#### D. Discussion & Debate Guides

**Title:** `Evidence-Room-Discussion-Guide-Teachers.pdf` (8 pages)

**Contains:**

| Section                           | Content                                                                                    |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Safe Discussion Setup**         | How to establish ground rules, create psychological safety, respond to challenging moments |
| **Discussion Questions by Grade** | 5-7 questions per grade level, with facilitation notes                                     |
| **Debate Topics**                 | 3 prepared debate topics with suggested evidence for each side                             |
| **Roleplay Scenarios**            | 2-3 scenarios where students take on historical perspectives                               |
| **What If Questions**             | Counterfactual questions to deepen thinking ("What if Washington had chosen differently?") |
| **Controversial Topics Guide**    | How to teach about slavery, Indigenous dispossession, fairness—without causing harm        |
| **Facilitator Notes**             | Anticipated difficult questions + suggested responses                                      |

**Discussion Questions Example (Grades 9-12):**

```
QUESTION 1: "Why did Washington choose Blount for this role?"
   Context: Students read Washington's letter and Blount's appointment
   Facilitation: Ask students to identify specific reasons in the documents
   Possible answers: Experience, loyalty, land interests, connections
   Deepen: "Would Washington have chosen the same person if he valued
           Cherokee rights? Why or why not?"

QUESTION 2: "What does the Treaty of Holston tell us about negotiating power?"
   Context: After reading treaty text and Cherokee signatories
   Facilitation: Point out the number of signatories, the location, the language
   Possible answers: Americans held more power, treaties were negotiated (not imposed),
                    Cherokee had agency in negotiations
   Deepen: "What indicators show relative power in negotiations?"
           "What concessions did each side make?"

QUESTION 3: "Whose perspective is missing from this archive?"
   Context: Reference "About This Archive" transparency statement
   Facilitation: Validate that this is sophisticated thinking
   Possible answers: Enslaved people, women (except Blount's family),
                    lesser-known Cherokee, settlers without land
   Deepen: "How might this archive look different if told from another perspective?"
```

**Debate Topics:**

```
DEBATE TOPIC 1: "The Treaty of Holston Was a Fair Agreement"
   Pro side evidence: Cherokee agreed to terms, received compensation,
                     retained substantial lands
   Con side evidence: Unequal power dynamics, Americans had military advantage,
                     later broken by westward expansion
```

## Part 9: Virtual Field Trip Program

### Why Virtual Field Trips Matter

**Physical barriers:**

- Distance (schools more than 2 hours away can't visit)
- Cost (transportation, admission)
- Time (school year constraints)
- Accessibility (students with mobility challenges)

**Virtual field trips:**

- Remove geographic barriers
- Reduce costs
- Allow flexible scheduling
- Include all students
- Supplement or replace physical visits
- Extend learning before/after visit

---

### Virtual Field Trip Program Structure

#### Phase 1: Foundation (Q2 2026)

**Component 1: Pre-Visit Interactive Content**

**"Before You Visit" Digital Experience** (30-45 min)

- Accessible to all teachers/students (free)
- Narrated tour of Rocky Mount (3D photography or video walkthrough)
- Key stops with historical context:
  - The Front Door (Blount's greeting)
  - The Study (where he wrote letters)
  - The Dining Room (where negotiations happened)
  - The Grounds (defensive position, view of river)
- Each stop has 1-2 minute narration + historical photo comparison
- Interactive timeline (place events on physical location)
- "Spot the Evidence" activity (students find documents related to physical location)

**Deliverable:**

- Web-based experience at `tennesseestartshere.com/virtual-tour`
- Mobile-friendly (tablets in classroom)
- No login required
- Estimated production: 3-4 weeks, $2,000-3,000 (contract videographer)

---

**Component 2: Guided Pre-Visit Lesson (45 min)**

**"Prepare for Your Visit"** lesson plan

- Part 1: What will you see? (15 min)
  - Brief history of Rocky Mount
  - Who lived here and why?
  - Timeline context
  - Student activity: "Predict What You'll See" (draw/write)

- Part 2: How to be a historian (20 min)
  - What is a historical source?
  - What is an artifact?
  - How do we read objects, not just words?
  - Activity: Analyze a household object for history (e.g., a chair)

- Part 3: Your mission (10 min)
  - Student observation worksheet for visit
  - Key questions to think about
  - "Scavenger hunt" style observation task

**Deliverable:**

- Lesson plan (PDF, editable Google Doc)
- Student worksheet (printable, 2 pages)
- Observation guide for teachers
- Free download on educator page

---

#### Phase 2: Live Virtual Tour (Q3 2026)

**"Live Visit with a Docent"** (60-90 min)

- Scheduled tours (2-3 times per month)
- Led by Rocky Mount site interpreter
- Small group (30-40 students max, to allow Q&A)
- Interactive (students ask questions in real-time)
- Recording provided for playback

**Technical Setup:**

- Zoom or YouTube Live
- Docent with handheld camera (or fixed position with pan/zoom)
- Chat moderation by second staff member
- Screen sharing for document display

**Tour Flow:**

```
0:00-0:05     Welcome & house rules
0:05-0:20     Docent intro + historical context
0:20-0:40     Living rooms tour (study, dining room, parlor)
0:40-1:00     Upstairs tour (bedrooms, artifacts)
1:00-1:15     Grounds tour (view, defensive position, gardens)
1:15-1:25     Q&A with students
1:25-1:30     Call to action: visit in person or use Evidence Room

[If 90 min version]
1:30-1:50     Extended Q&A + deeper stories
1:50-2:00     Connection to Evidence Room documents
2:00-2:05     Closing reflections
```

**Scheduling:**

- 2:00 PM ET (during school day)
- 6:00 PM ET (for evening/independent learners)
- Rotation: Tuesday & Thursday
- 2-3 different tour topics per month

**Tour Topics Rotation:**

1. "Life in the 1790s" (household, daily life)
2. "Government Formation" (how Blount used the house, meetings)
3. "Artifacts & Evidence" (what we know from objects)
4. "Behind the Scenes" (conservation, archaeology)
5. "You Ask the Questions" (student-led Q&A tour)

**Promotion:**

- Email to educator list (4 weeks before, 2 weeks, 1 week)
- Social media announcements
- Print flyers for schools
- Include in educator guide

**Recording Distribution:**

- Posted to YouTube within 48 hours
- Sharable link sent to registered teachers
- Can be incorporated into classroom (asynchronous)
- Keeps indefinitely (evergreen resource)

#### Phase 3: Post-Visit Integration (Q2-Q4 2026)

**"After Your Visit" Reflection Packet** (30-45 min)

**Activities:**

1. **Memory Mapping** (15 min)
   - "Draw the layout of Rocky Mount from memory"
   - Verify accuracy with actual images
   - Discuss what they remembered vs. forgot

2. **Document Connection** (15 min)
   - "Find Evidence Room documents related to what you saw"
   - Activity: Match documents to rooms/locations
   - Reflect: "How does seeing the place change your understanding?"

3. **Interpretive Writing** (15 min)
   - Option A: "A Letter from Rocky Mount" (write from historical perspective)
   - Option B: "If I Were Blount" (reflect on decisions)
   - Option C: "Questions for the Archaeologists" (wonder-based writing)

**Deliverable:**

- Worksheet packet (4 pages, printable)
- Teacher guide with discussion prompts
- Extension activities (debate, project ideas)
