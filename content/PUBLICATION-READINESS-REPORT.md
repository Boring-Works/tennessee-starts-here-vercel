- **Grade:** ⚠️ MINOR FIXES

- **Issues:**

  - [ ] Note about dendrochronology contradiction (buildings dated 1826-1830)
  - [ ] Explain what "frame house" vs "log house" meant in 1791

- **Estimated fix time:** 60 minutes
- **Priority:** HIGH (physical site interpretation)
- **What to add:**

  ```markdown
  ## Historical Context

  This inventory, prepared in October 1791 as Governor Blount prepared to move the territorial capital to Knoxville, provides the most detailed contemporary description of Rocky Mount's physical appearance during its time as the first capital.

  ### About the Buildings

  The "two-story frame house" described here is particularly significant. "Frame house" in 1791 meant a timber-framed structure, more sophisticated than typical log construction. The mention of a "stone foundation" and "glass windows in all principal rooms" indicates a level of refinement rare on the frontier.

  **Important Note:** Dendrochronology (tree-ring dating) analysis of the current Rocky Mount structures shows construction dates of 1826-1830, approximately 35-40 years after this inventory. The inventory describes the earlier buildings that stood on this site during Blount's residency. The current structures, while built by the same Cobb family, replaced the buildings described in this document.

  ### Comparison to Typical Frontier Dwellings

  Most frontier cabins of this period were single-story log structures with:

  - Wooden shutters instead of glass windows
  - Dirt or puncheon floors instead of finished floors
  - Single room or at most two rooms
  - No separate kitchen building

  Rocky Mount's glass windows alone would have cost as much as an entire typical cabin. The inventory's valuation of $2,000 represents extraordinary wealth - equivalent to approximately $60,000 today.
  ```
