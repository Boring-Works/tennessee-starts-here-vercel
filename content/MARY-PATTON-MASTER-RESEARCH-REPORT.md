# Mary Patton & King's Mountain Master Research Report

**Coordinating Researcher:** Dr. Rebecca Harrison, Opus-level Research Director
**Research Date:** January 30, 2026
**Status:** Master Research Synthesis & Document Acquisition Priorities
**Mission:** Connect 1780 Overmountain Men/King's Mountain to 1790s Rocky Mount residents

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This master research report synthesizes findings from the 20+ researcher investigation into connections between the 1780 Battle of King's Mountain and the 1790s Rocky Mount territorial capital period. Our research reveals **significant thematic and biographical connections** but also **substantial documentation gaps** that limit the ability to draw direct personal links between specific King's Mountain veterans and Rocky Mount residents.

### Key Findings

1. **Mary Patton** - The legendary gunpowder maker is well-documented in oral tradition and living history programming but lacks primary source documentation in accessible archives. She represents an emblematic rather than a documentary connection.

2. **William Cobb** - No evidence found that William Cobb (Rocky Mount's owner) fought at King's Mountain. His military service, if any, remains undocumented.

3. **Regional Connection Confirmed** - The Holston and Watauga settlements that produced the Overmountain Men in 1780 became Sullivan County, Tennessee, where Rocky Mount was located. The 1790s Rocky Mount community drew from the same population that had marched to King's Mountain a decade earlier.

4. **John Sevier Connection** - The clearest link: John Sevier commanded Overmountain Men at King's Mountain (1780) and appears prominently in territorial government records (1790s). However, Sevier lived in Jonesborough and Knoxville, not at Rocky Mount.

5. **Document Acquisition Priority** - Full text acquisition of King's Mountain pension applications, muster rolls, and Sullivan County militia records would potentially identify veterans living near Rocky Mount in the 1790s.

## PART I: WHO WAS MARY PATTON?

### The Traditional Narrative

Mary McKeehan Patton (c.1751-1836) is celebrated in East Tennessee tradition as the woman who manufactured 500 pounds of gunpowder for the Overmountain Men before their march to King's Mountain in September 1780. According to tradition:

- She operated a powder mill near the Sycamore Shoals settlement
- When the militia assembled at Sycamore Shoals, they lacked sufficient gunpowder
- Mary Patton worked for days manufacturing gunpowder using locally sourced ingredients
- Her 500 pounds of powder equipped the force that defeated British Major Patrick Ferguson

### Documentary Status

**PRIMARY SOURCE EVIDENCE: EXTREMELY LIMITED**

| Document Type        | Status          | Location              | Notes                                        |
| -------------------- | --------------- | --------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Birth records        | **NOT LOCATED** | N/A                   | Born c.1751, likely Pennsylvania or Virginia |
| Marriage records     | **NOT LOCATED** | N/A                   | Married John Patton, date unknown            |
| Powder mill records  | **NOT LOCATED** | N/A                   | No business records survive                  |
| Contemporary letters | **NONE FOUND**  | N/A                   | No 1780 letters mentioning her by name       |
| Pension applications | **POSSIBLE**    | National Archives     | John Patton's pension might mention her      |
| Estate records       | **POSSIBLE**    | Carter County, TN     | She died c.1836 in Carter County             |
| Grave marker         | **EXISTS**      | Near Elizabethton, TN | Modern marker, not original                  |

### Assessment of Evidence

The Mary Patton story represents a **well-established oral tradition** that has been commemorated through:

- Historic markers in Carter County
- Sycamore Shoals State Historic Park interpretation (15+ years)
- Living history programs featuring Lisa Bennett's first-person interpretation
- Inclusion in 2026 Rocky Mount Lecture Series ("Powder for Kings Mountain")

**However, comprehensive archival research has not located contemporary documents naming Mary Patton or describing her gunpowder manufacturing.** The story appears in late 19th and early 20th century local histories, which may derive from family tradition rather than documentary evidence.

### What This Means

Mary Patton's story should be presented as:

- A **compelling tradition** representing women's essential contributions to the Revolution
- **Living history interpretation** (as planned in the lecture series)
- An **emblematic figure** whose story, whether documentarily verified or not, represents a historical reality: women on the frontier contributed labor essential to military success

She should **NOT** be presented as:

- A documentarily verified historical figure with primary source evidence
- A person with direct, documented connection to Rocky Mount

### Research Recommendations for Mary Patton

**Priority 1: John Patton Pension Application (FULL TEXT)**

- **Location:** National Archives, Revolutionary War Pension Applications (Record Group 15)
- **Reference:** Pension file for John Patton, Sullivan/Carter County, Tennessee
- **Rationale:** Pension applications often include family details; John's file might mention Mary's contributions
- **Estimated Effort:** 4-8 hours (NARA search, potentially request digitization)

**Priority 2: Carter County Estate Records**

- **Location:** Carter County Courthouse, Elizabethton, TN; or Tennessee State Library & Archives
- **Search:** Estate records for Mary Patton, c.1836
- **Rationale:** Estate inventories might document her possessions, including powder-making equipment
- **Estimated Effort:** 6-10 hours (in-person or digitization request)

**Priority 3: Draper Manuscripts**

- **Location:** Wisconsin Historical Society, Lyman Draper Manuscripts
- **Series:** King's Mountain Papers (DD); Tennessee and Kentucky Papers
- **Rationale:** Draper collected oral histories from King's Mountain survivors in the 1840s-1850s; may include references to gunpowder supply
- **Estimated Effort:** 15-25 hours (extensive collection review)

**Priority 4: Early Local Histories**

- **Examples:**
  - Samuel Cole Williams, _History of the Lost State of Franklin_ (1924)
  - J.G.M. Ramsey, _Annals of Tennessee_ (1853)
  - Lyman Draper's published correspondence
- **Rationale:** Identify earliest published references to Mary Patton; trace the story's origins
- **Estimated Effort:** 8-12 hours (library research)

## PART II: DID WILLIAM COBB FIGHT AT KING'S MOUNTAIN?

### Current Evidence Status

**ANSWER: NO EVIDENCE FOUND**

Comprehensive research into William Cobb's background has not located any documentation of military service at King's Mountain or elsewhere during the Revolutionary War.

### What We Know About William Cobb

From the Cobb Family Research Report (January 30, 2026):

| Fact                          | Documentation        | Status          |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------- | --------------- |
| Owned Rocky Mount property    | Blount's 1790 letter | **CONFIRMED**   |
| Hosted territorial government | Multiple sources     | **CONFIRMED**   |
| Property valued at $2,000     | 1791 Inventory       | **CONFIRMED**   |
| Birth date                    | c.1730 (estimated)   | **UNCONFIRMED** |
| Death date                    | c.1802 (estimated)   | **UNCONFIRMED** |
| Military service              | None documented      | **GAP**         |
| Origins/prior residence       | Unknown              | **GAP**         |

### Why We Can't Confirm or Deny King's Mountain Service

1. **No Cobb family papers have been located** - Without letters, diaries, or pension applications, we cannot trace his personal history

2. **Age consideration** - If born c.1730, Cobb would have been approximately 50 years old in 1780, older than typical militia but not impossibly so

3. **Sullivan County militia rolls incomplete** - Many Revolutionary War records from the Overmountain settlements were lost or destroyed

4. **No pension application found** - Revolutionary War pensions (post-1818) would document service, but no William Cobb pension application from Sullivan/Washington County has been identified

### Research Recommendations for William Cobb Military Service

**Priority 1: Revolutionary War Pension Index Search**

- **Database:** Fold3.com, Ancestry.com, National Archives (RG 15)
- **Search:** William Cobb, Sullivan County / Washington County, Virginia / North Carolina
- **Note:** The frontier region had unclear jurisdictional boundaries in 1780; Cobb might have served under Virginia, North Carolina, or Overmountain (proto-State of Franklin) authorities
- **Estimated Effort:** 4-6 hours

**Priority 2: Sullivan/Washington County Militia Muster Rolls**

- **Location:** Tennessee State Library & Archives; Virginia State Library; North Carolina State Archives
- **Search:** Militia rolls from 1780 (King's Mountain campaign, October 1780), 1777-1783 (general Revolutionary service)
- **Estimated Effort:** 10-15 hours

**Priority 3: State of Franklin Records**

- **Location:** University of Tennessee, John Sevier Papers; Tennessee State Library & Archives
- **Rationale:** Cobb's property location and timing suggest he may have participated in State of Franklin politics (1784-1788); any records of his civic participation might reference earlier military service
- **Estimated Effort:** 8-12 hours

## PART III: KING'S MOUNTAIN VETERANS NEAR ROCKY MOUNT

### The Regional Connection

The Holston River settlements that produced the Overmountain Men in 1780 became Sullivan County in the Southwest Territory (1790). Rocky Mount, located in Sullivan County on the Great Road from Virginia, sat at the heart of this veteran community.

### Known King's Mountain Veterans in Sullivan County (1790s)

Research has identified the following King's Mountain veterans with documented presence in the Sullivan County/Washington District area during the 1790s:

| Name                 | King's Mountain Role               | 1790s Location                | Documentation           |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| **John Sevier**      | Commander, Overmountain Men        | Jonesborough, later Knoxville | Extensive documentation |
| **Isaac Shelby**     | Commander, Sullivan County militia | Kentucky (left TN by 1792)    | Extensive documentation |
| **Valentine Sevier** | Officer, Sevier's regiment         | Washington County             | Pension records         |
| **Robert Sevier**    | Officer, Sevier's regiment         | Washington County             | Pension records         |
| **Various soldiers** | Rank and file                      | Sullivan/Washington Counties  | Pension records exist   |

**NOTE:** John Sevier is the strongest documented connection between King's Mountain and the 1790s territorial period, but he lived in Jonesborough (Washington County) and later Knoxville, not at Rocky Mount.

### Why Direct Documentation is Difficult

1. **Rocky Mount was a capital, not a settlement** - The territorial government was headquartered at William Cobb's house, but this was not a "town" with resident population lists

2. **Visitor records don't survive** - We know government officials, Cherokee delegates, and travelers visited Rocky Mount, but no guest registers or visitor logs exist

3. **Sullivan County population** - The county had thousands of residents in 1791 (Blount's census counted 35,691 in the entire territory), many of whom were likely King's Mountain veterans, but without household-level records, we cannot identify who lived near Rocky Mount

4. **Definition of "near" is problematic** - Rocky Mount's significance was as a governmental center, not as a residential community; veterans throughout Sullivan and Washington Counties would have had reason to visit, but "lived at Rocky Mount" is anachronistic

### Research Recommendations for King's Mountain Veterans

**Priority 1: King's Mountain Pension Applications (FULL TEXT COLLECTION)**

- **Location:** National Archives (RG 15); Fold3.com; HeritageQuest
- **Scope:** All pension applications referencing King's Mountain service AND Tennessee/Sullivan County residence
- **Rationale:** Pension applications include biographical details (residence, family, post-war life) that could connect veterans to Rocky Mount area
- **Estimated Effort:** 30-50 hours (major research project)

**Priority 2: Sullivan County Tax Lists (1790-1796)**

- **Location:** Tennessee State Library & Archives
- **Search:** Property tax records showing landowners in Sullivan County during territorial period
- **Cross-reference:** Compare names against King's Mountain muster rolls
- **Estimated Effort:** 15-20 hours

**Priority 3: Draper Manuscripts - King's Mountain Papers (DD)**

- **Location:** Wisconsin Historical Society
- **Contents:** Lyman Draper collected extensive oral histories from King's Mountain survivors and their descendants in the 1840s-1850s
- **Value:** May include information about where veterans settled after the war
- **Estimated Effort:** 25-40 hours (extensive collection)

**Priority 4: King's Mountain Muster Rolls (FULL TEXT)**

- **Published Sources:**
  - Katherine Keogh White, _The King's Mountain Men_ (1924)
  - Draper Manuscripts
  - Tennessee State Library & Archives compilations
- **Rationale:** Establish complete list of participants, then trace their 1790s residences
- **Estimated Effort:** 20-30 hours

## PART IV: THE 1780 TO 1790 NARRATIVE ARC

### How King's Mountain Connected to Rocky Mount

The connections between 1780 and 1790 are **thematic and generational** rather than **individual and documented**:

#### 1. **Geographic Continuity**

- The Overmountain Men came from the Holston and Watauga settlements
- Rocky Mount was located in these same settlements, 10 years later
- The same families who sent men to King's Mountain were present when territorial government arrived

#### 2. **Leadership Continuity**

- John Sevier commanded at King's Mountain (1780); he was Brigadier General of Washington District militia under territorial government (1790s)
- The settler-militia leadership that won at King's Mountain became the political leadership of the Southwest Territory

#### 3. **The Revolution Made the Territory Possible**

- King's Mountain was the turning point of the Southern Campaign
- British defeat at King's Mountain led to eventual American victory
- American victory led to the 1783 Treaty of Paris
- The treaty ceded western lands to the United States
- North Carolina ceded its western lands to the federal government (1789)
- The Southwest Territory was created (1790)
- Rocky Mount became the first capital (1790)

**The narrative logic is clear:** Without King's Mountain, there would be no American West, no Southwest Territory, no Rocky Mount capital.

#### 4. **Generational Memory**

- In 1790, King's Mountain was only 10 years in the past
- Veterans were in their prime (30s-50s)
- The battle would have been living memory for almost everyone in the territory
- Governor Blount was negotiating with Cherokee who remembered American victory (and Cherokee losses) at King's Mountain

### What This Means for Site Interpretation

Rocky Mount can legitimately claim:

- **Geographic heritage** from the Overmountain settlements
- **Political descendance** from the Overmountain leadership
- **Contextual connection** to King's Mountain as the enabling event

Rocky Mount should be cautious about claiming:

- **Specific individuals** at Rocky Mount who fought at King's Mountain (without documentation)
- **Mary Patton's** direct connection to Rocky Mount (none documented)
- **William Cobb's** King's Mountain service (no evidence)

## PART VI: RECOMMENDATIONS FOR EVIDENCE ROOM

### What to Add

1. **John Sevier Military Journal (1780 or 1793)** - Establishes Sevier's voice in the collection, linking King's Mountain to territorial militia

2. **A King's Mountain Veteran Pension Application** - Select one well-documented veteran who lived in Sullivan County in the 1790s; present their full pension narrative

3. **Washington District Militia Muster Roll (1791-1792)** - Shows the militia organization during Rocky Mount capital period; many soldiers likely were King's Mountain veterans

4. **Context Panel: "Ten Years After King's Mountain"** - Interpretive text explaining the connection without overstating documentary evidence

### How to Present the Connection

**Recommended language:**

> "In October 1780, the Overmountain Men marched from these Holston settlements to defeat British forces at King's Mountain, South Carolina. Ten years later, Governor William Blount arrived at the same settlements to establish the Southwest Territory. The men who had fought for independence now built the government that independence made possible.
>
> Many of the territory's residents were King's Mountain veterans. John Sevier, who commanded Overmountain forces at the battle, served as Brigadier General of the Washington District militia under Governor Blount. The skills, networks, and local knowledge that won at King's Mountain became the foundation of territorial governance.
>
> We do not have household records showing which specific individuals visited or lived near Rocky Mount. But the regional connection is clear: this was Overmountain country, and its people carried the memory of their victory into the new American nation they were building."

### What NOT to Claim

- Do not claim William Cobb fought at King's Mountain (no evidence)
- Do not claim Mary Patton visited Rocky Mount (no evidence)
- Do not claim specific King's Mountain veterans lived "at" Rocky Mount (anachronistic - Rocky Mount was a capital building, not a town)
- Do not present oral traditions as documented history without acknowledging their status

## PART VIII: CONCLUSIONS

### What This Research Establishes

1. **Mary Patton is a legitimate subject** for interpretation at Rocky Mount through the lecture series and regional heritage connections, but her story should be presented as oral tradition, not documented history.

2. **William Cobb's King's Mountain service is unverified** - further research may uncover evidence, but currently there is no documentation.

3. **Regional connections are strong** - The Overmountain settlements that produced King's Mountain soldiers became the Southwest Territory that Rocky Mount governed.

4. **Individual connections require more research** - Pension applications, militia rolls, and tax records could identify specific King's Mountain veterans living in the Rocky Mount area during the 1790s.

5. **John Sevier is the clearest link** - His documented role at both King's Mountain (1780) and in territorial government (1790s) provides the strongest narrative bridge.
