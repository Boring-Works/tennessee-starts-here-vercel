## Letter from Governor Blount to Secretary Knox

_June 15, 1791_

Sir,

<passage id="chiefs-assembling">The Cherokee chiefs are assembling at White's Fort for the proposed treaty. Already more than thirty have arrived, including the Hanging Maw, John Watts, and the warrior known as Doublehead. Others are expected daily.</passage>

The negotiations are proceeding with deliberation. <passage id="boundary-discussions">The principal point of contention is the boundary line between the Cherokee lands and the territory of the United States. The chiefs are naturally reluctant to cede lands which they have long occupied, but they appear to recognize that some accommodation must be made.</passage>

I have endeavored to conduct the negotiations with fairness and to impress upon the chiefs that the United States desires their friendship and prosperity. <passage id="friendship-assurances">I have assured them that the President wishes to protect them in the enjoyment of their remaining lands and to promote their civilization and happiness.</passage>

Your obedient servant,

William Blount
