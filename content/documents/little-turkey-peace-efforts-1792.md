## Little Turkey's Peace Efforts

_October 1792_

**Cherokee Perspective:** This document preserves the words of Little Turkey (Kaneeda), principal chief of the Cherokee Upper Towns from 1788 until his death in 1802, as he worked to maintain peace with the United States during a time of crisis.

---

### Background

By October 1792, the Cherokee Nation was deeply divided. The Upper Towns, led by Little Turkey and other peace chiefs, sought to honor the Treaty of Holston. The five Lower Towns, influenced by Spanish agents and angered by continued settler encroachments, were preparing for war. Little Turkey's correspondence reveals a leader navigating between federal demands, settler violence, and his own warriors' anger.

---

## Little Turkey's Warning

<passage id="little-turkey-warning">
Little Turkey sent urgent word to American officials, stating:

"The 5 lower towns will go to war the 8th of this month by themselves; without the consent of the Nation—You may know the good from the bad—do not come to war against the good."

This plea distinguished between the peace faction and war faction, asking the Americans not to retaliate against Cherokee who sought peace.
</passage>

---

## Spanish Influence

<passage id="spanish-influence">
Little Turkey identified external forces driving the conflict:

"The Spaniards have given them ammunition and Guns, Hatchets Knives &ca—and told them not to go to war, but to keep them in reserve by them. You may blame no body for all this but the Spaniards."

This accusation placed responsibility for Cherokee militancy on Spanish agents arming and influencing the Lower Towns, rather than on Cherokee grievances alone.
</passage>

---

## Personal Danger

<passage id="personal-danger">
Little Turkey revealed the personal risks he faced for advocating peace:

"We have done all in our power to keep the lower towns from War—but they threaten us if we write or give any information to strip & perhaps kill us."

The peace chiefs risked retaliation from their own people for maintaining correspondence with American officials.
</passage>

---

## Intertribal Diplomacy

<passage id="intertribal-diplomacy">
Despite the crisis, Little Turkey pursued broader peace through Native diplomacy:

"The Little Turkey has sent to the Chickasaws and Choctaws, and has gone to the Creeks himself, to let these nations know that he was going to Philadelphia this Fall, that a head-man from each nation might accompany him, that the whole might agree on one thing, and all be at peace."

This remarkable effort sought to unite southeastern Native nations in a coordinated approach to peace with the United States.
</passage>

---

## Other Cherokee Peace Voices

### The Bloody Fellow and Glass

Bloody Fellow (Nenetooyah) and Glass worked to prevent war, but faced challenges. Glass reported that threatening messages from American General Robertson had provoked young warriors: "Their Young men had determined to strike."

### Hanging Maw

Hanging Maw and other Upper Towns chiefs remained "disposed to Peace," maintaining the position established at the Treaty of Holston.

---

## Historical Context

### Little Turkey's Leadership

Little Turkey was elected principal chief of the Cherokee in June 1788 after the murder of Old Tassel by American settlers. His entire tenure was marked by efforts to maintain peace while his people suffered from encroachments on treaty-guaranteed lands.

### Cherokee Internal Politics

The documents reveal a nation divided:

- **Upper Towns** (peace faction): Led by Little Turkey, Hanging Maw, Bloody Fellow
- **Lower Towns** (war faction): Led by John Watts, Dragging Canoe's successors, Spanish-allied warriors

### The Outcome

Despite Little Turkey's efforts, violence continued. The Lower Towns launched attacks on Cumberland settlements, and American settlers retaliated against Cherokee villages indiscriminately, including attacks on peace chiefs.

---

## Legacy

A Cherokee National Council statement from 1810 later reflected on this period:

"Our former treaties were concluded and confirmed by your beloved President General Washington and Our beloved Man the Little Turkey, they were both sincere in their engagements."

This tribute honored Little Turkey's commitment to peace and treaty obligations.
