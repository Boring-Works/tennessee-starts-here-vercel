<passage id="peace-observed">
Peace has been faithfully observed by my Nation since the conference of 1795.
</passage>

<passage id="boundary-demand">
I would insist on a formal boundary to be surveyed between the Cherokee Nation and the United States, following the line established with Governor Blount. The boundary must run from the Tennessee River to the Cumberland River, and I insist that no person should be appointed by the United States who is a Speculator.
</passage>

<passage id="cherokee-oversight">
Cherokee representatives—Wiley Elder, Ohadlokee, and myself—must be present during the running of this boundary line to verify its accuracy.
</passage>

<passage id="survey-timing">
The survey should commence just as the Trees begin to put out their Buds, when visibility is optimal for marking the line.
</passage>

<passage id="unpunished-violence">
I must express concern that murders of Indians by whites have gone unpunished, which has greatly weakened my belief in such promises from the United States government.
</passage>

## Historical Context

On December 22, 1796, Cherokee leader John Watts (Kunoskeskie) delivered this speech to President George Washington and Secretary of War James McHenry. The speech came five years after the signing of the Treaty of Holston (1791) and demonstrates Cherokee efforts to hold the United States government accountable to treaty terms.

### Key Details

- **Speaker:** John Watts (Kunoskeskie), head of war council, later principal chief
- **Date:** December 22, 1796
- **Audience:** President George Washington and Secretary of War James McHenry
- **Location:** Likely delivered in Philadelphia or transmitted through official channels
- **Context:** Five years after Treaty of Holston, ongoing settler encroachment issues

### Significance

This speech is remarkable for several reasons:

**1. Cherokee Agency and Sovereignty**
Watts speaks as a representative of a sovereign nation demanding treaty compliance, not as a subordinate requesting favors. His use of "I would insist" and specific technical requirements demonstrates Cherokee understanding of diplomatic protocols and legal obligations.

**2. Technical Knowledge**
Watts displays sophisticated knowledge of surveying practices:

- He specifies the optimal season for boundary surveys (when trees bud for visibility)
- He demands Cherokee representatives be present during the survey
- He identifies the precise geographic points of the boundary line
- He insists on a surveyor "of integrity" rather than a land speculator

**3. Accountability Demands**
The speech documents Cherokee tracking of U.S. violations:

- Five years after the treaty, the boundary line had not been properly surveyed
- White settlers continued to encroach on Cherokee territory
- Murders of Cherokee people by settlers went unpunished
- The federal government had failed to enforce its own treaty terms

**4. Diplomatic Strategy**
Watts carefully balances firmness with diplomatic protocol. He acknowledges peace has been "faithfully observed by my Nation" while noting American failures. This positions Cherokee as the reliable party and U.S. as the violator.

### About John Watts

John Watts (Kunoskeskie, also known as Young Tassel) was one of the most important Cherokee leaders of his era. He signed the Treaty of Holston in 1791 and became a key figure in Cherokee-U.S. relations during the 1790s. Initially a war leader, Watts later became a principal advocate for negotiated peace while insisting on Cherokee sovereignty and treaty compliance.

His 1796 speech shows the evolution from military resistance to diplomatic pressure—using the treaty itself as leverage to demand U.S. accountability.

## Translation and Interpretation Notes

**Language:** This speech was delivered through interpreters, as written Cherokee syllabary (developed by Sequoyah) did not yet exist in 1796. The English text represents translation of Watts's words, likely translated by James Carey, John Thompson, or another official interpreter of the period.

**Documentation:** The speech was recorded in official War Department correspondence, suggesting U.S. officials recognized it as significant diplomatic communication requiring preservation.

**Cultural Context:** Cherokee diplomatic speeches of this era followed specific protocols and formulas. Watts's direct demands would have been understood by both Cherokee and American audiences as serious assertions of national sovereignty.

## Connection to Rocky Mount

This speech references the boundary line "established with Governor Blount"—the same William Blount who planned treaty negotiations from Rocky Mount (his headquarters) and concluded them at White's Fort (Knoxville) in July 1791. John Watts was one of the 42 Cherokee leaders who signed that treaty.

Five years later, Watts's speech demonstrates that Cherokee leaders:

- Remembered the specific terms negotiated with Blount in 1791
- Tracked whether the United States honored those terms
- Used diplomatic channels to demand compliance
- Possessed the technical knowledge to specify enforcement mechanisms

This document shows Cherokee not as passive recipients of American policy but as active diplomatic agents holding the United States accountable to its promises.
