# Treaty of Holston

The Treaty of Holston was negotiated by William Blount, Governor of the Territory South of the River Ohio, representing the United States, and Cherokee Chiefs and Warriors representing their nation. Signed July 2, 1791.

## Preamble

The treaty aimed to establish permanent peace and remove causes of war through boundary clarification and friendly arrangements between the United States and the Cherokee Nation.

---

## Articles

<passage id="article-1-peace">

**Article I** — Perpetual peace and friendship shall exist between all the citizens of the United States of America and all the individuals composing the Cherokee Nation.

</passage>

<passage id="article-2-protection">

**Article II** — The Cherokee Nation acknowledges to be under the protection of the United States of America, and of no other sovereign; and they also stipulate that the said Cherokee Nation will not hold any treaty with any foreign power, individual state, or with individuals of any state.

</passage>

**Article III** — The Cherokee Nation shall deliver all prisoners taken from American citizens, at the treaty ground, on or before the first day of April next.

<passage id="article-4-boundary">

**Article IV** — The boundary between the United States and the Cherokee Nation shall be as follows: Beginning at the top of the Currahee mountain, where the Creek line passes it; thence a direct line to Tugelo river; thence northeast to the Occunna mountain, and over the same along the South-Carolina Indian boundary to the North-Carolina boundary; thence north to a point from which a line is to be extended to the river Clinch, that shall pass the Holston at the ridge which divides the waters running into Little River from those running into the Tennessee; thence up the river Clinch to Campbell's line, and along the same to the top of Cumberland mountain; thence a direct line to the Cumberland river where the Kentucky road crosses it; thence down the Cumberland river to a point from which a southwest line will strike the ridge which divides the waters of Cumberland from those of Duck river, forty miles above Nashville; thence down the said ridge to a point from whence a southwest line will strike the mouth of Duck river.

In consideration of which cession, the United States will pay to the Cherokee Nation, goods to the value of one thousand dollars, and will pay annually the sum of one thousand dollars.

</passage>

**Article V** — Citizens of the United States shall have free passage through the Cherokee country on the road leading from the Washington district to the Mero district.

**Article VI** — The United States shall have the exclusive right to regulate trade with the Cherokee Nation.

<passage id="article-7-guarantee">

**Article VII** — The United States solemnly guarantee to the Cherokee Nation all their lands not hereby ceded.

</passage>

<passage id="article-8-settlers">

**Article VIII** — Citizens of the United States settling on Cherokee lands shall forfeit the protection of the United States, and the Cherokee may punish them as they see fit.

</passage>

**Article IX** — No citizen of the United States shall hunt on Cherokee lands without a passport from the Cherokee Nation.

**Article X** — If any Cherokee shall commit a capital crime against a citizen of the United States, the Cherokee Nation shall deliver such offender to be punished according to the laws of the United States.

**Article XI** — If any citizen of the United States shall commit crimes against any Cherokee, such offender shall be punished in the same manner as if the crime had been committed against a citizen of the United States.

**Article XII** — In case of violence on either side, the party injured shall seek satisfaction through proper channels before retaliation.

**Article XIII** — The Cherokee shall give notice to the United States of any designs against the peace of the United States.

<passage id="article-14-implements">

**Article XIV** — The United States shall furnish the Cherokee Nation with useful implements of husbandry and shall appoint not more than four interpreters.

</passage>

**Article XV** — All animosities for past grievances shall cease, and the contracting parties will carry this treaty into full execution with good faith.

**Article XVI** — This treaty shall take effect when ratified by the President of the United States, with the advice and consent of the Senate.

---

## Additional Article (February 17, 1792)

The annual payment to the Cherokee Nation is increased from one thousand dollars to fifteen hundred dollars. Signed by Secretary of War Henry Knox and six Cherokee representatives in Philadelphia.

---

<passage id="signatories">

## Cherokee Signatories

The following Cherokee chiefs and warriors signed the Treaty of Holston:

- **Chuleoah** (The Boots)
- **Squollecuttah** (Hanging Maw)
- **Enoleh** (Black Fox)
- **Kanetetoka** (Standing Turkey)
- **Kunoskeskie** (John Watts)
- **Nenetooyah** (Bloody Fellow)
- **Chuquilatague** (Doublehead)

Plus approximately 35 additional named signatories.

**Interpreters:** John Thompson and James Carey

</passage>

---

## United States Representative

**William Blount**, Governor of the Territory South of the River Ohio, signed on behalf of the United States.
