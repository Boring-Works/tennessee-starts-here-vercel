## Cherokee Delegation to Philadelphia

_January 1792_

**Cherokee Perspective:** This document records Cherokee leaders' objections to the Treaty of Holston, presenting their direct concerns to the federal government.

---

### Background

Following the ratification of the Treaty of Holston on November 11, 1791, a delegation of five Cherokee chiefs traveled by ship from Charleston to Philadelphia. They met with Secretary of War Henry Knox on January 7, 9, and 11, 1792, to present objections to the treaty and demand increased annuity.

### The Delegation

The Cherokee delegation consisted of five chiefs:

1. **Nenetooyah (Bloody Fellow)** - delegation leader
2. **Chutloh (Kingfisher)**
3. **Nontuaka (the Northward)**
4. **Teesteke (the Disturber)**
5. **Katigoslah (the Prince)**

### Meetings with Secretary Knox

The delegation met with Secretary of War Henry Knox on January 7, 9, and 11, 1792, presenting their grievances and requests.

---

## Cherokee Demands

<passage id="cherokee-demands">
The Cherokee delegation presented six principal demands to Secretary Knox:

1. **Increased Compensation** - Higher payment for lands ceded under the July 2, 1791 treaty, specifically raising the annuity from $1,000 to $1,500 as originally discussed with Governor Blount

2. **Removal of Encroachers** - Removal of white settlers south of the ridge dividing the waters of the Tennessee River from those of the Little River

3. **Federal Representative** - Appointment of a government representative to reside permanently with the Cherokee Nation

4. **Prevention of Settlement** - Blocking the Tennessee Company's planned settlement at Muscle Shoals

5. **Agricultural Implements** - Delivery of the annual goods plus farming tools as promised in the treaty

6. **Interpreters** - Appointment of trusted interpreters, including George Miller
   </passage>

---

## The Annuity Dispute

<passage id="annuity-dispute">
Knox's report to Washington noted: "It is to be understood that the sum of fifteen hundred Dollars was urged by the Cherokees at the Treaty, but Governor Blount informed them that he was limited and therefore could not promise it, but that he would write relatively to it."

This recorded that the Cherokee delegation had requested $1,500 during the original treaty negotiations, but Blount had claimed he lacked authority to grant it. The Cherokee viewed this as an unfulfilled promise.
</passage>

---

## Resolution

President Washington submitted the Cherokee requests to the Senate on January 18, 1792. The Senate approved the annuity increase two days later, on January 20, 1792. The formal Additional Article to the Treaty of Holston was signed on February 17, 1792, increasing the annual payment to $1,500.

### What This Document Shows

This episode demonstrates that Cherokee leaders actively engaged with the federal government to advocate for their nation's interests. Rather than passive recipients of treaty terms, the Cherokee delegation traveled to Philadelphia, presented detailed demands, and successfully secured treaty amendments.
