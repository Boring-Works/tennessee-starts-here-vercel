# William Blount During the Revolutionary War (1776-1783)

**Research Report: William Blount's Activities During the American Revolution**
**Focus Period:** 1776-1783, with special attention to 1780
**Compiled:** January 30, 2026

---

## Executive Summary

William Blount (1749-1800) served the Patriot cause during the Revolutionary War primarily in administrative and financial roles rather than as a combat officer. His most significant position was as paymaster for North Carolina forces, where he was responsible for recruiting, supplying, and paying troops. In 1780, he was present at two major military disasters—the defense of Charleston and the Battle of Camden—and lost $300,000 in Continental currency during the latter. That same year, he entered politics, winning election to the North Carolina House of Commons from New Bern, where he would serve as a legislator through 1784 and help shape the future of Tennessee through land legislation.

## Timeline: William Blount's Revolutionary War Service

### 1776: Beginning of Service

**April 1776**

- Appointed paymaster for the New Bern District Brigade of the North Carolina militia

**December 1776**

- Appointed paymaster of the 3rd North Carolina Regiment
- Note: A regimental paymaster was not a commissioned officer with command responsibility on the battlefield; Blount served under a warrant on the regimental staff and drew the same pay and allowances as a captain

### 1777: With Washington's Army

**Early 1777**

- Spent the first few months with the 3rd North Carolina Regiment as it marched north to join George Washington's forces in the defense of Philadelphia
- Served in administrative capacity coordinating pay and supplies

**November 1777**

- Removed from his position as paymaster by political rivals in the North Carolina legislature
- Returned to North Carolina

### 1778: Restoration and Expanded Role

**April 1778**

- Restored to the office of paymaster by the North Carolina legislature

**1778-1780**

- Became chief paymaster of state forces
- Later appointed deputy paymaster general for North Carolina
- Spent three years intimately involved in the demanding task of recruiting and reequipping forces
- The Blount family (William and his brothers) provided provisions for the Continental Army and militias, profiting both financially and politically from the war

### 1780: The Critical Year

#### Early 1780: Charleston Defense

**Spring 1780**

- William helped organize regiments for the defense of Charleston, South Carolina
- His younger brother Thomas Blount was captured during the Siege of Charleston (city fell to British on May 12, 1780)
- Brothers Reading and Thomas Blount both accepted commissions in the Continental Army

#### Spring 1780: Appointment to Gates's Staff

**Early 1780**

- Appointed official commissary to General Horatio Gates, who had arrived in North Carolina to command Patriot forces in the South
- Served as paymaster general for Gates's troops

#### August 16, 1780: Battle of Camden

**The Disaster:**

- Blount was present at Gates's catastrophic defeat at the Battle of Camden
- General Horatio Gates hastily engaged British forces under Charles Cornwallis
- Due largely to the untrained nature of the North Carolinian militia, American forces were decimated in less than one hour
- In the confusion of battle, Blount lost $300,000 of paper dollar money intended to be used to pay Patriot soldiers

**Aftermath:**

- Blount apparently escaped injury but left the army after this experience
- The loss of $300,000 would haunt his financial records for years

#### October 7, 1780: Gates Letter

**Primary Source Document:**

- General Horatio Gates wrote to William Blount approximately 60 days after the Battle of Camden
- The letter addressed the missing $300,000 in paper money that Blount was responsible for during the chaotic retreat
- Original document exists but remains only partially transcribed due to 18th-century cursive handwriting challenges

#### Late 1780: Entry into Politics

**Late 1779 (Context):**

- Blount first ran for the vacant New Bern state House of Commons seat against Richard Dobbs Spaight in what was described as a "violent" campaign
- Spaight won by a narrow margin
- Blount successfully convinced election officials that voter fraud had occurred, and the election was voided

**Fall 1780:**

- In the weeks following the Battle of Camden, Blount ran again for the New Bern House of Commons seat
- This time he was successful
- At age 31, Blount entered politics as New Bern's representative (Craven County) to the North Carolina House of Commons

### 1781-1783: Legislative Service

**January 1781**

- Took his seat in the North Carolina House of Commons
- Served in the lower house continuously from 1780-1784, including service as speaker

**1782**

- Elected as one of North Carolina's four delegates to the Continental Congress (also served 1783, 1784, and later 1786-87)

**1782 Congressional Session:**

- Helped defeat a poll tax and a liquor tax
- Opposed a reduction of the army
- Agreed to consider a land cession act to satisfy North Carolina's massive tax debt owed to the Confederation

**January 1783**

- Left Philadelphia

**April 1783**

- Resigned from Continental Congress to accept appointment to the North Carolina House of Commons steering committee

**1783-1784 Legislative Sessions:**

- Introduced several bills that would prove critical in the early history of what is now Tennessee

**Key Bills:**

1. **The "Land Grab Act"**
   - Opened the state's lands west of the Appalachians (i.e., the parts of Tennessee not under Indian domain) to settlement
   - From late 1783 until the cession of western lands by North Carolina in 1784, roughly four million acres of land was sold
   - Blount's personal interest: Believed the future of the new nation lay beyond the Appalachian Mountains and was an aggressive land speculator himself

2. **Military Land Grants**
   - Rendered soldiers with at least two years of military service eligible for land grants
   - Many soldiers sold their grants to the Blounts and other land speculators

3. **Nashville Establishment**
   - In 1784, sponsored a bill establishing the city of Nashville in what was then the Cumberland settlements

4. **Land Cession Act**
   - June 2, 1784: North Carolina legislature passed an act for the cession of its western lands subject to conditions that North Carolina should satisfy her soldiers' claims, that all entries to land she had authorized should be recognized, and that a state or states should be formed from the territory

**1783: The Muscle Shoals Speculation**

- Six North Carolinians formed a company for the purpose of establishing a colony at Muscle Shoals on the Tennessee River
- William Blount served as delegate from North Carolina in Congress among the company members
- This was one of his earliest ventures into Trans-Appalachian land speculation

## Question: Where Was Blount in 1780?

**January-May 1780:**

- In North Carolina and South Carolina, helping organize regiments for the defense of Charleston
- Serving as commissary/paymaster preparing for Gates's southern campaign

**May 12, 1780:**

- Charleston fell to the British (his brother Thomas was captured)

**Late Spring/Summer 1780:**

- Attached to General Horatio Gates's staff as official commissary and paymaster general
- Moving with Gates's army through North Carolina

**August 16, 1780:**

- Present at the Battle of Camden, South Carolina (disastrous American defeat)
- Lost $300,000 in Continental currency during the chaotic retreat

**Fall 1780:**

- Left the army after the Camden disaster
- Returned to New Bern, North Carolina
- Ran for and won election to the North Carolina House of Commons representing New Bern (Craven County)

**Late 1780-January 1781:**

- Preparing to take his seat in the legislature
- Dealing with the financial aftermath of the lost $300,000

## Question: His Role in NC Legislature During Revolution

**1780-1784: North Carolina House of Commons (Lower House)**

- Served continuously from 1780-1784
- Served as speaker at some point during this period
- Initially represented New Bern (Craven County)

**Legislative Focus:**

1. **Western Land Policy**
   - Pressed fellow legislators to open lands west of the Appalachians for settlement
   - This was likely prompted by his personal interest in acquiring land there

2. **Military and Veteran Affairs**
   - Having served as paymaster, understood the needs of soldiers
   - Advocated for land grants for veterans with at least two years of service

3. **Tax Policy (1782 in Continental Congress)**
   - Helped defeat a poll tax
   - Helped defeat a liquor tax
   - Opposed reduction of the army

4. **Land Cession and State Finances**
   - Worked on land cession acts to satisfy North Carolina's massive tax debt to the Confederation
   - Helped establish framework for Tennessee statehood

**Style and Reputation:**

- Described by contemporaries as "plain, honest, and sincere"
- Not particularly vocal in debates
- More of a behind-the-scenes operator and coalition-builder

## Question: Did He Support King's Mountain Campaign?

**Short Answer:** No direct evidence found that William Blount participated in or directly supported the King's Mountain campaign.

**Context:**

- The Battle of King's Mountain occurred on October 7, 1780
- This was a decisive Patriot victory in the Southern Campaign
- Key leaders included William Campbell, Benjamin Cleveland, James Johnston, John Sevier, Joseph McDowell, and Isaac Shelby
- Major Patrick Ferguson led the Loyalist forces

**Blount's Timeline in Fall 1780:**

- August 16: Present at Battle of Camden (disaster)
- Late August-September: Left the army, returned to New Bern
- October 7: King's Mountain battle occurs (Blount not mentioned in any accounts)
- October 7: Horatio Gates writes letter to Blount about the lost $300,000
- Fall 1780: Blount running for House of Commons seat

**Analysis:**

- Blount had left military service after Camden
- He was back in New Bern dealing with political matters
- King's Mountain was fought by frontier militias from the west (over-mountain men)
- Blount was an eastern North Carolina figure at this time
- No primary sources connect him to the campaign

**Possible Confusion:**

- William Campbell (not William Blount) was a prominent leader at King's Mountain
- This may have led to confusion in some accounts

## Primary Source Documents from Revolutionary Period

### Documents Located (Partial Transcriptions or References)

1. **October 7, 1780: Letter from Horatio Gates to William Blount**
   - Subject: The $300,000 in paper money lost at Camden
   - Date: Approximately 60 days after the Battle of Camden
   - Location: Exists as scanned image, only partially transcribed
   - Source: History Heights & Maestro Heights (Gail Masinda blog)
   - Status: Ink bleeds between pages, period penmanship conventions complicate modern reading

2. **May 11, 1785: Financial Settlement Document**
   - Signers: Richard Caswell (as NC State Comptroller) and William Blount
   - Subject: Certifies that William Blount, late paymaster-general of Militia of North Carolina, exhibited his account whereby it appears he charged for £300,000 paper dollar money lost on August 16, 1780
   - Status: Document resolves and settles the issue with payment ultimately due and paid to William Blount by the State
   - Location: Has been sold at auction through Scott Winslow Associates and Early American History Auctions
   - Significance: Primary source document proving the financial accountability for Camden losses

### Document Collections (Not Fully Digitized)

3. **John Gray Blount Papers**
   - Location: 3 volumes, published by North Carolina State Department of Archives and History, 1952-1965
   - Content: Correspondence, business and financial interests of John Gray Blount and other members of the Blount family
   - Focus: Ships, shipping, naval stores, land transactions
   - Revolutionary War Content: Some correspondence from 1776-1783 period, though collection primarily focuses on later events

4. **Colonial and State Records of North Carolina**
   - Location: "Documenting the American South," University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill
   - Content: "CSR Documents by Blount, Reading, 1757-1807" (includes William Blount materials)
   - Volume 16: Contains "Roster of the continental line from North Carolina, 1783" on pages [1002]-1197
   - Access: Digital collection at UNC Chapel Hill

5. **Letters of Governor William Blount**
   - Editor: Philip M. Hamer
   - Publication: East Tennessee Historical Society's Publications 4 (January 1932): 122-37
   - Coverage: Primarily post-Revolutionary period, but establishes writing style and concerns

6. **Library of Congress Collections**
   - William Blount Papers: 1783-1823, 1 container
   - U.S. Army Pay Department records: 1791-1870 (contains some Blount correspondence)
   - Documents from the Continental Congress and the Constitutional Convention: 1774-1789

7. **North Carolina State Archives**
   - Military Collection: War of the Revolution, miscellaneous papers from 1776-1789
   - North Carolina Revolutionary War Soldiers: 1776-1783 (approximately 36,000 men from NC served)
   - Sources include: NC Daughters of the American Revolution, U.S. War Department Report of Pensions, Pierce's Register, State Records

### Documents Needed But Not Yet Located Online

- Paymaster records from 3rd North Carolina Regiment (1776-1777)
- Commissary records from Gates's southern campaign (spring-summer 1780)
- North Carolina House of Commons journals and bills from 1780-1784 sessions (specific Blount bills)
- Personal correspondence from 1776-1783 period
- Land transaction records from 1783-1784 "Land Grab Act" period

## The Blount Family's Revolutionary War Involvement

**Jacob Blount (Father)**

- Wealthy landowner and land speculator in eastern North Carolina
- Owned Blount Hall in what became Pitt County
- His land acquisitions (several thousand acres by end of 1760s) taught William and his sons the profit potential of aggressive land speculation

**William Blount (1749-1800)**

- Paymaster and commissary roles
- Political leadership in legislature and Continental Congress

**Reading Blount (Brother)**

- Accepted commission in the Continental Army
- Served in combat roles

**Thomas Blount (Younger Brother)**

- Accepted commission in the Continental Army
- Captured during Siege of Charleston (May 12, 1780)

**John Gray Blount (Brother, 1752-?)**

- Merchant and business partner
- Post-war, formed highly successful merchant business with brothers William and Thomas, based in Washington, North Carolina
- The Blount Brothers mercantile business was one of the largest in North Carolina

**Family Business Strategy:**

- Provided provisions for the Continental Army and militias
- Profited both financially and politically from the war
- Used military connections and land grants to amass Trans-Appalachian holdings
- Post-war business included ships, shipping, naval stores, and extensive land transactions

## What This Research Reveals About Blount Before Governorship

### Character and Capabilities

1. **Administrative Skills**
   - Successfully managed complex financial and logistical operations
   - Coordinated pay and supplies for entire regiments
   - Handled large sums of money ($300,000 loss shows the scale of his responsibilities)

2. **Political Acumen**
   - Turned military service into political career
   - Built coalitions in legislature
   - Understood how to leverage land policy for political and personal gain

3. **Resilience Under Pressure**
   - Survived removal from office and was restored
   - Emerged from Camden disaster (both military and financial) to win election
   - Eventually got the state to settle his $300,000 claim (1785 document)

4. **Vision for Western Expansion**
   - Even during the Revolution, thinking about Trans-Appalachian opportunities
   - By 1783, actively speculating in western lands
   - Saw Tennessee's potential before most eastern politicians

### Financial Situation

1. **Comfortable But Not Wealthy (Yet)**
   - Paymaster salary was equivalent to a captain's pay
   - Family was providing war supplies (profitable)
   - Not yet the major land baron he would become

2. **Understanding of Money and Credit**
   - Handled both Continental currency and specie
   - Understood inflation and depreciation (critical during Revolutionary War)
   - Knew how to work within government financial systems

3. **Building Wealth Through Land**
   - 1783-1784 legislation opened floodgates for land speculation
   - Personal and family holdings grew dramatically in this period
   - Acquired "millions of acres in Tennessee and the Trans-Appalachian West" over time

### Network and Connections

1. **Military Connections**
   - Worked directly with General Horatio Gates
   - Knew General Richard Caswell (who would later settle his accounts)
   - Provided supplies to Continental Army and state militias

2. **Political Network**
   - From eastern North Carolina (New Bern/Craven County)
   - Built relationships in state legislature 1780-1784
   - Served in Continental Congress 1782-1783 (and later)
   - Understood both state and national politics

3. **Business Network**
   - Blount Brothers merchant business (with Thomas and John Gray)
   - Ships, shipping, naval stores
   - Land speculation partnerships (Muscle Shoals Company, 1783)

### Preparation for Governorship

**When Washington appointed Blount governor of the Southwest Territory in 1790:**

1. **He had 10 years of legislative experience** (1780-1790)
2. **He had intimate knowledge of western land issues** (had been speculating since 1783)
3. **He understood frontier military matters** (from his paymaster experience)
4. **He had proven administrative capabilities** (managing complex financial operations during war)
5. **He had national connections** (Continental Congress service)
6. **He knew how to navigate state-federal relations** (land cessions, state debt issues)

**Most importantly:** By 1790, Blount was one of the largest landowners in what would become Tennessee. He had personal financial stakes in the territory's success, which made him both motivated and knowledgeable—though it would also lead to his later downfall through the "Blount Conspiracy."

## Gaps in the Historical Record

### What We Still Don't Know

1. **Personal Correspondence from 1776-1783**
   - Few letters survive from this period
   - Most Blount Papers collections focus on 1783 and later
   - Personal views on independence, the war, military strategy largely unknown

2. **Daily Activities as Paymaster**
   - How did he recruit soldiers?
   - What were his supply chain methods?
   - Who were his subordinates and assistants?

3. **Details of Camden Aftermath**
   - How did he escape?
   - What happened to the $300,000 immediately after the battle?
   - How did he explain the loss to Gates and to North Carolina authorities?

4. **Legislative Speeches and Debates**
   - What arguments did he make for the Land Grab Act?
   - How did he respond to opponents?
   - What was his speaking style?

5. **Early Land Speculation Details**
   - Exactly how much land did he acquire in 1783-1784?
   - What were his partnership arrangements?
   - How much did he profit?

6. **Relationship with Continental Congress**
   - Why was he chosen as a delegate in 1782?
   - What committees did he serve on?
   - What was his reputation among other delegates?

## Conclusion

William Blount's Revolutionary War service was primarily administrative and political rather than military in the traditional sense. As paymaster and commissary, he handled the unglamorous but critical work of keeping troops paid, fed, and equipped. His presence at two major disasters—Charleston and Camden—in 1780, and his loss of $300,000 at the latter, marked a turning point. Rather than destroying his career, these events propelled him into politics.

In 1780, at age 31, Blount began his transformation from military paymaster to political leader. His election to the North Carolina House of Commons that year gave him the platform to pursue his vision of western expansion. The legislation he championed in 1783-1784—opening western lands, granting soldiers land rights, establishing Nashville—laid the foundation for Tennessee statehood and for his own career as territorial governor.

By the time Washington appointed him governor of the Southwest Territory in 1790, Blount had:

- 10 years of legislative experience
- 7 years of aggressive land speculation
- Deep knowledge of frontier issues
- Proven administrative skills
- National political connections

The Revolutionary War didn't make William Blount a military hero, but it made him something perhaps more valuable: an experienced administrator with the political skills and land holdings to become Tennessee's founding father.

**What this research shows most clearly:** In 1780, William Blount stood at the crossroads of military disaster and political opportunity. He chose politics and western land speculation—a choice that would shape Tennessee history.
