# Evidence Room: Diversity, Inclusion & Underrepresented Voices Review

**Review Date:** January 30, 2026
**Reviewer:** Documentation Project Team
**Status:** Comprehensive gap analysis and strategic recommendations
**Mission:** Make the Evidence Room a more complete and honest account of Tennessee's territorial founding

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Evidence Room, while academically rigorous, tells a fundamentally incomplete story: **the voices documented are overwhelmingly elite, white, and male.**

Current representation:

- ✓ 7 white male officials (Blount, Washington, Knox, Jefferson, Jackson, Robertson, Sevier)
- ✓ 5 Cherokee male signatories (featured in Treaty of Holston)
- ✗ 0 women's voices
- ✗ 0 enslaved people's voices (only architectural evidence)
- ✗ 0 working-class settler voices
- ✗ 0 voices from free Black people
- ✗ 0 voices from indentured servants or laborers
- ✗ 0 voices from religious minorities
- ✗ 0 voices from Scots-Irish cultural communities
- ✗ 0 voices from non-elite Cherokee (only signatories, not ordinary people)

**Critical Gap:** The current collection documents **who held power**, not **who built Tennessee**.

This review:

1. **Identifies specific representation gaps** with research showing what sources exist
2. **Proposes concrete additions** to center marginalized voices
3. **Recommends language improvements** to acknowledge absences honestly
4. **Outlines editorial practices** to prevent further erasure
5. **Identifies partnership opportunities** with underrepresented communities

## PART I: REPRESENTATION GAPS ANALYSIS

### Gap 1: Women's Voices (Erasure Rate: ~100%)

**Current Evidence Room Count:** 0 women with documented voices

**Who Should Be There:**

#### Elite Women

- **Barsheba Cobb (Wife of William Cobb, Rocky Mount proprietor)**
  - Operated kitchen feeding 42 Cherokee chiefs (July 1791)
  - Managed household during territorial capital period (1790-1792)
  - **Documentary Status:** Zero first-person documents
  - **Why:** Elite women's correspondence was often destroyed as "unseemly" or not preserved by archives
  - **What exists:** Indirect evidence—kitchen inventory, Blount letters describing hospitality

- **Mary Blount (Wife of Governor Blount)**
  - Likely managed diplomatic entertaining and household affairs
  - **Documentary Status:** Zero documents (confirmed by researcher)
  - **Why:** Standard for elite women of the period—documents rarely survived

#### Working-Class & Frontier Women

- **Unnamed women in settler families** (estimated 15,000+ from 1791 population)
- **Enslaved women** (estimated 1,500-2,000 in territory)
- **Indentured women servants** (estimated 100-300 individuals)
- **Tavern keepers and traders** (documented in militia records but not named)
- **Cherokee women** (including She Reigns, treaty signatory, and 10,000+ women in Cherokee nation)

**Why the Gap Exists:**

- Frontier women left almost no written records—their labor was essential but invisible
- Elite women's correspondence was deliberately destroyed or not archived
- Archival practices prioritized male-authored political documents
- Census and economic records use "head of household" language, erasing women's economic roles
- Oral histories from women were not systematically collected in the 1790s

**The Historical Reality:**
Women comprised ~50% of the 35,691 territorial population. They:

- Performed agricultural labor, household labor, childcare, food production
- Participated in trade and commerce (some as merchants and tavern operators)
- Had legal status and property rights (albeit limited)
- Possessed knowledge of frontier survival, medicinal plants, food preservation
- Maintained cultural traditions and family history

**Representation Impact:**
When women's voices are absent, the historical record becomes a story of governance divorced from the labor that sustained it. A visitor learns how governors were appointed but not who cooked, cleaned, nursed, farmed, and managed households.

### Gap 2: Enslaved People (Erasure Rate: ~100%)

**Current Evidence Room Count:** 0 enslaved people named

**Documentary Evidence of Presence:**

- Rocky Mount inventory (October 15, 1791): "quarters for domestic servants" documented
- Architectural evidence: separate kitchen building with servant quarters (standard enslaved housing pattern)
- Economic context: William Blount was a significant slaveholder; Rocky Mount's $2,000 valuation (1791) indicates wealth sufficient for enslaved labor
- Population context: 3,417 enslaved people lived in Southwest Territory (1791)—roughly 10% of total population

**Who Should Be There:**

#### At Rocky Mount specifically

- Enslaved people in William Blount's household (numbers unknown)
- Enslaved people in William Cobb's household (numbers unknown)
- Kitchen workers who fed the territorial government (unnamed)

#### Across the territory

- Estimated 3,417 enslaved people total (9.6% of 1791 population)
- Concentrated in wealthy households of settlers like Blount, Robertson, Sevier
- Enslaved laborers on farms and in mills
- Enslaved women (domestic and field labor)
- Enslaved children (born into bondage)

**Why the Gap Exists:**

- **Deliberate erasure:** Enslavers did not document enslaved people's names, voices, or perspectives
- **Missing documentation:** The Blount Papers in Rocky Mount archives contain no references to enslaved workers
- **Archival silence:** No Knoxville Gazette advertisements for slave sales or runaways from this period currently located
- **No wills or inventories:** No estate documents listing enslaved individuals as property
- **No oral history:** Unlike later periods, 1790s Tennessee lacks collected slave narratives

**The Archival Challenge:**
Enslaved people rarely left written documents themselves. Their presence must be recovered through:

1. **Architectural evidence** (quarters, kitchen arrangements)
2. **Economic analysis** (who provided labor for large properties)
3. **Comparative research** (what similar plantations elsewhere used)
4. **Material culture** (artifacts, tools, food remains)
5. **Oral history** (descendants' family traditions)

**The Ethical Imperative:**
The absence of names does not mean absence of people. Rocky Mount's prosperity—the glass windows Blount marveled at, the meals served to officials, the cleared fields—was built on enslaved labor. To ignore this is to misrepresent the site's history entirely.

### Gap 3: Working-Class & Ordinary Settler Voices (Erasure Rate: ~95%)

**Current Evidence Room Count:** 0 ordinary settlers

**Who Should Be There:**

#### Farmers & Agricultural Workers

- 80%+ of the 32,013 free settlers (1791)
- Clearing land, growing crops, raising livestock
- Dealing with Cherokee raids, taxation, land disputes
- Militia service obligations

#### Traders & Merchants

- Licensed traders on Cherokee boundaries
- Tavern operators (documented in militia records)
- Peddlers and craftspeople

#### Common Militia Soldiers

- 14 militia companies documented
- Officers vs. enlisted men representing class divisions
- Service records in Colonel Henley's "Wastebook" (1793-1798)

#### People in Legal Disputes

- Court records: 2,500+ early settlers appear in Supreme Court cases (1791-1820)
- Land disputes, debt, contracts, theft, assault
- Settlers negotiating economic relationships

**Available Documentary Evidence:**

1. **Territorial Legislature Petitions (1790-1796)**
   - Tennessee State Library and Archives (Nashville)
   - Settlers organized and made demands: military protection, tax complaints, accountability
   - Example: August 1794 petition against 25-cent-per-100-acres tax
   - These show ordinary settlers as agents, not passive subjects

2. **Militia Muster Rolls**
   - Colonel David Henley's "Wastebook" (FamilySearch ID: 2245166)
   - Names ordinary soldiers with ranks and service dates
   - Distributed across 14 companies + cavalry troop

3. **Court Records & Testimony**
   - Charles A. Sherrill's _Tennesseans in Court: 2,500 Early Settlers_ (1999)
   - Supreme Court and District Court cases (1790-1820)
   - Shows working-class economic relationships, disputes, occupations

4. **Tax Records & Land Claims**
   - Preemption claims and land disputes
   - Tax rolls showing property valuations and obligations

**Why the Gap Exists:**

- **Archival bias:** Archives preserve official documents (treaties, proclamations) not petitions
- **Narrative focus:** Great Man history emphasizes leaders, not workers
- **Documentary scarcity:** Ordinary people's correspondence rarely survived
- **Institutional neglect:** Regional archives haven't systematically digitized court records or petitions
- **Publication gap:** Available sources (Sherrill's book, militia records) haven't been integrated into narrative history

**The Historical Reality:**
The 1791 territorial population of 35,691 included:

- Small farmers struggling with land preemption claims
- Militia soldiers resenting compulsory service
- Settlers complaining about taxes and demanding government protection
- Traders navigating Cherokee boundary regulations
- People suing over livestock, debts, and land boundaries

**Representation Impact:**
Without working-class voices, the founding narrative becomes: "Great men appointed governors, negotiated treaties, established government." With working-class voices: "Ordinary people petitioned for protection, disputed land claims, organized in militia, negotiated in courts."

### Gap 4: Free Black People (Erasure Rate: ~100%)

**Current Evidence Room Count:** 0

**Who Should Be There:**

#### Free Black Settlers (estimated 361 in 1791)

- Free-born people of color with property and legal status
- Mixed-race individuals with property rights
- Free Black traders and businesspeople
- Free Black militia members (served in militia, though often segregated units)

**Available Documentary Evidence:**

1. **Property Records**
   - Tax rolls showing free Black landowners
   - Land claims and preemption records
   - Deeds (if preserved)

2. **Militia Records**
   - Militia muster rolls noting race of soldiers
   - Some free men of color appear on rosters

3. **Court Records**
   - Free Black people appeared as parties in court cases
   - Marriage records, property disputes, commercial relationships

4. **Cherokee Nation Records**
   - Mixed-race children from Cherokee-settler relationships
   - Some adopted into Cherokee families with citizenship

**Why the Gap Exists:**

- **Racial erasure:** 18th-century records often don't identify free Black people explicitly
- **Deliberate obscuring:** Census and tax data used vague categories like "other"
- **Archival absence:** Few documents created by free Black people from this period
- **Historiographical neglect:** Free Black history of the 1790s Tennessee frontier is understudied

**The Historical Reality:**
Free Black people were economically present and legally documented:

- Owned property and paid taxes
- Participated in militia service
- Engaged in trade and commerce
- Had families and property rights (limited but real)
- Navigated a society increasingly restricting their rights

**Representation Impact:**
Absence of free Black voices suggests Tennessee's founding was purely a white endeavor, erasing the existence of Black agency, property ownership, and participation in early territorial governance.

### Gap 5: Religious Minorities (Erasure Rate: ~100%)

**Current Evidence Room Count:** 0

**Who Should Be There:**

#### Quakers (Society of Friends)

- Active missionary work in Southwest Territory (1780s-1790s)
- Established meetings and communities
- Documented correspondence from Quaker traveling ministers

#### Catholics

- Few but present in mixed-race and immigrant communities
- French connections in lower Holston settlements

#### Baptists (Non-Establishment)

- Growing evangelical presence (distinct from established Anglicanism)
- Camp meeting participants

#### Jews

- Small but documented merchants in Knoxville

#### Unaffiliated/Deist Settlers

- Secular settlers, freethinkers
- Settlers indifferent to religious institutional structures

**Available Documentary Evidence:**

1. **Methodist & Quaker Records**
   - Circuit rider journals documenting missionary work
   - Meeting house records

2. **Knoxville Gazette**
   - Advertisements for various churches and services
   - References to religious divisions and debates

3. **Individual Letters & Journals**
   - Quaker traveling ministers' correspondence
   - Diary entries discussing religious life

**Why the Gap Exists:**

- **Institutional bias:** Archives focus on governmental documents, not religious ones
- **Narrative focus:** Secular territorial governance story excludes religious context
- **Documentary scarcity:** Small religious communities left few formal records

**The Historical Reality:**
Religious diversity existed, even on the frontier:

- Quakers actively proselytized and organized communities
- Religious debate about slavery and Indian relations was emerging
- Various denominations competed for settlers' spiritual allegiance
- Settlers' worldviews were shaped by religious frameworks

**Representation Impact:**
Absence of religious diversity suggests a secular, homogeneous founding, erasing the role of spiritual frameworks and moral debates (especially about slavery and treatment of Native Americans) in shaping territorial governance.

### Gap 6: Scots-Irish & Ethnic Communities (Erasure Rate: ~90%)

**Current Evidence Room Count:** 0 explicit ethnic voices

**Who Should Be There:**

#### Scots-Irish Settlers (majority of settlers)

- Dominant ethnic group, estimated 60-70% of European settlers
- Distinctive culture, religion (Presbyterian), and historical memory (Ulster plantation experience)
- Specific patterns of land claim and settlement

#### German & Swiss Settlers

- Small but significant communities in upper settlements
- Specialized trades and agricultural practices

#### English Settlers

- Class-diverse group from various English regions
- Merchants, laborers, yeomen farmers

#### Mixed-heritage Settlers

- People with Native American ancestry claiming settler identity
- European-African mixed-race individuals

**Available Documentary Evidence:**

1. **Surnames & Genealogical Records**
   - Militia rosters showing naming patterns
   - Court records documenting family relationships

2. **Scots-Irish Histories**
   - Secondary sources documenting settlement patterns
   - Cultural practices (land claims, militia organization)
   - Religious identity (Presbyterian churches)

3. **Migration Records**
   - Port records from Ireland and Scotland
   - Letters from settlers to family members in Europe

4. **Archaeological Evidence**
   - Artifact patterns showing cultural practices
   - Food remains indicating diet patterns

**Why the Gap Exists:**

- **Ethnic erasure:** American founding narrative erases ethnic specificity in favor of generic "American" identity
- **Class blindness:** Scots-Irish settlers had their own class divisions not captured in records
- **Assimilationist history:** Early historians wrote as if settlers quickly became "American" and dropped ethnic identity
- **Documentation focus:** Formal records (treaties, proclamations) don't capture ethnic identity

**The Historical Reality:**
Ethnic identity shaped settlement patterns, institutional choices, and political culture:

- Scots-Irish settlers brought land claim traditions from Ulster
- Presbyterian settlers had different religious and social priorities than Anglicans
- Cultural memory of plantation and migration informed relationship with Native Americans
- Diverse European groups negotiated coexistence and competition

**Representation Impact:**
Absence of ethnic specificity erases the role of cultural identity in shaping territorial society, making it seem like a generic "settlement" rather than a complex cultural encounter with specific historical actors.

### Gap 7: Cherokee Nation Representation (Partial Gap)

**Current Evidence Room Count:** 5 named signatories (Hanging Maw, Bloody Fellow, John Watts, Doublehead, Black Fox)

**What's Missing:**

#### Women in Cherokee Nation

- **She Reigns (identified as treaty signatory)**
  - Documented as signing party to Treaty of Holston
  - Role in Cherokee governance not explained
  - Current treatment: Listed as "signatories" but no individual profile
  - **Gap:** No context about women's political roles in Cherokee matrilineal system

#### Non-Signatory Cherokee Leaders

- Dragging Canoe (deceased before 1791 but major figure)
- Other war chiefs and council members not at treaty table
- Ordinary Cherokee voices

#### Cherokee Perspectives on Treaty

- Why different leaders had different positions
- What Cherokee understood treaty terms to mean
- Subsequent Cherokee interpretation and disputes

#### Cherokee Community Impact

- How treaty affected ordinary Cherokee people
- Cherokee women's responses to land loss
- Cherokee trade relationships post-treaty

**Available Documentary Evidence:**

1. **Treaty of Holston (23-page manuscript)**
   - DigiTreaties has digitized full manuscript
   - Lists all 42 signatories
   - Includes witness signatures and official seals

2. **Cherokee Nation Records**
   - Oral histories and subsequent correspondence
   - Relations with Blount and other officials (1791-1796)
   - Cherokee perspectives on treaty compliance/violation

3. **Comparative Cherokee Materials**
   - Other contemporary treaties with Cherokee (1768, 1785, 1794)
   - Cherokee Nation's diplomatic correspondence

**Why the Gap Exists:**

- **Archival bias:** English-language documents in U.S. archives don't capture Cherokee perspectives
- **Power imbalance:** Treaty documents written and preserved by winning side
- **Oral vs. written:** Cherokee governance and perspectives were oral; written records don't capture them
- **Access:** Cherokee Nation's own records may not be digitized or available

**The Historical Reality:**

1. **Signatories were divided:** Five featured signers represent different factions and interests
2. **Women held power:** She Reigns' signature indicates women's role in Cherokee governance
3. **Treaty was contested:** Cherokee Nation itself disputed interpretation and enforcement
4. **Impacts were severe:** Treaty ceded vast lands, causing internal Cherokee conflict

**Representation Impact:**
Current treatment (names + roles only, no context) treats Cherokee as historical objects signing documents, not as sovereigns making strategic decisions or as a nation internally divided over those decisions.

**Recommendation:** Add individual profiles for:

- Hanging Maw (primary chief, what was his strategy?)
- She Reigns (woman's role in governance, what did she bring to negotiations?)
- Dragging Canoe (why was he fighting—provide context)
- Others as context for understanding Cherokee diplomatic approaches

## PART II: LANGUAGE & FRAMING IMPROVEMENTS

### Current Bias Disclosure Section (page.tsx, lines 647-686)

**Current Language:**

```
The documents here were created by government officials—predominantly white men of
property. They reflect the viewpoints and biases of that group.

Many perspectives are absent: enslaved people who worked this ground, Cherokee
communities whose lands were negotiated away, women whose labor sustained households.
```

**Assessment:** Good start, but vague and incomplete.

### Recommended Improvements

#### 1. Be Specific About Numbers

**Current:** "Many perspectives are absent"
**Improved:** "Of the approximately 35,691 people living in the Southwest Territory in 1791, this collection documents the voices of approximately 7 white men. That means 99.98% of the population's voices are absent."

**Why:** Specific numbers make erasure undeniable.

#### 2. Name the Communities

**Current:** "enslaved people who worked this ground, Cherokee communities whose lands were negotiated away, women"
**Improved:** "Enslaved people (approximately 3,417 individuals in the territory, whose labor built the infrastructure we document). Women (approximately 17,000 people, whose domestic and agricultural labor sustained households and the territorial economy). Working-class settlers (approximately 28,000 farmers, laborers, and traders whose petitions and court cases reveal their economic and political agency). Free Black people (approximately 361 people with property rights and legal status). Religious minorities (Quakers, Baptists, Catholics, and others who shaped moral and political debates). Scots-Irish and other ethnic communities whose cultural practices shaped settlement patterns."

**Why:** Specific names make absences concrete and unjustifiable.

#### 3. Acknowledge What CAN Be Known

**Current:** "Many perspectives are absent"
**Improved:** "While direct testimonies are rare, systematic research reveals evidence of their presence and agency:

- **Enslaved people:** Kitchen quarters at Rocky Mount; economic analysis of labor needs; architectural evidence of specialized buildings
- **Women:** Property inventories; letters by men about women's work; household goods; food remains; architectural evidence of domestic spaces
- **Working-class settlers:** Court records documenting 2,500+ ordinary settlers; militia rosters; territorial legislature petitions; tax records
- **Free Black people:** Property tax rolls; militia records; court appearances
- **Religious minorities:** Circuit rider journals; church records; Knoxville Gazette advertisements

These indirect sources allow us to acknowledge presence even when direct voices are absent."

**Why:** Shows that invisibility is not the same as absence; sources exist, they just require different interpretive frameworks.

#### 4. Revise "Missing Voices" Section Title

**Current:** "Missing Voices"
**Improved:** "Whose Voices This Collection Includes and Excludes—and Why"

**Why:** Clearer about both inclusion and exclusion.

#### 5. Add Commitment Statement

**New Section:**

```
Our Commitment to Representation

This collection is incomplete. As we expand, we commit to:

1. Intentionally seeking evidence of underrepresented communities (petitions, court records, militia rosters, property documents)
2. Interpreting evidence generously—kitchen quarters suggest enslaved labor; property records reveal women's economic roles; militia rosters show ordinary settlers' political participation
3. Using the absence of direct voices as evidence of historical erasure, not evidence of absence
4. Partnering with Cherokee Nation, descendants of enslaved people, and community historians to recover suppressed narratives
5. Acknowledging when we don't know and resisting the urge to fill gaps with assumptions
6. Updating interpretations as new sources are discovered or new scholarship emerges
```

**Why:** Moves from apology to action; shows what will be done about the gaps.

## PART III: INSTITUTIONAL & EDITORIAL PRACTICES

### Practice 1: Document Source Discovery Process

**Current state:** Evidence selected by researcher team; no transparent criteria

**Recommended practice:**

- Publish "Collection Development Policy" explaining:
  - How documents are selected
  - What communities/voices are being actively sought
  - Timelines for adding underrepresented sources
  - Process for community input on what matters

**Example:** "This year we are actively seeking: (1) militant petitions and court records documenting ordinary settlers' voices; (2) archaeological or architectural evidence of enslaved people's presence; (3) Cherokee Nation perspectives on the Treaty of Holston"

### Practice 2: Attribution & Representation Labels

**Current:** Documents labeled by archival ID (MSS.1790.001)
**Recommended additions:**

- Source creator's identity (race, gender, class, relationship to power)
- Whose perspective is represented
- Who is absent from this document

**Example label:**

```
MSS.1790.001 — Blount Correspondence

Written by: William Blount (white male, governor, landowner/enslaver)
Perspective: Colonial administrator
Who speaks: Federal authority, settler governance
Who is absent: Enslaved people (present at Rocky Mount, not speaking)
Women (managing household, not documented)
Ordinary settlers (not consulted)
Cherokee Nation (subject of treaty, not author of documents)
```

### Practice 3: Cross-Referencing Gaps

**Current:** Each document section stands alone
**Recommended:** For each major document, add:

```
What This Document Doesn't Tell Us:
- [specific gap related to underrepresented voice]
- Research opportunities: [where evidence might be found]
- Partnership opportunities: [communities who could add context]
```

**Example (Blount's Glass Windows letter):**

```
What This Document Doesn't Tell Us:
- How the "comfortable accommodations" were achieved
- Who performed the labor (domestic workers, enslaved people, Cobb family members)
- Women's roles in managing hospitality
- What ordinary Rocky Mount residents thought of federal arrival

Research Opportunities:
- Rocky Mount inventory documents "quarters for domestic servants"—research shows enslaved labor likely
- Barsheba Cobb's role can be documented through household records and Blount's descriptions
- Local oral history may preserve community memories

Partnership Opportunities:
- Interview descendants of enslaved people
- Consult with historians of women's domestic labor
- Engage local community members with family histories at Rocky Mount
```

### Practice 4: Temporal Transparency

**Current:** Documents presented as definitive
**Recommended:** Add metadata showing:

- When interpretation was last reviewed
- What new scholarship has emerged since
- How interpretation might change with new evidence

**Example:**

```
Last Reviewed: January 2026
Recent Scholarship:
- 2024: New research on enslaved labor on Tennessee frontier
- 2023: Study of women's economic roles in colonial households
This interpretation will be updated: [date]
```

## PART VI: ADDRESSING THE HARDEST QUESTION

### "How Do We Give Voice to People Who Left No Documents?"

This is the central ethical challenge of inclusive history.

#### Answer 1: Don't Claim Their Words

**What to do:** Never put quotations in the mouth of enslaved people, working-class women, ordinary settlers unless you have their actual words.

**What to do instead:**

- Describe what we know they experienced: "Enslaved people at Rocky Mount worked in the kitchen, cooked meals, cleaned structures"
- Identify what gaps remain: "We know their labor; their names are not preserved"
- Show how to research them: "Court records, militia rosters, property documents can reveal ordinary settlers' agency"
- Invite community input: "Descendants may have family stories we don't"

#### Answer 2: Use Indirect Evidence Generously

**Example 1—Enslaved People:**

- Kitchen quarters document = enslaved people worked there
- Economic analysis = scale of operations required enslaved labor
- Architectural evidence = specific kitchen arrangement suggests enslaved labor
- Interpretation: "These traces prove presence even though names are absent"

**Example 2—Women:**

- Blount's letter describes "comfortable accommodations" = women's labor created them
- Property inventory lists "kitchen building" = someone cooked and cleaned there
- Interpretation: "Barsheba Cobb's labor was essential even though her voice isn't preserved"

**Example 3—Ordinary Settlers:**

- Court cases show farmers suing over livestock = their economic concerns, disputes, relationships
- Militia records list private soldiers = their civic participation, service obligations
- Petitions demand government accountability = their political agency
- Interpretation: "Ordinary settlers negotiated with power, not just submitted to it"

#### Answer 3: Center the Absence

**What NOT to do:** Avoid saying "We don't know—moving on"

**What to do instead:**

- Make the absence the story: "The absence of enslaved people's voices in these documents is itself historical evidence of how slavery worked—through erasure"
- Explain why: "Enslavers deliberately did not document enslaved people's names or perspectives; that silence tells us something important about slavery's logic"
- Show what we can learn from it: "By asking why absence exists, we understand how power worked"

**Example framing:**

```
"The Treaty of Holston was signed by 42 Cherokee leaders. But we have no record of what enslaved people in Rocky Mount thought about this treaty, what women in settler families discussed, what ordinary farmers debated. That silence is not accidental. It tells us whose voices mattered in official history-making—and whose were systematically erased. By acknowledging this gap, we understand power more clearly."
```

#### Answer 4: Partner to Recover Suppressed Voices

**Oral history:** Descendant communities carry family stories
**Religious records:** Church/meeting records document communities
**Material culture:** Artifacts, food, tools tell stories
**Archaeological interpretation:** Dig sites reveal how people lived

**Recognition:** The most authentic voices to recover suppressed narratives are the communities themselves, not outside researchers.

## CONCLUSION: WHO GETS TO BE HISTORY?

The current Evidence Room answers: Government officials, wealthy men, white settlers.

An inclusive Evidence Room would answer: Government officials AND the people whose labor sustained government. Men AND women. Settlers AND the people they governed or enslaved. Documented voices AND the people whose presence we can prove through other means.

This isn't about "political correctness"—it's about truth-telling.

**The historical truth is:**

- Rocky Mount's glass windows were built and maintained by labor (some enslaved, some women's domestic work, some ordinary settlers')
- The Treaty of Holston affected 42 Cherokee leaders AND the 10,000+ people they represented
- The territorial government served white male settlers AND the 28,000+ ordinary settlers whose petitions and court cases show they demanded accountability
- The Southwest Territory included women, enslaved people, free Black people, religious minorities, and ethnic communities—not just elite men

An honest Evidence Room says: Here's what the surviving documents tell us. Here's who speaks in these documents. Here's who's absent and why. Here's where we're looking for their presence. Here's what communities can help us understand.

That's not less rigorous scholarship—it's more honest scholarship.
