# Evidence Room Social Media Strategy

**Project:** Tennessee Starts Here - Evidence Room Promotion
**Objective:** Drive awareness, engagement, and shares of the Evidence Room (digital archive of primary documents)
**Target Audience:** History educators, students, history enthusiasts, heritage tourism, local communities, genealogy researchers, Cherokee Nation members
**Timeline:** Ongoing (with seasonal peaks during school year and summer tourism)
**Platforms:** Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, TikTok

---

## Executive Summary

The Evidence Room is a unique digital resource featuring 40+ primary documents from 1790-1796 that tell the story of Rocky Mount as Tennessee's territorial capital and the Treaty of Holston. This strategy positions the Evidence Room as an engaging, shareable educational resource that drives traffic through storytelling, archival discovery, and community participation.

**Key Insight:** The Evidence Room's real value is in human stories—the people, decisions, and documents that shaped a nation. Every post should answer: "Why does this document matter to someone today?"

## Cherokee Treaty Signers Campaign

**Objective:** Highlight the 42 Cherokee leaders who signed the Treaty of Holston, centering their agency and voices

**Why This Matters:** The Evidence Room currently features many U.S. government documents about Cherokee relations but fewer primary sources by Cherokee leaders themselves. This campaign showcases the Cherokee signatories as decision-makers and negotiators.

### Campaign Structure: "42 Voices"

**Format:** 6-week campaign (repeating 3x/year)

**Week 1:** Introduction & Context

- Post: "Meet the 42 Cherokee leaders who signed the Treaty of Holston"
- Content: Map of Cherokee nations, brief intro to the treaty, why it matters
- Action: Ask followers to nominate which signer they want to learn about first

**Weeks 2-7:** Weekly Signer Profiles

- **Instagram:** Carousel post (name, Cherokee name, role, key fact, signature image, link to full profile)
- **TikTok:** 1-2 minute biography video ("This Cherokee leader refused to back down..." style)
- **Facebook:** Longer biography post (300-400 words) with discussion questions
- **Twitter:** Thread with 5-6 tweets covering their life, role, legacy

**Example Signers to Feature (in order of impact):**

1. **Doublehead** (Thalasawi) - War leader, later executed
2. **John Watts** (Kunoskeskie) - Young Tassel, war council head
3. **Hanging Maw** (Toskabe) - Principal chief, peace faction
4. **Bloody Fellow** (Kanoti) - Peace negotiator
5. **Chineses** (Tlanustiski) - Overhill leader
6. **Will or Corn Tassel** - Historical significance

**Engagement Tactics:**

- Ask: "Which signer's story surprised you most?"
- Polls: "Would you have signed this treaty? Why/why not?"
- Links: Direct to full biography profile pages on Evidence Room
- Challenge: "Find your family in Cherokee genealogy records—comment below"

**Campaign Hashtags:**
`#42Voices #CherokeeLeaders #TreatyOfHolston #IndigenousHistory #CherokeeTribalHistory`

**Expected Reach:**

- 5,000-10,000 accounts per cycle
- 500+ profile visits to Evidence Room Cherokee biography pages
- 200+ comments/shares (personal stories, genealogy connections)

## "On This Day" Historical Posts

**Objective:** Create regular engagement through exact historical anniversaries from the Evidence Room timeline

**Content Type:** 1-3 minute educational posts tied to specific dates from the timeline-events.json file

### Strategic Dates to Highlight

**High-Impact Dates:**

- **May 26:** Southwest Territory Created (1790) → "Congress creates a new frontier"
- **August 13:** Washington asks "Where ought the Governor reside?" (1790)
- **October 11:** Blount arrives at Rocky Mount (1790) → "Tennessee's capital moves to the frontier"
- **July 2:** Treaty of Holston Signed (1791) → "42 Cherokee leaders negotiated peace"
- **January-December Monthly "Anniversaries"** → Regular, recurring content

### "On This Day" Post Format

**Instagram Post:**

```
Today (Jan 12, 1791):
Governor William Blount wrote to Secretary Knox: "Treaty Preparations Begin"

Read Blount's actual words in the Evidence Room. What would YOU negotiate for as a frontier leader?

#OnThisDay #TennesseeHistory #EvidenceRoom
[LINK to document]
```

**TikTok Version:**

- Quick zoom into a scanned document
- Text overlay: "234 years ago today..."
- Read key excerpt aloud
- "Tap the link in bio to read the full letter"

**Facebook Long-Form:**

```
ON THIS DAY IN HISTORY (235 years ago):

July 2, 1791 – The Treaty of Holston is signed at White's Fort.

Forty-two Cherokee leaders gathered in one place to negotiate peace with the newly formed U.S. government. This was not a small thing. These men represented different factions, different towns, different opinions. Yet they came together.

We have the actual treaty document. We have letters from William Blount describing the negotiations. We have signatures—some in English letters, some in symbols (because reading/writing English wasn't required).

What we also have, increasingly, are the voices of the Cherokee negotiators themselves. Leaders like John Watts, Hanging Maw, and Bloody Fellow speaking in their own words.

Have you read the Treaty of Holston? What surprises you about it?

Read the full treaty, all related documents, and the stories of the signers in our Evidence Room...
```

- 3-4 "On This Day" posts per month
- Tied to actual dates in the timeline
- Creates anticipation: "What happened on the 28th? Find out tomorrow..."

## Shareable Content Formats

**Goal:** Create content that people WANT to share (not just like)

### High-Share Potential Formats

#### 1. **Quote Graphics**

- Document excerpt + background image (parchment, landscape of Rocky Mount, state map)
- Large, readable text (44pt+ font)
- Attribution clearly visible
- Dimensions: 1080x1350px (Instagram), 1200x628px (Facebook), 1024x512px (Twitter)

**Example:**

```
"Peace has been observed by our Nation
since the 1795 conference with Governor Blount."

— John Watts, Cherokee war leader, December 1796

[Signature image]
Evidence Room | Tennessee Starts Here
```

#### 2. **Educational Infographics**

- "Who signed the Treaty of Holston?" (family trees, faction maps)
- "Timeline of Events: 1790-1792" (visual timeline with key documents)
- "Then vs. Now: The Frontier in 1790" (comparing to modern Tennessee)
- "Read 230-Year-Old Handwriting: A Guide"

**Design:** Clean, minimal, high contrast for mobile readability

#### 3. **Document Comparison Grids**

- Side-by-side: Original document + Modern transcription
- Highlight specific passages
- Add context callouts
- Use arrows to show corrections/annotations

#### 4. **"Did You Know?" Fact Cards**

- One surprising fact per card
- Supported by document evidence
- Visually interesting (icons, colors, minimal text)
- Made for Stories, Reels, TikTok

**Example:**

```
DID YOU KNOW?

Andrew Jackson was only 21 years old
when he stayed at Rocky Mount
waiting for his law license.

He lived there for 6 weeks in 1788.

Evidence Room Document:
jackson-at-rocky-mount-1788.md
```

#### 5. **Transcription Graphics**

- Difficult historical document
- Clear, modern transcription underneath
- Annotated: definitions of archaic words, context notes
- "Can you read 1790s handwriting?" framing

#### 6. **"Spot the Difference" Images**

- Original document vs. facsimile/reproduction
- Challenge: "Find 3 differences" or "What's wrong with this version?"
- Encourages engaged viewing, screen time, shares
- Great for TikTok

#### 7. **Animated Timeline Videos**

- 30-60 seconds
- Events appear one by one on screen
- Key documents slide in
- Music + text overlay
- "Swipe up to explore the full timeline"

#### 8. **Interactive Polls**

- Facebook/Instagram: "Would you have signed this treaty?"
- Twitter: "Which document should we highlight next?"
- TikTok comments: "Comment the signer you find most interesting"
- Results posted as follow-up content

#### 9. **"This Day in 1791" Series**

- Daily or 3x/week posts
- Exact date + event + document excerpt
- Builds anticipation for "where the story goes"
- Cumulative narrative across a season

#### 10. **Student/Educator Spotlights**

- "How teachers are using the Evidence Room"
- Student essays using documents as sources
- Classroom photos (with permission)
- "Your students could be featured—tag us!"

## Crisis Communication & Moderation Policy

### Comment Moderation Guidelines

**Approve:**

- Substantive questions about documents or history
- Personal stories (family connections, genealogy)
- Teacher/educator inquiries
- Constructive corrections (with facts to back up)

**Flag for Review:**

- Requests for sensitive information
- Debunked historical claims (respond with evidence)
- Comments about Cherokee history (ensure respectful, non-appropriative)

**Delete:**

- Spam, self-promotion
- Hateful content targeting any group
- Misinformation presented as fact (unless educational opportunity)
- Profanity unrelated to historical context

### Sensitive Topics Protocols

**Cherokee History / Treaty of Holston:**

- Prioritize Cherokee voices and perspectives
- Acknowledge historical trauma and ongoing injustices
- Link to Cherokee Nation sources and education
- Do not speak for Cherokee people—amplify their voices

**Sensitive Historical Content (slavery, violence, dispossession):**

- Provide context and nuance
- Avoid sensationalizing
- Include primary sources
- Signal content warnings if appropriate
- Provide resources for deeper understanding

**Misinformation:**

- Respond with primary document evidence
- Link to fact-checks and scholarly sources
- Remain respectful but firm
- Don't amplify false claims with engagement

## Cross-Platform Repurposing Strategy

### Content Multiplication Framework

**1 Primary Content Piece → 7 Social Posts**

**Example:** William Blount's "Glass Windows Letter" (October 20, 1790)

Original: 1 primary document

**Repurposing:**

1. **Instagram Post** (Carousel)
   - Slide 1: Document image
   - Slide 2: Excerpt highlighting "glass windows"
   - Slide 3: Context about status symbols
   - Slide 4: Why it matters
   - Slide 5: "Read the full letter"

2. **Instagram Reel** (45 sec)
   - Quick cuts between document image and animation
   - Text: "Why were glass windows such a big deal?"
   - Trending audio

3. **Instagram Stories** (5 stories)
   - Story 1: Document image
   - Story 2: Question poll ("Why mention glass windows?")
   - Story 3: Answer reveal
   - Story 4: "Did you know?" fact
   - Story 5: Link to Evidence Room

4. **Facebook Post** (500 words)
   - Full transcription
   - Historical context
   - Discussion questions
   - Links to related documents

5. **Twitter Thread** (6 tweets)
   - Tweet 1: Hook ("This letter reveals something big about frontier status...")
   - Tweets 2-5: Key excerpts + context
   - Tweet 6: Link to full document

6. **TikTok Video** (1:30 min)
   - Zoom in on "glass windows" phrase
   - "Only rich people had glass windows in 1790"
   - Blount is bragging about his fancy house
   - "This letter proves it"

7. **Quote Graphic** (Instagram Story + Pinterest)
   - Document excerpt with attribution
   - Background image of Rocky Mount
   - Share-optimized dimensions

**Time Investment:** 3-4 hours to create 7 pieces of content from 1 primary document

**Reach Multiplier:** 1 document → 7K-15K potential reach across platforms

## Creative Brief for Visual Design

### Design System for Social Content

**Colors:**

- **Primary:** Deep burgundy (#6B3840) — Historic, stately, ties to era
- **Secondary:** Cream/parchment (#F5EFE6) — Document background
- **Accent:** Gold (#C9A961) — Wax seals, status, period feel
- **Dark:** Deep navy (#1a1a2e) — Text, contrast
- **Highlight:** Rust/terra cotta (#A0523D) — Call-to-action, emphasis

**Typography:**

- **Headers:** Serif (Georgia, Lora) — Period appropriate, authoritative
- **Body:** Sans-serif (Arial, Helvetica) — Readable, modern
- **Quotes:** Script (Dancing Script, Pacifico) — Elegant, document-like

**Imagery:**

- Use actual historical documents (primary source is most authentic)
- Historic locations: Rocky Mount exterior, Cobb House interior
- Relevant landscapes: East Tennessee topography, frontier imagery
- Avoid stock photos—source historically appropriate images only
- For graphics: White/cream backgrounds, minimal, readable text

**Style Notes:**

- "Handwritten" overlays should look authentic (use period-appropriate fonts)
- Document scans should be high-contrast and readable at mobile sizes
- Animations should feel "period" not modern (slow, deliberate, elegant)
- No modern memes or relatable humor (maintain historical dignity)

## Risks & Mitigation

### Risk 1: Low Initial Engagement

**Problem:** First months have low reach/engagement
**Mitigation:**

- Seed initial followers with Rocky Mount email list
- Reach out to educators/historians directly
- Cross-promote on main website
- Budget for initial paid promotion if needed

### Risk 2: Difficulty Reaching Educators

**Problem:** Teachers don't see evidence room content
**Mitigation:**

- Partner with teacher-focused platforms (Teachers Pay Teachers, TeachingHistory.org)
- Attend education conferences
- Create downloadable lesson plans (shareable, high-value)
- Build direct email list of interested teachers

### Risk 3: Insensitive Handling of Cherokee History

**Problem:** Content appears appropriative or disrespectful
**Mitigation:**

- Review all Cherokee-related content with tribal consultants first
- Prioritize Cherokee voices and sources
- Acknowledge historical injustices directly
- Partner with Cherokee Nation education team
- Regular sensitivity audits

### Risk 4: Viral Misinformation

**Problem:** False claims about documents spread widely
**Mitigation:**

- Fact-check everything before posting
- Link to scholarly sources in comments
- Respond quickly to misinformation
- Have a "correction" post template ready

### Risk 5: Burnout / Resource Constraints

**Problem:** Maintaining 35-40 posts/week is unsustainable
**Mitigation:**

- Use content repurposing heavily (1 document → 7 posts)
- Schedule content in batches (monthly prep sessions)
- Use scheduling tools (Buffer, Later)
- Build a content calendar 3 months ahead
- Consider hiring part-time social media coordinator ($500-1000/month)
