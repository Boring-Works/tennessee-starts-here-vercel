# Guide Scripts & Gift Shop Implementation
## How Interpreters Sell Events (Without Selling)

---

## INTRODUCTION FOR GUIDES

This is NOT a sales document. This is a context document.

Your job is to tell stories. Events are part of the story. When you're standing in the weaving cabin and a guest asks "do people still do this?", you're not being pushy by saying "yes, on Woolly Days." You're continuing the conversation.

This guide gives you:
1. **When** to mention events (5 natural moments during the tour)
2. **What** to say (scripts you can adapt)
3. **How** to handle common responses (rebuttals)

Use your own voice. These are templates, not speeches. If you naturally say things differently, that's fine—just hit the key points.

---

## THE 5 MOMENT MODEL

Most tours follow the same path:
1. Arrival & orientation
2. Weaving cabin
3. Blacksmith forge
4. Main house (Cobb House)
5. Kitchen & grounds
6. Gift shop

We've identified the 5 best moments to mention upcoming events. You won't mention events at every stop—just where it feels natural.

## MOMENT #1: WEAVING CABIN
**Duration:** 5-7 minutes in the cabin
**Topic:** Barsheba Cobb, textile production, frontier family economics
**Event connection:** Woolly Days (April 25-26)

### THE SETUP
You're showing the loom. Guests are engaged. Someone usually asks "did women really do all this?"

### THE SCRIPT (choose one)

**Version A (Short, casual):**
> "Yeah, and she did it constantly. Not just because she wanted to—because her family would freeze otherwise. No stores, no buying blankets. Barsheba made everything.
>
> Honestly, it's pretty eye-opening once you try it. We actually do an event called Woolly Days where visitors get their hands in the work. Spinning wheel, carding, the whole thing. Most people come expecting to watch. They end up making something they take home. If you're here in spring, it's worth it."

**Version B (Longer, more narrative):**
> "Barsheba Cobb didn't just work here. She was the entire textile supply chain for her family. Raw wool came in. She cleaned it, carded it, spun it into thread, wove the thread into cloth, then turned the cloth into clothes. That's a six-month process for one garment.
>
> The hard part isn't understanding it intellectually. It's feeling the weight of it. How much time? How much labor? We run an event called Woolly Days—late April, over two days. Visitors do the same work Barsheba did. Not a demonstration. Actual work. By day two, your hands know what 'labor-intensive' means. It changes how you see this cabin."

**Version C (If asked directly about participation):**
> "I know, it looks impossible. That's why we created Woolly Days. People always want to try it. So we made a space where you can. Two days, April 25-26. You'll get sore. You'll also understand Barsheba in a way no tour can explain. There's info in the gift shop if you want to know more, or you can book on our website."

### HANDLING COMMON RESPONSES

**"That sounds exhausting."**
> "It is. That's the point. Once you do it for two hours, you have a new respect for women who did it for life. Plus, it's actually kind of meditative. The rhythm is calming."

**"I don't have time for a two-day event."**
> "Totally fair. This tour gives you the overview. Woolly Days is for people who want to go deeper. Most folks don't need both—the tour is complete on its own. But if you find yourself thinking about Barsheba later, you know where to find us."

**"How much does it cost?"**
> "It's around $40 for the full experience. So about what you'd pay for a nice dinner out. But you leave with something you made and a new skill. Lots of families make it a yearly thing."

**"I'll think about it."**
> "Perfect. We'll have a card up front with dates and info. No pressure—but they do fill up, so if you're interested, book sooner rather than later."

### GIFT SHOP FOLLOW-UP
When this guest reaches checkout, the cashier should have printed Woolly Days cards visible. Prompt: "While I'm ringing you up, did [guide name] mention Woolly Days? We do that in late April. Most people who come on a tour like this end up coming back for it."

## MOMENT #2: BLACKSMITH FORGE
**Duration:** 5-8 minutes at/near the forge
**Topic:** Craft, tools, frontier economy, skill mastery
**Event connection:** Blacksmith workshops (monthly, Apr-Oct)

### THE SETUP
You're standing near the forge (lit or cold, depending on season). Someone usually says "that looks so cool" or "could I ever learn to do that?"

### THE SCRIPT (choose one)

**Version A (Short, responsive):**
> "Most people feel that way watching. The good news—you actually can learn. We run blacksmith workshops throughout the year. Four hours, you get to hold the hammer. The first piece you make is usually rough, but it's yours. You leave with something real you made from hot iron.
>
> Fair warning—it's hot, loud, and your forearms get sore. But people love it. We fill them up pretty quick."

**Version B (Longer, skill-focused):**
> "The craft looks magical, but it's actually a learnable skill. People think you need to be strong or trained. You don't. You need to understand the process: heat the iron, move fast, know where to hit, repeat.
>
> We offer workshops where you learn exactly that. Four hours with our blacksmith. You'll make a hook, maybe a nail, depending on your pace. Lots of adults take the class just to say they forged something. It's become kind of popular."

**Version C (If it's right after a demo):**
> "What you just watched—that's learnable. We have workshops where you do exactly that. You won't be that fast on day one, but by the end of four hours, you'll understand the craft. Some people come back for multiple workshops because they get hooked on it."

### HANDLING COMMON RESPONSES

**"I'm not strong enough."**
> "Honestly, strength isn't the limiting factor. It's finesse and timing. I've had 65-year-old women take the workshop and make beautiful pieces. The hammer does most of the work—you're just guiding it."

**"How much does that cost?"**
> "Around $60-80 depending on the date and materials. So less than a good dinner and drinks. And you leave with something you made."

**"When do you do them?"**
> "We schedule them throughout the spring and summer. They fill up about 3-4 weeks out, so if you're interested, best to book soon. Check the website or ask up front—they'll have all the dates."

**"I'm visiting from out of town, can't stay that long."**
> "I get it. The tour gives you the overview. If you live closer, the workshop is worth a weekend trip sometime. People drive from other states to take it."

### GIFT SHOP FOLLOW-UP
Cashier visual: Large printed card showing "Blacksmith Workshops—Forge Your Own History" with dates and booking info.
Cashier prompt: "Did [guide] mention our blacksmith workshops? He made it sound pretty cool, and people ask about it a lot."

## MOMENT #3: MAIN HOUSE - WILLIAM BLOUNT SECTION
**Duration:** 8-10 minutes in the house, Blount focus
**Topic:** Constitutional history, territorial governance, founding
**Event connections:**
- William Blount lecture (May 29)
- Lectures on constitutional history (March-June)
- July 4 Colonial Independence Day

### THE SETUP
You're in the main room where Blount conducted government. This is the highest-content moment of the tour. Guests are usually quiet, absorbing. Someone might ask "did he actually live here?" or "what did he do exactly?"

### THE SCRIPT (choose one)

**Version A (Short, current events tie - use March-May):**
> "Blount didn't just live here—he ran a government from this room. He was one of the signers of the U.S. Constitution. But here's what most people don't know: he didn't just sign it and leave. He came here and actually had to implement it. How do you govern 70,000 scattered settlers with no communication, no roads, no infrastructure?
>
> We have a historian named Dr. Caroline Whitfield who speaks on May 29 about exactly this. It's fascinating—the compromises, the land deals, how it led to Tennessee becoming a state. If this part of the tour interests you, that lecture goes deep."

**Version B (Longer, deep narrative):**
> "You're standing in the first federal capitol west of the Appalachians. That's not exaggeration. George Washington appointed Blount personally. Blount was one of the Constitutional Convention delegates—he signed the Constitution.
>
> But constitutional theory is one thing. Living it on the frontier is another. Blount had to negotiate with Cherokee nations, settle land disputes, establish laws, deal with militia conflicts. All from this room. All before Tennessee was a state.
>
> We've actually partnered with scholars who research this period. We have lectures throughout the spring that dig into different angles—the politics, the personal stories, the women's perspectives. Most people find that side just as interesting as the house tour."

**Version C (If asked about statehood):**
> "Tennessee wouldn't exist without this. The groundwork for statehood was laid right here. The decisions made in this room shaped what Tennessee became. If you want to understand where Tennessee came from—not just the facts, but the actual political moments and the people making the decisions—we have lecture events that specialists lead. They're really good."

### HANDLING COMMON RESPONSES

**"That's a lot of history."**
> "I know—it's easy to overdose. The tour is a good overview. The lectures are for people who want to go deeper. No pressure either way."

**"I'm not really a history person."**
> "That's fair. But this isn't dusty history—it's the literal founding of Tennessee. And it's told by real historians, not textbooks. Some of our best attendees are people who don't think they're history people."

**"Do I have to attend all the lectures?"**
> "Nope. They're all standalone. You can pick one that interests you. May 29 is about Blount and government. Others focus on women's roles, the Cherokee perspective, different angles. Pick whatever sounds interesting."

**"How much are the lectures?"**
> "Usually $10-15. Cheaper than a movie, honestly. We also have bundle pricing if you want to attend multiple events."

### GIFT SHOP FOLLOW-UP
Visual: Lecture series card with dates, speakers, and brief descriptions.
Cashier prompt: "Did [guide] mention the history lectures we do? There's one coming up on May 29 that's supposed to be really good—all about how Tennessee got founded."

## MOMENT #4: THE KITCHEN
**Duration:** 5-7 minutes
**Topic:** Daily life, food production, seasonal labor
**Event connections:**
- Woolly Days (April, food + fiber)
- Harvest Fest (October)
- Hearth Cooking workshops (quarterly)
- Frontier Christmas (November-December)

### THE SETUP
You're in the kitchen. This is the most visceral space—fire, artifacts, the smell of history. Someone usually asks "how did they cook everything?" or "how did they preserve food?"

### THE SCRIPT (seasonal selection)

**FOR APRIL-MAY (Woolly Days + Harvest preview):**
> "Everything you see here—the cooking methods, the time—multiplied by feeding a family of 8 or 10. And the mother of the household is also making cloth, tending animals, and everything else.
>
> There's actually two ways we help people understand this. One is Woolly Days in late April—you work fiber. The other is Harvest Fest in October—you work food. Both are participatory. You do the work yourself, then you eat what you made. It clicks when you try it."

**FOR JUNE-JULY (Summer lead-in, Independence Day tie):**
> "These kitchens were the heartbeat of the home. Every meal was a production. No convenience, no shortcuts. And this was happening while Blount was conducting government in the main house—all happening simultaneously in one home.
>
> In October, we do Harvest Fest where you get a hands-on taste of food production. But for now, you can do the tour and imagine the effort."

**FOR AUGUST-SEPTEMBER (Fall transition):**
> "Come October, we do Harvest Fest—two full days of food production the way they did it. Cider pressing, apple butter, corn shelling. You participate. You eat it. It's the full immersion version of what this kitchen was about.
>
> If food history interests you, that's the experience. Many families make it an annual tradition."

**FOR OCTOBER-NOVEMBER (Holiday season):**
> "Winter was preparation time for what you're seeing here in the kitchen. All the preserving, the planning, the winter stores. In just a few weeks, we open up Frontier Christmas—showing how families celebrated in the season. Same kitchen, different context."

### HANDLING COMMON RESPONSES

**"That sounds hard."**
> "It was. But there's something meditative about it too. Once you try it—even for an afternoon—you understand the rhythm. It's not as chaotic as you'd think."

**"I don't cook."**
> "Neither do most people who come to Harvest Fest. That's kind of the point. You show up, you learn, you make something, you eat it. No prior experience needed."

**"Is it appropriate for kids?"**
> "Totally. We have family sessions and kid-specific sessions. Kids especially love it—they actually get to touch the fire, do real work, not just watch. Parents tell us it's the most memorable thing they do all year."

### GIFT SHOP FOLLOW-UP
Visuals: Seasonal event cards (Woolly Days in spring section, Harvest Fest in fall section)
Cashier prompt: "Were you thinking about Harvest Fest when [guide] was talking about the kitchen? That seems to be what people remember most."

## MOMENT #5: GIFT SHOP (FINAL PUSH)
**Duration:** 5-10 minutes (checkout)
**Topic:** All events, bundles, next visit
**Event connections:** ALL

### THE SETUP
Guest is at checkout, buying a gift or refreshment. They're in a positive mood (tour is done, it was good). This is the lowest-pressure moment to mention future engagement.

### THE GIFT SHOP EVENT DISPLAY

**Physical Setup:**
- Printed event cards organized by season (4 stations)
- Spring: Woolly Days, lectures, Blacksmith Workshops
- Summer: Camps, Twilight tours, July 4
- Fall: Harvest Fest, Haunting, Frontier Christmas
- Year-round: Behind the Scenes, Blacksmith Workshops, Hearth Cooking

- QR code poster: "Plan Your Next Visit" → Links to FareHarbor event page
- First 250 enrollment forms (March-June only)
- Small chalkboard with rotating message:
  - "Woolly Days: April 25-26"
  - "Harvest Fest: October 10-11"
  - "Haunting: October 15-24"
  - (rotate monthly)

### THE CASHIER SCRIPT (choose one approach)

**Approach A (Warm, minimal):**
> "Thanks for coming today. Before you head out, just wanted to mention—we have some really popular events coming up [month]. If you want info on any of those, just grab a card on your way out. No pressure—just wanted you to know."

**Approach B (Specific to guide topic):**
> "Your guide mentioned Woolly Days, right? We have cards over here with all the details. A lot of people grab them and book later, but they do fill up, so if you're interested, book a week or two out."

**Approach C (Bundle pitch, subtle):**
> "Just so you know, if you're thinking about coming back for one of our events, we have bundle pricing—tour plus an event for a little less than buying separately. Let me know if you want details."

**Approach D (First 250, March-June only):**
> "Oh, and if you haven't enrolled in the First 250 yet—that's the program where your name gets read on July 4 as America celebrates 250 years. We have forms here, or you can enroll online. Enrollment closes June 1st."

### HANDLING COMMON RESPONSES AT CHECKOUT

**"I'm not a local."**
> "That's actually perfect—a lot of our events draw visitors from out of state. If you're planning a return trip, events are a great reason to come back. We have family packages and bundle deals."

**"I can't commit to anything right now."**
> "No problem. Grab a card, think about it. You can book anytime online—we usually have openings."

**"How far in advance do I need to book?"**
> "For most events, 2-3 weeks is fine. The really popular ones—like Harvest Fest and the Christmas events—those book faster. But generally not months ahead."

**"I don't see my interests here."**
> "What are you interested in? We might have something not on this card. [Listen, then direct.] Or I can put you on our email list—we send updates about coming events."

## STAFF TRAINING GUIDE

### FOR GUIDES (30-minute orientation)

**Goal:** Guides feel confident mentioning events without feeling like they're selling

**Training agenda:**
1. Read "Why Tours Matter for Events" (5 min)
2. Review the 5 moments (10 min)
3. Walk through your cabin/section with scripts (10 min)
4. Q&A (5 min)

**Key talking points:**
- "This is storytelling, not sales. You're not being pushy."
- "Pick ONE moment per tour. Don't overdo it."
- "If guests aren't interested, don't push. Move on naturally."
- "Your tone matters more than exact words."
- "If you don't remember the event, it's fine to say 'ask at checkout.'"

### FOR GIFT SHOP STAFF (15-minute quick training)

**Goal:** Cashiers feel comfortable mentioning events naturally while ringing up purchases

**Training agenda:**
1. Know where cards are (2 min)
2. Review 3 cashier scripts (5 min)
3. Practice one interaction (5 min)
4. Review common objections (3 min)

**Key talking points:**
- "One sentence is enough. You're just planting an idea."
- "Most people already heard about events from the guide."
- "Your job is to make the cards visible and memorable."
- "If they're not interested, no problem—you tried."

### MONTHLY REFRESHER

Once a month, share:
- Upcoming events
- Which event is "hot" (booking well)
- Highlights from recent events (photos, guest feedback)
- New scripts if events change

## MEASURING SUCCESS

### What to track:

1. **Events mentioned in tours:** Did guides actually use scripts?
   - Monthly survey: "Did your guide mention any events?" (Yes/No)
   - Target: 60% of tour guests should remember an event being mentioned

2. **Gift shop card pickup:** Are people taking printed cards?
   - Count cards used monthly
   - Target: 30-40% of tour guests take at least one card

3. **Tour → Event conversion:** How many tour-goers book events?
   - FareHarbor data: filter "source" = "tour"
   - Target: 25-30% of tour bookers also book an event within 3 months

4. **Guest feedback:** What are people saying?
   - Survey question: "Did the tour inspire you to attend any events?"
   - Open feedback: "What was the most memorable part?"

### Monthly reporting:

Create a simple dashboard showing:
- Tours given
- Events mentioned (by guide)
- Cards distributed
- Bookings traced back to tours
- Revenue impact

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Mentioning too many events.** Stick to one per tour, maximum two.

2. **Pushing too hard.** If a guest doesn't respond positively, drop it naturally.

3. **Not knowing details.** If you mention an event, know the date and rough price. Say "ask at checkout" if unsure.

4. **Inconsistent messaging.** If different guides say different things, it confuses guests. Use the scripts.

5. **Forgetting the gift shop follow-up.** The checkout moment is as important as the tour moment.

6. **Not rotating by season.** In November, don't push Woolly Days. Push Frontier Christmas.

7. **Treating it like a sales job.** You're a guide. Events are part of your story. Own it as such.

## SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE

After 3 months of implementation:

- Tours feel more connected to the larger Rocky Mount story

- 30%+ of tour-goers purchase event tickets within 3 months

- Staff feels confident talking about events
