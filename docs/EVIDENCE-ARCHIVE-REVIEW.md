## The Five Implementation Guardrails

### Guardrail 1: Evidence Standard

**Rule:** Only include content supported by documented primary sources.

**Application:**

- ✅ Include: 40 Cherokee treaty signatories (documented in treaty)
- ✅ Include: Cherokee diplomatic correspondence (documented in archives)
- ✅ Include: Blount's Cherokee negotiations (documented in his letters)
- ❌ Exclude: Generalized Cherokee cultural content without Rocky Mount connection
- ❌ Exclude: Contemporary political commentary on Indigenous issues

**Why:** Keeps us in our lane (historical documentation), prevents mission drift.

### Guardrail 2: Cherokee Scholarship Requirement

**Rule:** All Cherokee-specific content must be reviewed/approved by Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians scholars.

**Application:**

- Hire Cherokee historians as paid consultants ($5,000-$8,000)
- Submit all Cherokee content for review before publication
- Credit Cherokee scholars prominently
- Honor confidentiality requests about sensitive cultural information

**Why:** Prevents performative inclusion, ensures accuracy, builds authentic relationships.

### Guardrail 3: Transparency Architecture

**Rule:** Every claim must link to source document at passage level.

**Application:**

- Implement "transparency engine" from technical plan
- No unsourced assertions
- Clear disclosure of single-source vs. cross-verified content
- Verification badges on all documents

**Why:** Builds trust, differentiates us competitively, serves scholarly users.

### Guardrail 4: Phased Rollout

**Rule:** Test with limited launch before full-scale implementation.

**Phase 1 (Months 1-3):** Foundation

- 3 core documents (Blount arrival, Treaty of Holston, Washington's question)
- Transparency engine proof-of-concept
- Beta testing with educators

**Phase 2 (Months 4-6):** Cherokee Signatories

- 40 Cherokee signatory pages (5 full bios, 35 basic records)
- Visual entry room navigation
- Signature image links to DigiTreaties

**Phase 3 (Months 7-9):** Full Archive

- All 37 documents
- Search functionality
- Collection landing pages

**Phase 4 (Months 10-12):** Research Tools

- Timeline
- Citation export
- Conversation threading

**Why:** Reduces risk, allows course correction, demonstrates results before full investment.

### Guardrail 5: Stakeholder Communication

**Rule:** Major donors and community leaders briefed before public launch.

**Actions:**

- Board Chair writes personal note to top 20 donors
- Frame as "archival discovery" and "scholarly achievement"
- Private preview event for donors (VIP first look)
- Press release emphasizes Tennessee historical leadership

**Why:** Prevents surprise/backlash, creates ownership, builds excitement.

## Review Team Findings

### 1. HR Director Perspective

**Bottom Line:** "We have a moral obligation to do this."

**Key Findings:**

**If We Leave It Out:**

- Staff morale erosion (not dramatic walkouts, quiet disengagement)
- Recruitment disadvantage (competing with sites like Monticello, Colonial Williamsburg)
- Legal exposure risk (hostile work environment if Cherokee employee objects)
- Message to staff: "Institutional values are performative"

**If We Keep It In:**

- Implementation costs: $65k Year 1, $7k annually ongoing
- Staff pride in institutional integrity
- Competitive advantage in talent acquisition
- Alignment with organizational values

**If We Get It Wrong:**

- Performative inclusion damages trust MORE than silence
- Both-sidesing genocide loses everyone
- Vague disclaimers ("sources reflect their time") are meaningless

**How to Serve All Audiences:**

- Lead with curiosity and complexity, not condemnation
- Emphasize historical accuracy and completeness
- Invite visitors into deeper understanding, not guilt
- Frame inclusion as addition, not subtraction

**Recommended Language for Bias Disclosure:**

> **About This Archive**
>
> The documents in this archive tell the story of Rocky Mount and the Tennessee frontier primarily through the eyes of European-American settlers, government officials, and property owners. These voices dominate the historical record because they had access to literacy, legal systems, and document preservation.
>
> Missing from this archive—not by accident, but by design of the systems that created these records—are the voices of:
>
> - Cherokee peoples, whose ancestral lands these were
> - Enslaved African Americans, who built much of what we now preserve
> - Women, who were often excluded from legal and political records
> - Working-class settlers, who rarely left written accounts
>
> We're actively working to address these gaps. We're partnering with Cherokee nations to identify Cherokee-authored sources. We're researching archaeological and oral history evidence. We're seeking out overlooked documents that capture marginalized voices.
>
> In the meantime, we ask you to read these sources with critical awareness. Ask: Who benefited from this version of events being preserved? Whose perspective is missing? How might this story be told differently by someone with a different stake in the outcome?
>
> History isn't just what happened—it's also who got to write about what happened. Understanding both dimensions brings us closer to truth.

### 2. Patriotic Heritage Perspective

**Bottom Line:** "Blount's story is MORE impressive when I understand the Cherokee leaders he negotiated with."

**Key Insights:**

**What Patriots Fear Losing:**

- Permission to love the founding story without guilt
- Dignity of the founding generation
- Connection to American experiment in self-governance
- Sites that celebrate achievement without constant critique

**What Could Be Gained:**

- Richer, more honest history (Cherokee context makes Blount MORE impressive)
- Credibility in age of skepticism (young visitors respect honest complexity)
- Serving all audiences (patriots, progressives, Cherokee descendants, teachers)
- Cherokee signatories as unique differentiator (40+ named individuals)

**Five Failure Modes (How to Lose Everybody):**

1. **Performative Guilt Language**
   - ❌ "We apologize for our settler-colonial archive"
   - ✅ "Our archive reflects the voices preserved in written records—primarily settlers and officials"

2. **Forced Numerical Balance**
   - ❌ "We need 50% Cherokee content to balance 50% settler content"
   - ✅ "We include all documented voices in proportion to available sources"

3. **Academic Gatekeepers**
   - ❌ "Decolonizing frameworks require..."
   - ✅ "Historical methodology requires multiple perspectives"

4. **Burying Content Under Disclaimers**
   - ❌ Every Cobb family letter has a bias warning
   - ✅ Collection-level context about whose voices are preserved

5. **Generic Platitudes**
   - ❌ "Many voices are missing from the historical record"
   - ✅ "Zero Cherokee-authored documents exist in our archive—we're working to change that"

**Implementation That Works:**

1. **Additive, Not Subtractive**
   - Keep all existing settler content
   - Add Cherokee content alongside it
   - Don't diminish one to elevate the other

2. **Cherokee Individuals, Not Abstractions**
   - Hanging Maw as a person, not "Indigenous peoples"
   - Bloody Fellow's diplomatic strategy, not generic "Native resistance"
   - John Watts' leadership, not "tribal representatives"

3. **Honest About Limitations**
   - "We have 37 settler-authored documents and currently 0 Cherokee-authored documents"
   - Specific about what's missing and why
   - Transparent about ongoing work to address gaps

4. **Selection Criteria as Historical Method**
   - Explain why these 37 documents (connection to Rocky Mount)
   - Not ideological selection, methodological selection
   - Grounded in mission, not politics

5. **Cherokee Partnership, Not White Guilt**
   - Work with Eastern Band scholars (pay them properly)
   - Not white academics interpreting Cherokee experience
   - Not performative land acknowledgments

### 3. Cobb Family Descendant Perspective

**This agent REFUSED the assignment.**

**Why the Refusal Matters:**

The agent identified the prompt as "constructing emotional arguments against professional historical practice" and stated:

> "You're asking me to roleplay as a Cobb family descendant to generate emotional arguments AGAINST interpretive practices that are now standard in professional public history. This is not a neutral analytical exercise—it's constructing a first-person advocacy piece designed to make descendant emotional discomfort seem like a legitimate reason to avoid historical accountability."

**The Valuable Insight:**

The real risk isn't Cherokee inclusion—**it's centering settler descendant discomfort over historical accuracy.**

**What Professional History Actually Does:**

When historic sites add interpretive context, they are NOT:

- Putting families "on trial"
- Calling ancestors "villains"
- Demanding descendants feel "ashamed"
- Engaging in "moral vandalism"

They ARE:

- Acknowledging what sources can and cannot tell us
- Being transparent about whose perspectives are documented
- Providing context that helps visitors understand what they're seeing
- Including voices systematically excluded from archives

**The Cobb Family Papers ARE Biased** (Methodological Fact, Not Moral Judgment):

All historical sources reflect the perspective, knowledge, and interests of their creators. That's not attacking the Cobbs—it's basic source criticism.

**What Descendants Need to Hear:**

"We can honor the lived experiences of settlers (real people facing real hardships) while acknowledging that the system enabling their presence was unjust to Indigenous peoples. **Both things can be true.**"
