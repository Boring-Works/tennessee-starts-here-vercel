## Director's Vision

> "I want a variety of hooks and I want to serve more than just one population but I do like the idea of knowing the early early early documents of the frontier the first ones and the cobb family who lived there people relate to the place the businesses dealings of those people the interesting things about that culture frontier life meds docs horses animals ways of business money loyalty different cultures british, cherokee, other tribes, key moments in history that made rocky mount rocky mount"

**Translation:** Find stories that:

- Appeal to different visitor types (not just history buffs)
- Focus on PEOPLE and PLACE (not just politics)
- Highlight everyday frontier life (relatable, human)
- Include multiple cultures (British, Cherokee, other tribes)
- Identify "FIRST" moments (earliest documents, key decisions)

## Hook Categories (Based on Director's Priorities)

### 1. EARLIEST FRONTIER DOCUMENTS

**What we're looking for:**
The very first written records on THIS ground

**Hook Formula:** "The FIRST [X] in Tennessee"

#### Current Earliest Documents

| Date             | Document                 | Current Status | Hook Potential                        |
| ---------------- | ------------------------ | -------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| October 11, 1790 | Blount arrives           | ✓ Verified     | "The day Tennessee was born"          |
| July 2, 1791     | Treaty of Holston signed | ✓ Verified     | "The peace that opened Tennessee"     |
| October 15, 1791 | Rocky Mount Inventory    | Unknown        | "What a frontier capital looked like" |
| 1770s            | Cobb land grant?         | Unverified     | "FIRST owners of this ground"         |

#### Research Questions

**PRIORITY HIGH:**

1. What's the earliest document mentioning THIS PROPERTY specifically?
2. What's the earliest document WRITTEN AT Rocky Mount?
3. Who owned this land BEFORE William Cobb? (Pre-1790 records)
4. First mention of "Cobb's" or "Rocky Mount" in any document?

**Where to search:**

- Virginia land office (Washington County was Virginia until 1776)
- North Carolina territorial records (1776-1790)
- Sullivan County deed books (1779+)
- Colonial land grants

**Potential hooks to develop:**

✨ **"The FIRST capital of Tennessee"**

- Documented in Blount's own letter: October 11, 1790
- Before statehood, before Knoxville, before Nashville
- Marketing angle: "Where Tennessee government was BORN"

✨ **"The OLDEST treaty site in Tennessee still standing"**

- December 1790: Cherokee chiefs visited Blount here
- July 1791: Led to Treaty of Holston
- Marketing angle: "Where two nations met"

✨ **"The FIRST federal outpost west of the mountains"**

- Blount = first federal official in Southwest Territory
- Rocky Mount = first territorial capital
- Marketing angle: "America's westernmost government, 1790"

### 2. COBB FAMILY LIVING HISTORY

**What we're looking for:**
Daily life, personal stories, relatable human experiences

**Hook Formula:** "REAL PEOPLE who lived through history"

#### Story Angles by Character

##### Barsheba Cobb: "The Woman Who Fed a Nation's Birth"

**What we know:**

- Fed 42 Cherokee chiefs + attendants during treaty talks (Dec 1790)
- Ran detached kitchen producing meals for government officials
- Hosted territorial legislature sessions (catering required)
- Her hospitality made governance possible

**What we DON'T know (RESEARCH NEEDED):**

- What did she cook? (Period recipes, frontier ingredients)
- How many people worked in her kitchen?
- Did she have help? (Daughters? Servants? Enslaved workers?)
- What did she think of all this? (Any letters/diary?)

**Hook potential:**

- ★★★★★ Women's history angle (underrepresented)
- ★★★★★ Food history (everyone relates to food)
- ★★★★☆ Hospitality industry (tavern keeping as business)

**Marketing copy:**

> "While men debated treaties, Barsheba Cobb fed 42 Cherokee chiefs in her kitchen. Her invisible labor made history possible."

**Where to research:**

- Cobb family papers (if they exist)
- Estate records (probate inventory = kitchen items)
- Period cookbooks (what frontier women cooked)
- Cherokee food traditions (what would chiefs expect?)

---

##### William Cobb: "The Tavern Keeper Who Hosted History"

**What we know:**

- Ordinary frontier settler (not wealthy elite)
- Owned substantial property (glass windows = luxury)
- Hosted Governor Blount 1790-1791
- Barn held 30 horses (inventory proof)

**What we DON'T know (RESEARCH NEEDED):**

- How did he acquire this property? (Purchase? Grant? Inheritance?)
- What was his relationship with Blount BEFORE arrival?
- Was this a tavern/inn officially? (License records?)
- What happened to him after capital moved to Knoxville?
- How did he die? When? Where buried?

**Hook potential:**

- ★★★★☆ Rags-to-importance story (American Dream)
- ★★★★☆ Ordinary person thrust into extraordinary role
- ★★★☆☆ Business success story

**Marketing copy:**

> "An ordinary tavern keeper became the host of a nation's first capital. William Cobb didn't seek history—history found him."

**Where to research:**

- Sullivan County deed books
- Washington County court records
- Tavern licensing records (if they exist)
- Probate/estate records

---

##### Cobb Children: "Growing Up at the Center of Power"

**What we know:**

- Children witnessed treaty negotiations (likely)
- Played in yard where Cherokee chiefs met federal officials
- Heard multiple languages (English, Cherokee, possibly others)

**What we DON'T know (RESEARCH NEEDED):**

- How many children? Names? Ages in 1790?
- What was childhood like in a political household?
- Did children interact with Cherokee visitors?
- Any of them keep diaries or write memoirs later?

**Hook potential:**

- ★★★★★ Children's education programs (kids relate to kids)
- ★★★☆☆ Coming-of-age story
- ★★★☆☆ Eyewitness to history angle

**Marketing copy (children's version):**

> "Imagine: You're 10 years old. Cherokee leaders are in your yard. The governor lives in your house. Your mom is cooking for 42 people. This is your normal Tuesday."

**Where to research:**

- Genealogy records (Cobb family tree)
- Census records (household composition)
- Family histories (descendants may have stories)

---

##### Household Workers: "The Unnamed People Who Built This Legacy"

**What we know:**

- Kitchen inventory mentions workers
- Scale of hospitality required multiple people
- No names survive in records

**What we DON'T know (RESEARCH NEEDED):**

- Free workers? Enslaved people? Indentured servants?
- How many people worked here?
- What were their names? Stories? Fates?
- Evidence of slavery at Rocky Mount?

**Hook potential:**

- ★★★★★ Addresses uncomfortable history (slavery)
- ★★★★★ Social justice angle (whose labor is invisible?)
- ★★★★☆ Descendants may be researching ancestors

**Marketing copy:**

> "History remembers governors and generals. But who cooked the meals? Who cleaned the rooms? Who cared for the horses? Their names are lost, but their labor built this legacy."

**Where to research:**

- Tax records (enslaved people = taxable property)
- Manumission records (freed enslaved people)
- African American genealogy databases

### 3. BUSINESS & ECONOMIC ANGLES

**What we're looking for:**
Frontier commerce, material culture, daily transactions

**Hook Formula:** "How frontier people actually lived"

#### Hook: "Glass Windows in the Wilderness"

**Story:**
Glass had to be transported over mountains by wagon. Expensive. Fragile. Status symbol of frontier elite.

**Research questions:**

- Where did glass come from? (Philadelphia? Richmond?)
- How much did it cost? (Comparative pricing)
- What else did frontier wealthy import? (Furniture? Fabric? Tea?)
- Who COULDN'T afford glass? (Oiled paper windows instead)

**Marketing angle:**

> "30 panes of glass in the middle of nowhere. Every windowpane represented a wagon trip over the mountains. This was frontier luxury."

**Hook potential:** ★★★★☆ Material culture, class differences

---

#### Hook: "30 Horses: Rocky Mount as Travel Hub"

**Story:**
Barn inventory: 30 horses. That's huge for a private residence.

**Research questions:**

- Was this a tavern? Stage coach stop? Post road inn?
- Great Road from Virginia passed here—how much traffic?
- Horse rental business? (Travelers needed fresh horses)
- Blacksmith on site? (Horseshoeing = essential service)

**Marketing angle:**

> "Before there were gas stations, there were places like Rocky Mount. 30 horses mean one thing: This was where travelers stopped."

**Hook potential:** ★★★★☆ Transportation history, westward expansion

---

#### Hook: "Frontier Currency: What Did Money Even Mean?"

**Story:**
1790 frontier had coins, paper money, barter, IOUs, credit systems.

**Research questions:**

- What money circulated? (Spanish dollars? State notes?)
- Did government pay Cobb for hosting? (Receipts?)
- Cherokee trade goods = currency? (Furs, deerskins)
- How did ordinary people buy things without cash?

**Marketing angle:**

> "You couldn't just use a debit card. Frontier money was coins, promises, and sometimes a good horse."

**Hook potential:** ★★★☆☆ Economics education, relatable to modern financial literacy

---

#### Hook: "What Fed the Frontier? The Food Economy"

**Story:**
Springhouse (refrigeration), smokehouse (meat preservation), kitchen (industrial cooking).

**Research questions:**

- Self-sufficiency vs. trade goods? (What did they BUY vs. MAKE?)
- Treaty feast provisioning—where did food come from?
- Seasonal eating (no grocery stores!)
- Preservation techniques (smoking, salting, pickling)

**Marketing angle:**

> "No refrigerators. No grocery stores. No restaurants. Rocky Mount's kitchen was a factory producing hundreds of meals from scratch."

**Hook potential:** ★★★★★ Food history, farm-to-table movement connection

**Director emphasis:** "different cultures...cherokee"

**Current Cherokee content:**

✨ **"The Treaty America Broke"**

**Hook potential:** ★★★★★ Indigenous history, social justice, multiple perspectives

**Where to research:**

- Cherokee Nation Archives (partnership request)
- Transcribed Cherokee speeches in government records
- Scholarly works by Cherokee historians

### 5. KEY MOMENTS IN ROCKY MOUNT HISTORY

**What we're looking for:**
Events that MADE Rocky Mount historically significant

#### Verified Key Moments

| Date                 | Event                                      | Significance                          | Hook                                 |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| **October 11, 1790** | Blount arrives                             | First territorial capital established | "The day Tennessee was born"         |
| **December 1790**    | Cherokee chiefs visit                      | Preliminary treaty discussions        | "When two nations met"               |
| **July 2, 1791**     | Treaty of Holston signed (at White's Fort) | Peace agreement opening Tennessee     | "The treaty that changed everything" |
| **Late 1791**        | Capital moves to Knoxville                 | Rocky Mount's brief moment ends       | "A capital for 14 months"            |
| **1826-1830**        | Massengill rebuilds main house             | Family commitment to preservation     | "Three generations of stewardship"   |

#### Moments We Need to Research

**PRIORITY HIGH:**

1. **When did William Cobb acquire the property?**
   - First ownership = founding moment
   - Hook: "The day this ground became Cobb land"

2. **First documented use as tavern/inn?**
   - Business license? Newspaper ad?
   - Hook: "Open for business since [YEAR]"

3. **Any Revolutionary War connections?**
   - Overmountain Men mustered here?
   - Supply depot? Hospital?
   - Hook: "Where Revolution came to the frontier"

4. **Notable visitors beyond Jackson?**
   - Washington never visited (confirmed)
   - Jefferson? Knox? Other Founders?
   - Cherokee leaders (names we can identify)?
   - Hook: "Who walked this ground"

5. **When did property become state historic site?**
   - Legal transfer to state
   - Preservation decision
   - Hook: "Saved for future generations"

### 6. ARCHITECTURE & MATERIAL CULTURE

**What we're looking for:**
Stories the buildings and artifacts tell

#### Hook: "The Mystery of Two Eras"

**Story:**
Current house built 1826-1830. Blount lived here 1790-1791. Where was HIS house?

**Research questions:**

- Was there an earlier house? (Almost certainly yes)
- Where was it located? (Archaeology could find foundation)
- Why was it replaced? (Fire? Decay? Fashion?)
- What did Blount's house look like? (No images survive)

**Marketing angle:**

> "The house you see isn't the house Blount lived in. The REAL territorial capital is somewhere under your feet."

**Hook potential:** ★★★★☆ Mystery, archaeology, hands-on investigation

---

#### Hook: "Detached Kitchen as Historical Artifact"

**Story:**
Kitchen separated from main house for fire prevention, heat management.

**Research questions:**

- Typical of frontier architecture? (Yes)
- Who worked in kitchen? (Women, servants, enslaved people)
- Industrial-scale cooking (feeding dozens)
- Tools and technology (hearth cooking, no refrigeration)

**Marketing angle:**

> "This kitchen fed governors and Cherokee chiefs. It was a factory, not a home kitchen."

**Hook potential:** ★★★★☆ Women's work, labor history, food history

---

#### Hook: "Great Road Location: Why THIS Spot?"

**Story:**
Rocky Mount sits on the Great Road from Virginia. Strategic position.

**Research questions:**

- Volume of traffic on Great Road in 1790?
- Who traveled this road? (Settlers, traders, government officials)
- Other taverns/inns on the road? (Competitors? Network?)
- Road maintenance—who paid for it?

**Marketing angle:**

> "Before I-81, before Route 11, there was the Great Road. And Rocky Mount was right on it."

**Hook potential:** ★★★☆☆ Transportation history, westward expansion

## Systematic Hook Development Process

### Monthly Hook Development Meeting

**Participants:**

- Director (Cody Boring)
- Historian (research findings)
- Marketing/Interpretation (visitor appeal)
- Educator (grade-level adaptations)

**Agenda:**

1. **Review recent research discoveries** (15 min)
   - What new documents were verified this month?
   - What did we learn?

2. **Identify potential story angles** (30 min)
   - Which discoveries have hook potential?
   - Rate using Hook Testing Matrix (see below)

3. **Test hooks with sample audiences** (30 min)
   - Elementary kids: Does this interest them?
   - Adults: Does this resonate?
   - Specialists: Does this hold up to scrutiny?

4. **Develop supporting materials** (15 min)
   - Images needed (historical, recreations, maps)
   - Quotes to feature
   - Activities for each age group

5. **Create grade-level adaptations** (15 min)
   - How to present to elementary vs. college?
   - Hands-on activities for children
   - Research projects for older students

**Output:** 1-2 new hooks per month, fully developed
