# Audience Adaptation Framework

**Purpose:** Serve multiple populations from elementary students to scholars

**Created:** January 30, 2026
**Status:** Active content adaptation system
**Goal:** Same content, multiple presentations for different cognitive/educational levels

---

## Director's Vision

> "I want to serve more than just one population...school kids can cite us with confidence...college and researchers and everyone can trust..."

**Challenge:** Make Rocky Mount useful for:

- Elementary students (ages 8-10)
- Middle school students (ages 11-13)
- High school students (ages 14-18)
- College students & researchers (ages 18+)
- Educators (all levels)
- Tourists & casual visitors
- Genealogists & family historians

**Solution:** Create TIERS of content complexity, all derived from the same verified source material.

---

## The 7-Tier System

```
TIER 1: Elementary (Grades 3-5)
   ↓
TIER 2: Middle School (Grades 6-8)
   ↓
TIER 3: High School (Grades 9-12)
   ↓
TIER 4: College/Researchers
   ↓
TIER 5: Educators (Teaching Resources)
   ↓
TIER 6: Tourists (Engagement-Driven)
   ↓
TIER 7: Genealogists (Name/Date Focus)
```

**Key Principle:** Start with TIER 4 (scholarly version), then simplify down for younger audiences.

## TIER 1: Elementary Students (Grades 3-5)

**Cognitive Level:** Concrete thinking, narrative-driven, visual learners

**Reading Level:** 3rd-5th grade (Lexile 600-850)

### Adaptation Strategies

#### Language Simplification

**Original (Scholar level):**

> "The Treaty of Holston, negotiated pursuant to federal directive under Article IX of the United States Constitution, established territorial boundaries between the United States and the Cherokee Nation, contingent upon mutual recognition of sovereignty and cessation of hostilities."

**Adapted (Elementary):**

> "In 1791, American leaders and Cherokee chiefs signed a peace treaty at Rocky Mount. They agreed on which lands belonged to whom. The treaty said both sides would respect each other's borders and stop fighting."

**Simplification Rules:**

- Sentences under 15 words
- Avoid complex clauses
- Define or eliminate jargon
- Active voice, present tense when possible
- Concrete examples over abstract concepts

#### Visual Support Requirements

**Every elementary page must include:**

- [ ] Main illustration (drawing, painting, or photo)
- [ ] Map showing locations mentioned
- [ ] Timeline graphic (visual chronology)
- [ ] Vocabulary picture cards (if new words introduced)
- [ ] Character portraits (if people mentioned)

**Example:** Treaty of Holston page needs:

- Illustration of treaty signing ceremony
- Map showing Cherokee lands vs. U.S. territory
- Timeline: 1790 (Blount arrives) → 1791 (Treaty signed)
- Vocabulary cards: "treaty" (picture of document with signatures)
- Portraits: William Blount, Standing Turkey

#### Activity Integration

Make learning hands-on:

**For Treaty of Holston:**

- **Activity 1:** "Write your own peace treaty"
  - Students negotiate playground rules with another class
  - Sign a "treaty" on parchment paper
  - Discuss: Was it easy to agree? Why or why not?

- **Activity 2:** "Draw the treaty signing ceremony"
  - Provide reference images
  - Include: Who was there? Where did it happen? What did they wear?

- **Activity 3:** "Then vs. Now comparison"
  - Compare 1791 Tennessee map to current state boundaries
  - How has Tennessee changed?

#### Citation Template (Simplified)

```
Rocky Mount Website. "Treaty of Holston." Accessed January 30, 2026.
tennesseestartshere.com/evidence/documents/treaty-holston-1791
```

**Teaching tip:** Show students where to find this citation at bottom of page.

## TIER 2: Middle School (Grades 6-8)

**Cognitive Level:** Developing abstract thinking, identity exploration, justice-focused

**Reading Level:** 6th-8th grade (Lexile 850-1050)

### Adaptation Strategies

#### Complexity Level Increase

**Elementary version:**

> "They signed a peace treaty."

**Middle school version:**

> "The treaty attempted to establish lasting peace, but conflicts continued because American settlers kept moving onto Cherokee lands despite the agreement."

**What changes:**

- Introduce cause-and-effect relationships
- Acknowledge complexity ("attempted" not "achieved")
- Multiple perspectives (American vs. Cherokee experience)
- Moral/ethical dimensions (broken promises)

#### Critical Thinking Prompts

**For Treaty of Holston page:**

**Analyze multiple perspectives:**

> "Why might Cherokee leaders have different opinions about this treaty? What did they hope to gain? What risks did they take?"

**Evaluate evidence:**

> "What evidence can you find about women's roles during treaty negotiations? Whose perspectives are missing from historical records?"

**Question reliability:**

> "How do we know if a historical claim is true? Check this document's verification status. Why does it matter?"

#### Primary Source Skills Development

**Teach students to:**

- Close read historical passages (not just skim)
- Identify bias in documents (who wrote this? why?)
- Compare accounts of same event from different sources
- Use verification status to evaluate reliability
- Distinguish primary from secondary sources

**Activity:** Compare two accounts of Treaty of Holston

- U.S. government version (formal treaty text)
- Cherokee oral history (recorded later)
- What do they agree on? Disagree on? Why?

#### Research Projects (Scaffolded)

**Project 1: Cherokee Biography**

> "Choose one of the 42 Cherokee signatories. Research their life story using Rocky Mount sources. What did they do before the treaty? After? What happened to them?"

**Project 2: Frontier Life Comparison**

> "Compare frontier life for three different groups: Cherokee people, American settlers, and women (any background). How were their experiences different? Similar?"

**Project 3: Treaty Timeline**

> "Create a detailed timeline showing treaty negotiations from first meeting (Dec 1790) to signing (July 1791). What happened at each stage?"

## TIER 3: High School (Grades 9-12)

**Cognitive Level:** Abstract reasoning, analytical skills, preparing for college

**Reading Level:** 9th-12th grade (Lexile 1050-1335)

### Adaptation Strategies

#### Full Complexity (Minimal Simplification)

**Provide complete documents** with original 18th-century language:

> "Sir, On the 11th instant, I arrived in this Country, and found myself under the necessity of residing here, for want of the proper and necessary preparations at the Town of Knoxville..."

**Teach document analysis skills** through challenging texts, not around them.

#### Academic Skills Development

**Proper Citation Formats:**

- MLA 9th Edition (for English classes)
- APA 7th Edition (for social sciences)
- Chicago 17th Edition (for history)

**Students must learn:**

- Primary vs. secondary source distinction
- When to cite original date vs. access date
- How to evaluate source credibility (verification status)
- Constructing evidence-based arguments
- Recognizing historical bias and perspective

#### Research Capabilities

**Rocky Mount as primary source base:**

**Research Paper Topics:**

- "Cherokee Diplomatic Strategies in Treaty Negotiations"
- "Women's Invisible Labor on the Frontier"
- "The Economics of Frontier Tavern Keeping"
- "Broken Promises: Treaty Violations 1791-1800"

**Students can:**

- Use Rocky Mount sources as primary research materials
- Engage with disputed documents critically (teaching moment!)
- Compare Rocky Mount sources with other archives
- Contribute findings back to archive (student research program)

#### AP/Honors Extensions

**AP U.S. History alignment:**

- **Period 3 (1754-1800):** Rocky Mount = perfect primary source collection
- **Key Concept 3.3:** Migration and settlement
- **Key Concept 3.2:** Revolutionary government formation

**AP Seminar/Research:**

- Use Rocky Mount's verification system as case study in historical methodology
- Comparative treaty analysis (Holston vs. other federal-tribal treaties)
- Thesis development using primary sources

**Special Projects:**

**1. Disputed Document Analysis**

> "Examine the Andrew Jackson 1788 claim. What evidence exists? What's missing? How did historians reach 'oral-tradition' status? What does this teach us about evaluating historical claims?"

**2. Treaty Comparison Study**

> "Compare Treaty of Holston (1791) with Treaty of Hopewell (1785) and Treaty of Tellico (1798). How did Cherokee negotiating position change? Why?"

**3. Primary Source Digital Exhibit**

> "Curate a digital exhibit using Rocky Mount sources on a focused topic. Include verification notes, citations, and historical analysis."

## TIER 4: College Students & Researchers

**Cognitive Level:** Advanced analysis, original research, scholarly contribution

**Reading Level:** College+ (Lexile 1335+)

### Adaptation Strategies

#### No Simplification

**Full documents in original language/spelling:**

- Preserve "ye" (the), long s (ſ), period abbreviations
- Original date formats
- 18th-century capitalization and punctuation
- Spelling variations

**Complete scholarly apparatus:**

- Verification notes (all 3 layers documented)
- Provenance (where document came from, how acquired)
- Source citations (original archive catalog numbers)
- Cross-references (related documents, responses, context)

#### Research Support Features

**Citation-Ready Format:**

**MLA 9th Edition:**

```
Blount, William. "Letter to Secretary of War Henry Knox." Rocky Mount State Historic Site Evidence Room, 1790. Web. 30 Jan. 2026. <https://tennesseestartshere.com/evidence/documents/blount-arrival-1790>
```

**APA 7th Edition:**

```
Blount, W. (1790). Letter to Secretary of War Henry Knox. Retrieved from https://tennesseestartshere.com/evidence/documents/blount-arrival-1790
```

**Chicago 17th (Notes-Bibliography):**

```
William Blount, "Letter to Secretary of War Henry Knox," Rocky Mount State Historic Site Evidence Room, 1790, accessed January 30, 2026, https://tennesseestartshere.com/evidence/documents/blount-arrival-1790.
```

**Permanent URLs:** Never change, lesson plans built on these won't break

**Downloadable PDFs:** (Future feature - not yet implemented)

- High-resolution scans
- Transcription with annotations
- Metadata export (BibTeX, RIS formats)

#### Advanced Features

**Document-to-Document Connections:**

```yaml
connections:
  responds_to: blount-to-washington-1790-08
  responses:
    - knox-to-blount-1790-09
    - washington-to-blount-1790-10
  related_events:
    - treaty-holston-1791
  mentioned_people:
    - william-blount
    - henry-knox
```

**Timeline Filtering:**

- View all documents from 1790-1791
- Filter by document type (letters, treaties, speeches)
- Filter by person (all documents mentioning Standing Turkey)
- Filter by topic (all treaty-related documents)

#### Scholarly Collaboration

**Corrections Welcome:**

- Submit corrections with documentation
- Researchers can flag errors
- Suggest additional sources for verification

**Additional Sources:**

- Submit newly discovered documents for review
- Researchers share archive findings
- Contribute to collective knowledge

**Co-Authoring Opportunities:**

## TIER 6: Tourists & Casual Visitors

**Cognitive Level:** Engagement-driven, entertainment + education

**Purpose:** Make history accessible and interesting to general public

### Adaptation Strategies

#### Engagement Style

**NOT data-driven:**
❌ "The Treaty of Holston, ratified July 2, 1791, pursuant to Constitutional Article IX..."

**Story-driven:**
✅ "In 1791, 42 Cherokee chiefs traveled to Rocky Mount to negotiate peace. This is their story."

**Content Focus:**

- Most interesting/surprising stories
- "Did you know?" fascinating facts
- Local connections ("This happened HERE")
- Famous people connections (Washington, Cherokee leaders)
- Relatable human experiences (food, family, daily life)

#### "Did You Know?" Style Content

**Examples for different documents:**

**Treaty of Holston:**

> "Did you know 42 Cherokee chiefs visited Rocky Mount? Imagine feeding that many people in 1790—no grocery stores, no refrigeration, no restaurants. Barsheba Cobb's kitchen was a factory."

**Blount Arrival:**

> "Did you know the first governor of Tennessee lived RIGHT HERE? Before Nashville was capital, before Knoxville, this ground was where Tennessee government began."

**Rocky Mount Inventory:**

> "Did you know this house had GLASS WINDOWS in 1791? Glass had to be transported over mountains by wagon. Each pane was a luxury item. This was frontier wealth."

#### Quick Reads (2-3 Minutes)

**Structure:**

1. **Hook:** Surprising fact or question
2. **Story:** 2-3 short paragraphs, narrative style
3. **Visual:** Image, map, or timeline
4. **Takeaway:** What makes this significant

**Example:**

**Hook:** "Before Tennessee was Tennessee, it was THIS."

**Story:**

> When William Blount arrived on October 11, 1790, there was no Tennessee. No Nashville. No Knoxville. Just frontier settlements and Cherokee lands. This ground became the first territorial capital—America's westernmost government.

> For 14 months, Rocky Mount WAS Tennessee. Laws were written here. Treaties negotiated here. The first steps toward statehood happened right where you're standing.

> Then in late 1791, the capital moved to Knoxville. Rocky Mount's moment was brief, but it was the beginning of everything Tennessee became.

**Visual:** [Map showing Rocky Mount's location in 1790 + timeline]

**Takeaway:** "Tennessee started here. That's not tourism marketing—that's documented history."

#### Interactive Elements

**"Explore Connections" Links:**

- Click on person's name → See their biography
- Click on event → See related documents
- "What happened next?" → Follow story threads

**Social Media Sharing:**

- "Share this story" buttons
- Pre-written social media text: "I just learned that Tennessee's first capital was Rocky Mount! Before Nashville, before Knoxville, history happened here."

**Photo Opportunities:**

- "Stand where Cherokee chiefs stood" photo spot
- Historical costume photo booth (digital overlay)
- "Then and now" comparison photos

#### Accessibility Features

**Mobile-Friendly:**

- Readable on phones (large text, simple navigation)
- Works on slow connections (low image sizes)
- Offline mode (downloadable tours)

**Audio Descriptions:**

- Screen reader compatible
- Audio tours (listen while walking)
- Descriptions of visual elements for blind/low-vision visitors

**Plain Language:**

- 8th-grade reading level maximum
- Jargon-free
- Translations (Spanish, others as needed)

## TIER 7: Genealogists & Family Historians

**Cognitive Level:** Name/date focused, relationship mapping

**Purpose:** Help people research ancestors and family connections

### Adaptation Strategies

#### Specialized Features

**Name Index (Comprehensive):**

- All people mentioned in all documents
- Alphabetical listing with document references
- Variant spellings cross-referenced
- Maiden names (when known)

**Example Entry:**

```
Cobb, Barsheba [maiden name unknown]
  Mentioned in: rocky-mount-inventory-1791 (as "Barsheba Cobb")
  Role: Property owner's wife
  Related people: William Cobb (husband)
  Estimated dates: b. ca. 1750, d. unknown
  See also: cobb-william-biography
```

**Family Relationship Mapping:**

```yaml
person: william-cobb
relationships:
  spouse: mary-cobb
  children:
    - [names if known]
  parents:
    - [names if known]
  siblings:
    - [names if known]
```

**Geographic Origin Tracking:**

- Where did this person come from?
- Migration path (Virginia → North Carolina → Tennessee)
- Land ownership records

#### Genealogy-Specific Information

**Birth/Death Estimates:**

> "William Cobb: b. ca. 1740 (estimated from 1790 land records), d. after 1796 (last documented reference)"

**Naming Patterns:**

> "Cherokee names often appear in multiple forms: Standing Turkey = Kunnagatasee = Cunne Shote (different spellings, same person)"

**Family Connections:**

> "The Cobb family intermarried with Massengill family. William Cobb's grandson rebuilt the main house 1826-1830."

#### Research Guidance

**"Looking for Ancestors?" Guides:**

**Cobb Family Researchers:**

> "Start with rocky-mount-inventory-1791 (lists property). Then check Sullivan County deed books (1779+). Known Cobb descendants include Massengill family."

**Cherokee Genealogy:**

> "Cherokee genealogy is complex due to name variations and forced removals. Start with our Cherokee signatory biographies. Consult Cherokee Nation genealogy office for tribal records."

**Frontier Naming Patterns:**

> "18th-century spellings vary wildly. 'Blount' appears as 'Blunt,' 'Blownt,' etc. Search multiple variants. Use phonetic matching."

#### Contribution Opportunities

**Submit Family Documents:**

> "Do you have Cobb family letters, photos, or records? We're building the archive. Contact us to share (with permission)."

**Correct Family Relationships:**

> "If you've researched Cobb genealogy, help us fill gaps. Submit corrections with documentation."

**Connect Disparate Records:**

> "Found a Cobb in Virginia records + a Cobb in Tennessee records? Help us connect the dots. Family migration paths matter."

#### Caveat Notes

**Name Spelling Variations:**

> "18th-century spelling was not standardized. 'Cobb' might be 'Cob,' 'Cobbe,' 'Kobbe.' Same person, different clerk."

**Date Uncertainties:**

> "Birth/death dates before 1800 are often estimated. Census records began 1790 but didn't include names (just household counts) until 1850."

**Incomplete Records:**

> "Women's maiden names rarely appear in documents. Children's names often missing. Slavery = even fewer records. Genealogy has gaps."

## Content Adaptation Workflow

**For each new document added to Rocky Mount Evidence Room:**

### Step 1: Create Master Version (TIER 4 - Scholarly)

**Start with complete, unfiltered content:**

- Original language preserved
- Full scholarly apparatus
- All verification notes
- Complete citations

**Time required:** 6-8 hours (includes triple-check verification)

### Step 2: Write Interpretive Summary (TIER 6 - Tourists)

**Create engaging narrative version:**

- Story-driven, not data-driven
- "Did you know?" hooks
- 2-3 minute read
- Visual emphasis

**Time required:** 2-3 hours

### Step 3: Develop Three Simplified Versions

**Create grade-level adaptations:**

**TIER 1 (Elementary):**

- Simplify language (3rd-5th grade reading level)
- Add visual supports (maps, timelines, illustrations)
- Create hands-on activities
- Time required: 3-4 hours

**TIER 2 (Middle School):**

- Moderate complexity (6th-8th grade level)
- Add critical thinking prompts
- Include multiple perspectives
- Time required: 2-3 hours

**TIER 3 (High School):**

- Full complexity with minimal simplification
- Add citation examples
- Create research project suggestions
- Time required: 2 hours

### Step 4: Add Special Features

**TIER 5 (Educators):**

- Discussion questions for each level
- Activity suggestions
- Standards alignment
- Assessment ideas
- Time required: 2-3 hours

**TIER 7 (Genealogists):**

- Add to name index
- Map family relationships
- Note name variants
- Add birth/death estimates
- Time required: 1-2 hours

### Total Time per Document:

**18-25 hours for complete multi-tier adaptation**

### Resource Requirements:

- **Staff:** Historian + Educator + Web Developer
- **Review:** Director approval on all versions
- **Quality check:** Test with sample audience from each tier

## Quality Assurance

### Testing with Real Audiences

**Before publishing any adaptation:**

**Elementary version:**

- Test with 3rd-5th grade class
- Can they understand it?
- Do visuals help?
- Are activities age-appropriate?

**Middle school version:**

- Test with 6th-8th grade class
- Are critical thinking prompts challenging enough?
- Do students engage with complexity?

**High school version:**

- Test with AP/Honors class
- Is citation guidance clear?
- Are research projects feasible?

**Tourist version:**

- Test with non-expert adults
- Is it interesting? (Not just accurate)
- 2-3 minute read time confirmed?

### Revision Based on Feedback

**If testing reveals problems:**

- Too complex → Simplify further
- Too simple → Add depth
- Boring → Add narrative hooks
- Confusing → Add visual aids

**Continuous improvement:**

- Survey teachers after using materials
- Track which content gets used most
- Update annually based on feedback
