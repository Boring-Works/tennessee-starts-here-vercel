# Evidence Archive Cherokee Representation Audit

**Date:** January 29, 2026
**Scope:** Comprehensive analysis of how Cherokee peoples are represented across the Evidence archive (documents, people, collections, timeline)
**Total Documents Reviewed:** 38 documents + 50 people entries + 5 collections + 1 timeline

---

## Executive Summary

The Evidence archive contains **strong representations of Cherokee agency and leadership** but suffers from fundamental **framing problems** inherited from settler-written sources. The archive successfully preserves Cherokee signatories and leaders but frames events primarily through settler voices, emphasizing American expansion rather than Cherokee perspectives on their own dispossession.

**Key Finding:** 68% of documents (26/38) mention Cherokee peoples, but 89% of those are settler-authored (letters, proclamations, newspapers). Only 1 document is the treaty itself (which includes Cherokee signatures).

## 2. HOW ARE CHEROKEE DESCRIBED?

### Voice Analysis

#### Passive Voice (Cherokee as objects)

**Pattern 1: "Cherokee lands ceded/opened"**

- `/content/documents/blount-to-knox-1791-11.md:28` — "The lands ceded by the Cherokee are being surveyed and opened for settlement"
- `/content/documents/knoxville-gazette-1791-11-05.md:34` — "the lands recently acquired from the Cherokee Nation are being opened for settlement"
- `/content/documents/knox-to-blount-1791-04.md:28` — "you will endeavor to obtain a cession of as much land as can be procured"

**Problem:** These passages use passive construction that obscures who is doing the action. "Lands are opened" vs. "American settlers are colonizing Cherokee lands."

**Pattern 2: "Cherokee acknowledged/recognized"**

- `/content/documents/treaty-holston-1791.md:41` — "The Cherokee Nation acknowledges to be under the protection of the United States"
- `/content/documents/knox-to-blount-1791-04.md:26` — "The treaty should establish definite boundaries"

**Problem:** American officials "establish" or "clarify" boundaries; Cherokee perspectives on their own territorial claims are rarely centered.

#### Active Voice (Cherokee as agents)

**Strong Examples:**

- `/content/people/hanging-maw.md:21-25` — "Hanging Maw was the leading chief... His stature among the Overhill towns gave him credibility... Hanging Maw was instrumental in bringing reluctant Cherokee factions to the negotiating table"
- `/content/people/bloody-fellow.md:21-26` — "Bloody Fellow rose to prominence... became one of the Cherokee Nation's most effective diplomatic spokesmen... his willingness to sign the treaty gave it legitimacy among Cherokee"
- `/content/people/john-watts.md:23` — "John Watts succeeded him as head of the Cherokee war council... quickly proved himself a capable military commander"

**Assessment:** Cherokee people pages (42 individuals) demonstrate strong agency, but these are marginalized compared to settler narratives in documents.

## 3. WHAT'S MISSING?

### Critical Absences

#### 1. Cherokee-Authored Perspectives on the Treaty

**Missing:** Any Cherokee written account or speech explaining their rationale for the Treaty of Holston.

**Context:** We have 10+ letters from Blount explaining his negotiations, but zero Cherokee accounts of what they gained, lost, or feared.

**Why It Matters:** The archive frames the treaty as "Blount achieves peace" when Cherokee leaders were making calculated decisions under duress (Dragging Canoe's resistance, settler encroachment, military defeat of Cherokee towns).

#### 2. Context of Land Seizure and Broken Agreements

**Missing:** Documentation of American settlers illegally occupying Cherokee lands AFTER the treaty.

**What We Have:** ONE brief mention in `/content/documents/knoxville-gazette-1793-03-16.md:25-27`:

- "Some disturbances have occurred on the frontier, arising from the encroachments of settlers on lands reserved to the Cherokee"
- "A deputation of Cherokee chiefs has visited Knoxville to complain of these encroachments"

**What We Don't Have:**

- The scale of illegal settlement
- Cherokee formal objections (beyond "they complained")
- The government's failure to enforce the treaty
- How this drove Cherokee back to warfare

**Impact:** Readers see "peace established" but not "peace immediately violated."

#### 3. Dragging Canoe and the Chickamauga Resistance

**What We Have:** Brief mentions that some Cherokee rejected the treaty.

**What We Don't Have:**

- Dragging Canoe's speeches or justifications
- Why the Chickamauga rejected accommodation (rightfully, as history proved)
- The legitimacy of their resistance argument

**Archive Bias:** The peace faction (Hanging Maw, Bloody Fellow after 1792) gets detailed biographies. The war faction (Dragging Canoe's legacy, John Watts' military campaign) gets 1-2 sentences framing them as obstacles to peace.

#### 4. Cherokee Women's Roles

**Missing:** Any mention of Cherokee women's political authority (Cherokee had matrilineal society where women held significant power).

**Exception:** Only `/content/people/hanging-maw.md:33` mentions "his wife" was wounded in a settler attack, but not her political significance.

**Archive Impact:** Entirely male-centric leadership structure presented; Cherokee social/political system invisible.

#### 5. Economic Context of the Treaty

**What We Have:**

- "annual payment of $1,000" (increased to $1,500)

**What We Don't Have:**

- What this money represented (compensation? tribute?)
- How it compared to the value of ceded lands
- Whether Cherokee actually received goods "suitable to their circumstances"

**Framing Problem:** Blount's letter says payment will "contribute to their comfort and civilization" (patronizing), but no documents verify whether it actually helped or created dependency.

#### 6. Specific Cherokee Leadership Context

**Named signatories present:** Hanging Maw, Bloody Fellow, John Watts, Doublehead, Black Fox, Standing Turkey, The Boots

**Not explained in Archive:**

- Why THESE leaders represented Cherokee (not all Cherokee approved)
- What factions they came from
- Why approximately 35+ others signed but aren't individually featured

**Archive gap:** We have 42 Cherokee signatories named in the treaty, but only 7 have dedicated people pages.

## 5. PROBLEMATIC FRAMINGS (With Specific Examples)

### Frame 1: "Settlement as Progress"

**Examples:**

- `/content/documents/blount-to-knox-1791-11.md:28` — "The lands ceded by the Cherokee are being surveyed and opened for settlement, which will add to the strength and resources of the territory."
- `/content/documents/knoxville-gazette-1791-11-05.md:34` — "New settlers arrive constantly from the old states, and the lands recently acquired from the Cherokee Nation are being opened for settlement."
- `/content/documents/knoxville-gazette-1792-07-07.md:29` — "The population of the territory has increased remarkably since the conclusion of the treaty. New families arrive weekly from Virginia, North Carolina, and other states, and settlements are extending rapidly into the lands acquired from the Cherokee."

**Problem:** Frames Cherokee land loss as positive "progress" and "prosperity." No equivalent perspective: "The Cherokee Nation lost ancestral lands; their territorial control shrank."

**Fix:** Add contextual note: "This framing represents the settler perspective. Cherokee peoples viewed the same events as dispossession and treaty violation."

### Frame 2: "Civilization" as Goal

**Examples:**

- `/content/documents/blount-to-knox-1791-07.md:31` — "The sum agreed upon is one thousand dollars, to be paid in goods suitable to their circumstances, which I hope will contribute to their comfort and civilization."
- `/content/documents/blount-to-knox-1791-06.md:31` — "I have assured them that the President wishes to protect them in the enjoyment of their remaining lands and to promote their civilization and happiness."

**Problem:** 18th-century language using "civilization" as code for "assimilation." Implies Cherokee society was uncivilized. Patronizing tone masked as benevolence.

**Fix:** Add historian's note: "This language reflects the document author's bias. Cherokee had complex, sophisticated governance, agriculture, and trade systems."

### Frame 3: "Reluctance" as Weakness

**Examples:**

- `/content/documents/blount-to-knox-1791-06.md:29` — "The chiefs are naturally reluctant to cede lands which they have long occupied, but they appear to recognize that some accommodation must be made."

**Problem:** Frames Cherokee resistance to land loss as "reluctance" (emotional/irrational) rather than principled resistance. "Some accommodation must be made" treats dispossession as inevitable.

**Fix:** "The chiefs resisted ceding lands, understanding the long-term consequences of American expansion, but faced military pressure and settler encroachment that forced negotiations."

### Frame 4: "Peace as Cherokee Victory"

**Examples:**

- `/content/documents/treaty-holston-1791.md:27` — "The treaty aimed to establish permanent peace and remove causes of war through boundary clarification and friendly arrangements"

**Problem:** Positions treaty signing as Cherokee choice, hiding that it was signed under coercion:

- American military superiority (attacks on Cherokee towns 1780)
- Ongoing settler encroachment
- Divided Cherokee leadership (war faction skeptical)

**Reality:** Cherokee got a temporary ceasefire and $1,000/year for millions of acres.

**Fix:** Acknowledge "While presented as a peace treaty, Cherokee leaders signed under pressure from military defeats and continued settler expansion."

### Frame 5: "Government Protects Cherokee"

**Examples:**

- `/content/documents/washington-to-knox-1790-08.md:29` — "The Cherokee Nation, being the most considerable of the tribes in that quarter, should be cultivated with care"
- `/content/documents/knox-to-blount-1791-04.md:28` — "you will endeavor to obtain a cession of as much land as can be procured consistently with the principles of justice"

**Problem:** Positions federal government as Cherokee protector while actively removing them from their lands.

**Reality Check:** Within 3 years (1793), settlers occupied Cherokee lands in violation of the treaty. Government did little to stop it.

**Fix:** "The federal government's stated intent was to 'protect' Cherokee lands while actively encouraging settler expansion into them."

Every document involving Cherokee land and treaty should include a visible note:

```markdown
**Perspective Note:** This document was created by [American official/settler].
```

### Priority 2: Connect to Cherokee-Centered Resources

Add a "See Also" section to every document mentioning Cherokee:

```markdown
**Cherokee Perspectives & Context:**
```

The treaty lists 42 names. Expand the page to include all names, then gradually research biographical pages for the 35+ missing signatories.

### Priority 4: Create "Missing Perspectives" Documents

- "Cherokee-Authored Accounts: What's Missing"

## 12. QUESTIONS STILL UNANSWERED BY THE ARCHIVE

1. **What did the Cherokee signatories actually believe they were agreeing to?**
   - Treaty says "permanent peace" but also involves land cessions
   - Were all signatories aware they were ceding lands?
   - Did Cherokee understand "under the protection of the United States" language?

2. **Why did the Chickamauga faction allow Dragging Canoe's death (1792) to end their leadership without fully endorsing the treaty?**
   - John Watts and others signed but continued raids
   - What was their actual position?

3. **How did the $1,000 annual payment actually function?**
   - Did Cherokee receive goods promised?
   - Did it create dependency or economic support?
   - What did Cherokee think of receiving money for stolen land?

4. **What happened to the 35+ signatories after 1791?**
   - How long did they live?
   - Did they maintain the treaty commitment?
   - Were any later executed or exiled?

5. **Did any Cherokee leaders publicly repudiate the treaty afterward?**
   - No evidence in the archive
   - Absence of evidence or evidence of absence?

## CONCLUSION

The Tennessee Starts Here Evidence archive represents Cherokee peoples with **more nuance than typical settler histories**, but remains fundamentally limited by:

1. **Source base:** Almost exclusively settler-authored documents
2. **Narrative framing:** Settlement/expansion as progress, not dispossession
3. **Missing voices:** No Cherokee accounts of their own experiences
4. **Incomplete coverage:** 42 signatories named, ~7 documented
5. **Unchallenged assumptions:** "Civilization," inevitability, protectionism

**The fix is not to erase settler voices**, but to:

- Mark them as such
- Acknowledge what's missing
- Document the Cherokee leaders who are named
- Create space for counter-narratives
- Show that Cherokee had legitimate grievances within months of the treaty

With these additions, the archive could model how to tell a complex, multi-voiced colonial history rather than a simple settler narrative.
